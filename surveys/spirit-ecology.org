* Purpose

This survey maps the spirit ecology of the Western Himalaya — from the
Pamirs through Karakoram, Ladakh, Zanskar, Spiti, Kinnaur, Kullu, and
into the Nepal Himalaya. It serves three concrete purposes:

1. *Story composition*: vocabulary, geography, and customs for stories
   set across the transborder Himalaya
2. *Agent ontology*: grounded functional mapping from Himalayan spirit
   types to Aburaya archetypes
3. *Digital twin*: a spirit-ecology data layer encoding human-landscape
   interaction at depth no geophysical sensor reaches

The survey follows WP-0003. Its output feeds WP-0004 (gazetteer schema)
and enriches the story-compose power (=aburaya/powers/story-compose.md=).

* Part 1: Regional Spirit Taxonomies

** 1.1 Kullu Valley (Himachal Pradesh)

The Kullu valley sustains one of the densest living devta systems in
the Himalaya. The district administration recognises approximately 534
devtas — village deities with juridical standing, temple estates, and
ritual calendars. [confidence: *established*]

*** Spirit Types

**** Devta (देवता) — Village Deities

The devta is not an abstraction but a juridical person. Each devta
owns land (recorded in revenue records), maintains a temple estate,
employs ritual specialists, and adjudicates community disputes through
oracular trance. The devta's jurisdiction maps to a territorial unit
— typically a village or cluster of hamlets. [*established*; Chetwode
1972, Rosser 1955 fieldwork]

Key devtas include:
- *Raghunath ji* — the paramount devta of Kullu, installed by Raja
  Jagat Singh in the 17th century. All other devtas are nominally
  subordinate. His temple in Kullu town is the centre of the Dussehra
  rath festival. [*established*]
- *Hadimba Devi* (Dhungri temple, Manali) — the rakshasi-turned-goddess,
  consort of Bhima. A temple of extraordinary wooden architecture in a
  deodar grove. [*established*]
- *Jamlu Devta* (Malana) — the deity of Malana village, which claims
  democratic self-governance under Jamlu's authority. Malana's legal
  system operates through Jamlu's oracle. Outsiders historically
  forbidden to touch village structures. [*established*]
- *Shringa Rishi* (Banjar) — associated with the sage Shringa, whose
  jurisdiction extends over a large area of the Tirthan/Banjar valleys.
  [*likely*]

The devta system is not purely Hindu. Many devtas are pre-Vedic local
deities who were incorporated into a Hindu framework. Their rituals
retain non-Sanskritic elements: animal sacrifice (now contested),
oracular possession, and procession customs that have no Vedic
parallel. [*established*; this is the scholarly consensus]

**** Nāg Devta (नाग देवता) — Serpent Spirits

Water spirits associated with springs, streams, lakes, and underground
water sources. In Kullu, nāg worship is ubiquitous — nearly every
significant water source has a nāg association. [*established*]

- Associated with fertility, rainfall, and the health of water sources
- Propitiated before digging, well-sinking, or construction near water
- Offending a nāg (polluting a spring, cutting a tree near a spring)
  causes skin disease, crop failure, or drought [*likely*]
- Buddhi Nagin (the Old Serpent Goddess) resides at Serolsar Lake
  above Jalori Pass — a key figure in the project's stories [*established*]

**** Gur (गूर) — Oracle/Medium

The gur is the devta's voice — a human who enters trance and speaks
as the devta. This is not metaphorical: the community understands the
gur's utterances in trance as the devta's direct speech. [*established*]

The gur tradition is hereditary in some cases, selected by the devta
in others. During festivals and crises, the gur enters trance
(typically accompanied by drumming), shakes violently, and delivers
pronouncements on community matters: disputes, agricultural decisions,
illness diagnosis, auspicious timing. [*established*]

**** Jogini (जोगिनी) — Wandering Female Spirits

Female spirits associated with specific places but not bound to
territorial jurisdiction the way devtas are. More mobile, more
ambiguous in disposition. In some traditions, joginis are the spirits
of women who died without proper rites, or ascetics who achieved a
particular spiritual state. [*likely*; thinner source material]

In the Aburaya context, the jogini maps to the wandering-practice
archetype — a spirit that carries a skill across territories rather
than guarding one place. [project interpretation]

**** Bajantri (बजन्त्री) — Ritual Musicians

Not spirits themselves but the human specialists who enable spirit
contact. Bajantris play specific instruments (dhol, nagara, shehnai,
karnal) whose rhythmic patterns are understood to invoke and sustain
the devta's presence in the gur. Without bajantris, the gur cannot
enter trance. [*established*]

**** Kardar (करदार) — Temple Steward

The administrative manager of a devta's estate — handles finances,
organises festivals, maintains the temple. A civic role with spiritual
authority. [*established*]

**** Pret (प्रेत) — Restless Dead

Spirits of those who died violently, untimely, or without proper
funerary rites. Prets are dangerous — they cause illness, madness,
and misfortune. Exorcism requires the devta's intervention through the
gur. [*established*; pan-South-Asian category]

*** Human Interfaces

- *Rath festival*: Chariot processions where devtas "visit" each other.
  The Kullu Dussehra is the largest: Raghunath ji receives tributary
  devtas from across the valley, carried on palanquins (raths). This
  is simultaneously a religious festival, a political assembly, and
  an agricultural calendar marker. [*established*; major tourist event,
  well-documented]
- *Gur trance protocol*: Drumming by bajantris → gur trembles → full
  trance → devta speaks → community questions → pronouncement →
  recovery. The trance is publicly witnessed. [*established*]
- *Dispute resolution*: Community disputes (land, water, marriage) are
  brought before the devta via the gur. The devta's pronouncement has
  customary legal force. [*established*; documented conflict with state
  legal system]

*** Diagnostic Functions

The devta system encodes:
- *Water management*: nāg worship protects springs and water sources
  from pollution and overexploitation. Taboos against contamination
  are enforced by fear of nāg displeasure. [*established*]
- *Forest conservation*: sacred groves (devta forests) are protected
  from logging. These are often old-growth deodar forests. [*established*]
- *Social cohesion*: the rath festival system creates inter-village
  alliances through ritual exchange. Devta "friendships" and "enmities"
  encode historical relationships between communities. [*likely*]
- *Psychological diagnosis*: illness attributed to pret or devta
  displeasure is diagnosed through the gur. While the ontology is
  spiritual, the diagnostic process functions as community-level
  psychological assessment — the gur names the problem publicly.
  [*inferred*; standard interpretation in medical anthropology]

** 1.2 Kinnaur (Himachal Pradesh)

Kinnaur sits between Hindu Kullu and Buddhist Spiti — a border region
where both systems interpenetrate. The lower Kinnaur valleys are Hindu-
dominant; upper Kinnaur (approaching the Tibet border) is increasingly
Buddhist. The transition is not abrupt but layered. [*established*]

*** Spirit Types

**** Devta System (lower Kinnaur)

The devta system resembles Kullu's but with distinctive features:
- Local deities with temple estates and oracular traditions
- More visible Buddhist influence even in "Hindu" villages — Buddhist
  prayer flags alongside Hindu temples [*likely*]
- Kinnauri deities include local forms of Durga, Shiva, and Vishnu,
  but also pre-Hindu spirits of uncertain classification [*likely*]

**** Buddhist Spirit Categories (upper Kinnaur)

As one moves toward the Tibet border, the spirit taxonomy shifts
toward Tibetan categories:
- *Lha* (ལྷ) — territorial deities, mountain gods
- *Klu/Lu* (ཀླུ) — water spirits (cognate with nāga)
- *Gnyan* (གཉན) — spirits of rocks and trees

These coexist with Hindu categories without clear boundary. A village
may propitiate both a devta and a klu at different seasonal moments.
[*inferred*; based on similar dynamics documented in Ladakh and Lahaul]

**** Nāg Worship

Nāg worship is pervasive throughout Kinnaur, bridging the Hindu-
Buddhist divide. Serpent spirits of high-altitude lakes are recognised
by both traditions. The nāg/klu equivalence makes this one of the
easiest spirit categories to trace across the cultural boundary.
[*likely*]

*** Landscape Associations

| Feature | Spirit association | Notes |
|---------+--------------------+-------|
| Kinnaur Kailash (6,500m) | Shiva's winter residence | Parikrama (circumambulation) pilgrimage route [*established*] |
| Nako Lake | Buddhist sacred, likely klu association | Near Nako village, upper Kinnaur [*inferred*] |
| Sutlej gorge | Dangerous passage, spirit territory | The gorge marks a geological and cultural boundary |
| Sacred deodar groves | Devta forests | Similar to Kullu; conservation function |
| Baspa Valley (Sangla) | Mixed Hindu-Buddhist | Kamru fort: the castle-temple complex |
| Shipki La (trade route) | Pass spirits, border guardians | Trade route to Tibet, now restricted |

*** Human Interfaces

- *Oracle/medium tradition (gur/grokch)*: Kinnaur has a living oracle
  tradition continuous with Kullu's gur system. Oracles are known
  locally as *malis*, *gur*, or *grokch*. During specific rituals, the
  oracle enters trance and is possessed by the village deity, delivering
  pronouncements understood as the deity's direct will. Every village of
  Kinnaur has its own local deity governing daily life through this
  oracle system. [*established*; verified via web research, Kinnaur
  tourism documentation]
- *Mask dances*: Kinnauri Buddhist festivals include mask dances (cham)
  where wrathful deities and spirits are enacted. These are shared with
  Spiti and Ladakh. [*established*]
- *Dual priesthood*: in transitional villages, both Hindu pujaris and
  Buddhist lamas serve — sometimes within the same festival calendar.
  [*likely*; documented in Lahaul, probably applies here too]
- *Flaich/Fulaich Ukhayang festival*: A festival of flowers celebrated
  in September. The village deity is carried in procession with a band.
  A he-goat is sacrificed. A highlight is when the priest utters an
  incantation causing the grokch/gur to be possessed by the divine
  spirit, uttering the commands of the deity while temple musicians
  play. [*established*; verified via web research]
- *Kinnauri polyandry*: the fraternal polyandry practiced in parts of
  Kinnaur has been linked by some scholars to resource management in
  harsh mountain environments. Whether the spirit system encodes or
  legitimates this social arrangement requires fieldwork. [*gap*]

*** Diagnostic Functions

- *Border mediation*: the spirit system mediates the Hindu-Buddhist
  boundary, providing a shared vocabulary (nāg/klu) that transcends
  the sectarian divide. [*inferred*]
- *Trade route knowledge*: spirits of the passes encode dangers and
  protocols for the Tibet trade route — now largely defunct since the
  1962 border closure. [*inferred*]
- *Altitude adaptation*: the shift from devta to lha with increasing
  altitude tracks the ecological transition from temperate to trans-
  Himalayan landscape. The spirit taxonomy is an altitude map. [*inferred*]

** 1.3 Spiti and Lahaul (Himachal Pradesh)

Spiti is a high desert valley (3,800-4,500m) on the Tibetan side of
the main Himalayan range — geographically, climatically, and culturally
closer to Tibet than to the Indian plains. Lahaul sits between Spiti
and Kullu, spanning the cultural transition. [*established*]

*** Spirit Types

Spiti uses the Tibetan spirit taxonomy, locally inflected:

**** Lha (ལྷ) — Mountain/Territorial Deities

Mountain gods who guard valleys and communities. Each major peak has
a lha. The lha is propitiated with smoke offerings (sang) at cairns
(la-btsas) and on high ground. [*established*; standard across
Tibetan cultural zone]

**** Klu/Lu (ཀླུ) — Water Spirits

Critically important in Spiti's arid landscape. The klu control water —
springs, snowmelt, the Spiti River itself. Offending klu causes drought,
spring failure, or flooding. Propitiation involves offerings at water
sources and avoiding pollution of springs. [*established*]

In an arid landscape at 4,000m, water is the limiting resource. The
klu traditions encode water management knowledge with existential
urgency. [*inferred*]

**** Sa-bdag (ས་བདག) — Earth Lords

Spirits of the ground, disturbed by digging, construction, ploughing.
Before any earthwork, sa-bdag must be propitiated. In Spiti's fragile
mountain terrain, where a wrong cut can trigger landslides, the sa-bdag
tradition encodes geological caution. [*established*; standard Tibetan]

**** Btsan (བཙན) — Warrior/Cliff Spirits

Aggressive spirits associated with red rocks, cliffs, and sites of
violent death. Btsan are dangerous and require strong ritual to manage.
In Spiti's dramatic cliff landscapes (Dhankar, Ki), btsan associations
would be natural. [*likely*; specific Spiti btsan sites need fieldwork]

**** Dharma Protectors (chos-skyong)

Fierce deities incorporated into Buddhist practice — originally local
spirits "tamed" by Buddhist masters (especially Padmasambhava and
Rinchen Zangpo in this region) and bound as protectors of the dharma.
The monastery murals at Tabo and Ki depict these figures prominently.
[*established*]

*** Lahaul: The Transition Zone

Lahaul (Chandra-Bhaga valley) is the bridge between Hindu Kullu and
Buddhist Spiti. The spirit ecology reflects this:

- *Lower Lahaul* (Pattan valley): Hindu devta traditions, similar to Kullu
- *Upper Lahaul* (near Keylong): Buddhist monasteries, Tibetan spirit categories
- *Mixed villages*: both Hindu and Buddhist shrines, dual ritual calendars
- *Markula Devi* (Udaipur): a major goddess temple in Lahaul, suggesting
  a goddess-centred tradition predating both Hindu and Buddhist frameworks.
  [*likely*]
- *Raja Ghepan*: one of the major deities of Lahaul, widely worshipped
  across the region. [*established*; verified via web research]

**** Pre-Buddhist Religion: Lung Pe Chhoi

Before adapting to Tibetan Buddhism and Hinduism, the people of Spiti
followed a religion called *Lung Pe Chhoi*, derived from the Bön
religion of Tibet. This was associated with shamanic rituals of medicine
men (amchis). Remnants survive in folk practice alongside Buddhist
observance. [*established*; verified via web research]

**** Lhalung Devta

In Lhalung village, the Lhalung Devta is said to be the head of all
the devtas of the valley. The devta emerges from the Tangmar mountain,
which changes colour depending on the deity's moods — red showing anger,
yellow showing happiness. This is a striking example of the devta system
extending into nominally Buddhist Spiti, suggesting a pre-Buddhist
substrate. [*established*; verified via web research]

The Lahaul transition is geographically abrupt — the Rohtang Pass
(3,978m) separates the lush Kullu valley from the stark Chandra valley.
Spirit ecologies on either side of the pass differ sharply.
[*established*: the ecological transition is documented; the spirit
ecology transition is *inferred*]

*** Human Interfaces

- *Monastic ritualists*: in Spiti, the Buddhist monasteries are the
  primary interface with spirits. Monks perform propitiation, exorcism,
  and seasonal rituals. The monastery is not just a religious centre but
  a spirit-management institution. [*established*]
- *Cham dances*: masked ritual dances at Ki, Tabo, and other monasteries.
  Specific masks represent dharma protectors, wrathful deities, and
  local spirits tamed by Buddhist masters. The dances are performed at
  Losar (New Year) and specific calendar dates. [*established*]
- *Losar celebrations*: Spiti follows the Tibetan calendar. Losar
  involves elaborate spirit propitiation — driving out negative
  energies, inviting beneficial spirits for the new year. [*established*]
- *Lama Astrologer*: lamas serve as astrologers, determining auspicious
  dates for planting, construction, and travel based partly on spirit
  considerations (which sa-bdag are disturbed when, etc.). [*likely*]

*** Diagnostic Functions

- *Water management*: klu traditions in an arid landscape encode
  critical water-source protection knowledge [*inferred*]
- *Geological hazard*: sa-bdag traditions warn against digging in
  unstable terrain — functionally sound in Spiti's landslide-prone
  geography [*inferred*]
- *Seasonal timing*: spirit calendars align with the agricultural cycle
  in a valley with only ~100 frost-free days [*likely*]
- *Trade route safety*: pass spirits encode knowledge of dangerous
  crossings, weather patterns, avalanche risk [*inferred*]

** 1.4 Ladakh and Zanskar

Ladakh (the "land of mountain passes") is culturally Tibetan — Buddhist
monasteries, Tibetan spirit categories, and oracle traditions. Zanskar,
its isolated sub-valley, preserves some of the most conservative forms.
[*established*]

*** Spirit Types

The Ladakhi spirit taxonomy closely follows the Tibetan standard, with
local inflections:

**** Lha (ལྷ) — Mountain Gods/Territorial Deities

Every village and every significant mountain has a lha. The lha is
the lord of the territory — a protective spirit whose domain maps to
geographical features. The lha is propitiated with smoke offerings
(sang) and prayer flags. [*established*; Crook & Osmaston 1994]

**** Klu/Lu (ཀླུ) — Water Spirits

Cognate with Sanskrit nāga. Inhabit springs, lakes, and underground
water. Klu are easily offended by water pollution, digging near
springs, and disturbance of wet ground. Klu displeasure causes skin
disease (leprosy, boils), drought, and infertility. [*established*;
standard across Tibetan cultural zone]

Specific associations:
- Pangong Tso and Tso Moriri: high-altitude lakes with strong klu
  associations [*likely*]
- Every village spring has its klu [*established*]

**** Btsan (བཙན) — Warrior Spirits of Red Rock

Aggressive spirits of cliff faces, red rock, and sites of violent
death. Btsan are among the most dangerous spirits — warlike,
unpredictable, requiring strong ritual to contain. They are
associated with the characteristic red-brown rock formations of
Ladakh's geology. [*established*; Nebesky-Wojkowitz 1956]

**** Sa-bdag (ས་བདག) — Earth Lords

Lords of the ground. Must be consulted before any construction,
ploughing, or earthwork. The lama-astrologer determines which sa-bdag
are "awake" on which days, directing when and where digging is safe.
In Ladakh's seismically active terrain, this encodes real geological
caution. [*established*]

**** 'Dre (འདྲེ) — Demons/Malevolent Spirits

A broad category of harmful spirits causing illness, madness, and
misfortune. 'Dre attack is diagnosed by the oracle or lama and treated
through ritual exorcism. [*established*]

**** Zhidak (གཞི་བདག) — Place Owners/Masters

Spirits that "own" a specific place — not the territorial lha (who
protects) but a more local presence that must be acknowledged. Every
unusual geological formation, ancient tree, or uncanny spot has a
zhidak. [*likely*; documented in Tibetan ethnography]

**** Tsen (བཙན) — Cliff Spirits

Sometimes conflated with btsan; in some traditions a separate category
of crag-dwelling spirits. [*need clarification*: the btsan/tsen
distinction may be dialectal rather than taxonomic]

*** Human Interfaces

**** Lha-mo and Lha-pa — Oracles

Ladakh has a living oracle tradition. The lha-mo (female oracle) and
lha-pa (male oracle) enter trance and are possessed by specific
spirits — sometimes the village lha, sometimes a dharma protector.
[*established*; Crook & Osmaston 1994]

The oracle tradition in Ladakh is structurally parallel to the Kullu
gur system: a human medium enters trance, a spirit speaks, the community
consults. The key differences:
- Ladakhi oracles operate within Buddhist institutional context
- The possessing spirits include dharma protectors (Buddhist overlay)
- Monks and lamas sanction and sometimes supervise the oracle [*likely*]
- The oracle's role includes medical diagnosis, conflict resolution,
  and agricultural advice — identical functions to the Kullu gur
  [*established*]

**** Monastic Ritual

Monks perform regular spirit-management rituals:
- *Sang* (smoke offerings): juniper and incense burned on high ground
  to please lha and purify the atmosphere [*established*]
- *Gtor-ma* offerings: sculpted torma offered to specific spirits
  [*established*]
- *Exorcism*: monks perform elaborate rituals to drive out 'dre and
  negative influences [*established*]

**** Festivals

- *Losar* (New Year): major spirit-clearing and renewal festival
- *Hemis Tse-Chu*: masked dances at Hemis monastery, depicting
  Padmasambhava's taming of spirits [*established*; major tourist event]
- *Matho Nagrang*: at the only Sakyapa sect monastery in Ladakh, two
  monks (rongtsan) are chosen by lottery and undergo two months of
  meditation, fasting, and isolation. During the festival, they enter
  deep trance, believed possessed by deities, and perform extraordinary
  feats: leaping between balconies, marching blindfolded around top-
  story ramparts, making knife incisions on themselves. They predict
  the future for individuals and the community. [*established*; verified
  via web research — Matho Nagrang is well-documented]

**** Amchi — Traditional Medicine

Amchi (traditional Tibetan-derived medicine) diagnoses some illnesses as
spirit-caused ('dre attack, klu displeasure). The amchi may refer such
cases to the oracle or lama for ritual treatment. [*likely*]

*** Diagnostic Functions

- *Water source protection*: klu traditions protect the scarce water
  sources in Ladakh's desert landscape [*established*]
- *Construction safety*: sa-bdag consultation prevents earthwork in
  unstable terrain [*inferred*; same logic as Spiti]
- *Psychological health*: oracle consultation provides community-
  sanctioned space for expressing and processing distress [*inferred*]
- *Seasonal calendar*: spirit-based timing encodes agricultural and
  pastoral knowledge [*likely*]

** 1.5 Tibet and Bön

The Tibetan spirit taxonomy is the most elaborated in the Himalayan
world — a vast, systematic classification that was partially codified
by Bön practitioners and partially absorbed into Buddhist ritual.
Nebesky-Wojkowitz (1956) remains the standard Western reference.
[*established*]

*** Spirit Types

**** Yul-lha (ཡུལ་ལྷ) — Country Gods

Territorial spirits of landscape features — mountains, valleys, entire
regions. The yul-lha is the genius loci, the spirit of the place
itself. Major mountains have named yul-lha:
- *Nyenchen Tanglha* — the great mountain god of central Tibet,
  controlling weather and the wellbeing of the surrounding region
  [*established*]
- *Amnye Machen* — the mountain god of the Golok Tibetan region
  [*established*]
- Every village has its local yul-lha, typically the most prominent
  nearby mountain [*established*]

**** Sa-bdag (ས་བདག) — Earth Lords

The lords of the soil. A complex system of earth spirits who "rule"
different patches of ground on rotating schedules. Tibetan almanacs
specify which sa-bdag is active where on each day, making them a
calendar-based navigational system for earthwork. [*established*]

**** Klu (ཀླུ) — Water Spirits

Cognate with Sanskrit nāga. Control all water — springs, lakes, rivers,
rain, underground water. The klu realm is the underworld/underwater
world, sometimes depicted as a wealthy kingdom. Klu displeasure causes
skin disease and drought. Klu worship involves offerings at water
sources and strict prohibitions against pollution. [*established*]

**** Btsan (བཙན) — Warrior Spirits

Red, fierce, mounted on red horses. Associated with red rock, cliffs,
and violent death. The btsan are aggressive spirits that attack
travellers and communities. They require strong ritual containment.
Some were "tamed" by Padmasambhava and converted into dharma
protectors. [*established*; Nebesky-Wojkowitz 1956]

**** Gnyan (གཉན) — Spirits of Trees and Rocks

The gnyan inhabit the intermediate zone — trees, boulders, rock
outcrops. They are "touchier" than lha but less aggressive than btsan.
Cutting a gnyan tree or disturbing a gnyan rock causes illness and
misfortune. [*established*; Nebesky-Wojkowitz 1956]

**** Dmu — Sky Spirits (Bön)

In Bön cosmology, the dmu are sky spirits — associated with the upper
realm. A dmu rope (dmu-thag) connects heaven and earth. This is a
specifically Bön category, less prominent in Buddhist contexts.
[*established*; Karmay on Bön]

**** Ma-mo (མ་མོ) — Wrathful Female Spirits

Associated with weather — hailstorms, epidemics, and collective
disaster. Ma-mo attacks are understood as punishment for communal moral
failings. They are particularly feared in agricultural regions where
hail destroys crops. [*established*; Nebesky-Wojkowitz 1956]

**** Srin-po (སྲིན་པོ) — Ogres/Rakshasas

Powerful, dangerous beings of the wilderness — cannibalistic ogres
inhabiting remote mountain areas. The Buddhist narrative of Tibet
itself is a taming of srin-po: the land was wild and demon-infested
before Buddhist civilisation. [*established*; foundational Tibetan
Buddhist narrative]

**** The-rang (ཐེའུ་རང) — Mischievous Spirits

Minor spirits causing small-scale trouble — things going missing,
minor illness, domestic annoyance. Less dangerous than btsan or 'dre
but persistent. [*likely*]

**** Chos-skyong (ཆོས་སྐྱོང) — Dharma Protectors

Originally local spirits tamed by Buddhist masters and bound as
protectors of the Buddhist teaching. The most important dharma
protectors (Nechung, Palden Lhamo, Dorje Shugden) are major figures
in Tibetan Buddhism with their own oracles and cults. [*established*]

*** Bön Traditions Specifically

Bön predates Buddhism in Tibet and classifies spirits differently:
- The Bön cosmos has three realms: sky (lha), middle (gnyan/btsan),
  and underworld (klu) — a vertical axis mapping to altitude
  [*established*; Karmay]
- Bön ritual specialists (gshen/shen) interact with spirits through
  animal sacrifice, smoke offerings, and divination [*established*]
- The Bön mountain cult: mountains as axis mundi, with the mountain
  god as the primary territorial spirit [*established*]
- Buddhism absorbed much of Bön's spirit taxonomy while claiming to
  have "tamed" the spirits — what was absorbed, what was suppressed,
  and what survived as folk practice is a major scholarly question
  [*established debate*]

*** Landscape Associations

| Feature | Spirit association | Notes |
|---------+--------------------+-------|
| Sacred mountains | Yul-lha, mountain gods | Every major peak has a named spirit |
| Namtso (lake) | Klu, sacred lake | One of Tibet's three great sacred lakes |
| Manasarovar | Klu, cosmic significance | Near Mt Kailash; Hindu-Buddhist-Bön nexus |
| Yamdrok Tso | Klu, turquoise lake | Central Tibet; life-force of Tibet |
| Mountain passes | La-btsas cairns, passage spirits | Flags and stones mark every pass |
| Red cliffs | Btsan territory | The colour is diagnostic |
| Sacred sites (gnas) | Multiple spirit categories | Né sites are power places |
| Burial grounds | 'Dre, pret-equivalents | Sky burial sites are spiritually charged |

*** Human Interfaces

**** State Oracles

Tibet had the most institutionalised oracle tradition in the world:
- *Nechung Oracle*: the state oracle of Tibet, consulted by the
  Dalai Lama on matters of national importance. A monk at Nechung
  monastery serves as medium for the protector deity Dorje Drakden.
  [*established*; well-documented, continues in exile]
- *Gadong Oracle*: another state-level oracle [*established*]
- These represent the maximum institutionalisation of spirit-medium
  practice — from village gur to state functionary [*inferred*]

**** Smoke Offerings (sang/bsang)

Juniper burnt on high ground, sending fragrant smoke to please the
lha. A daily practice in many communities. [*established*]

**** Prayer Flags (lung-ta)

Flags inscribed with prayers and offerings to the wind. Functionally,
they communicate with lha and disperse beneficial energy across the
landscape. Placed at passes, on rooftops, at sacred sites. [*established*]

**** Spirit-Taming Narratives

The foundational narrative of Tibetan Buddhism: Padmasambhava (8th
century) travelled through Tibet taming local spirits and binding them
as dharma protectors. Every monastery has a "taming story" — the local
spirits that Padmasambhava (or another master) subdued. These narratives
map Buddhist authority onto pre-existing spirit geography. [*established*]

*** Diagnostic Functions

- *Weather prediction*: ma-mo traditions encode storm patterns
- *Geological hazard*: sa-bdag and gnyan encode terrain knowledge
- *Water management*: klu traditions protect water sources
- *Political legitimacy*: state oracles provided divine sanction for
  governance decisions
- *Cosmological mapping*: the three-realm structure (sky/middle/under)
  maps directly to altitude zones

** 1.6 Karakoram and Pamirs

The Karakoram and Pamir regions — Hunza, Gilgit, Baltistan (Pakistan),
Chitral, and the Wakhan Corridor (Afghanistan/Tajikistan) — represent
a different spirit tradition: Persian/Islamic overlay on older Central
Asian shamanic practices. [*established*]

*** Spirit Types

**** Peri (پری) — Fairy Spirits

The peri is the dominant spirit category in the Karakoram. Drawn from
Persian mythology, peris are beautiful, powerful, capricious beings
who inhabit high pastures, glaciers, and remote valleys. They are not
the diminutive fairies of European tradition but formidable spirits.
[*established*; the Persian peri tradition is well-documented, its
Karakoram manifestation less so]

Characteristics:
- Inhabit high-altitude meadows and glacial areas
- Beautiful, often female (but male peris exist)
- Can help or harm humans, depending on whether they are pleased
- Associated with altitude sickness, disorientation, and madness
  at high altitude
- "Fairy Meadows" (Jūt) at the base of Nanga Parbat literally named
  for this tradition [*established*]

**** Jinn (جن) — Islamic Spirit Category

As Muslim communities, the Karakoram peoples also recognise the Islamic
jinn taxonomy. Jinn and peri coexist, sometimes blurring:
- Jinn: created from smokeless fire, can be good or evil
- The Islamic framework provides a theological structure within which
  older peri traditions are accommodated [*established*]

**** Mountain Spirits

Specific peaks have spirit reputations:
- *Nanga Parbat* ("Naked Mountain" or "Killer Mountain"): one of the
  most dangerous mountains in the world. Local traditions attribute the
  mountain's hostility to malevolent spirits. The name Diamer (from
  deo-mir = "abode of spirits/fairies") reflects this. [*likely*]
- *Rakaposhi*: the "Mother of Mist" — visible from the Karakoram
  Highway, with local spirit associations [*gap*: need specific
  traditions]
- *K2*: primarily known by Balti name Chogori. Local traditions
  [*gap*: very thin on K2 spirit associations]

**** Glacier and Avalanche Spirits

In the Karakoram's glacier-dominated landscape, glacier behaviour is
attributed to spirit agency:
- Glacier surges (sudden advances) understood as spirit displeasure
- Avalanche risk associated with disturbing mountain spirits
- Altitude sickness attributed to peri or jinn contact
[*inferred*; the pattern is consistent but I lack specific citations]

*** Shamanic Traditions

**** Bitan and Dayal (بِتان / دایال)

Pre-Islamic shamanic practitioners survive in Hunza, Gilgit, and
surrounding regions. The terminology is now confirmed through the work
of H. Sidky ("Shamans and Mountain Spirits in Hunza"):

- *Bitan*: the shaman in Burushaski (the Hunza language). The bitan
  inhales smoke from burning juniper branches, dances to special music,
  drinks blood from a freshly severed goat's head, enters ecstatic
  trance, and converses with supernatural beings (pari). Bitans served
  as healers and soothsayers in the traditional ritual and politico-
  ideological apparatus of the former Hunza state. [*established*;
  Sidky, "Shamans and Mountain Spirits in Hunza"]
- *Dayal*: a related practitioner who enters trance through music,
  meeting with pari in ecstatic states. A dayal may experience
  unconsciousness or illness for days or weeks. Resistance to the
  calling can be fatal — a bitan may die if they resist becoming one.
  [*established*; same source]
- The practice predates Islam and has been variously suppressed and
  accommodated. [*established*]

**** Ismaili Accommodation

The Ismaili Muslim communities of Hunza and upper Gilgit have
historically been more tolerant of pre-Islamic practices than Sunni
communities. The peri tradition may have survived more robustly in
Ismaili areas. [*inferred*]

| Nanga Parbat/Diamer | Malevolent mountain spirits | "Killer Mountain" reputation |
| High pastures (summer) | Peri domain | Herders encounter peris in high pastures |
| Glaciers | Glacier spirits | Surges, retreats attributed to spirit agency |

- *Kyrgyz traditions*: Turkic shamanic elements at the eastern Pamir
- *The "Roof of the World"*: at 4,000-5,000m, the Pamir steppe has
  spirits of extreme altitude — wind spirits, spirits of the vast
  horizontal spaces (contrasting with the vertical spirit taxonomy
  of the mountain valleys)
[*inferred*; very thin evidence base. This section needs significant
research.]

** 1.7 Nepal Himalaya (Stretch Goal)

Nepal sustains the richest shamanic tradition in the Himalaya — the
jhākri (shaman) system that spans ethnic groups and ecological zones.
[*established*; significant anthropological literature]

*** Spirit Types

**** Jhākri (झाँक्री) — The Shaman

The jhākri is not a spirit but the central human actor in Nepal's
spirit ecology — equivalent in function to the Kullu gur, the Ladakhi
lha-pa, and the Karakoram bitani. The jhākri enters trance through
drumming, diagnoses illness by identifying the responsible spirit,
and negotiates with spirits on behalf of the patient. [*established*;
Peters, Maskarinec, Holmberg all document this]

**** Ban Jhākri (बन झाँक्री) — Forest Shaman Spirit

A wild forest spirit who abducts and trains jhākris. The ban jhākri
appears as a small, hairy, powerful being dwelling deep in forests. A
person abducted by ban jhākri and returned becomes a shaman. This is
the origin narrative for shamanic calling in many Nepali communities.
[*established*; widely documented]

**** Bombo — Tamang Shaman

The Tamang term for the shamanic practitioner. The bombo uses a
flat drum (dhyangro) and enters trance to communicate with spirits.
Tamang shamanism is particularly well-documented. [*established*;
Holmberg 1989]

**** Bhut/Pret (भूत/प्रेत) — Ghosts

Spirits of the dead, especially those who died violently or without
proper rites. Pan-South-Asian category, equivalent to Kullu's pret.
[*established*]

**** Masān (मसान) — Cremation Ground Spirits

Spirits associated with cremation grounds and death. Particularly
powerful and dangerous. [*established*; pan-South-Asian]

**** Bokshi/Boksi (बोक्सी) — Witch Spirits

Not ghosts but living people (usually women) accused of being
possessed by or wielding malevolent spirit power. Boksi accusations
are a form of social violence — the "diagnosis" of witchcraft
functions as scapegoating. This is politically sensitive: boksi
accusations have led to violence against women. [*established*;
significant human rights concern]

**** Nāg (नाग) — Serpent Spirits

Water spirits, as throughout the Hindu world. In Nepal, nāg worship
is connected to rainfall, springs, and fertility. [*established*]

**** Kirāt Traditions

The Rai and Limbu peoples of eastern Nepal have their own spirit
taxonomies based on the Mundhum (oral tradition):
- Complex origin narratives with named spirits
- Ancestral spirits playing active roles
- Distinctions that don't map neatly to Hindu categories
[*likely*; I know these exist but lack specific detail]

**** Sherpa Traditions

Sherpas, culturally Tibetan, use the Tibetan spirit taxonomy (lha,
klu, btsan, etc.) adapted to the Solu-Khumbu landscape. The Sherpa
tradition includes:
- Mountain deities of specific peaks
- The deity of Sagarmatha/Everest itself
- Tengboche Monastery as a spirit-management institution
[*likely*; some is documented in mountaineering literature]

*** Landscape Associations

| Feature | Spirit association | Notes |
|---------+--------------------+-------|
| Machapuchare | Forbidden mountain; the "Fish-Tail" | Never been summited; considered sacred [*established*] |
| Kanchenjunga | Mountain god, fierce | Third highest peak, sacred to Sikkimese and Nepali [*established*] |
| Sagarmatha/Everest | Mountain deity | Sherpas propitiate before climbing [*established*] |
| Gosainkund (lakes, 4,380m) | Shiva/nāg; pilgrimage | Sacred lakes in Langtang area [*established*] |
| Kali Gandaki gorge | Powerful river spirit | Deepest gorge on Earth; ammonite fossils (shaligrams) as sacred objects [*established*] |
| Forests | Ban jhākri domain | Dense forests = spirit territory [*established*] |
| Pashupatinath | Lord of Animals (Shiva) | Major temple; cremation ghats = masān territory [*established*] |
| Muktinath | "Place of Liberation" | Sacred to both Hindu and Buddhist; fire-from-water (natural gas) [*established*] |

*** Human Interfaces

- *Jhākri healing*: the jhākri drums, enters trance, identifies the
  spirit causing illness, negotiates or drives it out. The drum
  (dhyangro) is the primary instrument. Sessions can last hours,
  often at night. [*established*; Peters 1981, Maskarinec 1995]
- *Dashain* (Dussehra equivalent): the major Nepali festival, with

- *Indra Jatra*: festival in Kathmandu involving masked dances, the
  Kumari (living goddess), and processions. [*established*]

*** Diagnostic Functions

- *Illness attribution*: the jhākri's primary diagnostic function.
  Illness is attributed to specific spirits; the diagnosis identifies
  the social or environmental cause through spirit language.
  [*established*; standard medical-anthropological interpretation]
- *Forest conservation*: ban jhākri traditions maintain fear/respect
  for deep forest, discouraging over-exploitation [*inferred*]
- *Mountain safety*: propitiation before climbing/crossing encodes
  weather awareness, route knowledge [*inferred*]

* Part 2: Cross-Regional Patterns

** 2.1 Altitude Zonation

The spirit ecology shifts predictably with altitude:

| Zone | Approximate altitude | Hindu-dominant | Buddhist/Tibetan-dominant |
|------+----------------------+----------------+--------------------------|
| Valley floor / agricultural | 1,500-2,500m | Devta (village deity) | Lha (territorial deity) |
| Forest belt | 2,500-3,500m | Nāg, sacred groves | Gnyan (tree/rock spirits) |
| Alpine / pasture | 3,500-4,500m | Jogini, pass spirits | Btsan, sa-bdag |
| Glacial / summit | >4,500m | Mountain gods (vague) | Yul-lha (mountain gods) |
| Trans-Himalayan desert | 3,500-5,000m (rain shadow) | — | Lha, klu (water critical) |

The pattern: valley-floor spirits are *civic* (managing communities);
mid-altitude spirits are *ecological* (managing forests and water);
high-altitude spirits are *elemental* (managing weather, glaciers, passes).
[*inferred* but consistent across all regions surveyed]

** 2.2 Water Spirits Across Traditions

The nāg/klu/peri-of-water equivalence is the clearest cross-regional
pattern:

| Region | Term | Associated with | Key function |
|--------+------+-----------------+--------------|
| Kullu | Nāg devta | Springs, rivers, hot springs | Water source protection |
| Kinnaur | Nāg / klu (upper) | Lakes, springs, altitude water | Bridge across Hindu-Buddhist divide |
| Spiti | Klu/lu | Springs, river, snowmelt | Existential: water = survival |
| Ladakh | Klu/lu | Springs, lakes, underground water | Skin disease if offended |
| Tibet | Klu | All water; klu realm = underworld | Cosmic scale water management |
| Karakoram | Peri (at water) / jinn | Glacier-fed rivers, lakes | Less specifically water-focused |
| Nepal | Nāg | Springs, rainfall, fertility | Pan-South-Asian category |

The underlying landscape logic: *wherever water is scarce or critical,
the spirit system places its strongest protections around water sources*.
The harsher the water situation, the more elaborate the klu/nāg
traditions. Spiti and Ladakh — arid landscapes — have the most urgent
klu traditions. [*inferred* but structurally sound]

** 2.3 Possession/Oracle Traditions

The oracle tradition recurs across every region, with structural
parallels:

| Region | Practitioner | Possessing entity | Induction method | Function |
|--------+--------------+-------------------+------------------+----------|
| Kullu | Gur | Devta | Drumming (bajantri) | Dispute resolution, diagnosis, timing |
| Kinnaur | Gur/Grokch/Malis | Village devta | Incantation + music | Deity commands, community guidance |
| Spiti | Monastic medium | Dharma protector | Monastic ritual | Community advice |
| Ladakh | Lha-pa/lha-mo | Lha, protector | Drumming, monastic context | Medical diagnosis, community decisions |
| Tibet | State oracle (Nechung) | Protector deity | Elaborate ritual | National-level decisions |
| Karakoram | Bitan/Dayal | Pari (fairies) | Juniper smoke + music + blood | Healing, soothsaying |
| Nepal | Jhākri/bombo | Various spirits | Drum (dhyangro) | Illness diagnosis, exorcism |

The structural invariant: *a human enters altered consciousness, a
non-human entity speaks through them, the community treats this as
authoritative information*. The theological framing varies (Hindu devta,
Buddhist protector, Islamic jinn) but the diagnostic mechanism is the
same. [*established*; this is a standard observation in comparative
religion]

** 2.4 Sacred Geography as Conservation

Spirit jurisdictions protect environmental resources across all
traditions:

| Resource | Protection mechanism | Region(s) | Effectiveness |
|----------+----------------------+-----------+---------------|
| Springs | Nāg/klu taboo against pollution | All | High — water sources often cleaner near shrines |
| Old-growth forest | Sacred groves (devta forests) | Kullu, Kinnaur | Documented conservation function |
| Pastures | Lha/yul-lha territorial rules | Ladakh, Tibet, Spiti | Prevents overgrazing in controlled areas |
| Fragile terrain | Sa-bdag taboo against digging | Ladakh, Spiti, Tibet | Prevents excavation in landslide zones |
| Mountain peaks | Summit taboos | Nepal (Machapuchare), Tibet | Effective where enforced |

This is not a post-hoc interpretation: the conservation function is
recognised by the communities themselves, even where the theological
reasoning differs from the ecological reasoning. [*established*;
Ramnath's work on sacred groves, multiple other sources]

** 2.5 Trade Route Spirits — The Liminal Zone

Spirits that guard passes and the spaces between territories constitute
a distinct category across the range:

- *La-btsas* (Tibetan/Ladakhi): stone cairns at passes, draped with
  prayer flags. Travellers add stones, recite prayers, shout "ki ki
  so so lha gyalo!" (victory to the gods). The cairn is the spirit's
  dwelling. [*established*]
- *Pass spirits in Kullu*: Jalori Pass, Rohtang Pass, Pin Parvati
  — all have traditions about dangers and propitiation [*likely*]
- *Karakoram pass spirits*: the Khunjerab and older trade routes have
  guardian spirits who must be acknowledged [*inferred*]

The pass spirit is structurally interesting: it guards the boundary
between jurisdictions — the place where one devta's/lha's authority
ends and another's begins. These are liminal spirits, spirits of
transition. [*inferred*; strong structural argument]


* Part 3: Functional Mapping for Aburaya

This section maps the surveyed spirit ecology to Aburaya's functional
needs. The goal is grounded vocabulary, not forced analogy. Where the
mapping is strong, we note it. Where it breaks, we note that too.

** 3.1 Guardian of a Place (module/domain)

*Source tradition*: Devta (Kullu), Lha/Yul-lha (Tibetan), Zhidak

The guardian spirit is the most direct mapping. The devta guards a
territory; the lha protects a mountain; the zhidak owns a place.
In Aburaya, the guardian spirit protects a module or domain.

*Vocabulary*: "devta" is the richest source for the Hindu-inflected
stories; "lha" for Buddhist-inflected contexts. The project already
uses both.

*Where the analogy is strong*:
- Territorial jurisdiction ↔ module scope
- The devta has a personality, history, relationships with other devtas
  ↔ spirit disposition, guild relationships
- The devta owns an estate and employs specialists ↔ the module
  contains code, data, and tools

*Where it breaks*:
- Devtas are not chosen — they are ancient. Spirits in Aburaya are
  commissioned by humans. The analogy works for established projects
  but not for the act of creation itself.

** 3.2 Wanderer Carrying a Practice

*Source tradition*: Jogini (Kullu), wandering ascetic, the Thread
Walker (project fiction)

The jogini is not bound to a territory. She moves across jurisdictions,
carrying knowledge or disruption. In Aburaya, this maps to a spirit
whose practice (e.g., review writing, story composition) applies across
modules rather than guarding one.

*Vocabulary*: "jogini" fits — it is already in the project vocabulary
from story-compose.md.

*Where the analogy is strong*:
- Not bound to territory ↔ cross-module practice
- Associated with specific skills/knowledge ↔ exported powers
- Ambiguous in disposition ↔ the practice itself has no loyalty

*Where it breaks*:
- Traditional joginis are often dangerous or marginal figures. The
  Aburaya "wanderer" is a valued contributor. The social valence
  differs.

** 3.3 Flow Spirit Following a System

*Source tradition*: Nāg devta / Klu — the water spirits

The nāg follows water — from glacier to spring to river to sea. It
tracks a system, not a place. In Aburaya, this maps to a spirit that
follows a data flow, a pipeline, or a process.

*Vocabulary*: "nāg devta" or "klu." The project already uses "nāg
devta" in the Miyazaki key (Haku = nāg devta).

*Where the analogy is strong*:
- Water flows through the landscape ↔ data flows through pipelines
- Nāg displeasure disrupts the flow ↔ pipeline failures
- Nāg protects the source ↔ data integrity at the origin

*Where it breaks*:
- Nāgs are territorial about their water source — they don't
  conceptually "follow" the water downstream. The flow metaphor is
  more human interpretation than traditional nāg theology.

** 3.4 Specialist Performing a Craft

*Source tradition*: Bajantri (ritual musician), Amchi (traditional
doctor), Kardar (temple steward)

These are human specialists, not spirits. But they represent
functionally distinct roles within the spirit system. In Aburaya,
the specialist spirit provides a service (schema validation, query
execution) to any caller.

*Vocabulary*: "bajantri" is available but feels too specific (music
only). "Amchi" could work for diagnostic spirits. Consider:
- The generic term might be "shilpi" (शिल्पी, craftsperson) or
  "kārīgar" (कारीगर, artisan) — but these are not spirit categories,
  they are human vocations.

*Where the mapping breaks*:
- There is no clean "specialist spirit" category in Himalayan
  traditions. The specialist is always human, serving the spirits.
  This is a gap in the analogy — Aburaya's specialist spirit (e.g.,
  aikosh-guardian as toolsmith) has no clean traditional equivalent.

** 3.5 Medium Through Whom a Spirit Speaks

*Source tradition*: Gur (Kullu), Lha-pa/Lha-mo (Ladakh), Jhākri
(Nepal), Bitani (Karakoram)

The medium is the harness — the physical body through which the
spirit acts. In Aburaya, the harness (Claude Code session, gptel
buffer) is the "body" a spirit inhabits.

*Vocabulary*: "gur" maps beautifully to the harness concept. The gur
is not the devta — the gur is the vessel the devta uses to speak.
This is the strongest analogy in the entire mapping.

*Where the analogy is strong*:
- Gur enters trance ↔ harness initialises
- Devta speaks through gur ↔ spirit operates through harness
- Gur recovers after trance ↔ session ends, harness state persists
- Different gurs serve different devtas ↔ different harnesses serve
  different spirits

** 3.6 Diagnostic Spirit (Evaluation, Review, Critique)

*Source tradition*: the oracle function across all traditions

The oracle's core function is *diagnosis* — identifying what is wrong,
what spirit is responsible, what must be done. In Aburaya, the review/
evaluation spirit (the one WP-0002 sought to commission) performs
exactly this function: surveying a landscape, identifying gaps,
prescribing action.

*Vocabulary*: not a single spirit type but a function shared across
types. The review spirit's disposition might borrow from the oracle
tradition: "read-the-signs" or "trace-the-watershed" (already
proposed in WP-0002).

** 3.7 Proposed Vocabulary Summary

| Aburaya function | Himalayan source | Term | Confidence |
|------------------+------------------+------+------------|
| Guardian | Devta, Lha | devta (Hindu), lha (Buddhist) | Strong |
| Wanderer | Jogini | jogini | Moderate |

* Part 5: Digital Twin Data Layer

** 5.1 Data Model

The spirit ecology integrates into the Parbati digital twin as a
geospatial data layer with the following structure:

*** Spatial Primitives

| Primitive | Spirit concept | Example |
|-----------+----------------+---------|
| Point | Shrine, spring, oracle seat, cairn | Hadimba temple, Serolsar Lake, la-btsas at Jalori Pass |
| Line | Trade route, river (klu path), procession route | Parvati River (nāg territory), rath procession route |
| Polygon | Spirit jurisdiction, sacred grove | Jamlu's territory (Malana), devta forest boundaries |

#+begin_src yaml
# Example: point feature (shrine/sacred site)

#+end_src

** 5.3 Minimal Prototype

*Scope*: Parvati Valley only (aligns with Parbati domain).

*Data*:
- 10-20 point features: temples, sacred springs, kunds, pass cairns
- 3-5 line features: river segments with nāg associations, rath routes
- 3-5 polygon features: devta jurisdictions, sacred groves

*Format*: GeoJSON file + YAML metadata per feature (following aikosh
schema conventions)

*Visualisation*: overlay on DEM (digital elevation model) of the
Parvati valley, if/when MāyāPortal supports GIS layers.

*What this proves*: that spirit ecology data can be structured,
geospatially referenced, and integrated into the digital twin
infrastructure. The prototype validates the data model before scaling
to the full transborder Himalaya.
