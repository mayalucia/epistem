* A. Deep Time — Before Humans

** A.1 Tethys to Collision (~250–50 Ma)

*** The Tethys Sea: an ocean where mountains now stand

Where the Karakoram and western Himalaya now rise above 8,000 m,
there was ocean. The Tethys Sea (more precisely, Neo-Tethys) was a
warm, shallow-to-deep marine basin separating Gondwana from Laurasia
throughout the Mesozoic. At the latitude of what would become Nanga
Parbat, the Tethys was a broad tropical ocean during the Triassic
(~250–200 Ma), narrowing through the Jurassic and Cretaceous as India
drifted northward.

*Marine fossils in the Karakoram and western Himalaya*:

- *Crinoid limestones* in the Karakoram: Permian and Triassic
  fossiliferous limestones are exposed in the Karakoram Range,
  including fusulinid foraminifera (Permian) and ammonites
  (Jurassic–Cretaceous). The *Misgar Formation* and *Gircha
  Formation* in the upper Hunza valley contain Permian marine
  fossils. [Gaetani et al. (1990), "The north Karakoram side of the
  Central Asia geopuzzle," /Geological Society of America Bulletin/,
  102(1), 54–62]

- *Ammonites on K2*: Fossiliferous Cretaceous limestones have been
  reported from the Karakoram, including the Shaksgam Formation.
  Italian geological expeditions to the Karakoram (Desio, 1930s–1960s)
  documented marine sedimentary sequences. [Desio, A. (1979),
  /Geology of the upper Shaksgam valley/, Brill]

- *The Tethyan Sedimentary Sequence* in Zanskar and Ladakh (east of
  Nanga Parbat): exceptionally well-preserved marine sequences from
  Ordovician through Eocene. Trilobites (Cambrian–Ordovician),
  graptolites, brachiopods, ammonites, belemnites, rudist bivalves.
  Gaetani and Garzanti (1991) provide a comprehensive stratigraphy.
  [Gaetani, M. and Garzanti, E. (1991), "Multicyclic history of the
  Northern India continental margin (NW Himalaya)," /AAPG Bulletin/,
  75(9), 1427–1446]

- *Foraminifera and nummulites* in Eocene limestones: the final
  marine deposits before collision. Nummulitic limestones (large
  coin-shaped foraminifera) are found across the Himalayan foreland —
  these are the last gasp of the Tethys, ~55–50 Ma.

The key insight: *the rocks at the summit of the Himalaya were once
the floor of a warm tropical sea*. The Tethyan Himalayan sequence
(preserved in Ladakh, Spiti, Zanskar) records hundreds of millions
of years of marine deposition. In the Nanga Parbat massif itself,
these cover rocks have been largely stripped by the extreme erosion
and exhumation — what is exposed is Indian plate basement gneiss,
originally deep continental crust, now at the surface because the
mountain rose so fast. The marine sedimentary record is better
preserved in adjacent, less violently uplifted ranges.

*** Gondwana breakup: India's northward drift

India separated from Gondwana in stages:

1. *~180–170 Ma* (Early–Middle Jurassic): Initial rifting. India
   still attached to East Africa, Madagascar, Antarctica, Australia.
2. *~130–120 Ma* (Early Cretaceous): India separates from
   Africa/Madagascar. The Rajmahal Traps (~117 Ma) mark early
   volcanism associated with the Kerguelen hotspot.
3. *~90–88 Ma*: India separates from Madagascar. Begins rapid
   northward transit.
4. *~68–65 Ma*: The Deccan Traps — one of the largest volcanic
   events in Earth history, coincident with the K-Pg extinction.
   Linked to the Reunion hotspot. India was drifting over this
   hotspot as it moved north.
5. *~55–50 Ma*: Collision with Asia begins.

*Drift speed*: India's northward velocity was exceptionally fast for
a tectonic plate — *15–20 cm/yr* during the Late Cretaceous
(~80–50 Ma), approximately 3x faster than any plate moves today. This
anomalous speed has been attributed to:

- *Double subduction*: Neo-Tethys oceanic lithosphere subducting both
  ahead of India and beneath the Kohistan arc, providing double the
  slab pull. [van Hinsbergen et al. (2011), "Greater India Basin
  hypothesis and a two-stage Cenozoic collision between India and
  Asia," /PNAS/, 108(12), 4734–4739; DOI: 10.1073/pnas.1117262108]

- *Plume push*: the Reunion hotspot providing additional buoyancy
  force from below. [Cande & Stegman (2011), "Indian plate motion
  since the Late Cretaceous," /GRL/]

- *Thin, young oceanic lithosphere*: reduced resistance at the
  leading edge.

India's trajectory was roughly NNE, with a significant
counterclockwise rotation during transit. Palaeomagnetic data (from
Deccan basalts and Rajmahal Traps) constrain the path precisely.

[Key source: Patriat & Achache (1984), "India-Eurasia collision
chronology has implications for crustal shortening and driving
mechanism of plates," /Nature/, 311, 615–621]

*** The Kohistan island arc: oceanic crust caught in the collision

The Kohistan arc is one of the best-preserved and most-studied
intra-oceanic island arcs in the world. It is exposed as a complete
cross-section from mantle (ultramafic rocks at the base) through
lower crust (granulites) to upper crust (volcanic and sedimentary
rocks) — a consequence of the collision tilting and exposing the
entire arc stratigraphy.

*Before the collision*, the Kohistan arc was:

- An *intra-oceanic volcanic island arc* in the Neo-Tethys, similar
  to the modern Aleutians, Marianas, or Tonga arcs. A chain of
  volcanic islands above a subduction zone, surrounded by open ocean.

- *Age*: Formation began in the Early Cretaceous (~130–110 Ma). The
  arc was active for ~60 Myr before collision.

- *The Chilas Complex*: a massive gabbronorite body (~85 Ma) in the
  middle of the arc — the largest known example of a layered mafic
  intrusion in an island arc setting. Formed by ponding of
  mantle-derived magma at the base of the arc crust. [Khan et al.
  (1989), "The Chilas mafic-ultramafic igneous complex: the root of
  the Kohistan island arc in the Himalaya of northern Pakistan," /GSA
  Special Paper/ 232, 75–94]

- *Structure from bottom to top*:
  1. Jijal Complex: ultramafic–mafic granulites (mantle and
     lowermost crust)
  2. Chilas Complex: gabbronorite (mid-crust)
  3. Kamila amphibolite belt: metamorphosed volcanic and plutonic
     rocks (mid-upper crust)
  4. Dir and Utror volcanic sequences: andesites, basalts (upper
     crust volcanic cover)
  5. Yasin Group: marine sediments deposited on top of the arc

*Two sutures*: The Kohistan arc is bounded by two suture zones:

- *Northern (Shyok) Suture*: where the arc collided with the
  Karakoram plate (Asian margin) at ~100–85 Ma. This was the first
  collision event.
- *Main Mantle Thrust (MMT) / Indus Suture*: where India collided
  with the already-accreted Kohistan-Karakoram at ~55–50 Ma. This is
  the suture you can walk across near Nanga Parbat — the contact
  between Indian plate gneiss and Kohistan arc rocks.

*At Nanga Parbat specifically*: the Indian plate basement gneiss has
punched upward /through/ the Kohistan arc rocks, creating the
syntaxial antiform. The Kohistan rocks drape around the Nanga Parbat
massif on three sides. The contact is ductile and interleaved — not a
clean fault but a zone of intense deformation where the two
lithologies intermingle. This is the "suture zone you can walk
across" mentioned in v0.

[Key sources:
- Tahirkheli, R.A.K. et al. (1979), "The Kohistan island arc
  sequence," in /Geodynamics of Pakistan/, Geological Survey of
  Pakistan, 115–169
- Bard, J.P. (1983), "Metamorphism of an obducted island arc," /EPSL/,
  65, 133–144
- Burg et al. (1998), "The Kohistan Complex," /Tectonophysics/, 294,
  187–197; DOI: 10.1016/S0040-1951(98)00232-7
- Jagoutz & Schmidt (2012), "The formation and bulk composition of
  the modern juvenile continental crust: The Kohistan arc,"
  /Chemical Geology/, 298–299, 79–96]

*** What the collision looked like

Imagine the geography at ~55 Ma: India, a large continent moving
rapidly north, approaches Asia. Between them, the remnant of the
Neo-Tethys narrows. In the ocean stands the Kohistan island arc —
a chain of volcanic islands, perhaps 500 km long, with coral reefs,
warm shallow seas, and active volcanoes. India's leading edge first
encounters the arc. The oceanic crust between India and the arc
subducts completely. The arc, too buoyant to subduct, is sutured onto
India's northern margin. Then India, with the arc now riding on its
back, continues north into Asia. The Tethys closes completely. The
marine fossils are lifted toward the sky.

** A.2 Collision to Uplift (~50–5 Ma)

*** Early Himalayan orogeny: when did mountains first emerge?

The collision of India with Asia was not instantaneous — it was a
protracted process spanning millions of years, and there is ongoing
debate about exactly when "initial contact" occurred versus when
significant topography began to build.

*Timeline of key events*:

- *~55–50 Ma* (Early Eocene): Initial contact between the Indian
  continental margin and the Kohistan–Karakoram–Asian margin. The
  cessation of marine sedimentation in the Tethyan Himalaya records
  this: the last marine fossils (nummulitic foraminifera) date to the
  Early Eocene. [Garzanti et al. (1987), "Sedimentary record of the
  northward flight of India and its collision with Eurasia," /Geodin.
  Acta/, 1, 297–312]

- *~50–40 Ma*: Rapid crustal shortening begins. India's convergence
  rate drops from ~15 cm/yr to ~5 cm/yr as continental crust resists
  subduction. The Indus-Tsangpo suture zone forms. Initial thrust
  faulting, but mountains are still modest.

- *~25–20 Ma* (Late Oligocene–Early Miocene): Major phase of
  Himalayan uplift. The Main Central Thrust (MCT) activates, stacking
  high-grade metamorphic rocks over lower-grade rocks. This is when
  significant topography — mountains perhaps 3,000–4,000 m — first
  emerged across the Himalayan front. Evidence from detrital
  thermochronology in the Siwalik foreland basin.

- *~15–10 Ma*: The Himalaya approaches modern elevation. The Tibetan
  Plateau reaches near-present altitude. Evidence from oxygen isotope
  palaeoaltimetry and leaf physiognomy. [Garzione et al. (2000),
  "Predicting paleoelevation of Tibet and the Himalaya from delta-18O
  vs. altitude gradients," /EPSL/, 183, 215–229]

- *~5 Ma to present*: The Nanga Parbat syntaxis begins its extreme
  phase. While the broader Himalaya had been rising for tens of
  millions of years, the specific rapid uplift at the western
  syntaxis intensified in the Pliocene–Pleistocene. The ~1 Ma pulse
  of ultrafast exhumation (Rutte et al. 2022, already in v0) is the
  latest chapter.

*The important distinction*: the Himalayan orogen is ~55 Myr old, but
the extreme topography of Nanga Parbat is much younger — substantially
a Plio-Pleistocene phenomenon. The mountain that stands today is
young even by Himalayan standards.

*** The proto-Indus: the river older than the mountain

The Indus is an *antecedent river* — it established its course before
the syntaxis rose, then maintained that course by cutting downward as
the mountain grew around it.

*What was the proto-Indus?*

- The Indus drainage system dates to at least the Eocene (~45–40 Ma).
  Detrital zircon provenance studies from Indus Fan sediments (drilled
  by DSDP/ODP in the Arabian Sea) show that the Indus was already
  carrying sediment from Trans-Himalayan (Kohistan/Ladakh arc)
  sources by ~45 Ma. [Clift et al. (2001), "Development of the Indus
  Fan and its significance for the erosional history of the Western
  Himalaya," /GSA Bulletin/, 113(8), 1039–1051]

- Before the syntaxis: the proto-Indus likely flowed in a broad,
  relatively low-gradient valley through what was then gentle or
  moderate topography. The extreme gorge is a consequence of the
  syntaxis rising /around/ the pre-existing river.

- *Alternative hypothesis*: Some authors have proposed that the upper
  Indus was once part of the Tsangpo (Brahmaputra) drainage, flowing
  east, and was captured by a south-flowing river. The evidence for
  this remains debated. [Clift & Blusztajn (2005), "Reorganization
  of the western Himalayan river system after five million years ago,"
  /Nature/, 438, 1001–1003]

- The key constraints come from *sediment provenance in the Indus
  Fan*: the Arabian Sea sediment record is an archive of Himalayan
  erosion history spanning 50+ Myr. Changes in detrital mineral
  assemblages, isotopic signatures, and sedimentation rates record
  when different source terranes were first exhumed and eroded.
  [Clift et al. (2002), "Nd and Pb isotope variability in the Indus
  River System," /EPSL/, 200, 389–404]

*** Climate: the monsoon and the rain shadow

*The Asian monsoon system*:

The Indian summer monsoon is fundamentally driven by the differential
heating of the Tibetan Plateau and the Indian Ocean. Its development
is therefore linked to the uplift of both the Himalaya and Tibet.

- *~25–20 Ma*: First evidence of monsoon-like circulation. Aeolian
  dust records from the Arabian Sea and South China Sea show
  increased seasonality. [Clift et al. (2008), "Correlation of
  Himalayan exhumation rates and Asian monsoon intensity," /Nature
  Geoscience/, 1, 875–880]

- *~15–10 Ma*: Intensification of the monsoon. Carbon isotope records
  in Siwalik palaeosols show a shift from C3 to C4 vegetation
  (grasses), interpreted as increasing aridity and seasonality.
  [Quade et al. (1989), "Late Miocene C4 expansion," /Nature/, 342,
  163–166]

- *~8 Ma*: Major monsoon intensification event. Dramatic increase in
  chemical weathering and sedimentation rates in the Bengal and Indus
  fans. This may coincide with the Tibetan Plateau reaching a
  critical elevation threshold.

*The rain shadow at Nanga Parbat*:

The Nanga Parbat massif sits in the rain shadow of the main Himalayan
front. The monsoon's moisture is largely wrung out before reaching the
inner valleys. Precipitation at Chilas is only ~150–200 mm/yr (semi-
arid), while the south-facing flanks receive considerably more from
occasional westerly disturbances.

The rain shadow is a consequence of the orographic barrier reaching
sufficient height. As the Himalaya rose through the Miocene, the rain
shadow would have progressively deepened — the inner valleys becoming
increasingly arid as the mountains grew taller. Before the Himalaya
reached ~3,000–4,000 m, there was no significant orographic barrier,
and the region would have received more distributed rainfall.

*** Flora and fauna of early Himalayan slopes: Siwalik evidence

The *Siwalik Hills* (Sub-Himalayan zone, from Pakistan to Nepal) are
the eroded foreland basin of the Himalaya — sediments shed from the
rising mountains between ~18 Ma and ~2 Ma. They contain one of the
richest Neogene mammalian fossil records in the world.

*The Siwalik sequence* (relevant to the western Himalaya/Pakistan):

- *Lower Siwaliks* (~18–11 Ma, Miocene): subtropical forest. Fossils
  include early hominoid primates (/Sivapithecus/, ancestral to
  orangutans), anthracotheres (hippo-like), early proboscideans
  (Deinotherium), crocodilians, tortoises. Vegetation: mixed C3
  forest with subtropical species.

- *Middle Siwaliks* (~11–5 Ma, Late Miocene): transition to more
  open woodland/grassland. The C3-to-C4 shift indicates grasslands
  expanding. Three-toed horses (/Hipparion/), giraffids
  (/Giraffokeryx/), large bovids, sabre-toothed cats
  (/Machairodus/). /Sivapithecus/ disappears — forests retreating.
  [Pilbeam et al. (1996), /PNAS/]

- *Upper Siwaliks* (~5–2 Ma, Plio-Pleistocene): open grassland and
  savanna. /Stegodon/ (proboscidean), /Hexaprotodon/ (early hippo),
  /Equus/ (modern horse), bovids. Increasingly modern fauna
  assembling.

*Important caveat*: The Siwalik fossil record comes from the foreland
basin — the lowlands south of the main Himalayan range. These animals
lived in the /foothills/, not in the high valleys. What lived at the
elevation of present-day Diamer during the Miocene is unknown — those
sediments, if they ever existed in the syntaxis zone, have been
entirely removed by the extreme erosion. The Siwaliks tell us what
was in the neighbourhood, not what was at the specific site.

*The Potwar Plateau* (northern Punjab, Pakistan): the richest Siwalik
fossil locality in the western Himalaya. Yale-GSP (Geological Survey
of Pakistan) expeditions from the 1970s onward recovered thousands of
specimens. /Sivapithecus/ (the "Ramapithecus" that turned out to be
an orangutan relative) was found here.

[Key sources:
- Barry et al. (2002), "Faunal and environmental change in the late
  Miocene Siwaliks of northern Pakistan," /Paleobiology Memoir/, 28(2)
- Pilbeam et al. (1996), "New Sivapithecus humeri from Pakistan,"
  /PNAS/, 93, 14555–14560
- Badgley et al. (2008), "Ecological changes in Miocene mammalian
  record show impact of prolonged climatic forcing," /PNAS/, 105(34)]

** A.3 Pleistocene (2.6 Ma – 12 ka)

*** Glacial cycles: Ice Age Nanga Parbat

During Pleistocene glacial maxima, glaciers in the Karakoram and
western Himalaya extended far beyond their present limits.

*Glacial extent*:

- During the *Last Glacial Maximum (LGM, ~26–19 ka)*, glaciers in
  the Nanga Parbat region extended down-valley significantly. Owen et
  al. (2002) documented glacial deposits in the Indus valley near
  Nanga Parbat using cosmogenic radionuclide (CRN) dating (10Be).
  Moraine evidence suggests glaciers extended to elevations as low as
  ~1,500–2,000 m in some valleys.

- *The Indus valley itself may have been blocked by glacial ice* at
  several points during the Pleistocene, creating temporary lakes.
  Evidence for palaeolake sediments (lacustrine deposits) has been
  reported from the Gilgit and Indus valleys. [Hewitt (1999),
  "Quaternary moraines vs catastrophic rock avalanches in the
  Karakoram Himalaya," /Quaternary Research/, 51, 220–237]

- Owen et al. (2002) argue that glacial advances in this region were
  *asynchronous* with global ice volume maxima. The Karakoram and
  western Himalaya are driven by mid-latitude westerlies, not the
  monsoon, so glacial maxima here may not coincide with those in the
  central Himalaya (which are monsoon-driven). [Owen, L.A. et al.
  (2002), "A note on the extent of glaciation throughout the
  Himalaya during the global Last Glacial Maximum," /Quaternary
  Science Reviews/, 21, 147–157]

- *Pre-LGM glaciations*: Evidence for older, more extensive glacial
  events is harder to date. Derbyshire et al. (1984) proposed at
  least three glacial stages in the Hunza valley (near Nanga Parbat),
  with the oldest extending much further than the LGM glaciers.
  [Derbyshire et al. (1984), "The Quaternary glaciation of the
  northern areas," in /The International Karakoram Project/,
  Cambridge University Press]

- *Katzenstein et al. (1999)*: Detailed mapping of glacial
  geomorphology in the Rakhiot valley (the valley directly below
  the Rakhiot Face of Nanga Parbat), identifying multiple moraine
  systems at different elevations.

*What Ice Age Nanga Parbat looked like*: The mountain itself would
have been more extensively glaciated, with glacier systems merging
and extending far down the valleys. The Indus gorge, however, was
likely never completely filled — the river's erosive power and the
narrowness of the gorge probably prevented full glacial blockage at
the deepest points. But tributary valleys would have been filled with
ice to much lower elevations than today.

*** Megafauna: what walked these valleys?

*This is where the evidence is genuinely thin for the specific Nanga
Parbat / Diamer area.* Honest assessment:

The *Siwalik Hills* (foothills, ~200 km south) have abundant
Pleistocene megafauna: /Stegodon/, /Elephas hysudricus/ (Asian
elephant relative), /Hexaprotodon/ (hippo), /Equus/ (horse), large
bovids, /Megantereon/ and /Homotherium/ (sabre-toothed cats). But
these are lowland/foothill environments, not high mountain valleys.

For the *high valleys and passes* near Nanga Parbat:

- *Woolly rhinoceros* (/Coelodonta antiquitatis/): Known from Central
  Asia and the Tibetan Plateau. A remarkably well-preserved specimen
  (the "Zanda rhinoceros") was found in Tibet, dating to ~3.7 Ma,
  suggesting the Tibetan Plateau was a /cradle/ for Ice Age megafauna
  before they spread to the Arctic. [Deng et al. (2011), "Out of
  Tibet: Pliocene woolly rhino suggests high-plateau origin of Ice
  Age megafauna," /Science/, 333, 1285–1288; DOI:
  10.1126/science.1206594]. Whether woolly rhinos ranged as far west
  as Nanga Parbat is *not directly evidenced* — but the Tibetan
  provenance and their later range across northern Eurasia makes
  passage through the western Himalayan/Karakoram passes plausible.

- *Snow leopard* (/Panthera uncia/): Molecular phylogenetics dates
  the snow leopard lineage to ~4–2 Ma. They are Pleistocene animals,
  evolved for cold high-altitude environments. They certainly
  inhabited the Nanga Parbat region during the Pleistocene, as they
  still do. [Janecka et al. (2017), "Range-wide snow leopard
  phylogeography," /Journal of Heredity/, 108, 597–607]

- *Himalayan brown bear* (/Ursus arctos isabellinus/): Present in the
  region throughout the Pleistocene. The Deosai population (very near
  Nanga Parbat) is a relict.

- *Wild yak* (/Bos mutus/): Present on the Tibetan Plateau during
  the Pleistocene, likely ranged into the higher passes of the
  western Himalaya.

- *Stegodon*: Primarily a lowland/foothill genus in South Asia. The
  chances of /Stegodon/ in the high valleys near Nanga Parbat
  (2,000+ m, narrow gorges) are *low*. These were warm-adapted
  proboscideans. Their range in Pakistan was the Siwalik foothills
  and the Potwar Plateau, not the inner mountain valleys.

*The fundamental problem*: The Nanga Parbat syntaxis is an erosional
engine. Sedimentary deposits are rapidly removed. The conditions for
fossil preservation (quiet deposition, burial, lithification) are
almost exactly opposite to the conditions prevailing in this region
(violent erosion, mass wasting, tectonic deformation). Any fossils
that might have existed in valley-fill sediments have likely been
eroded away. The absence of megafauna fossils from Diamer is not
evidence of absence of megafauna — it is evidence of an environment
hostile to fossil preservation.

*** The decompression melting pulse (~1 Ma)

v0 covers this well (Rutte et al. 2022, leucogranite dikes with
crystallisation ages as young as 0.7 Ma). The deep-time framing
adds this:

*At ~1 Ma, while the mountain was producing new granite through
decompression melting*:

- Pleistocene megafauna (brown bears, snow leopards, ibex, wild
  goats, possibly wild yak) inhabited the slopes and valleys
- Glaciers were more extensive than today
- The Indus gorge was already deeply incised (but less so than today —
  the last 1 Ma of incision at 9–12 mm/yr accounts for ~9–12 km of
  vertical erosion, though not all at one point)
- Early /Homo/ species may have been present in the broader region
  (see below)

The juxtaposition is remarkable: *the mountain was simultaneously
constructing new rock from melt and having its surface shredded by
glaciers and the Indus*. Creation and destruction operating on the
same structure at the same time, at geologically observable rates.

*** Palaeolithic humans: pre-modern human presence

*Evidence for Palaeolithic human presence in the Gilgit-Baltistan /
Diamer region is extremely sparse.* Honest assessment:

- *No confirmed Palaeolithic sites in Diamer district* are known to
  me from the published literature. The archaeological record in this
  region begins with the rock carvings (earliest claimed: ~10,000–
  8,000 BCE), which are post-Palaeolithic (Mesolithic/early
  Neolithic).

- *The broader context*: /Homo erectus/ was present in the Indian
  subcontinent from at least ~1.5 Ma (Attirampakkam, Tamil Nadu:
  Acheulean tools). Soanian culture (pebble tools) is documented from
  the Soan River valley (Potwar Plateau, Pakistan) — these are
  Lower/Middle Palaeolithic sites, ~500,000–100,000 years old. The
  Potwar Plateau is ~300 km south of Nanga Parbat. [Dennell (2004),
  "Hominid dispersals and Asian biogeography during the Lower and
  Early Middle Pleistocene," /Asian Perspectives/, 43, 205–226]

- *Denisovans and high altitude*: The Denisovan jaw from Baishiya
  Karst Cave (Xiahe, Tibet, ~160 ka) demonstrates that archaic humans
  adapted to altitudes above 3,000 m. Denisovan DNA is enriched in
  modern Tibetan populations, contributing the EPAS1 high-altitude
  adaptation gene. Whether Denisovans ranged as far west as the
  Karakoram is *unknown but plausible*. [Chen et al. (2019), "A late
  Middle Pleistocene Denisovan mandible from the Tibetan Plateau,"
  /Nature/, 569, 409–412; DOI: 10.1038/s41586-019-1139-x]

- *The KKH corridor*: The Indus gorge and its tributaries would have
  been a natural (if difficult) corridor between the lowlands and
  Central Asia throughout the Pleistocene. Any Palaeolithic humans
  transiting between the Potwar lowlands and Central Asia would have
  passed through approximately this region. But no tools or shelters
  have been published from Diamer itself.

- *Why the gap?* Same reason as the megafauna gap: extreme erosion
  destroys the sedimentary record. Rock shelters are unstable in an
  environment with 7 mm/yr uplift and corresponding erosion. Any
  Palaeolithic occupation sites would have been obliterated by
  landslides, river incision, or glacial scouring long ago. The
  petroglyphs survive because they are carved into hard gneiss
  bedrock, not deposited in soft sediments.

** A.4 Holocene Deep History (12 ka – 3 ka)

*** Earliest rock carvings at Thalpan and Chilas

The rock carvings along the Indus in the Diamer-Chilas region are
among the densest concentrations of petroglyphs in the world.
v0 covers the dam threat and overall statistics (50,000 carvings,
5,000 inscriptions, 10+ writing systems). Here we go deeper on
chronology and content.

*Chronology*:

Karl Jettmar and the Pak-German Archaeological Mission (from 1980)
established a relative chronology. Absolute dating of petroglyphs is
notoriously difficult (no organic material for radiocarbon;
re-patination rates are uncertain), so dates are approximate, based
on style, patination, and comparison with dated artefacts elsewhere.

- *Period I (pre-5000 BCE?)*: The oldest carvings. Deeply patinated,
  often obscured by later overcarving. These depict:
  - *Large wild animals*: ibex (overwhelmingly the most common
    motif), wild goats, possible wild cattle/aurochs
  - *Hunting scenes*: stick-figure humans with bows pursuing ibex
  - *Abstract symbols*: circles, spirals, dots — interpretation
    uncertain
  - The style is schematic, with little anatomical detail. The ibex
    are depicted with exaggerated, sweeping horns.

- *Period II (~3000–1000 BCE)*: More diverse motifs:
  - Ceremonial/ritual scenes: figures with elaborate headdresses,
    possible dance scenes
  - More animal species represented
  - *Maskoid motifs*: Jettmar linked these to the Okunev culture of
    southern Siberia (~2500–1500 BCE) — suggesting long-distance
    cultural connections across Central Asia. [Jettmar, K. (1985),
    "Die Felsbilder am Oberlauf des Indus," /Allgemeine und
    Vergleichende Archäologie Beiträge/, 2]
  - Possible chariots or wheeled vehicles (Bronze Age)

- *Period III (~1000 BCE – 100 CE)*: Increasing complexity:
  - Mounted riders (horse domestication reaches the region)
  - Iron Age weapons depicted
  - First writing: Kharoshthi and Brahmi inscriptions appear

- *Period IV (~100–700 CE)*: Buddhist period:
  - Stupas, Buddha figures, Jataka scenes
  - The Thalpan "Altar Rock" (Jettmar's designation): elaborate
    Buddhist narrative panels
  - Inscriptions in Brahmi, Sogdian, Middle Persian, Chinese —
    reflecting the active trade route

- *Period V (post-700 CE)*: Islamic period and later:
  - Geometric patterns, Arabic script
  - Frequency decreases

*The Okunev connection*: Jettmar's observation that maskoid/face
motifs near Chilas resemble those of the Okunev culture in the Minusinsk
Basin (southern Siberia, ~2500–1500 BCE) is provocative. It implies
cultural exchange across a vast distance — from the Yenisei River to
the Indus. The mechanism could be pastoral nomadic migration through
the Central Asian steppe corridor, or relay-style cultural
transmission. This remains debated and the chronological alignment is
imprecise. [Jettmar, K. (1994), "Petroglyphs as Evidence for
Religious Confrontation," in /Antiquities of Northern Pakistan/,
Heidelberg; Francfort, H.-P. (2005) has also discussed these
connections]

*** Neolithic transition: when did agriculture arrive?

*The Neolithic transition in the Gilgit-Baltistan region is poorly
documented.* Honest assessment:

- The Indus Valley civilisation (Harappan, ~3300–1300 BCE) was a
  lowland phenomenon — its sites are in Sindh, Punjab, and
  Balochistan, not in the high mountains. Agriculture in the
  Karakoram would have arrived later than in the lowlands.

- The petroglyphic evidence (hunting scenes dominate the earliest
  period) suggests that the Diamer region was inhabited by
  *hunter-gatherers* until at least the 4th–3rd millennium BCE.

- Agriculture in the high valleys likely arrived as irrigation-based
  terrace farming, probably from multiple directions:
  - From the south (Indus Valley / Gandhara tradition)
  - From Central Asia (exchange via the passes)
  - *Barley and wheat* are the staple crops of the high Karakoram
    valleys. Both are Near Eastern domesticates. Their arrival in
    this region probably dates to ~3000–2000 BCE, but hard evidence
    (archaeobotanical remains) from Diamer specifically is *absent*.

- *Burzahom* (Kashmir, ~3000–1000 BCE): the nearest well-excavated
  Neolithic site, showing pit-dwellings, polished stone tools, and
  domesticated wheat and barley. This demonstrates Neolithic
  agriculture was established in the western Himalayan foothills by
  ~3000 BCE. [Sharma, A.K. (2000), /Early Man in Jammu, Kashmir and
  Ladakh/, Agam Kala Prakashan]

- *The apricot*: Central to Karakoram agriculture and to the peri
  tradition (blossom season = peri descend). The apricot (/Prunus
  armeniaca/) is a Central Asian domesticate, likely arriving via the
  same trade routes that brought Sogdian inscriptions to the rocks.
  Its cultivation in the Karakoram valleys may date to the 1st
  millennium BCE but could be older.

*** Trade routes: the Karakoram as corridor

The Indus gorge at Diamer-Chilas was a *critical bottleneck* on one
of the most important trade routes in pre-modern Eurasia.

- The route connected the *Gandhara plain* (Peshawar basin, now KPK
  Pakistan) with *Central Asia* (the Tarim Basin, Ferghana, Sogdiana)
  via the Karakoram passes.

- *Before Buddhism* (~pre-500 BCE): The route was used by pastoral
  nomads and traders. The petroglyph evidence (Period II motifs with
  Central Asian parallels) suggests movement of people and ideas
  along this corridor from at least the Bronze Age (~3000 BCE).

- *The Buddhist period* (~100 BCE – 700 CE): The route became one of
  the major arteries of Buddhism's spread from Gandhara to China.
  Chinese pilgrims (Faxian, ~399 CE; Xuanzang, ~630 CE) travelled
  through or near this region. The density of Buddhist inscriptions
  and images at Thalpan reflects heavy traffic by Buddhist merchants,
  monks, and pilgrims.

- *The Silk Road connection*: The Karakoram route was a *branch of
  the Silk Road*, connecting to the main east-west route via the
  passes (Khunjerab, Mintaka, Kilik). Sogdian inscriptions at
  Thalpan are particularly significant — the Sogdians were the
  premier merchant community of the Central Asian Silk Road.
  Sogdian-language graffiti at a remote gorge on the Indus means
  Sogdian merchants physically passed through here. [Sims-Williams,
  N. (1989–1992), /Sogdian and other Iranian inscriptions of the
  Upper Indus/, Corpus Inscriptionum Iranicarum, London]

- *The Hebrew/Aramaic inscriptions*: Also reported from the Chilas
  complex. If verified, they would attest to an extraordinary
  diversity of travellers along this route. [The attribution to
  "ancient Hebrew" in some sources may be contested — Aramaic is
  more likely for a Central Asian trade context. Verification needed.]

*** Climate oscillations: Holocene effects on the region

*The Holocene Climatic Optimum (~9,000–5,000 BCE)*:

- Warmer and wetter than present across much of the Northern
  Hemisphere. In the western Himalaya, this likely meant:
  - Higher treeline (perhaps 200–400 m above present)
  - Expanded alpine meadows
  - Reduced glacier extent (less ice than today)
  - More favourable conditions for human habitation at high altitude
- The earliest petroglyphs (~8,000–5,000 BCE) coincide with the
  Holocene optimum. This is probably not coincidental — *the climate
  window made the gorge more accessible and habitable*.

*The 4.2 ka event (~2200 BCE)*:

- A global aridification event. In the Indian subcontinent, this is
  widely discussed as a contributing factor to the decline of the
  Indus Valley civilisation (Harappan culture).
- *Effect on the Karakoram/Diamer*: Uncertain. The region is already
  arid (rain shadow). The 4.2 ka event's primary signature was
  weakened monsoon — but the Diamer region depends more on westerlies
  and glacial melt than on monsoon rain. The effect may have been
  muted relative to the monsoon-dependent lowlands.
- However, *glacial advance/retreat* in the Karakoram during this
  period could have affected water availability. A warmer, drier
  climate would initially increase meltwater (more ablation) but
  ultimately reduce glacier mass (less accumulation).
- [Staubwasser et al. (2003), "Climate change at the 4.2 ka BP
  termination of the Indus valley civilization and Holocene south
  Asian monsoon variability," /Geophysical Research Letters/, 30(8);
  DOI: 10.1029/2002GL016822]

*Late Holocene neoglaciation (~3000–1500 BCE)*:

- After the Holocene optimum, glaciers re-advanced in many mountain
  regions. In the Karakoram, evidence for "neoglacial" advances has
  been documented from moraine dating. [Owen (2009), "Latest
  Pleistocene and Holocene glacier fluctuations in the Himalaya and
  Tibet," /Quaternary Science Reviews/, 28, 2150–2164]
- This would have made high passes more difficult to cross, possibly
  influencing trade route patterns.

* Gaps and Uncertainties

This section documents what was *not found* or remains uncertain:

1. *No direct megafauna fossil evidence from Diamer*. All megafauna
   information is extrapolated from the Siwaliks (300 km south,
   different elevation) or from the Tibetan Plateau (different
   region). The extreme erosion environment at Nanga Parbat destroys
   the sedimentary record needed for fossil preservation.

2. *No confirmed Palaeolithic sites in Diamer*. Soanian tools are
   known from the Potwar Plateau (300 km south). The Denisovan jaw
   from Tibet demonstrates archaic humans at altitude, but >2,000 km
   east. The gap in between is unsampled.

3. *Absolute dating of the earliest petroglyphs is uncertain*. The
   "8,000 BCE" date is based on style comparison and patination, not
   radiometric dating. The earliest carvings could be older or younger.

4. *The Neolithic transition in Diamer is undocumented*. No
   archaeobotanical evidence. The timing of agriculture's arrival is
   inferred from neighbouring regions (Kashmir, lowland Pakistan).

5. *The Okunev-Chilas connection* (maskoid motifs): Jettmar's
   observation is suggestive but the mechanism of transmission and
   exact chronological alignment need further work.

6. *Hebrew/Aramaic inscriptions at Chilas*: The "ancient Hebrew"
   attribution in some popular sources may be inaccurate. Aramaic or
   another Semitic script is more likely in a Central Asian trade
   context. Specialist epigraphy needed to verify.

7. *Web sources not verified in this session*. All citations are from
   training knowledge. URLs and DOIs should be checked before this
   material is promoted to v1.
