This survey covers the winemaking process from harvest through bottling,
organised by production category. Each section is tagged with the WSET level
at which the topic becomes examinable:

- *L1* — WSET Level 1 Award in Wines
- *L2* — WSET Level 2 Award in Wines

- *L4* — WSET Level 4 Diploma in Wines

* 1 Still Wine Production [L1/L2/L3]
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: still-wine
:END:

** 1.1 Harvesting [L2/L3]

Harvest timing is the first critical winemaking decision. Grapes must reach
optimal sugar, acid, and phenolic ripeness — the balance between these
determines the style of wine.[fn:1]

*** Hand Harvesting

- Selective picking possible (multiple passes, botrytis selection)
- Essential for steep slopes (Mosel, Douro, Côte-Rôtie)
- Gentler on fruit — less oxidation, fewer broken berries
- Labour-intensive and expensive
- Required by some appellations (Champagne, Beaujolais cru for whole-cluster)

*** Machine Harvesting

- Straddle harvesters shake berries off the vine using rubber rods
- Rapid: can pick at night to preserve acidity (common in warm climates — Australia, Central Valley)
- Cannot be used on steep slopes or for whole-cluster pressing
- Lower cost per tonne; some MOG (material other than grapes) inclusion
- Quality has improved significantly with modern destemming attachments

** 1.2 Crushing and Destemming [L2]

After harvest, grapes are typically destemmed and crushed. The crusher-stemmer
separates berries from rachis (stem) and gently breaks skins to release juice.

- *White wines*: usually pressed immediately after crushing (minimal skin contact)
- *Red wines*: crushed then transferred to fermentation vessel with skins
- *Whole-cluster fermentation* (L3): stems retained for some or all of the
  must — adds tannin structure, spice character, lowers pH slightly.
  Common in Burgundy Pinot Noir, some Syrah producers[fn:1][fn:2]

** 1.3 Maceration [L2/L3]

Maceration is the contact between juice and solid matter (skins, seeds, stems)
to extract colour, tannin, and flavour compounds.

*** Pre-fermentation maceration (cold soak)

- Must held at 5–15°C for 2–5 days before fermentation begins
- Extracts colour and fruit aromatics without harsh tannins
- Common for Pinot Noir, some Grenache

*** Extended maceration (post-fermentation)

- Wine left on skins after fermentation completes
- Polymerises tannins — initially harsh, but can produce softer mouthfeel
  if managed well
- Used for age-worthy reds (Barolo, Bordeaux blends)

*** Carbonic maceration [L3]

- Whole berries placed in a sealed vessel flooded with CO₂
- Intracellular fermentation occurs within intact berries
- Produces fruity, low-tannin wines — definitive for Beaujolais Nouveau
- Semi-carbonic maceration: only bottom grapes crushed by weight above

** 1.4 Fermentation [L1/L2/L3]

*** Alcoholic fermentation

The core reaction: yeast converts grape sugars (glucose + fructose) to ethanol
and CO₂.

C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2 C₂H₅OH + 2 CO₂

**** Yeast types [L3]

- *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*: workhorse yeast. Cultured (inoculated) strains
  give predictable, reliable fermentations
- *Indigenous/wild/ambient yeast*: naturally present on grape skins and in
  the winery. More complex flavour development but higher risk of stuck
  fermentation or off-flavours. Includes non-Saccharomyces genera
  (Kloeckera, Candida, Hanseniaspora) that dominate early fermentation
  before being outcompeted by S. cerevisiae[fn:2][fn:3]

**** Temperature control [L2/L3]

- *White wines*: 12–22°C. Lower temperatures preserve primary fruit
  aromas and volatile thiols
- *Red wines*: 20–32°C. Higher temperatures aid colour and tannin
  extraction. Risk of stuck fermentation above 35°C
- Cooling achieved via jacketed stainless steel tanks, refrigeration
  coils, or cold water drip over exterior
- Some winemakers use warm-up techniques to initiate fermentation

**** Fermentation vessels [L3]

- Stainless steel: inert, temperature-controllable, easy to clean
- Oak barrels: micro-oxygenation + oak flavour integration during ferment
  (common for white Burgundy)
- Concrete: thermal mass provides natural temperature stability; can be
  egg-shaped (Nomblot) for natural lees suspension[fn:12]
- Amphora/qvevri: see [[#orange-wine][Orange Wine]]

*** Malolactic fermentation (MLF) [L2/L3]

Not a true fermentation but a bacterial conversion:

Malic acid → Lactic acid + CO₂

Carried out by lactic acid bacteria, principally /Oenococcus oeni/.[fn:4]

Effects on wine:
- Reduces perceived acidity (converts diprotic malic to monoprotic lactic)
- Adds body and mouthfeel
- Produces diacetyl — responsible for "buttery" character
  (especially in oaked Chardonnay)
- Improves microbial stability

MLF is standard for virtually all red wines. For whites, it is a stylistic
choice: encouraged in Chardonnay (especially Burgundy, California),
deliberately prevented in aromatic varieties (Riesling, Sauvignon Blanc)
where freshness and primary fruit are desired.[fn:4][fn:5]

** 1.5 Pressing [L2/L3]

The point at which juice is separated from solid matter.

- *White wines*: pressed before fermentation (whole-cluster pressing preferred
  for delicate wines — Champagne, Alsace)
- *Red wines*: pressed after fermentation. Free-run wine (vin de goutte) is
  drained first, then the remaining must is pressed to produce press wine
  (vin de presse), which is more tannic and deeply coloured

Press types:
- *Basket press*: traditional, gentle. Used in Champagne (Coquard)
- *Pneumatic/bladder press*: inflatable bladder applies even, gentle pressure.
  Now standard for quality wine
- *Continuous screw press*: high volume, lower quality — extracts harsh tannins

** 1.6 Aging Vessels [L2/L3/L4]

*** Oak [L2/L3]

Oak barrels contribute flavour, tannin, and controlled micro-oxygenation.

**** French oak (Quercus robur, Q. petraea) [L3]

- Tighter grain than American oak
- Subtle flavours: baking spice, clove, cedar, hazelnut, light smoke
- Major forests: Allier, Tronçais, Vosges, Nevers, Limousin
- More expensive (€800–1200 per barrel)
- Preferred for Pinot Noir, Chardonnay, Nebbiolo, Bordeaux blends[fn:6]

**** American oak (Quercus alba) [L3]

- Wider grain, more porous
- Bolder flavours: vanilla, coconut, dill, sweet spice
- 2–4× more lactones than French oak
- Less expensive (€300–500 per barrel)
- Sawn rather than split — more efficient yield
- Classic for Rioja, some Australian Shiraz, Bourbon-barrel Zinfandel[fn:6]

**** New vs old oak [L2/L3]

- New oak: maximum flavour extraction. 100% new oak is a strong statement
  (First Growth Bordeaux, premium Napa Cabernet)
- 1-year-old (second-fill): softer oak influence, textural benefit
- 2–3 years: largely "neutral" — provides micro-oxygenation without
  significant flavour contribution
- After 4–5 uses: fully neutral; no flavour transfer remaining[fn:6]

**** Oak alternatives [L3]

- Oak chips, staves, spirals, powder — inserted into stainless steel tanks
- Much cheaper; can replicate some flavour effects
- Cannot replicate micro-oxygenation of a barrel
- Prohibited in some appellations (e.g., classified Bordeaux)

*** Stainless steel [L2]

- Inert: no flavour contribution
- Excellent temperature control
- Preserves primary fruit character and freshness
- Standard for aromatic whites (Sauvignon Blanc, Riesling, Albariño)

*** Concrete [L3/L4]

- Traditional in Bordeaux (cuves); modern egg-shaped vessels (Nomblot, from
  2001) create natural lees convection
- Semi-porous: some oxygen exchange, but neutral flavour
- Excellent thermal mass — stable fermentation temperatures
- Gaining popularity in quality-focused estates globally[fn:12]

*** Amphora/clay [L3/L4]

- Qvevri tradition (Georgia): 8,000 years of use
- Modern amphorae used by Italian, Spanish, and New World producers
- Porous: allows oxygen exchange similar to oak, but neutral flavour
- Adds textural complexity without oak character
- See [[#orange-wine][Orange Wine]] for extended treatment[fn:12]

** 1.7 Fining and Filtration [L3]

*** Fining [L3]

Fining agents bond with unwanted particles (proteins, tannins, colour
molecules) and precipitate them out of solution.

Common fining agents:[fn:7]
| Agent          | Target               | Source        | Vegan? |
|----------------+----------------------+---------------+--------|
| Bentonite      | Proteins (whites)    | Clay mineral  | Yes    |
| Egg white      | Harsh tannins (reds) | Animal        | No     |
| Isinglass      | Haze (whites)        | Fish bladder  | No     |
| Casein         | Colour, oxidation    | Milk protein  | No     |
| Gelatine       | Tannins, colour      | Animal        | No     |
| PVPP           | Browning phenolics   | Synthetic     | Yes    |
| Pea protein    | Tannins              | Plant         | Yes    |
| Activated carbon| Off-colours, taints | Mineral       | Yes    |

Vegan wines use bentonite, pea protein, activated carbon, or silica gel
instead of animal-derived agents.[fn:7]

*** Filtration [L3]

- *Pad/sheet filtration*: wine passes through cellulose pads
- *Membrane filtration*: precise pore size (0.45 μm for sterile filtration)
- *Cross-flow filtration*: continuous; wine flows across membrane surface
- *Unfiltered wines*: some producers eschew filtration to preserve body
  and mouthfeel, accepting slight haze risk

** 1.8 Red vs White vs Rosé: Key Differences [L1/L2]

| Step              | White                     | Red                        | Rosé                          |
|-------------------+---------------------------+----------------------------+-------------------------------|
| Skin contact      | Minimal (press before     | Extended (ferment on       | Limited (2–24 hrs) or         |
|                   | fermentation)             | skins)                     | saignée (bleeding off juice)  |
| Fermentation temp | 12–22°C                   | 20–32°C                    | 12–22°C (like white)          |
| MLF               | Optional (style choice)   | Standard                   | Rarely                        |
| Oak aging         | Optional                  | Common                     | Rare                          |
| Colour source     | N/A                       | Anthocyanins from skins    | Brief skin contact            |

Rosé methods:
- *Direct press*: black grapes pressed immediately — very pale colour
  (Provence style)
- *Saignée*: juice "bled off" red fermentation after short maceration —
  by-product that also concentrates the remaining red wine
- *Blending*: red + white wine mixed (only permitted in Champagne rosé
  within the EU)[fn:1][fn:2]

** 1.9 Bottling [L2]

- Sterile bottling: wine passes through membrane filter (0.45 μm)
  immediately before filling
- Inert gas (N₂, CO₂) used to purge bottles and headspace
- SO₂ adjusted to target free SO₂ level for protection
- Bottling date increasingly declared on labels

* 2 Sparkling Wine [L2/L3/L4]
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sparkling-wine
:END:

All sparkling wine methods require CO₂ dissolved in wine at pressure
(typically 5–6 atmospheres in fully sparkling; 1–2.5 in frizzante/pétillant).

** 2.1 Traditional Method (Méthode Traditionnelle) [L2/L3]

The defining characteristic: second fermentation occurs *in the bottle* from
which the wine will be served.[fn:8][fn:9]

Steps:
1. *Base wine*: still wine produced (often blended — assemblage)
2. *Tirage*: base wine + liqueur de tirage (yeast + sugar + adjuvant)
   bottled under crown cap
3. *Second fermentation*: ~1.2 atm CO₂ generated per 4 g/L sugar added;
   typically 24 g/L added → ~6 atm
4. *Lees aging (autolysis)*: dead yeast cells break down, releasing
   mannoproteins — gives biscuit, brioche, toasty complexity. Minimum
   aging on lees varies by appellation:
   - Champagne NV: 15 months (12 on lees)
   - Champagne vintage: 36 months
   - Cava: 9 months (Reserva 18, Gran Reserva 30)
   - Crémant: 9 months
   - Franciacorta: 18 months (Riserva 60)
5. *Riddling (remuage)*: bottles gradually tilted neck-down to collect
   lees in the neck. Traditionally by hand (pupitres), now mostly
   by gyropalette (automated)
6. *Disgorgement (dégorgement)*: neck frozen, crown cap removed, lees
   plug ejected by internal pressure
7. *Dosage*: liqueur d'expédition (wine + sugar) added to adjust sweetness:
   - Brut Nature / Zero Dosage: 0–3 g/L
   - Extra Brut: 0–6 g/L
   - Brut: 0–12 g/L
   - Extra Dry: 12–17 g/L
   - Sec/Dry: 17–32 g/L
   - Demi-Sec: 32–50 g/L
   - Doux: >50 g/L
8. *Cork and cage (muselet)*: final closure

*** Key traditional-method wines [L2/L3]

| Wine           | Region/Country    | Key grapes                          |
|----------------+-------------------+-------------------------------------|
| Champagne      | Champagne, France | Chardonnay, Pinot Noir, Pinot Meunier |
| Crémant        | Various (Alsace,  | Varies by region                    |
|                | Loire, Burgundy…) |                                     |
| Cava           | Spain (Penedès)   | Macabeo, Xarel·lo, Parellada        |
| Franciacorta   | Lombardy, Italy   | Chardonnay, Pinot Noir              |
| Cap Classique  | South Africa      | Chardonnay, Pinot Noir              |
| English sparkling | England        | Chardonnay, Pinot Noir, Pinot Meunier |

** 2.2 Charmat / Tank Method [L2/L3]

Second fermentation occurs in a pressurised stainless steel tank (autoclave),
not in the bottle.[fn:8][fn:9]

- Patented by Federico Martinotti (1895), improved by Eugène Charmat (1907)
- Shorter lees contact: preserves primary fruit and floral aromatics
- Faster and cheaper than traditional method
- Ideal for aromatic varieties: Muscat, Glera (Prosecco)
- Prosecco: the definitive Charmat-method wine. Glera grapes,
  Veneto/Friuli. Frizzante (spumante at higher pressure)
- German Sekt: most produced by Charmat; Winzersekt may use traditional
  method

** 2.3 Transfer Method [L3]

Hybrid approach:[fn:8]
1. Second fermentation in bottle (as traditional method)
2. Bottles emptied into pressurised tank
3. Wine filtered, dosage added
4. Re-bottled under pressure

- Benefits: autolytic complexity without individual riddling/disgorgement
- Used for odd-sized bottles (half-bottles, magnums) and some
  Australian sparkling wines

** 2.4 Ancestral Method (Méthode Ancestrale / Pét-Nat) [L3/L4]

The oldest sparkling winemaking method:[fn:8][fn:9]

- Wine bottled *before primary fermentation is complete*
- Residual sugar ferments in bottle → natural CO₂
- No second fermentation, no dosage, no disgorgement (usually)
- Often cloudy, with residual lees
- Lower pressure than traditional method (2–4 atm)
- Modern pét-nat revival: natural wine movement; often sealed with
  crown cap; intentionally rustic
- Historical: Blanquette de Limoux (claimed as first sparkling wine, pre-Champagne)

** 2.5 Carbonation [L2]

- CO₂ injected into still wine under pressure (like soft drinks)
- Large, aggressive bubbles; poor mousse persistence
- Lowest quality method; only for cheapest sparkling wines
- Easily identified by coarse bead and rapid dissipation

* 3 Fortified Wine [L2/L3/L4]
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: fortified-wine
:END:

Fortified wines have a distilled grape spirit (typically 77% ABV neutral
brandy) added during or after fermentation to raise the final alcohol to
15–22% ABV.[fn:10]

The timing of fortification is the key stylistic lever:
- *During fermentation* → residual sugar retained → sweet wine (Port, sweet Madeira)
- *After fermentation* → dry base wine → dry wine (Fino Sherry, Sercial Madeira)

** 3.1 Port [L2/L3]

Produced in the Douro Valley, Portugal. Grapes (Touriga Nacional, Touriga
Franca, Tinta Roriz, Tinta Barroca, Tinto Cão) are fermented for 2–3 days,
then fortified with aguardente (77% ABV grape spirit) at ~6–9% ABV to arrest
fermentation, leaving ~70–100 g/L residual sugar.[fn:10][fn:11]

*** Port styles [L2/L3/L4]

| Style              | Aging regime           | Character                           |
|--------------------+------------------------+-------------------------------------|
| Ruby               | Tank (concrete/steel)  | Fresh, fruity, deep red             |
| Reserve Ruby        | 3–6 yrs wood          | More complex ruby                   |
| Late Bottled Vintage| Single vintage,       | Ready-to-drink vintage character.   |
| (LBV)              | 4–6 yrs wood          | Filtered or unfiltered              |
| Vintage Port        | Single exceptional    | Bottled after 2–3 yrs; requires     |
|                    | vintage, declared      | 10–40 yrs bottle aging. Unfiltered  |
| Crusted            | Multi-vintage blend    | Unfiltered; throws sediment         |
| Tawny              | Extended wood aging    | Oxidative: amber, nutty, caramel    |
| Tawny (10/20/30/40)| Indicated age blend   | Increasing complexity and cost      |
| Colheita           | Single vintage tawny,  | Min 7 yrs wood. Oxidative           |
|                    | dated                  |                                     |
| White               | White grapes           | Dry to sweet; aperitif style        |

Key distinction: Ruby-style ports preserve fruit through reductive aging
(tank/bottle); Tawny-style ports develop oxidative character through
extended barrel aging.[fn:11]

** 3.2 Sherry [L3/L4]

Produced from Palomino Fino grapes in the "Sherry Triangle" (Jerez de la
Frontera, El Puerto de Santa María, Sanlúcar de Barrameda), Andalucía,
Spain.[fn:13]

*** The two aging regimes [L3]

**** Biological aging (under flor)

- Wine fortified to ~15–15.5% ABV after fermentation (dry base wine)
- /Flor/: a film of Saccharomyces yeast that forms on the wine surface
  inside partially-filled barrels (butts)
- Flor protects wine from oxidation, consumes glycerol, produces
  acetaldehyde → distinctive tangy, yeasty, almond character
- Requires humid conditions and specific temperature range (15–20°C)

**** Oxidative aging (without flor)

- Wine fortified to 17–18% ABV → flor cannot survive at this alcohol level
- Wine ages in contact with air through partially-filled barrels
- Develops mahogany colour, walnut, dried fruit, toffee character

*** Sherry styles [L3/L4]

| Style        | Aging            | ABV       | Character                          |
|--------------+------------------+-----------+------------------------------------|
| Fino         | Biological       | 15–15.5%  | Pale, dry, almond, yeasty          |
| Manzanilla   | Biological       | 15–15.5%  | Like Fino but from Sanlúcar only;  |
|              | (Sanlúcar)       |           | maritime influence, lighter, saline |
| Amontillado  | Bio → Oxidative  | 16–22%    | Amber, hazelnut, complex. Starts   |
|              |                  |           | under flor, then loses flor        |
| Palo Cortado | Bio → Oxidative  | 17–22%    | Rare (~1–2%). Nose of Amontillado, |
|              |                  |           | palate of Oloroso. Anomalous       |
| Oloroso      | Oxidative only   | 17–22%    | Dark, rich, walnut, dried fruit    |
| PX (Pedro    | Sun-dried grapes,| 15–22%    | Intensely sweet, raisined,         |
| Ximénez)     | oxidative        |           | viscous, dark brown/black          |
| Cream        | Oloroso +        | 15.5–22%  | Sweetened blend; commercial style  |
|              | sweetening wine  |           |                                    |

*** The solera system [L3]

A fractional blending system that ensures consistency across vintages:[fn:10][fn:13]

- Barrels (butts) arranged in tiers called /criaderas/ ("nurseries")
- Bottom tier (/solera/): oldest wine, from which wine is drawn for
  bottling (max 1/3 per year)
- Each criadera is replenished from the tier above it
- Top criadera receives the youngest wine (/sobretabla/ or /añada/)
- Result: no vintage date; perpetual blend that maintains house style
- A solera is never fully emptied — the average age of wine in the
  solera increases over time

** 3.3 Madeira [L3/L4]

Produced on the island of Madeira, Portugal. Unique among wines for being
deliberately heated and oxidised during aging.[fn:14]

*** Heating methods [L3]

| Method    | Description                                     | Quality   |
|-----------+-------------------------------------------------+-----------|
| Estufagem | Wine heated in steel tanks (estufas) to 45–50°C | Standard  |
|           | for 3 months, then rested 90 days               |           |
| Canteiro  | Wine aged in barrels on wooden beams (canteiros) | Premium   |
|           | in naturally warm attics; slow, gentle heating   |           |
|           | over years to decades                            |           |

*** Madeira styles (driest to sweetest) [L3]

| Style    | Grape       | Sweetness    | Character                       |
|----------+-------------+--------------+---------------------------------|
| Sercial  | Sercial     | Dry          | Crisp apple, almond, high acid  |
| Verdelho | Verdelho    | Medium dry   | Smoke, spice, light caramel     |
| Bual     | Bual        | Medium sweet | Coffee, salted caramel, dates   |
| Malmsey  | Malvasia    | Sweet        | Dried fruit, toffee, very rich  |

Madeira is virtually indestructible once opened due to its pre-oxidised
nature — a bottle can remain drinkable for months or years after opening.

** 3.4 Marsala [L3/L4]

Produced in western Sicily from Grillo, Catarratto, and Inzolia (white) or
Nero d'Avola and Perricone (red) grapes. Classified by:
- *Colour*: Oro (gold), Ambra (amber), Rubino (ruby)
- *Sweetness*: Secco (<40 g/L), Semisecco (40–100 g/L), Dolce (>100 g/L)
- *Aging*: Fine (1 yr), Superiore (2 yrs), Superiore Riserva (4 yrs),
  Vergine/Soleras (5 yrs), Vergine Riserva/Stravecchio (10 yrs)

** 3.5 Vin Doux Naturel (VDN) [L3/L4]

French fortified wines from southern France (primarily Roussillon and
Rhône).[fn:15]

- Fortified during fermentation at ~6% ABV with 96% grape spirit
- Final ABV: 15–16% (lower than Port/Sherry)
- Two grape families:
  - *Muscat* (Muscat à Petits Grains): floral, grapey, fresh.
    Muscat de Beaumes-de-Venise (Rhône), Muscat de Rivesaltes,
    Muscat de Frontignan
  - *Grenache*: dark fruit, oxidative potential.
    Banyuls, Maury, Rivesaltes
- Grenache-based VDN can be aged oxidatively (rancio style) for decades,
  developing toffee, coffee, dried fruit — conceptually similar to Tawny Port

* 4 Sweet Wine [L2/L3/L4]
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sweet-wine
:END:

Sweet wines retain residual sugar (RS) through one or more concentration
mechanisms. The challenge: achieving extreme sugar concentration while
preserving balancing acidity.[fn:16]

** 4.1 Late Harvest (Vendange Tardive / Spätlese) [L2/L3]

- Grapes left on vine beyond normal harvest to dehydrate and concentrate sugars
- Risk of rot (grey rot, not noble rot), bird damage, weather loss
- German Prädikat system (ascending ripeness):[fn:16]
  - Spätlese ("late harvest"): 76–90 °Oechsle
  - Auslese ("selected harvest"): 83–105 °Oechsle
  - Beerenauslese (BA, "selected berries"): 110–128 °Oechsle (botrytis usual)
  - Trockenbeerenauslese (TBA, "dried berries"): 150–154 °Oechsle (botrytis required)
- Alsace: Vendange Tardive and Sélection de Grains Nobles (SGN)

** 4.2 Botrytis / Noble Rot [L3/L4]

/Botrytis cinerea/, when it infects ripe, thin-skinned grapes under specific
conditions (morning mist followed by warm, dry afternoons), produces noble rot
rather than destructive grey rot.[fn:16][fn:17]

Effects on the grape:
- Fungal hyphae penetrate skin → water evaporates → sugars, acids, glycerol
  concentrate
- Produces unique flavour compounds: sotolon (honey, curry), phenylacetaldehyde
  (honey)
- Berry shrivels to a raisined state ("botrytised")

Key botrytis wines:
| Wine                 | Region            | Grape(s)                    |
|----------------------+-------------------+-----------------------------|
| Sauternes/Barsac     | Bordeaux          | Sémillon, Sauvignon Blanc   |
| Tokaji Aszú          | Tokaj, Hungary    | Furmint, Hárslevelű         |
| Beerenauslese (BA)   | Germany/Austria   | Riesling and others         |
| Trockenbeerenauslese | Germany/Austria   | Riesling and others         |
| SGN Alsace           | Alsace            | Gewürztraminer, Riesling    |
| Quarts de Chaume     | Loire             | Chenin Blanc                |
| Bonnezeaux           | Loire             | Chenin Blanc                |

*** Tokaji Aszú [L3/L4]

- Botrytised berries (aszú) hand-selected and collected into hod baskets
  (puttonyos — historically 3–6 puttonyos indicating sweetness)
- Aszú berries mashed into a paste, soaked in base must or wine for 24–48 hrs
- Since 2013: minimum 120 g/L RS (effectively the old 5 puttonyos)
- Aged minimum 18 months in oak (often Zempléni oak) + 6 months in bottle
- Tokaji Eszencia: free-run juice from aszú berries, 450–900 g/L sugar;
  ferments for years to just 1–5% ABV[fn:17]

** 4.3 Ice Wine / Eiswein [L3]

- Grapes left on vine until winter freeze (typically -7°C to -13°C)
- Pressed while frozen: water remains as ice crystals, concentrated juice
  flows out
- Very low yields, extreme risk (crop may be lost entirely)
- Not botrytised (unlike BA/TBA)
- Major producers: Canada (Niagara, Okanagan), Germany (Mosel, Rheingau),
  Austria
- Canadian icewine: must reach minimum 35° Brix at pressing
- Typical grapes: Riesling, Vidal (Canada), Grüner Veltliner (Austria)[fn:16]

** 4.4 Passito / Dried Grape Wines [L3/L4]

Grapes dried after harvest to concentrate sugars — the /appassimento/ process.

|--------------+-----------------+-----------------------+----------------------------|

| Amarone della| Veneto, Italy   | Same as Recioto       | Same drying; fermented dry |

|              |                 |                        | (vinsantaia); aged in small|
|              |                 |                        | barrels (caratelli)        |

* 5 Orange / Skin-Contact Wine [L3/L4]
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: orange-wine
:END:

** 5.1 Definition [L3]

Orange wine is white wine made like a red wine — white grapes fermented in
extended contact with their skins, seeds, and sometimes stems. The skin
contact extracts phenolics (tannins, anthoxanthins) that give the wine its
amber/orange colour, tannic structure, and textural grip.[fn:18]

Not to be confused with wine made from oranges or wine flavoured with orange.

** 5.2 History: Georgia and the Qvevri [L3/L4]

- Georgia: 8,000 years of continuous winemaking tradition — the oldest
  known wine-producing region on Earth
- Archaeological evidence: grape pips and qvevri fragments from the 6th
  millennium BCE in Kvemo Kartli[fn:18][fn:19]
- UNESCO recognition: traditional Georgian qvevri winemaking inscribed on
  the Intangible Cultural Heritage list (2013)
- Qvevri: large, egg-shaped clay vessels (200–2,000 litres) lined with
  beeswax, buried up to the neck in the ground
- Traditional process:
  1. Grapes crushed (often with stems — /chacha/)
  2. Must and solids poured into qvevri
  3. Natural fermentation (ambient yeast) — stirred daily
  4. Maceration for weeks to months (amber/red wines)
  5. Qvevri sealed with stone or wooden lid, topped with earth
  6. Wine rests on skins and lees through winter
  7. Racked to a second, clean qvevri for aging
- Currently ~10% of Georgian wine production uses qvevri[fn:18][fn:19]

** 5.3 Modern Renaissance [L3/L4]

- 1990s: Josko Gravner (Friuli, Italy) visits Georgia, begins making
  skin-contact wines in imported qvevri
- Stanko Radikon (Friuli): parallel experiments with extended maceration
  of white grapes in wooden vats
- Term "orange wine" coined by British wine importer David A. Harvey (2004)
- Now produced globally: Slovenia, Croatia, Austria, France, Spain,
  Australia, California, South Africa
- Associated with (but not exclusive to) the natural wine movement[fn:18]

** 5.4 Production Method [L3]

1. White grapes harvested (often by hand for quality)
2. Crushed/destemmed (or whole-cluster)
3. Must fermented *on skins* — maceration from days to months
4. Vessel: qvevri, amphora, oak barrel, concrete, stainless steel
5. Often spontaneous fermentation (indigenous yeast)
6. Minimal intervention: little or no SO₂, no fining, no filtration
7. Extended lees contact common
8. Result: amber to deep orange colour, tannic structure, dried fruit
   and nut character, oxidative notes, significant textural weight

** 6.1 Lees Aging [L3]

Lees are the sediment remaining after fermentation — primarily dead yeast
cells (with some grape solids, tartrate crystals, bacteria).[fn:20]

*** Gross lees vs fine lees [L3]

- *Gross lees*: heavy sediment that settles within hours. Racked off
  promptly to avoid off-flavours (H₂S, mercaptans)
- *Fine lees*: lighter particles that remain suspended. Source of
  beneficial autolytic compounds

*** Sur lie aging [L3]

Wine aged on its fine lees ("on the lees" = /sur lie/).

Effects:[fn:20][fn:21]
- Yeast autolysis releases mannoproteins and polysaccharides
- Increased body, creaminess, and mouthfeel
- Improved integration of oak and fruit
- Enhanced complexity — bready, biscuity, yeasty notes
- Natural antioxidant protection (lees consume dissolved oxygen)

Key wines aged sur lie:
- Muscadet Sur Lie (Loire): minimum until 1 March following vintage
- White Burgundy (Chardonnay): typically 6–18 months in barrel
- Champagne: on lees after second fermentation (see [[#sparkling-wine][Sparkling Wine]])

*** Bâtonnage (lees stirring) [L3/L4]

- Stainless steel rod ("bâton") inserted through barrel bung hole
  to resuspend settled lees (~8–10 seconds per barrel)
- Frequency varies: weekly during early aging, tapering to monthly
- Enhances extraction of autolytic compounds
- Increases wine richness and textural weight
- Risk: excessive bâtonnage can produce overly heavy, reductive wines
- Must be done carefully — if lees are unhealthy (bacterial
  contamination), stirring amplifies faults[fn:20][fn:21]

** 6.2 Sulphur Dioxide (SO₂) Management [L3/L4]

Sulphur dioxide is the most important additive in winemaking — a
preservative used since Roman times.[fn:23]

*** Functions

- *Antioxidant*: prevents oxidation by scavenging oxygen and inhibiting
  oxidative enzymes (polyphenol oxidase, laccase)
- *Antimicrobial*: inhibits spoilage bacteria (Acetobacter,
  Lactobacillus) and wild yeasts (Brettanomyces) at appropriate levels
- *Colour stabilisation*: binds with anthocyanins to protect red wine
  colour

*** Forms in Wine

- *Free SO₂*: the active, protective fraction. Only molecular SO₂
  (a pH-dependent equilibrium) is antimicrobially active. At wine pH
  3.0--3.5, roughly 3--6% of free SO₂ exists in molecular form —
  lower pH = more effective protection per unit of free SO₂.
- *Bound SO₂*: fraction bound to carbonyl compounds (acetaldehyde,
  sugars) — no longer protective.
- *Total SO₂*: free + bound.

*** When SO₂ Is Added

| Stage                   | Purpose                                     |
|-------------------------+---------------------------------------------|
| At crush (must)         | Inhibit wild yeast, prevent oxidation        |
| Post-fermentation       | Stabilise, prevent MLF if desired            |
| During aging            | Top-up protection after racking              |
| At bottling             | Final adjustment to target free SO₂          |

*** Legal Limits (mg/L total SO₂)

| Region/Style             | Max Total SO₂ |
|--------------------------+---------------|
| EU dry red (<5 g/L RS)   | 150           |
| EU dry white (<5 g/L RS) | 200           |
| EU sweet (>5 g/L RS)     | 200--400      |
| US (conventional)        | 350           |
| EU organic               | 100 (red), 150 (white) |
| Natural wine (informal)  | Minimal or zero |

*** The "No Added Sulphites" Debate

- All fermentation produces some SO₂ naturally (10--30 mg/L)
- "Contains sulphites" labelling required above 10 mg/L in EU, US
- Natural wine movement advocates minimal or zero SO₂ addition
- Risk without SO₂: premature oxidation, microbial instability,
  volatile acidity — requires impeccable fruit quality and winemaking
  hygiene
- Some argue low-SO₂ wines show more terroir expression; others
  argue they are simply less stable

** 6.3 Blending [L2/L3]

Blending occurs at multiple levels:[fn:1]

- *Varietal blending*: combining different grape varieties
  (Bordeaux: Cabernet Sauvignon + Merlot + Cabernet Franc…)
- *Lot blending*: combining different vineyard parcels, press fractions,
  or barrel selections of the same variety
- *Vintage blending*: combining wines from different years
  (standard in Champagne NV, Sherry solera, some Port)

Purpose: achieve balance, complexity, consistency, and house style.

** 6.4 Aging Potential [L3/L4]

Factors that contribute to a wine's ability to age:

| Factor          | Effect                                           |
|-----------------+--------------------------------------------------|
| Tannin          | Polymerises over time → softer mouthfeel          |
| Acidity         | Preservative; maintains freshness                 |
| Sugar           | Preservative (sweet wines age exceptionally well) |
| Alcohol         | Preservative at higher levels (fortified wines)   |
| Phenolics       | Undergo oxidative and reductive transformations    |
| SO₂             | Antioxidant and antimicrobial protection           |
| Bottle closure  | Controls oxygen ingress rate                      |
| Storage conditions | Cool (10–15°C), dark, humid, vibration-free    |

Most wines (>90%) are designed for consumption within 1–3 years of release.
Fine wine aging (5–50+ years) requires specific structural components and
appropriate storage.

** 6.5 Closure Types [L3]

| Closure         | Material        | O₂ transmission | TCA risk | Best for              |
|-----------------+-----------------+-----------------+----------+-----------------------|
| Natural cork    | Bark of Quercus | Low, variable   | ~3–5%    | Age-worthy wines      |
|                 | suber           |                 |          |                       |
| Agglomerated    | Ground cork +   | Moderate        | Lower    | Early-drinking wines  |
| cork            | binder          |                 |          |                       |
| Technical cork  | Cork disc +     | Low, consistent | Very low | Mid-range wines       |
| (1+1, Diam)     | agglomerate     |                 |          |                       |
| Screw cap       | Aluminium +     | Very low        | ~0%      | Aromatic whites,      |
| (Stelvin)       | plastic liner   | (controllable)  |          | early-drinking reds   |
| Synthetic cork  | Plastic polymer | Variable        | ~0%      | Entry-level wines     |
| Glass stopper   | Glass           | None (hermetic) | ~0%      | Premium whites (rare) |
| Crown cap       | Metal           | None            | ~0%      | Sparkling (tirage),   |
|                 |                 |                 |          | pét-nat               |

*** Cork taint (TCA) [L3]

2,4,6-trichloroanisole (TCA): produced when chlorine compounds interact with
fungi in cork. Imparts musty, wet-cardboard aromas at very low thresholds
(~2 ng/L). Estimated to affect 3–5% of wines under natural cork (down from
historical ~8–10% due to improved cork processing).[fn:22]

*** The closure debate [L3/L4]

- Natural cork advocates: micro-oxygenation essential for fine wine evolution;
  tradition; sustainability (cork forests are carbon sinks)
- Screw cap advocates: consistency, no TCA risk, preservation of fruit
  freshness, proven 20+ year aging capability
- The industry is trending toward segmentation: screw cap for everyday and
  aromatic wines, cork for premium reds intended for long aging
- Australia and New Zealand: >90% screw cap adoption
- Burgundy: ~5% screw cap; strong cork tradition
- The debate remains unresolved and regionally determined[fn:22]
