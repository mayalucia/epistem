* Introduction

This survey covers the sensory evaluation dimension of wine education as
structured by the Wine & Spirit Education Trust (WSET): the Systematic
Approach to Tasting, aroma classification, food pairing principles, wine
faults, quality assessment, and service. Each section is tagged with the
WSET level(s) at which the material is examined.

* WSET Systematic Approach to Tasting (SAT)                     :L2:L3:L4:

The SAT is a structured, progressive method for evaluating wine
objectively — separating quality assessment from personal preference.
The grid becomes more granular at higher levels. [cite/t:@wset-sat-official;
@mysterytasting-sat; @napavalley-sat; @grapepursuit-l3]

** Appearance

| Parameter  | L2 Options                                                    | L3 Additions                      |
|------------+---------------------------------------------------------------+-----------------------------------|
| Clarity    | (not assessed)                                                | Clear, Hazy                       |
| Intensity  | Pale, Medium, Deep                                            | same                              |
| Colour (W) | Lemon, Gold, Amber                                            | Lemon-Green, Brown                |
| Colour (R) | Purple, Ruby, Garnet, Tawny                                   | Brown                             |
| Colour (Rosé)| Pink, Pink-Orange, Orange                                   | same                              |
| Other      | —                                                             | Viscosity, Petillance, Deposit    |

|---------------------+-----------------------------------+-------------------------------------------|

** L4 Diploma Tasting

At Diploma level the SAT is used for blind assessment with mark
allocation: Appearance (3 marks), Nose (7 marks), Palate (12 marks),
Conclusions (3 marks). Candidates must demonstrate precision,
consistency, and the ability to identify wines from structural and
aromatic evidence alone. [cite/t:@wset-l4-cag]

* Wine Aromas and Flavours                                      :L2:L3:L4:

** Three-Tier Classification

Wine aromas are classified by origin into three tiers.
[cite/t:@deborahparkerwong-aromas; @sommtv-aromas; @decanter-aromas; @winefolly-aroma-wheel]

*** Primary Aromas — grape and terroir

Derived from the grape variety itself and its growing environment.

| Category   | Examples                                                              |
|------------+-----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Floral     | Rose, violet, jasmine, elderflower, blossom, lilac, lavender, hibiscus|
| Green fruit| Apple, gooseberry, pear, quince, grape                                |
| Citrus     | Grapefruit, lemon, lime, orange zest                                  |
| Stone fruit| Peach, apricot, nectarine                                             |
| Tropical   | Mango, pineapple, passion fruit, lychee                               |
| Red fruit  | Cherry, strawberry, raspberry, red plum, redcurrant                   |
| Black fruit| Blackberry, blackcurrant, black plum, blueberry                       |
| Herbal     | Grass, bell pepper, tomato leaf, eucalyptus, mint                     |
| Spice      | Black pepper, liquorice (from grape, not oak)                         |
| Earth      | Slate, wet gravel, volcanic rocks, petroleum                          |

*Varietal markers*: Cabernet Sauvignon = blackcurrant + green bell pepper
(pyrazines); Gewurztraminer = lychee + rose; Sauvignon Blanc = gooseberry +
cut grass; Riesling = lime + petroleum (with age).

*** Secondary Aromas — winemaking

Derived from fermentation and early winemaking processes.

| Source                        | Aromas                                          |
|-------------------------------+-------------------------------------------------|
| Alcoholic fermentation (yeast)| Bread, biscuit, brioche, sourdough, toast        |
| Malolactic fermentation (MLF) | Butter, cream, cheese, yoghurt                   |
| Lees contact (autolysis)      | Bread dough, pastry, biscuit, umami              |

MLF converts sharp malic acid to softer lactic acid. Prominent in
Chardonnay, Champagne (autolysis), and many reds.

*** Tertiary Aromas — aging

Develop through oxidative and reductive aging processes.

| Source          | Aromas                                                        |
|-----------------+---------------------------------------------------------------|
| Oak aging       | Vanilla, coconut, cedar, smoke, clove, nutmeg, toast, cigar box |
| Bottle aging (oxidative) | Nutty, caramel, honey, marmalade, coffee, toffee     |
| Bottle aging (reductive) | Leather, earth, mushroom, truffle, forest floor, game, tobacco |
| Dried fruit     | Fig, raisin, prune, dried apricot                             |

** The Aroma Wheel

The UC Davis Wine Aroma Wheel (Ann C. Noble, 1984) organises aromas
hierarchically from general centre categories (fruity, floral, spicy,
vegetative, earthy, chemical, etc.) outward to specific descriptors.
Wine Folly's updated wheel catalogues 100+ flavours. The wheel is a
training tool, not a prescriptive vocabulary. [cite/t:@ucdavis-aroma-wheel; @winefolly-aroma-wheel]

* Food and Wine Pairing                                          :L2:L3:

** Scientific Principles

The five basic tastes (sweet, sour, salt, bitter, umami) interact with
wine components to create harmony or clash.
[cite/t:@wset-four-rules; @scienceofcooking-pairing; @sfwineschool-pairing; @winefolly-pairing; @winesnark-salt]

| Food Component | Effect on Wine                                              |
|----------------+-------------------------------------------------------------|
| Salt           | Reduces perceived bitterness and tannin. Makes wine taste fruitier, smoother, less acidic. Softens body. |
| Acid           | Balances fat and richness. Acid-in-food + acid-in-wine can cancel each other, making wine taste softer.  |
| Fat            | Coats the palate, moderating tannin perception. Protein and fat soften tannic bitterness.                 |
| Sugar (food)   | Makes dry wines taste more acidic, bitter, and thin. Sweet food needs wine at least as sweet.             |
| Umami          | Amplifies bitterness, tannin, and acidity in wine. Challenging pairing — use fruity or off-dry wines.    |
| Chili / heat   | Amplifies alcohol burn. Choose lower-alcohol, fruitier, off-dry styles.                                   |

The "magic triangle": salt + acidity + fat in food tames even the most
tannic wines. A salty, fatty rib eye with acidic Barolo is the canonical
example.

** WSET Four Rules of Pairing

1. *Choose a wine you enjoy* — personal preference is the foundation.
2. *Understand your palate* — taste-bud density varies between individuals.
3. *Know how food changes wine* — salt, acid, fat alter perception.
4. *Stay flexible* — the same flavour principles work across proteins and
   vegetarian dishes alike.

** Complementary vs Contrasting

- *Complementary*: Matching flavour profiles — oaked Chardonnay with
  butter sauce, Sauternes with foie gras.
- *Contrasting*: Opposing elements that balance — high-acid Champagne
  with salty fried chicken, sweet Riesling with spicy Thai.

** Weight and Body Matching

Match wine body to dish weight: light wine with delicate food, full-bodied
wine with rich, heavy dishes. A Muscadet with oysters; a Barossa Shiraz
with braised short ribs.

| Region          | Wine                | Dish                                  |
|-----------------+---------------------+---------------------------------------|

* Wine Faults                                                    :L2:L3:L4:

[cite/t:@amsterdamwineacademy-faults; @winefolly-faults; @awri-faults; @bibendum-faults; @wineenthusiast-faults; @sommwine-lightstrike]

** Cork Taint (TCA)

- *Compound*: 2,4,6-trichloroanisole (TCA)
- *Cause*: Micro-organisms + chlorine compounds in cork bark
- *Aroma*: Wet cardboard, damp basement, musty newspaper, wet dog
- *Effect*: Mutes fruit, flattens complexity
- *Threshold*: Detectable at 2-4 ng/L (parts per trillion); some
  individuals detect at 1 ng/L
- *Prevalence*: 2-3% of wines sealed with natural cork
- *Fixable*: No. Always a fault at any concentration.

** Oxidation

- *Cause*: Excessive oxygen exposure (poor seal, damaged cork, improper storage)
- *Visual*: Whites turn deep gold/brown; reds lose colour, shift brown/brick
- *Aroma*: Windfall apples, bruised fruit, flat cola, sherry-like (unintended), nuts
- *Palate*: Flat, lifeless, vinegary in advanced cases
- *Fixable*: No

** Reduction

- *Cause*: Insufficient oxygen during winemaking or post-bottling
- *Aroma*: Struck match, rubber, rotten eggs, garlic, cooked cabbage
- *Compounds*: Hydrogen sulphide (H2S), mercaptans, disulphides
- *Fixable*: Mild cases dissipate with 15-20 minutes of decanting/swirling.
  Mercaptans and disulphides are more persistent.

** Volatile Acidity (VA)

- *Compounds*: Acetic acid + ethyl acetate
- *Cause*: Acetobacter/Gluconobacter bacteria, too much air + heat
- *Aroma*: Vinegar (acetic acid), nail polish remover (ethyl acetate)
- *Threshold*: Legal limits vary by jurisdiction; typically noticeable
  at >0.7 g/L acetic acid in whites, >1.0 g/L in reds. Small amounts
  can lift aromatics (some Italian reds).
- *Fixable*: No

** Brettanomyces

- *Agent*: Wild yeast /Brettanomyces bruxellensis/
- *Aroma*: Band-aid, barnyard, horse stable, leather, sweat, wet dog, medicinal
- *Compounds*: 4-ethylphenol (4-EP), 4-ethylguaiacol (4-EG)
- *Threshold*: Perception varies greatly between individuals. Low levels
  are considered part of terroir character in some traditions (Rhone,
  parts of Burgundy). High levels are universally a fault.
- *Fixable*: No

** Lightstrike

- *Cause*: Exposure to UV or blue light
- *Aroma*: Wet wool, cooked cabbage, rotten eggs, stinky cheese
- *Compounds*: Volatile sulphur compounds formed photochemically
- *Vulnerable wines*: Delicate whites — Champagne, Pinot Grigio,
  Sauvignon Blanc. Clear glass bottles are most at risk.
- *Prevention*: Dark glass, proper storage
- *Fixable*: No

** Heat Damage (Cooked Wine)

- *Cause*: Exposure to temperatures above ~25-30°C during storage or transport
- *Visual*: Cork may push out; seepage around closure
- *Aroma*: Stewed/jammy fruit, cooked prunes, roasted nuts, caramel
- *Palate*: Disjointed; alcohol protrudes; fruit structure collapses
- *Ideal storage*: 10-15°C, constant
- *Fixable*: No

** Refermentation

- *Cause*: Residual sugar referments in bottle (yeasts survive bottling
  in non-sterile conditions)
- *Markers*: Unexpected bubbles in still wine, yeasty/bready aroma,
  slight spritz, possible haze
- *Context*: Intentional in some styles (pétillant naturel, vinho verde).
  Fault when unexpected.
- *Fixable*: No

* Wine Quality Assessment                                        :L2:L3:L4:

** The BLIC Framework

Quality is assessed through four structural pillars.
[cite/t:@vitaminvino-quality; @mysterytasting-sat; @cardsofwine-blic]

| Pillar      | Definition                                                      |
|-------------+-----------------------------------------------------------------|
| *Balance*   | Harmonious integration of alcohol, acidity, tannin, fruit concentration, and (where applicable) sweetness. No single component dominates. |
| *Length*     | Duration and evolution of flavour after swallowing. ~15 seconds = long. Outstanding wines show evolving flavours on the finish. Single most important factor separating very good from outstanding. |
| *Intensity*  | Concentration and depth of aromas and flavours. Note: finest wines may show elegant restraint rather than sheer power. |
| *Complexity* | Range and interplay of distinct aromas/flavours across primary, secondary, and tertiary tiers. Outstanding wines show specificity beyond typical varietal expectation. |

** Quality Levels

| Level        | BLIC Criteria                    | Description                         |
|--------------+----------------------------------+-------------------------------------|
| Poor/Faulty  | 0 of 4                          | Faulty or fundamentally flawed      |
| Acceptable   | 1 of 4                          | Correct but simple                  |
| Good         | 2 of 4                          | Sound with some interest            |
| Very Good    | 3 of 4                          | Pronounced quality, minor gaps      |
| Outstanding  | 4 of 4                          | All pillars present, fully resolved |

** Quality vs Preference

WSET explicitly separates objective quality assessment from personal
preference. A well-made Muscadet may be "good" on BLIC criteria even if
you personally prefer big Napa Cabernet. Examiners want professional wine
evaluation, not marketing copy. Identify what prevents a wine from
reaching the next quality level.

** Price-Quality Relationship                                        :L3:

Not linear. Factors inflating price beyond quality: brand, scarcity,
fashion, region prestige, critic scores. Factors depressing price below
quality: unknown region, unfashionable variety, bulk logistics. The
WSET framework trains the ability to assess intrinsic quality
independent of label, price, or expectation.

* Serving Wine                                                   :L1:L2:

[cite/t:@wset-serving-temperatures; @wineenthusiast-serving; @wsetglobal-decanting]

** Temperature Guidelines

| Style                        | Temperature         | Notes                                    |
|------------------------------+---------------------+------------------------------------------|
| Sparkling                    | 6-10°C (43-50°F)    | Below 6°C masks flavour                  |
| Light-bodied whites, rosé    | 7-10°C (45-50°F)    | Pinot Grigio, Vinho Verde, dry rosé      |
| Full-bodied whites           | 10-13°C (50-55°F)   | Oaked Chardonnay, white Burgundy         |
| Light-bodied reds            | 13-15°C (55-59°F)   | Beaujolais, Valpolicella                 |
| Medium to full-bodied reds   | 15-18°C (59-64°F)   | Bordeaux, Barolo, Shiraz                 |
| Sweet wines                  | 6-8°C (43-46°F)     | Sauternes, Tokaji                        |
| Fortified (dry)              | 10-12°C (50-54°F)   | Fino, Manzanilla                         |
| Fortified (sweet/aged)       | 14-18°C (57-64°F)   | Tawny Port, Oloroso                      |

No wine should be served above 20°C. "Room temperature" is a pre-central-heating
European concept (~15-18°C), not modern indoor temperature.

** Decanting

Two distinct purposes:

1. *Separating sediment*: Old wines with deposit. Decant gently, stop
   when sediment reaches the neck. Stand bottle upright 24h beforehand
   if possible.
2. *Aeration*: Young tannic wines benefit from oxygen contact — softens
   tannins, opens aromatics. 30 minutes to 2 hours depending on wine.

Counterargument: prolonged air exposure can make aromatics more diffuse.
For many wines, swirling in the glass provides sufficient aeration.
Fragile old wines risk falling apart with aggressive decanting.

** Glassware

Glass shape affects aroma concentration and delivery to different palate
zones.

| Glass Type        | Characteristic                    | Used For                      |
|-------------------+-----------------------------------+-------------------------------|
| Flute             | Tall, narrow — preserves bubbles  | Sparkling wines               |
| Small bowl        | Directs aromas to nose            | Light whites, rosé            |
| Large bowl (tulip)| Allows swirling, aroma development| Full whites, Pinot Noir       |
| Bordeaux          | Tall, broad bowl, narrow rim      | Full reds (Cabernet, Merlot)  |
| Burgundy          | Wide bowl, narrower rim           | Pinot Noir, Nebbiolo          |
| Small tulip       | Concentrates aromas               | Fortified, sweet wines        |

Key principle: the glass should be large enough to swirl without spilling,
thin-rimmed for precise delivery, and tulip-shaped to concentrate aromas.
Colour-distorting glass (coloured, cut crystal) hinders visual assessment.

** Order of Service

1. Sparkling before still
2. White before red
3. Dry before sweet
4. Light-bodied before full-bodied
5. Young before old
6. Lesser before greater

Purpose: prevent palate fatigue and allow each wine to show without being
overshadowed by a more powerful predecessor.
