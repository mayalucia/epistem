** 1.1 Global Wine Production

Global wine production in 2024 was estimated at 225.8 million hectolitres
(mhl), a 4.8% decline from 2023 and the *lowest level since 1961*.
The OIV forecast for 2025 is 228--235 mhl, a modest recovery.

Top producers (2024, mhl):

| Country       | 2024 (mhl) | YoY change |
|---------------+------------+------------|
| Italy         |       44.1 | +15%       |
| France        |       ~37  | -23%       |
| Spain         |       ~33  | +9.3%      |
| United States |       23.6 | -3%        |

- Italy maintained global leadership; France recorded its lowest
  output since 1957.
- The EU collectively recorded 138.3 mhl, its lowest volume since the
  early 2000s.
- Southern Hemisphere: 45.8 mhl, down 3.6%.

** 1.2 Global Wine Consumption [L3/L4]

Wine consumption has been declining globally, driven by health
consciousness, demographic shifts (younger consumers drinking less),
and economic pressures. Consumer preferences continue shifting toward:

- White and sparkling wines
- Low- and no-alcohol wines
- Premium segments ("trading up" while buying fewer bottles)

The US, UK, and Germany remain the three largest import markets,
collectively accounting for 38.3% of global imported volume and
37.2% of value.

Source: [[https://wine-intelligence.com/blogs/wine-analytics-pricing-report-data/global-wine-trade-in-2024-low-volumes-high-prices-and-shifting-dynamics-vinovistara-wine-intelligence-oiv][Wine Intelligence: Global Wine Trade 2024]]

** 1.3 Wine Trade: Imports and Exports [L3/L4]

Total global wine export value in 2024: ~35.9 billion EUR (down 0.3%
from 2023). Volume rose 0.8%; value fell 0.5% -- trade remained
remarkably stable despite declining production and consumption.

*** Top Exporters (2024)

- *Italy*: largest exporter by volume (21.7 mhl, +3.2%); export value
  8.1 billion EUR (+5.6%).
- *France*: largest exporter by value (11.7 billion EUR, -2.4% YoY).
- *Spain*: third by volume.
- Fastest-growing exporters: Australia (+29.6%), Chile (+6.2%), Italy
  (+4.8%), South Africa (+4.5%).
- France, Italy, Spain, Australia, and Chile collectively account for
  72% of global exports by dollar value.

*** Top Import Markets

Germany, UK, and USA remain dominant. The US showed tentative recovery
in 2024, partly driven by anticipatory stockpiling ahead of feared
tariff renewals.

Bulk wine exports rose 3.3% in volume and 9.8% in value -- a notable
bright spot.

** 1.4 Retail Channels: On-Trade vs Off-Trade [L3/L4]

- *Off-trade* (retail, supermarkets, online): 76.6% of wine revenue
  in 2024. Post-pandemic home consumption trends persist.
- *On-trade* (restaurants, bars, hotels): higher margins per bottle
  but lower total volume share.

*** Pricing Tiers (On-Trade)

The widely-taught tiered approach:

| Tier          | Typical Markup |
|---------------+----------------|
| Entry wines   | ~3x cost       |
| Mid-tier      | 2.2--2.5x      |
| Prestige      | ~1.8x          |

Approximately 10--12% of on-trade selling price is allocated to
service labour, glass breakage, and cellar holding costs.

*** Pricing Factors

A bottle price is additive: vineyard costs + winery production +
packaging + overhead + compliance + freight + channel margins.

Key influences:
- Internal: production costs, taxes, rent, salaries, shipping
- External: location, global events (tariffs, pandemics), demand
- The sub-$10 category is shrinking sharply; many US retailers report
  double-digit declines under $8

Sources:

** 1.5 Wine Investment and En Primeur [L4]

*** En Primeur System

En primeur ("wine futures") is Bordeaux's annual campaign where
buyers purchase grand cru classe wines while still in barrel,
typically 18--24 months before bottling and delivery.

Mechanics:
1. Harvest occurs (e.g. autumn 2024)
2. Spring campaign (April-June 2025): critics taste barrel samples
3. Chateaux release tranches at set prices
4. Buyers pay upfront (wine cost only; tax, shipping, storage added later)
5. Delivery 18--24 months after purchase

*** 2024 Bordeaux Campaign

- Launched against a cautious market backdrop
- 20--30% price cuts across leading properties to stimulate demand
- Chateau Lafite Rothschild released at ~30% below prior vintage price
- 2024 was the wettest Bordeaux year since 1992, one of the lowest
  yields in a decade
- Quality: comfortably above 2013, not a blockbuster like 2010 or 2016
- Context: US tariff fears, Chinese economic slowdown, two-year fine
  wine price correction

Sources:
- [[https://www.wineinvestment.com/learn/magazine/2025/04/bordeaux-en-primeur-2024-vintage-a-detailed-analysis/][Cult Wines: Bordeaux En Primeur 2024 Analysis]]

** 2.1 EU Overarching Framework: PDO and PGI [L2/L3]

The EU geographical indications system provides the umbrella under
which all member-state classifications operate.

| Designation | Meaning                           | Grape Origin Requirement |
|-------------+-----------------------------------+--------------------------|
| PDO         | Protected Designation of Origin   | 100% from the area       |
| PGI         | Protected Geographical Indication | >= 85% from the area     |

*** 2024 Labelling Regulations

New EU labelling rules apply to all wines from the 2024 harvest onward:
- Mandatory nutritional information (energy value, ingredients list)
- Ingredient list can be provided via electronic means (e.g. QR code)
- Wines produced before 8 December 2023 are exempt until stocks
  are exhausted

The unified regulation (approved February 2024) covers wines, spirits,
and agricultural products with geographical indications in a single text.

Sources:
- [[https://agriculture.ec.europa.eu/media/news/new-rules-wine-labelling-enter-application-2023-12-07_en][EU: New Rules for Wine Labelling]]
- [[https://grapely.io/complete-guide-eu-wine-labeling-rules-compliance-2024/][Grapely: Complete Guide to EU Wine Labeling 2024]]
- [[https://www.wine-searcher.com/wine-label-eu][Wine Searcher: EU Wine Classifications]]

** 2.2 France: AOC/AOP [L2/L3]

The Appellation d'Origine Controlee (AOC) system, created in the
1930s-1950s, is the archetype for quality wine classification worldwide.

French wine hierarchy (ascending quality):

| Level                  | EU Equivalent | Notes                                      |
|------------------------+---------------+---------------------------------------------|
| Vin de France          | --            | Table wine, no geographic restriction       |
| IGP (Indication        | PGI           | Larger regional areas (e.g. Pays d'Oc)      |
| Geographique Protegee) |               |                                             |
| AOC / AOP              | PDO           | Strict rules: varieties, yields, methods,   |
|                        |               | geographic boundaries                       |

AOC wines must use authorised grape varieties grown in designated
areas; must be vinified, packaged, and marketed per appellation rules.

Source: [[https://winefolly.com/deep-dive/what-is-aoc-wine/][Wine Folly: AOC Wine Explained]]

** 2.3 Italy: DOCG/DOC/IGT [L2/L3]

Italian classification, created in the 1960s modelled on the French
system, with a four-tier hierarchy:

| Level | Full Name                              | Notes                                    |
|-------+----------------------------------------+------------------------------------------|
| DOCG  | Denominazione di Origine Controllata   | Highest tier (since 1980); tasting panel  |
|       | e Garantita                            | required; numbered government seal        |
| DOC   | Denominazione di Origine Controllata   | 329 DOCs; variety, yield, aging rules     |
| IGT   | Indicazione Geografica Tipica          | Broader regions; more flexibility         |
| VdT   | Vino da Tavola                         | Basic table wine                          |

The DOCG category was created in 1980 to address criticism that too
many DOCs existed with variable quality. Each DOCG wine must pass a
quality tasting panel.

Note: the "Super Tuscan" phenomenon emerged because innovative
producers used non-traditional varieties (Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot)
that did not qualify for DOC/DOCG, so wines were classified as IGT or
even VdT despite commanding premium prices.

Sources:
- [[https://www.masterclass.com/articles/what-is-doc-and-docg-wine-differences-between-doc-docg-igt-and-vdt-on-italian-wine-labels][MasterClass: DOC and DOCG Wine Explained]]
- [[https://bonnerprivatewines.com/the-wine-explorers-letter/doc-docg-igt-vdt/][Bonner Private Wines: Italian Wine Classification Guide]]

** 2.4 Spain: DO/DOCa [L3]

| Level          | Notes                                              |
|----------------+----------------------------------------------------|
| DOCa / DOQ     | Highest (only Rioja and Priorat currently qualify)  |
| DO             | Denominacion de Origen; equivalent to French AOC    |
| Vino de Pago   | Single-estate wines with recognised terroir         |
| VT             | Vino de la Tierra (country wine; = PGI)             |
| Vino de Mesa   | Table wine                                          |

Spain also uses aging classifications within DO wines:
- *Joven*: young, little or no oak
- *Crianza*: min 2 years aging (6+ months oak for reds)
- *Reserva*: min 3 years (12+ months oak for reds)
- *Gran Reserva*: min 5 years (18+ months oak for reds)

Source: [[https://winefolly.com/deep-dive/looking-for-good-wine-start-with-the-appellation/][Wine Folly: Wine Appellations of the US, France, Italy, and Spain]]

** 2.5 Germany: Pradikat System [L3/L4]

Germany's classification is uniquely based on *grape ripeness at
harvest* rather than vineyard hierarchy.

Two main categories:
- *Qualitatswein* (quality wine): from a specified region (Anbaugebiet)
- *Pradikatswein* (quality wine with distinction): six ascending levels

*** The Six Pradikat Levels (ascending ripeness)

| Level                | Description                                             |
|----------------------+---------------------------------------------------------|
| Kabinett             | Fully ripe grapes, normal harvest; light, often low-alc |
| Spatlese             | Late harvest; riper, more concentrated                  |
| Auslese              | Selectively harvested; medium-sweet to sweet            |
| Beerenauslese (BA)   | Individually selected overripe berries; often botrytis  |
| Eiswein              | Grapes frozen on vine, pressed frozen; intensely sweet  |
| Trockenbeerenauslese | Individually selected dried botrytised grapes; rarest,  |
| (TBA)                | most concentrated, extraordinary longevity              |

Note: "trocken" (dry) and "halbtrocken" (off-dry) on the label
indicate residual sugar level independent of the Pradikat.

The VDP (Verband Deutscher Pradikatsweinguter) overlays a Burgundian
vineyard-based hierarchy: Gutswein, Ortswein, Erste Lage, Grosse Lage
-- but this is a private classification, not legally mandated.

Sources:
- [[https://bestofwines.com/blog/classification-of-german-wine-pradikatsweine/][Best of Wines: Classification of German Wine]]
- [[https://mag.sommtv.com/2023/03/vdp-wine-classification-system/][SOMM TV: Understanding the VDP Classification]]
- [[https://germanwineusa.com/basics/classifications/][German Wines USA: Classifications]]

** 2.6 New World Labelling [L2/L3]

New World countries (US, Australia, Chile, Argentina, South Africa,
New Zealand) favour *varietal labelling* over geographic/appellation
naming.

*** Key Rules

| Country    | Varietal minimum | Vintage minimum | Region/GI source |
|------------+------------------+-----------------+------------------|
| USA (TTB)  | 75%              | 95%             | AVA system       |
| Oregon     | 90% (except Cab) | 95%             | AVA system       |
| Australia  | 85%              | 85%             | GI system        |
| EU (export)| 85%              | 85%             | PDO/PGI          |

- AVA (American Viticultural Area): designates geographic boundaries
  but does *not* regulate varieties, yields, or winemaking practices
  (unlike European appellations)
- Australian GI: similar to AVA -- boundary-only, no production rules

The fundamental Old World / New World divide:
- *Old World* labels emphasise place (Burgundy, Barolo, Rioja)
- *New World* labels emphasise grape (Cabernet Sauvignon, Shiraz)

Source: [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wine_law][Wikipedia: Wine Law]]

** 3.1 Climate Change and Viticulture [L3/L4]

Climate change is the single largest challenge facing the wine
industry. Its effects are measurable, accelerating, and region-specific.

*** Observable Impacts

- *Earlier harvests*: most vineyards harvest 2--3 weeks earlier than
  40 years ago. Bordeaux and Burgundy document harvests occurring up
  to two weeks earlier than a few decades ago.
- *Rising alcohol*: accelerated ripening raises sugar content, lowers
  acidity. Languedoc data show a clear trend toward increased potential
  alcohol in grapes.
- *Shifting wine map*: England now has 800+ vineyards producing
  quality sparkling and still wines. Denmark, Canada's British
  Columbia, and Patagonia are emerging regions.
- *Regional extinction risk*: up to 90% of traditional wine regions
  in lowland coastal areas of Greece, Italy, Southern California, and
  Spain could disappear by end of century if warming exceeds 2C.

*** Adaptation Strategies

- Planting heat-tolerant varieties (e.g. Touriga Nacional in Bordeaux)
- Higher-altitude and cooler-facing vineyard sites
- Canopy management to reduce sun exposure
- Night harvesting to preserve acidity
- Drought-resistant rootstocks

Sources:
- [[https://www.nature.com/articles/s43017-024-00521-5][Nature Reviews Earth & Environment: Climate Change Impacts on Wine Production]]
- [[https://eos.org/articles/climate-change-threatens-70-of-winemaking-regions][Eos: Climate Change Threatens 70% of Winemaking Regions]]
- [[https://www.internationalwinechallenge.com/Canopy-Articles/weathering-the-storm-how-climate-change-is-reshaping-the-wine-industry-in-2025.html][IWC: Climate Change Reshaping Wine Industry 2025]]

** 3.2 Water Scarcity [L3/L4]

Water is becoming the critical constraint for viticulture in many
traditional regions.

- Spain experienced one of its smallest harvests in decades due to
  multi-year drought; water scarcity is an existential threat to
  Spanish viticulture
- France recorded lowest harvest since 1957 (heatwaves + drought +
  storms)
- Moldova: grape harvests declined ~35% in 2024 vs prior years
- California: ongoing investment in irrigation infrastructure,
  drought-resistant varieties, and water recycling

Modern responses: soil moisture sensors, underground drip irrigation,
precision weather stations, regulated deficit irrigation (RDI) to
deliberately stress vines for quality concentration.

Sources:
- [[https://smartwatermagazine.com/blogs/graham-mann/wine-industry-water-crisis][Smart Water Magazine: Wine Industry and the Water Crisis]]
- [[https://grapeandwinemag.com/2025/05/23/vineyards-confront-drought-and-rising-heat-with-smarter-farming-solutions/][Grape and Wine Magazine: Vineyards Confront Drought]]

** 3.3 Sustainability: Organic, Biodynamic, Natural [L3/L4]

*** Market Scale

The global organic wine market: USD 11.87 billion in 2024, projected
to reach USD 21.48 billion by 2030 (CAGR 10.4%).

*** Definitions

- *Organic*: no synthetic pesticides or fertilisers in the vineyard;
  limited additives in the cellar (EU organic wine regulation since 2012)
- *Biodynamic*: organic + following Rudolf Steiner's agricultural
  calendar and preparations (horn manure, quartz, compost preps);
  certified by Demeter
- *Natural wine*: no official legal definition; broadly means organic
  or biodynamic grapes, wild/indigenous yeasts, minimal or no
  sulphur additions, no fining or filtration
- *Regenerative viticulture*: focus on rebuilding soil health,
  increasing biodiversity, carbon sequestration

*** Trends

- ~22% of French vineyards are certified organic or in conversion
  (up from 6% in 2010)
- ~60% of millennial and Gen Z consumers willing to pay more for
  eco-friendly wine
- Asia-Pacific organic wine market growing at 12.5% CAGR
- Sustainable packaging (canned wine, lighter bottles, bag-in-box)
  gaining traction

Sources:
- [[https://www.grandviewresearch.com/industry-analysis/organic-wine-market-report][Grand View Research: Organic Wine Market]]
- [[https://tastewise.io/blog/organic-wine-trends][Tastewise: Organic Wine Trends 2025]]
- [[https://www.vinerra.com/blog/wine-business-management-natural-and-organic-wines-an-overview-of-consumer-trends][Vinerra: Natural and Organic Wines Consumer Trends]]

** 3.4 Technology in Winemaking [L4]

*** Precision Viticulture

The global precision viticulture market: $3.24 billion in 2024,
projected $9.51 billion by 2032 (CAGR 14.4%).

Key technologies:
- *Drones and satellite imaging*: NDVI (normalised difference
  vegetation index) canopy mapping; vine detection via YOLO deep
  learning algorithms achieving ~90% accuracy
- *GPS-guided steering systems*: 39% market share in 2024
- *Soil and weather sensors*: real-time moisture, temperature,
  humidity monitoring
- *AI-driven analytics*: predictive models for disease, harvest
  timing, irrigation scheduling

*** Industry Adoption (2025-2026)

- E. & J. Gallo Winery: AI-driven robotics-assisted monitoring
  (January 2026)
- Deere & Company: $35M investment in automated vineyard management
  (November 2025)
- Japan: pilot projects using drones and AI weather prediction for
  harvest timing

** 3.5 Diversity and Inclusion [L4]

The wine industry has historically lacked diversity at leadership levels.

Key data points:
- Black Americans: 12% of alcohol consumers but 7.8% of industry
  workforce and only 2% of executive leadership
- Post-2020 initiatives: BIPOC-led wine clubs, mentorship programmes,
  scholarship funds
- Grassroots efforts are increasingly effective, though top-down
  corporate DEI programmes have lost momentum in some markets
- Companies with greater diversity report higher revenue on average
  vs industry peers

Challenges remain around inclusivity messaging, attracting younger
and more diverse consumer demographics, and representation in
winemaking, ownership, and criticism.

** 3.6 Tariffs, Trade Policy, and Geopolitics [L3/L4]

Trade policy has become a major determinant of wine market dynamics.

*** US-EU Tariff History

- *2019--2021*: US imposed 25% tariffs on still wines from France,
  Germany, Spain, and the UK (under Section 301, Airbus-Boeing
  dispute). Sparkling wines and wines from Italy, Portugal exempted.
  Impact: French wine exports to the US fell 50%+ in value over the
  tariff period.
- *March 2021*: Tariffs suspended for five years under Biden
  administration.
- *2025--2026*: Tariff uncertainty renewed under second Trump
  administration. Reciprocal tariff proposals of 10--20% on EU goods
  announced, with wine specifically named. European producers
  stockpiling in US warehouses in anticipation.

*** China Tariffs

- *2020--2024*: China imposed 175--218% combined anti-dumping and
  countervailing duties on Australian wine (political retaliation
  linked to COVID-19 inquiry). Australian wine exports to China
  collapsed from ~AUD 1.3 billion (2019) to near zero.
- *March 2024*: Duties lifted. Australian exports recovering but the
  supply chain disruption forced permanent market diversification.
- Broader China context: Chinese wine consumption declining (~5% CAGR
  negative since 2017); domestic production shrinking; young Chinese
  consumers shifting toward spirits and beer.

*** Impact on Industry Structure

- Tariff uncertainty drives short-term behaviour: stockpiling,
  price adjustments, market diversion
- Long-term: producers diversify export markets to reduce single-
  market dependence
- Bulk wine trade partially sheltered (tariffs often applied to
  bottled wine, not bulk)
- Small producers disproportionately affected — cannot absorb tariff
  costs or pivot markets quickly

Sources:

- [[https://www.wine-searcher.com/m/2024/03/china-lifts-tariffs-on-australian-wine][Wine-Searcher: China Lifts Australian Wine Tariffs]]

** 3.7 Direct-to-Consumer (DTC) Sales [L3/L4]

DTC channels bypass the traditional three-tier distribution system
(producer -> distributor -> retailer). DTC bypasses intermediary
markups but requires wineries to invest in marketing, compliance,
and fulfilment infrastructure.

Trends:
- Wine club memberships remain important for small/medium producers
- E-commerce growth accelerated post-pandemic but has plateaued
- Regulatory complexity: US interstate shipping laws vary by state
- Emphasis on authenticity, sustainability, and wellness messaging
  to drive loyalty

** 3.8 Wine and Health [L3/L4]

A contested and evolving area with significant commercial and
regulatory implications.

*** The "French Paradox" and Resveratrol

- Coined by Serge Renaud (1991): French people have lower
  cardiovascular disease rates despite high saturated fat consumption.
  Red wine was proposed as the protective factor.
- Resveratrol (a polyphenol in grape skins) shown to have
  anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties in laboratory studies.
- However: the doses required for therapeutic effect far exceed what
  can be consumed through wine. The French Paradox is now attributed
  more broadly to Mediterranean diet, healthcare system, and lifestyle
  factors.

*** Current Medical Consensus (2024--2026)

- WHO (2023): "No safe level of alcohol consumption for health."
- Global Burden of Disease Study (Lancet, 2018, updated 2022):
  alcohol is the seventh leading risk factor for death and disability
  worldwide. The risk curve starts at zero consumption.
- Canadian guidance (2023): revised to recommend maximum 2 standard
  drinks per week (down from 15 for men, 10 for women previously).
- Ireland (2025): first country to mandate cancer warning labels on
  all alcohol products.
- Nuance: some epidemiological studies still show a J-curve
  (moderate consumption slightly lower risk than abstention for
  certain cardiovascular outcomes), but this is increasingly
  attributed to confounding factors.

*** Low- and No-Alcohol Wine

- Global low/no-alcohol wine market growing at ~7% CAGR
- Production methods: vacuum distillation, reverse osmosis, spinning
  cone column to remove alcohol post-fermentation
- Dealcoholised wines (< 0.5% ABV) are the fastest-growing segment
- Quality improving rapidly but remains variable; aroma and body
  are the main challenges
- EU: "dealcoholised wine" labelling permitted from 2021

*** Regulatory Trends

- Health warning labels expanding: Ireland (cancer warning), South
  Korea, Russia mandated warnings; EU considering harmonised labels
- Calorie/ingredient labelling now mandatory in EU (2024 rules)
- Some US states and Canadian provinces restricting alcohol marketing
- "Neo-prohibitionist" sentiment in Scandinavian and Anglophone
  markets; industry responding with premiumisation and moderation
  messaging

** 4.2 Monastic Tradition (500-1500 CE) [L2/L3]

With the fall of the Roman Empire, Christian monasteries became the
guardians of viticulture.

- *Benedictines* (founded 529 CE): preserved winemaking knowledge;
  expanded vineyards across Europe; Dom Perignon was a Benedictine monk
  at Hautvillers (Champagne)
- *Cistercians* (founded 1098 CE): systematically mapped terroir in
  Burgundy; created the concept of clos (walled vineyards); identified
  quality differences between vineyard plots that became the basis for
  the modern cru system
- Monasteries developed: cellars for aging, barrel technology,
  preservation methods, detailed viticultural records

The monastic contribution was not merely preservation -- it was the
first systematic, empirical approach to understanding how site affects
wine quality. This is the intellectual ancestor of modern terroir theory.

Source: [[https://www.carneroswinecompany.com/blogs/news/where-does-wine-come-from-historically][Carneros Wine Company: Origins of Wine]]

** 4.3 Phylloxera Crisis (1860s-1900s) [L2/L3]

The Great French Wine Blight -- phylloxera vastatrix, a root-feeding
aphid native to eastern North America -- was accidentally introduced
to Europe around 1858-1862 via imported American vine specimens.

Timeline:
- *1863*: First documented attack at Pujaut (Gard, Languedoc)
- *1878*: 915,000 acres killed, 620,000 acres dying (>25% of French
  vineyard acreage)
- *By 1895*: French wine production had halved
- *The French government offered 300,000 francs* reward for a solution
- *Solution*: grafting Vitis vinifera onto phylloxera-resistant
  American rootstock (Vitis rupestris, V. riparia, V. berlandieri)

The crisis:
- Devastated virtually all European wine regions
- Drove thousands of vignerons to emigrate (many to Algeria, Argentina,
  Chile)
- Permanently changed global viticulture: nearly all commercial
  vinifera vines today grow on American rootstock
- A few regions remain ungrafted (parts of Chile, some sandy soils in
  southern France, old vines in Australia)

- [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_French_Wine_Blight][Wikipedia: Great French Wine Blight]]

** 4.4 The Judgment of Paris (1976) [L2/L3]

On 24 May 1976, English wine merchant Steven Spurrier organised a
blind tasting in Paris comparing top California wines against
prestigious French counterparts, intended to celebrate the American
Bicentennial.

*** Results

- *White*: 1973 Chateau Montelena Chardonnay (Napa) topped the
  white Burgundies
- *Red*: 1973 Stag's Leap Wine Cellars S.L.V. Cabernet Sauvignon
  (Napa) topped the Bordeaux reds

The judges were among the most respected French tasters. The result
shattered the assumption that fine wine could only come from Europe
and catalysed the global expansion of quality winemaking.

French response: "The California wines won't age." Subsequent
re-tastings over 30 years largely confirmed the California wines'
quality.

Source: [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judgment_of_Paris_(wine)][Wikipedia: Judgment of Paris (Wine)]]

** 4.5 Globalisation of Wine (1980s-present) [L3]

Post-Judgment of Paris, wine production globalised rapidly:

- *1980s-1990s*: "flying winemakers" (Australian, French oenologists
  consulting worldwide); New World production surges
- *Chile, Argentina, South Africa, New Zealand* establish export
  industries
- *China* emerges as both producer and consumer (now ~5th largest
  vineyard area globally)
- *Technology transfer*: temperature-controlled fermentation,
  stainless steel, modern oak programs spread globally
- *Terroir debate*: New World initially emphasised fruit and
  winemaker style; increasing adoption of terroir-driven approaches

** 4.6 Rise of Wine Criticism [L3/L4]

*** Robert Parker and the 100-Point System

Robert Parker launched /The Wine Advocate/ in 1978. His 100-point
scoring system (modelled on US high school grades, range 50-100) was
designed to counter what he saw as conflicted, obscure wine criticism.

Impact:
- Democratised wine evaluation -- any consumer could understand a
  numerical score
- Accused of homogenising wine style ("Parkerisation"): rewarding
  big, ripe, oaky wines
- Centralised unprecedented power in a single critic
- The scale was widely imitated (Wine Spectator, Wine Enthusiast, etc.)

*** Other Major Critics and Publications

- *Jancis Robinson MW*: British critic; uses a 20-point scale;
  emphasises that numerical scores are inherently problematic given
  tasting subjectivity; author of /The Oxford Companion to Wine/
- *James Suckling*: formerly Wine Spectator; now independent;
  100-point scale; strong Italian wine focus
- *Wine Spectator*: major US publication; 100-point scale; panel tasting
- *Decanter*: UK-based; Decanter World Wine Awards
- *Neal Martin, Antonio Galloni, Lisa Perrotti-Brown MW*: subsequent
  generation of critics via Vinous and The Wine Advocate

The era of single-critic dominance is fading. Crowdsourced platforms
(Vivino, CellarTracker), sommelier influence, and diverse media
voices are fragmenting critical authority.

** 5.1 WSET (Wine & Spirit Education Trust) [L1/L2/L3/L4]

Founded in 1969, London-based. The world's largest provider of wine
and spirits education.

| Level | Name                        | Focus                                       | Duration       |
|-------+-----------------------------+---------------------------------------------+----------------|
| 1     | Award in Wines              | Introduction: main types and styles          | 1 day          |
| 2     | Award in Wines              | Grape varieties, regions, tasting technique  | 3-5 days       |
| 3     | Award in Wines              | Detailed: factors of quality, style, price;  | ~5 days + study|
|       |                             | major world regions in depth                 |                |
| 4     | Diploma in Wines            | Near-professional: 6 units covering wines,   | 18 months to   |
|       |                             | spirits, fortified, sparkling, business      | 3 years        |

Level 4 Diploma is the stepping stone to the Master of Wine programme.
It consists of six units including a research assignment.

WSET also offers separate qualifications in spirits and sake.

** 5.2 Court of Master Sommeliers (CMS) [L3/L4]

Founded in 1977, UK-based (now with Americas and European chapters).
Oriented toward restaurant service and hospitality.

| Level        | Exam Components                        | Pass Rate     |
|--------------+----------------------------------------+---------------|
| Introductory | Theory only                            | ~85%          |
| Certified    | Theory, tasting, service               | ~50-60%       |
| Advanced     | Theory, tasting (6 wines), service     | ~30%          |
| Master       | Theory, tasting (6 wines), service     | ~5-8%         |
| Sommelier    |                                        |               |

Fewer than 300 people have ever earned the Master Sommelier title.

The CMS includes a *practical service component* at every level above
introductory -- candidates must demonstrate tableside manner, bottle
service, decanting, and wine list management.

** 5.3 Master of Wine (MW) [L4]

The Institute of Masters of Wine was founded in 1955 in London.

- Requires WSET Diploma (or equivalent) as a prerequisite
- Programme typically takes 3-5 years
- Three stages: Stage 1 (theory + tasting), Stage 2 (dissertation)
- As of 2025, approximately 420 MWs exist worldwide
- MW is considered the most prestigious academic wine qualification
- MWs tend toward careers in writing, consulting, education, trade,
  and production -- not typically restaurant service

** 5.4 CMS vs WSET: Key Comparison [L3/L4]

| Dimension          | WSET                            | CMS                            |
|--------------------+---------------------------------+--------------------------------|
| Orientation        | Academic / trade / education    | Restaurant service / hospitality|
| Study mode         | Extensive class time            | Largely independent study       |
| Service component  | None                            | Central (from Certified onward) |
| Tasting method     | WSET Systematic Approach (SAT)  | CMS deductive method            |
| Career path        | Trade, education, buying, MW    | Sommelier, restaurant, hotels   |
| Global reach       | 75+ countries, 700+ providers   | Primarily US, UK, Europe        |
| Highest credential | Leads to MW programme           | Master Sommelier                |

*** Which to Choose?

- Restaurant / hospitality career: CMS
- Trade, buying, education, journalism: WSET -> MW
- General wine knowledge: WSET (more structured curriculum)
- Both are respected; they are complementary, not competing

** 5.5 Other Wine Education Programmes [L3/L4]

- *Wine Scholar Guild*: deep-dive programmes by country (French Wine
  Scholar, Italian Wine Scholar, Spanish Wine Scholar)
- *Society of Wine Educators (SWE)*: Certified Specialist of Wine (CSW),
  Certified Specialist of Spirits (CSS) -- US-focused
- *University programmes*: UC Davis (viticulture and enology), University
  of Adelaide, Universite de Bordeaux, Geisenheim University
- *National programmes*: Weinakademiker (Austria/Germany), Cape Wine
  Academy (South Africa)
