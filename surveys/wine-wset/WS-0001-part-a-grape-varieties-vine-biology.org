** 1.01 Cabernet Sauvignon
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Bordeaux, France. A natural cross of Cabernet Franc and
  Sauvignon Blanc.
- *Color*: Deep, dark ruby to purple.
- *Tannin*: High. Firm, structured tannins that soften with age.
- *Acidity*: Medium to medium-high.
- *Body*: Full-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Blackcurrant (cassis), black cherry, green pepper
  (cool climate), cedar, tobacco, vanilla (oak-aged), eucalyptus, mint.
- *Where it thrives*: Bordeaux (Left Bank: Medoc, Graves), Napa Valley,
  Coonawarra (Australia), Maipo Valley (Chile), Stellenbosch (South
  Africa), Bolgheri (Italy — Super Tuscans).
- *Notable wines*: Chateau Latour, Chateau Mouton Rothschild, Opus One,
  Almaviva, Sassicaia.

cite:[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabernet_Sauvignon][Wikipedia: Cabernet Sauvignon]]
cite:[[https://winefolly.com/deep-dive/common-types-of-wine/][Wine Folly: Common Types of Wine]]

** 1.02 Merlot
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Bordeaux, France.
- *Color*: Medium to deep ruby.
- *Tannin*: Medium, softer and more approachable than Cabernet Sauvignon.
- *Acidity*: Medium (lower than Pinot Noir).
- *Body*: Medium to full-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Plum, black cherry, raspberry, pomegranate,
  chocolate, herbal notes (bell pepper, green olive in cooler areas),
  vanilla and mocha when oak-aged.
- *Where it thrives*: Bordeaux (Right Bank: Saint-Emilion, Pomerol),
  Napa Valley, Washington State, northern Italy (Veneto, Friuli),
  Chile, New Zealand (Hawke's Bay).
- *Notable wines*: Chateau Petrus, Chateau Le Pin, Chateau Cheval Blanc.

cite:[[https://bettertastingwine.com/red_wine_types.html][Better Tasting Wine: Red Wine Types]]
cite:[[https://halleckvineyard.com/pinot-noir-vs-cabernet-vs-merlot/][Halleck Vineyard: Pinot Noir vs Cabernet vs Merlot]]

** 1.03 Pinot Noir
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Burgundy, France. One of the oldest cultivated varieties.
- *Color*: Pale to medium ruby, translucent.
- *Tannin*: Low to medium. Smooth, silky texture.
- *Acidity*: Medium-high to high.
- *Body*: Light to medium-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Strawberry, red cherry, raspberry, mushroom, tea
  leaf, truffle, forest floor (aged), clove, cinnamon. Develops
  leather, game, and sous-bois with age.
- *Where it thrives*: Burgundy (Cote de Nuits, Cote de Beaune), Champagne
  (as a blending grape), Oregon (Willamette Valley), New Zealand
  (Central Otago, Martinborough), Sonoma Coast, Tasmania, Alto Adige,
  Baden (Germany).
- *Notable wines*: Romanee-Conti, Chambertin, Volnay, Domaine Leroy.
  Also the principal grape in Champagne and many sparkling wines.

cite:[[https://halleckvineyard.com/pinot-noir-vs-cabernet-vs-merlot/][Halleck Vineyard]]
cite:[[https://tasteofpurple.com/learn/pinot-noir-vs-cabernet/][Taste of Purple: Pinot Noir vs Cabernet]]

** 1.04 Syrah / Shiraz
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Northern Rhone Valley, France. DNA analysis showed it is a
  cross of Dureza and Mondeuse Blanche.
- *Color*: Deep, inky dark purple.
- *Tannin*: Medium-plus to high.
- *Acidity*: Medium to medium-high.
- *Body*: Medium to full-bodied (moderate climate) to consistently
  full-bodied (warm climate).
- *Aromas/flavors*:
  - *Cool/moderate climate (Syrah)*: Blackberry, black pepper, mint,
    violet, smoked meat, olive.
  - *Warm climate (Shiraz)*: Jammy blackberry, licorice, anise, leather,
    chocolate, vanilla.
- *Where it thrives*: Northern Rhone (Hermitage, Cote-Rotie, Cornas),
  Barossa Valley, McLaren Vale, Hunter Valley (Australia), Walla Walla
  (Washington), Stellenbosch, Rhone blends (GSM) worldwide.
- *Notable wines*: Hermitage (Jaboulet, Chapoutier), Penfolds Grange,
  Henschke Hill of Grace.

cite:[[https://www.wine-searcher.com/grape-1112-shiraz-and-syrah][Wine-Searcher: Syrah/Shiraz]]
cite:[[https://wineinsiders.com/blogs/wine-101-types-of-wines-grapes/syrah-shiraz][Wine Insiders: Syrah/Shiraz]]

** 1.05 Tempranillo
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Spain. Name derives from /temprano/ (early), referring to
  its early ripening.
- *Color*: Medium ruby, tends to pale with age.
- *Tannin*: Medium.
- *Acidity*: Low to medium (naturally low acid and low sugar).
- *Body*: Medium to full-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Plum, strawberry, cherry, leather, tobacco, vanilla,
  dried fig. Takes well to oak (American oak = coconut, dill; French
  oak = spice, cedar).
- *Where it thrives*: Rioja, Ribera del Duero, Toro, La Mancha (Spain);
  Douro (Portugal, as Tinta Roriz); Argentina; Australia.
- *Notable wines*: Vega Sicilia Unico, Pingus, Lopez de Heredia Vina
  Tondonia.
- *Synonyms*: Tinta Roriz (Portugal), Cencibel (La Mancha), Tinto Fino
  (Ribera del Duero), Ull de Llebre (Catalonia).

cite:[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tempranillo][Wikipedia: Tempranillo]]

** 1.06 Sangiovese
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Tuscany, Italy. Name means "blood of Jove" (Jupiter).
- *Color*: Medium ruby with garnet rim.
- *Tannin*: Medium-plus to high. Firm, sometimes rustic.
- *Acidity*: High. Naturally high-acid grape.
- *Body*: Medium to full-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Sour cherry, red plum, dried herbs, tobacco, leather,
  tomato leaf, earth, tea. With age: dried fruit, leather, game.
- *Where it thrives*: Tuscany (Chianti Classico, Montalcino, Montepulciano,
  Scansano), Romagna, Umbria (Montefalco blends), Corsica (as
  Nielluccio), Argentina, California.
- *Notable wines*: Brunello di Montalcino (100% Sangiovese Grosso),
  Chianti Classico Riserva, Vino Nobile di Montepulciano, Super Tuscans
  (Tignanello, blended).
- *Clones*: Sangiovese Grosso (larger berry, used in Brunello),
  Sangiovese Piccolo.

cite:[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sangiovese][Wikipedia: Sangiovese]]
cite:[[https://winefolly.com/deep-dive/guide-to-sangiovese-wine/][Wine Folly: Sangiovese]]
cite:[[https://www.totalwine.com/discover/learn/sangiovese][Total Wine: Sangiovese]]

** 1.07 Nebbiolo
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L3
:END:

- *Origin*: Piedmont, Italy. Name from /nebbia/ (fog), referring to the
  autumn fogs of the Langhe hills, or the fog-like bloom on ripe berries.
- *Color*: Pale garnet/brick — deceptively light for the tannin level.
- *Tannin*: Very high. Powerful, gripping tannins that require years of
  aging to soften.
- *Acidity*: High.
- *Body*: Full-bodied despite pale color.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Roses, violets, tar, cherry, raspberry coulis,
  truffle, dried herbs, licorice, leather. The classic descriptor is
  "tar and roses."
- *Where it thrives*: Piedmont (Barolo DOCG, Barbaresco DOCG, Roero,
  Langhe, Gattinara, Ghemme), Lombardy (Valtellina, as Chiavennasca).
  Limited plantings outside Italy.
- *Notable wines*: Barolo (Giacomo Conterno, Bruno Giacosa, Bartolo
  Mascarello), Barbaresco (Gaja, Produttori del Barbaresco).
- *Aging*: Barolo requires minimum 38 months aging (18 in wood);
  Barbaresco minimum 26 months (9 in wood).

cite:[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebbiolo][Wikipedia: Nebbiolo]]
cite:[[https://winefolly.com/deep-dive/guide-to-nebbiolo-wine/][Wine Folly: Nebbiolo]]
cite:[[https://winefolly.com/deep-dive/barolo-barbaresco-italy-nebbiolo-regions/][Wine Folly: Barolo & Barbaresco]]

** 1.08 Grenache / Garnacha
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Aragon, Spain (as Garnacha). Spread to southern France,
  Sardinia (as Cannonau).
- *Color*: Medium ruby, tends toward pale/translucent.
- *Tannin*: Low to medium.
- *Acidity*: Low to medium.
- *Body*: Medium to full-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Raspberry, strawberry, white pepper, candied fruit,
  cinnamon, orange peel, leather. Tends to produce wines with high
  alcohol and soft texture.
- *Where it thrives*: Southern Rhone (Chateauneuf-du-Pape, Cotes du
  Rhone), Priorat, Garnacha de Aragon (Spain), Roussillon,
  South Australia (Barossa old-vine), Sardinia (Cannonau di Sardegna).
- *Notable wines*: Chateau Rayas, Alvaro Palacios L'Ermita (Priorat),
  GSM blends.
- *Key role*: Principal grape in GSM (Grenache-Syrah-Mourvedre) blends.
  Provides the fruit and warmth; Syrah adds color and pepper;
  Mourvedre adds tannin and structure.

cite:[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grenache][Wikipedia: Grenache]]
cite:[[https://www.vinerra.com/education/wines/grenache-an-in-depth-wine-profile][Vinerra: Grenache]]
cite:[[https://boundbywine.com/blogs/wine-guide/grenache-garnacha-cannonau][BoundbyWine: Grenache]]

** 1.09 Malbec
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Southwest France (Cahors), probably from northern Burgundy.
  Also known as Cot.
- *Color*: Deep, inky dark purple — one of the darkest red grapes.
- *Tannin*: Medium to high (higher and more rustic in Cahors; softer,
  plusher in Argentina).
- *Acidity*: Medium.
- *Body*: Full-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*:
  - *Cahors*: Damson plum, tobacco, garlic, raisin, dark tannic structure.
  - *Argentina*: Ripe plum, blackberry, black cherry, violet, milk
    chocolate, vanilla.
- *Where it thrives*: Mendoza, Argentina (especially Uco Valley at high
  altitude); Cahors, France; also planted in Chile, Australia, South Africa.
- *Notable wines*: Catena Zapata, Achaval-Ferrer, Cahors "black wine."
- *Note*: Argentina holds ~70% of world's Malbec plantings. Altitude
  (900--1500 m in Mendoza) produces intense color and concentration.

cite:[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malbec][Wikipedia: Malbec]]
cite:[[https://winefolly.com/tips/mendoza-malbec-vs-cahors-malbec/][Wine Folly: Mendoza vs Cahors Malbec]]
cite:[[https://www.decanter.com/wine/grape-varieties/malbec/][Decanter: Malbec]]

** 1.10 Zinfandel / Primitivo
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Croatia (as Tribidrag/Crljenak Kastelanski). DNA confirmed
  in 1994 that Zinfandel, Primitivo, and the Croatian grape are
  genetically identical.
- *Color*: Deep ruby to purple.
- *Tannin*: Medium to high, sometimes chewy.
- *Acidity*: Medium.
- *Body*: Medium to full-bodied. Often high alcohol (14--17%).
- *Aromas/flavors*:
  - *Cooler sites*: Raspberry, cranberry, red pepper.
  - *Warmer sites*: Blackberry, black pepper, anise, jam, bramble.
  - Oak-aged: Vanilla, baking spice, sweet tobacco.
- *Where it thrives*: California (Sonoma: Dry Creek Valley, Lodi,
  Amador County, Paso Robles), Puglia (Italy, as Primitivo di Manduria),
  Croatia (small plantings).
- *Notable wines*: Ridge Geyserville, Turley Wine Cellars, Primitivo
  di Manduria DOC.
- *Style note*: Also used for White Zinfandel (blush/rose), which
  accounts for a large volume of California production.

cite:[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinfandel][Wikipedia: Zinfandel]]
cite:[[https://winefolly.com/grapes/zinfandel/][Wine Folly: Zinfandel/Primitivo]]
cite:[[https://www.vivino.com/wine-news/the-origin-of-zinfandel-and-primitivo][Vivino: Origin of Zinfandel and Primitivo]]

** 1.11 Gamay
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Burgundy, France. Officially: Gamay Noir a Jus Blanc.
  Banned from Burgundy in 1395 by Duke Philip the Bold in favor of
  Pinot Noir; found its home in Beaujolais.
- *Color*: Light to medium ruby, translucent.
- *Tannin*: Low. Deliberately kept low through carbonic maceration.
- *Acidity*: Medium-high to high. Vibrant.
- *Body*: Light to medium-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Cherry, raspberry, strawberry, banana (carbonic
  maceration), violet, peony, wet stone. Cru wines develop complexity:
  earthy, spicy, mineral notes.
- *Where it thrives*: Beaujolais (10 Crus: Morgon, Moulin-a-Vent,
  Fleurie, Brouilly, Cote de Brouilly, Chiroubles, Julienas, Chenas,
  Regnie, Saint-Amour), Loire Valley (Touraine), Switzerland,
  Ontario (Canada).
- *Notable wines*: Beaujolais Nouveau (released third Thursday of
  November), Cru Beaujolais (Morgon, Moulin-a-Vent for aging).
- *Vinification*: Carbonic maceration — whole clusters sealed in CO2;
  intracellular fermentation at ~2% ABV; produces fresh, fruity,
  low-tannin wines.

cite:[[https://www.willakenzie.com/blog-gamay-wine-guide.html][WillaKenzie: Gamay Wine Guide]]
cite:[[https://www.wine-searcher.com/grape-182-gamay][Wine-Searcher: Gamay]]

** 1.12 Mourvedre / Monastrell
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L3
:END:

- *Origin*: Spain (as Monastrell, likely from the Sagunto/Murviedro
  region of Valencia).
- *Color*: Deep, dark purple.
- *Tannin*: High. Robust, firm structure.
- *Acidity*: Medium.
- *Body*: Full-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Wild game, earth, blackberry, dark plum, leather,
  black pepper, smoked meat, garrigue (wild herbs). Can be rustic.
- *Where it thrives*: Bandol (Provence — where it must be at least 50%
  of blends), Chateauneuf-du-Pape, Jumilla and Yecla (Spain),
  South Australia, California (Rhone Rangers).
- *Notable wines*: Domaine Tempier (Bandol), GSM blends worldwide.
- *Key role*: The "M" in GSM blends. Provides tannin backbone,
  dark color, and savory complexity.

cite:[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mourv%C3%A8dre][Wikipedia: Mourvedre]]

** 1.13 Pinotage
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L3
:END:

- *Origin*: South Africa. Created in 1925 by Professor Abraham Izak
  Perold at Stellenbosch University as a cross of Pinot Noir x Cinsaut
  (then called Hermitage in South Africa — hence the name).
- *Color*: Deep ruby to dark purple.
- *Tannin*: Medium to high.
- *Acidity*: Medium.
- *Body*: Medium to full-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Bramble, dark berries, smoke, earthy notes,
  sometimes tropical fruit, banana, and a characteristic acetone/
  paint-like note when poorly made. Top examples show plum, chocolate,
  espresso.
- *Where it thrives*: Stellenbosch, Paarl, Swartland (South Africa).
  Limited plantings elsewhere (New Zealand, Brazil, Canada, Zimbabwe).
- *Notable wines*: Kanonkop, Beyerskloof.
- *Significance*: South Africa's signature grape; symbolizes the
  country's winemaking identity.

cite:[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinotage][Wikipedia: Pinotage]]

** 1.14 Touriga Nacional
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L3
:END:

- *Origin*: Portugal. Disputed between Dao and Douro Valley — both
  regions claim it.
- *Color*: Very deep, intense purple-black. Thick-skinned grape rich in
  color compounds.
- *Tannin*: High. Excellent structure and aging capacity.
- *Acidity*: Medium to medium-high.
- *Body*: Full-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Violet, blackberry, cherry, plum, blueberry, dark
  chocolate, cedar, rocky/mineral notes. Intense floral character is
  distinctive.
- *Where it thrives*: Douro Valley, Dao, Alentejo (Portugal).
  Increasingly planted in Australia (Barossa) and South Africa.
- *Notable wines*: Port (traditionally the most prized grape for vintage
  Port), Barca Velha, Quinta do Crasto, Chryseia.
- *Significance*: Considered Portugal's finest red grape variety. Small
  berries with high skin-to-juice ratio produce concentrated wines.

cite:[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Touriga_Nacional][Wikipedia: Touriga Nacional]]
cite:[[https://winefolly.com/deep-dive/exploring-touriga-nacional-wine/][Wine Folly: Touriga Nacional]]
cite:[[https://winesofportugal.com/en/portuguese-wines/grape-varieties/touriga-nacional/][Wines of Portugal: Touriga Nacional]]

** 1.15 Carmenere
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L3
:END:

- *Origin*: Bordeaux, France. One of the original six Bordeaux red
  grapes. Name from French /carmin/ (crimson), for autumn leaf color.
- *Color*: Deep ruby to dark red.
- *Tannin*: Medium to high (softer than Cabernet Sauvignon).
- *Acidity*: Medium.
- *Body*: Medium to full-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Blackberry, plum, dark cherry, green pepper,
  green peppercorn, eucalyptus, cocoa, cedar, sweet spice. Higher
  levels of pyrazines give herbaceous character.
- *Where it thrives*: Chile (Central Valley: Rapel, Colchagua, Maule).
  Almost extinct in France after phylloxera; survived in Chile where
  it was mislabeled as Merlot until 1994.
- *Notable wines*: Concha y Toro Terrunyo, Santa Rita Casa Real
  Carmenere.
- *Historical note*: In 1994, French ampelographer Jean-Michel
  Boursiquot identified that much of Chile's "Merlot" was actually
  Carmenere. It was grown mixed with Merlot for most of the 20th
  century (up to 50% of volume). Now Chile's signature red grape.

** 2.01 Chardonnay
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L1--L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Burgundy, France. DNA analysis: a cross of Pinot Noir and
  Gouais Blanc.
- *Color*: Pale gold (unoaked) to deep gold (oaked).
- *Acidity*: Medium. Malleable through malolactic fermentation.
- *Body*: Medium to full-bodied (the most full-bodied of the major whites).
- *Aromas/flavors*:
  - *Unoaked/cool climate*: Green apple, lemon, chalk, mineral,
    white peach.
  - *Oaked/warm climate*: Butter, vanilla, toast, coconut, toffee,
    pineapple, mango, hazelnut.
- *Where it thrives*: Burgundy (Chablis, Meursault, Puligny-Montrachet,
  Pouilly-Fuisse), Champagne (Blanc de Blancs), Napa/Sonoma, Margaret
  River, Adelaide Hills, Casablanca Valley (Chile), Limari (Chile).
- *Notable wines*: Montrachet, Corton-Charlemagne, Kistler, Leeuwin
  Estate Art Series.
- *Key concept*: Chardonnay is a "winemaker's grape" — its relatively
  neutral character acts as a canvas for winemaking techniques (oak,
  lees stirring, MLF).

cite:[[https://bettertastingwine.com/white_wine_types.html][Better Tasting Wine: White Wine Types]]
cite:[[https://sommelierbusiness.com/en/articles/wine-technical-84/summary-of-major-white-wine-varieties-192.htm][Sommelier Business: Major White Varieties]]

** 2.02 Sauvignon Blanc
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L1--L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Loire Valley, France. Parent grape (with Cabernet Franc) of
  Cabernet Sauvignon.
- *Color*: Pale straw to light gold.
- *Acidity*: High. Bracing, refreshing acidity.
- *Body*: Light to medium-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*:
  - *Cool climate*: Gooseberry, green pepper, cut grass, asparagus,
    cat's pee (thiol-driven), lime, grapefruit.
  - *Warm climate*: Passion fruit, melon, peach, tropical notes.
  - *Oaked (Fume Blanc style)*: Smoke, vanilla, roasted notes.
- *Where it thrives*: Loire Valley (Sancerre, Pouilly-Fume), Bordeaux
  (blended with Semillon), Marlborough (New Zealand), Casablanca
  (Chile), Stellenbosch, Friuli.
- *Notable wines*: Cloudy Bay, Didier Dagueneau Silex (Pouilly-Fume),
  Domaine Vacheron (Sancerre).

cite:[[https://casaloce.com/pages/what-is-the-difference-between-chardonnay-vs-sauvignon-blanc][Casa Loce: Chardonnay vs Sauvignon Blanc]]

** 2.03 Riesling
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Rhine region, Germany. First documented in 1435.
- *Color*: Pale straw, often with green tinge. Can develop deep gold
  with age.
- *Acidity*: High. Among the highest-acid white grapes. Acidity is the
  backbone that balances residual sugar in off-dry/sweet styles.
- *Body*: Light to medium-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Green apple, lime, peach, apricot, honey, white
  flowers, rose blossom, slate/mineral (Mosel), petrol/kerosene
  (aged — from TDN compound).
- *Where it thrives*: Germany (Mosel, Rheingau, Pfalz, Nahe), Alsace,
  Austria (Wachau), Clare Valley and Eden Valley (Australia),
  Finger Lakes (New York), Ontario.
- *Notable wines*: Egon Muller Scharzhofberger, Trimbach Clos Sainte
  Hune, Grosset Polish Hill.
- *Style range*: Dry (Trocken), off-dry (Kabinett, Spatlese), sweet
  (Auslese, BA, TBA, Eiswein). German Pradikat system classifies by
  ripeness at harvest.

cite:[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riesling][Wikipedia: Riesling]]
cite:[[https://floridawineacademy.com/blogs/blog/riesling-chardonnay-sauvignon-blanc-exploring-white-wine-stars-with-wset-courses-at-florida-wine-academy][Florida Wine Academy: White Wine Stars]]

** 2.04 Pinot Grigio / Pinot Gris
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Burgundy, France. A color mutation of Pinot Noir. Grey-blue
  to brownish-pink skins.
- *Color*: Pale straw (Italian style) to deep golden or copper-toned
  (Alsace style).
- *Acidity*: Medium (Italian style: higher, crisper) to low-medium
  (Alsace style: richer).
- *Body*: Light-bodied (Italian: Pinot Grigio) to medium-full (Alsace:
  Pinot Gris).
- *Aromas/flavors*:
  - *Pinot Grigio (Italy)*: Lemon, green apple, pear, mineral, clean
    and neutral.
  - *Pinot Gris (Alsace)*: Peach, apricot, honey, smoke, spice. Richer,
    more textured.
- *Where it thrives*: Northeastern Italy (Veneto, Friuli, Trentino-Alto
  Adige), Alsace, Oregon, New Zealand, Germany (as Grauburgunder).
- *Notable wines*: Zind-Humbrecht Pinot Gris (Alsace), Livio Felluga
  (Friuli).

cite:[[https://www.coravin.com/blogs/community/21-essential-types-of-white-wine-to-know][Coravin: 22 Types of White Wine]]
cite:[[https://www.jancisrobinson.com/learn/grape-varieties/white/pinot-grigio][Jancis Robinson: Pinot Grigio]]

** 2.05 Gewurztraminer
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Origin*: Traminer grape from Alto Adige (Tramin), Italy. /Gewurz/ =
  "spiced" in German.
- *Color*: Deep gold, sometimes with pink tinge.
- *Acidity*: Low. Can feel flabby if not handled well.
- *Body*: Medium to full-bodied.
- *Aromas/flavors*: Lychee, rose petal, Turkish delight, ginger, citrus
  peel, cinnamon, nutmeg, clove, pumpkin pie spice, passion fruit.
  Extremely aromatic and perfumed.
- *Where it thrives*: Alsace (France — its spiritual home), Germany

** 3.1 Vitis vinifera and Other Species
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Vitis vinifera*: The European grapevine species responsible for
  virtually all quality wine production worldwide. Thousands of
  cultivars exist. Susceptible to phylloxera on its own roots.
- *American species*:
  - *V. labrusca*: Native to eastern North America. "Foxy" aromas
    (Concord grape). Used for juice, jelly, and some wines. Weak
    phylloxera resistance.
  - *V. riparia*: Highly phylloxera-resistant. Used extensively as
    rootstock.
  - *V. rupestris*: Phylloxera-resistant. Important rootstock parent.
  - *V. berlandieri* (syn. V. cinerea var. helleri): Deep-rooted,
    lime-tolerant. Key rootstock parent for calcareous soils.
  - *V. aestivalis*: Strong phylloxera resistance. Norton grape is a
    cultivar.
- *Hybrids*: Crosses between V. vinifera and American species. Used for
  rootstocks and, in some regions, for cold-hardy or disease-resistant
  wine grapes (e.g., Seyval Blanc, Chambourcin, Marquette).
  Generally prohibited in EU quality wine designations.

** 3.2 The Annual Vine Cycle
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2--L3
:END:

The vine's annual cycle governs the rhythm of the vineyard year. In the
Northern Hemisphere, timing is approximately:

*** 3.2.1 Dormancy (November--March)

From leaf fall to the beginning of growth in spring, the vine is
dormant and consists entirely of woody tissue. Carbohydrate reserves
are stored in the trunk and root system to fuel the following season's
growth. Winter pruning is carried out during dormancy.

*** 3.2.2 Bud Break (March--April)

As soil temperatures reach ~10 C, stored starch is converted to sugar
and sap begins to move. Buds swell and burst. This is a vulnerable
period — late spring frosts can destroy new growth. Methods to combat
frost include smudge pots, wind machines, aspersion (sprinklers that
form a protective ice layer), and helicopters.

*** 3.2.3 Shoot Growth and Flowering (May--June)

Rapid shoot growth occurs. Flowering typically begins 6--9 weeks after
bud break, requiring daily temperatures of 15--20 C. V. vinifera
flowers are hermaphroditic (self-pollinating); no external pollinators
are required. Poor weather during flowering causes /coulure/ (poor
fruit set) and /millerandage/ (uneven berry size).

*** 3.2.4 Fruit Set and Berry Development (June--July)

Successfully pollinated flowers develop into green, hard berries.
Berry size increases through cell division and expansion. Acids
accumulate; sugars are still low.

*** 3.2.5 Veraison (July--August)

The onset of ripening. Red grapes change color from green to purple
(anthocyanin accumulation). White grapes become translucent. Sugar
levels rise rapidly, acidity drops, flavor compounds and tannins
develop. Veraison to harvest is typically 30--70 days depending on
variety and climate.

*** 3.2.6 Harvest (August--October in N. Hemisphere)

Timing is the most critical viticultural decision. The winemaker
balances sugar (potential alcohol), acidity, tannin ripeness, and
flavor development. Harvest can be by hand (selective, gentle, required
for certain appellations) or machine (faster, cheaper, works at night
in warm climates).

*** 3.2.7 Post-Harvest (October--November)

After harvest, leaves continue photosynthesis until frost, replenishing
carbohydrate reserves for the following year. This period is important
for vine health and longevity. Leaf fall marks the return to dormancy.

** 3.3 Rootstock and Phylloxera
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2--L3
:END:

*** 3.3.1 The Phylloxera Crisis

Phylloxera (Daktulosphaira vitifoliae) is a microscopic aphid-like
insect native to eastern North America. It feeds on the roots of V.
vinifera, causing galls, secondary infections, and vine death.

The Great French Wine Blight of the 1860s--1890s devastated European
vineyards. The insect was accidentally introduced on American vine
specimens. At its peak, phylloxera destroyed approximately two-thirds
of European vineyards.

*** 3.3.2 The Solution: Grafting

The solution, developed by Charles Valentine Riley with J.E. Planchon
and promoted by T.V. Munson, was to graft V. vinifera scions onto
American rootstock. Native American grapevines (particularly V.
riparia, V. rupestris, and V. berlandieri) have natural resistance.

Today, the vast majority of the world's V. vinifera vines are grafted
onto American or hybrid rootstocks. Notable exceptions include:
- Chile (protected by geographic isolation — Andes, Pacific, desert)
- Parts of South Australia (sandy soils, which phylloxera cannot
  traverse easily)
- Some old pre-phylloxera vineyards (e.g., Bollinger Vieilles Vignes
  Francaises, ungrafted Pinot Noir in Champagne)

*** 3.3.3 Rootstock Selection

Rootstocks are chosen for:
- Phylloxera resistance
- Nematode resistance
- Adaptation to soil type (calcareous, acidic, saline)
- Vigor control
- Drought tolerance
- Lime tolerance

Common rootstocks: SO4, 3309C (Couderc), 101-14, 110R (Richter),
Paulsen 1103, 41B. The rootstock influences vine vigor, yield, and
indirectly grape composition.

** 3.4 Terroir
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L3--L4
:END:

Terroir is the French concept that a wine expresses the complete
natural environment in which it is produced — the interaction of soil,
climate, topography, and (in broader definitions) human practice.

*** 3.4.1 Soil

- *Drainage*: Well-drained soils (gravel, sand, limestone) stress the
  vine moderately, concentrating flavors. Waterlogged soils produce
  dilute, vigorous growth.
- *Mineral composition*: Limestone/chalk (Burgundy, Champagne) — tends
  to give wines finesse and acidity. Slate (Mosel) — retains heat,
  gives mineral notes. Volcanic (Etna, Santorini) — smoky, earthy
  character. Granite (Beaujolais Crus, N. Rhone) — elegant wines.
  Clay (Pomerol) — water-retentive, suits Merlot.
- *Fertility*: Paradoxically, poor soils often produce the best wine
  grapes. Low fertility limits vigor and concentrates flavor.

*** 3.4.2 Climate

- *Macroclimate*: The broad regional climate (continental, maritime,
  Mediterranean).
- *Mesoclimate*: The climate of a specific vineyard site.
- *Microclimate*: The climate within the vine canopy.
- *Diurnal variation*: Large temperature difference between day and
  night preserves acidity while allowing sugar accumulation (important
  in Mendoza, Central Otago, Adelaide Hills).
- *Growing degree days (GDD)*: Winkler scale classifies regions I--V
  by accumulated heat.
- *Key thresholds*: 10 C minimum for vine growth. The 10--20 C growing
  season average defines suitable wine regions (roughly 30--50 degrees
  latitude in both hemispheres).

*** 3.4.3 Aspect and Altitude

- *Aspect (slope orientation)*: South-facing slopes (N. Hemisphere)
  receive more sunlight, are warmer. North-facing slopes (S. Hemisphere)
  equivalent. In marginal climates (Mosel, Cote d'Or), aspect is
  critical for ripening.
- *Altitude*: Every 100 m rise in altitude drops temperature ~0.6 C.
  Higher-altitude vineyards (Salta at 1700+ m, Etna at 900+ m) get
  more UV, cooler nights, higher diurnal range — producing wines with
  intense color, concentrated aromatics, and preserved acidity.
- *Slope*: Steeper slopes improve drainage, increase sun exposure angle,
  and reduce frost risk (cold air drains to valley floor).

** 3.5 Viticulture Practices
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2 (overview), L3 (detail), L4 (advanced)
:END:

*** 3.5.1 Pruning
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L2
:END:

- *Purpose*: Controls yield, directs vine energy, shapes the vine for
  the coming season. Lower yield generally = higher concentration.
- *Winter pruning*: Done during dormancy. Determines the number of buds
  (and thus potential clusters) for the next season.
- *Training systems*:
  - *Spur pruning*: Short spurs (2--3 buds). Used in warm climates.
    Systems: Gobelet (bush vine), Cordon de Royat.
  - *Cane pruning*: Long canes (6--12 buds). Used in cooler climates
    for more even bud break. Systems: Guyot (single or double).
- *Green harvest (vendange verte)*: Summer pruning — removing excess
  clusters to concentrate flavor in remaining fruit.

*** 3.5.2 Canopy Management
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L3
:END:

- *Objective*: Optimize the balance of sunlight exposure and shade within
  the vine canopy.
- *Techniques*:
  - *Shoot positioning*: Directing shoot growth for even light
    distribution.
  - *Shoot thinning*: Removing excess shoots to improve airflow and light
    penetration.
  - *Leaf removal*: Exposing the fruit zone — reduces disease pressure,
    improves ripeness, but can cause sunburn in hot climates.
  - *Hedging*: Trimming shoot tips to redirect energy to fruit.
  - *Cluster thinning*: Removing excess clusters (yield reduction).
- *Trellising systems*: VSP (Vertical Shoot Positioning), Scott Henry,
  Smart-Dyson, Geneva Double Curtain, Lyre. Choice depends on climate,
  vigor, and variety.
- *Principle*: Favorable ratio of fruit to foliage. Approximately 10--14
  leaves per cluster for adequate photosynthesis.

*** 3.5.3 Irrigation
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L3
:END:

- *Dry farming (rainfed)*: Traditional in Europe. Many appellations
  prohibit or restrict irrigation (e.g., much of France, traditional
  European regions). Vines develop deep root systems.
- *Drip irrigation*: Precise water delivery to root zone. Used in New
  World (Australia, California, Chile, Argentina). Allows deficit
  irrigation — controlled water stress to concentrate flavors.
- *Regulated Deficit Irrigation (RDI)*: Water is withheld at specific
  growth stages to control berry size and concentration. Common in
  premium Australian and Californian vineyards.
- *Flood irrigation*: Traditional but water-intensive. Still used in
  some regions (e.g., Mendoza's historic /acequias/ channels).

*** 3.5.4 Organic Viticulture
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L3
:END:

- *Principles*: No synthetic pesticides, herbicides, or fertilizers.
  Certified by bodies such as Ecocert, USDA Organic, EU Organic.
- *Permitted treatments*: Copper-based sprays (Bordeaux mixture) for
  fungal diseases, sulfur, natural predators, cover crops for soil
  health and biodiversity.
- *Certification*: Typically requires 3 years of organic conversion.
  Note: "organic wine" regulations differ between EU and US — in the
  EU, a small amount of added SO2 is permitted; in the US, "organic
  wine" means no added sulfites, while "made with organic grapes"
  allows limited sulfites.

*** 3.5.5 Biodynamic Viticulture
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L4
:END:

- *Origin*: Based on Rudolf Steiner's 1924 agricultural lectures.
  Certified by Demeter International.
- *Principles*: Treats the vineyard as a self-sustaining organism.
  Activities are timed to lunar and cosmic cycles (the biodynamic
  calendar divides days into root, flower, fruit, and leaf days).
- *Preparations*:
  - *500 (horn manure)*: Cow manure fermented in a cow horn buried
    over winter. Applied to soil.
  - *501 (horn silica)*: Ground quartz in a cow horn. Applied as
    foliar spray.
  - *Compost preparations 502--507*: Using yarrow, chamomile, stinging
    nettle, oak bark, dandelion, valerian.
- *Practices*: Cover crops, composting, no synthetic inputs. Biennial
  composting, biweekly weed control.
- *Notable practitioners*: Domaine de la Romanee-Conti, Domaine Leroy,
  Nicolas Joly (Coulee de Serrant), Zind-Humbrecht, Nikolaihof.
- *Debate*: Scientifically, the cosmic timing and preparation theory
  lack empirical support. However, many biodynamic producers make
  exceptional wines, which may reflect the overall care and attention
  the method demands.

** 3.6 Vineyard Pests and Diseases
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L3--L4
:END:

Beyond phylloxera (see [[*3.3 Rootstock and Phylloxera][Section 3.3]]),
vineyards face a range of biological threats that influence viticultural
practice and wine quality.

*** 3.6.1 Fungal Diseases

- *Powdery mildew (Oidium / Erysiphe necator)*: White powdery coating
  on leaves, shoots, and berries. Thrives in warm, dry conditions with
  moderate humidity. Reduces photosynthesis, causes off-flavours if
  infected berries are vinified. Controlled by sulfur sprays, canopy
  management (airflow), and resistant rootstocks.
- *Downy mildew (Plasmopara viticola)*: Yellow oily spots on upper
  leaf surface, white downy growth underneath. Favoured by warm, wet
  conditions. Devastating in humid regions. Controlled by Bordeaux
  mixture (copper sulfate + lime), developed in the 1880s — still the
  mainstay of organic viticulture.
- *Botrytis cinerea (grey rot)*: Destructive form of the same fungus
  that produces noble rot (see Part B, Section 4.2). Grey rot occurs
  when rain or humidity persists through harvest — splits berries,
  causes sour rot and acetic bacteria. Bunch thinning, leaf removal,
  and good airflow are key preventions.
- *Eutypa dieback*: Fungal disease entering through pruning wounds.
  Causes stunted shoots and eventual vine death. No cure once
  established; prevention through wound protectants and delayed pruning.
- *Esca / Black measles*: Complex of fungal pathogens affecting the
  wood. Causes leaf discolouration ("tiger stripes") and sudden vine
  collapse. Increasing incidence across Europe since the ban on
  sodium arsenite treatment (2003).

*** 3.6.2 Bacterial and Viral Diseases

- *Pierce's disease (Xylella fastidiosa)*: Bacterial infection spread
  by glassy-winged sharpshooter (leafhopper). Blocks xylem vessels,
  killing the vine within 1--5 years. Devastating in southern
  California; limits V. vinifera cultivation in the southeastern US.
  No cure; management through insect vector control and resistant
  rootstock research.
- *Grapevine leafroll virus (GLRaV)*: Most economically significant
  viral disease. Delays ripening, reduces colour (in reds), lowers
  sugar. Spread by mealybugs. Managed through certified virus-free
  planting material and roguing infected vines.
- *Grapevine fanleaf virus (GFLV)*: Transmitted by soil nematodes.
  Causes leaf deformation, poor fruit set, shortened vine lifespan.

*** 3.6.3 Insect Pests (beyond phylloxera)

- *Grape berry moth (Eupoecilia ambiguella, Lobesia botrana)*:
  Larvae bore into berries, creating entry points for Botrytis.
  Managed by pheromone traps (sexual confusion), targeted insecticides.
- *Mealybugs*: Vectors for leafroll virus. Excrete honeydew promoting
  sooty mould. Biological control (parasitoid wasps) increasingly used.
- *Spider mites*: Cause leaf bronzing and reduced photosynthesis.
  Favoured by hot, dry conditions. Predatory mites used as biological
  control.

** 3.7 Soil Science for Viticulture
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L3--L4
:END:

*** 3.7.1 Soil pH

- Most V. vinifera performs best in soils with pH 5.5--8.0.
- *Acidic soils* (pH < 6): common in granite regions (Beaujolais,
  parts of N. Rhone). May require liming. Aluminium toxicity risk
  below pH 5.
- *Alkaline/calcareous soils* (pH > 7): Champagne, Burgundy, Chablis,
  Jerez. Chalk and limestone buffer pH. Iron chlorosis risk — requires
  lime-tolerant rootstocks (41B, Fercal).

*** 3.7.2 Soil Nutrients (NPK and CEC)

- *Nitrogen (N)*: drives vegetative growth. Excess N → excessive
  canopy, shading, disease pressure, diluted fruit. Deficit → stunted
  growth, poor fermentation (insufficient yeast-assimilable nitrogen).
- *Phosphorus (P)*: root development, energy transfer. Deficiency rare
  in vineyards.
- *Potassium (K)*: regulates water use, sugar transport. Excess K →
  elevated must pH → flat, flabby wines. Common in clay-rich soils.
- *Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC)*: measure of a soil's ability to hold
  and release nutrient cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, K⁺, Na⁺). High CEC
  (clay, organic matter) = good nutrient retention. Low CEC (sand) =
  nutrients leach quickly, requiring more frequent but lighter
  fertilisation.

*** 3.7.3 Soil Types and Their Influence

| Soil Type     | Properties                         | Wine Effect                      | Benchmark Region         |
|---------------+------------------------------------+----------------------------------+--------------------------|
| Limestone/chalk| Alkaline, well-drained, cool      | Finesse, acidity, minerality     | Champagne, Burgundy      |
| Clay          | Water-retentive, high CEC          | Power, richness                  | Pomerol, parts of Barossa|
| Gravel        | Excellent drainage, heat-retaining | Concentrated, tannic reds        | Medoc (Haut-Medoc)       |
| Sand          | Low CEC, poor water retention      | Light, aromatic, phylloxera-proof| Colares (Portugal)       |
| Slate         | Heat-retaining, mineral-rich       | Mineral, petrol notes            | Mosel                    |
| Volcanic      | Mineral-rich, well-drained         | Smoky, earthy, saline            | Etna, Santorini          |
| Granite       | Acidic, well-drained, low fertility| Elegant, perfumed                | N. Rhone, Beaujolais Crus|

** 3.8 Climate Change and Viticulture
:PROPERTIES:
:WSET_LEVEL: L3--L4
:END:

Climate change is the single most consequential long-term challenge
facing the wine industry. Its effects are already measurable.

*** 3.8.1 Observable Trends

- *Earlier harvests*: Average harvest dates across European regions
  have advanced 2--3 weeks over the past 40 years. Burgundy and
  Bordeaux document the shift clearly.
- *Rising potential alcohol*: Warmer growing seasons produce higher
  sugar at harvest. Many warm-climate regions now routinely produce
  musts at 14--16% potential ABV.
- *Declining natural acidity*: Higher temperatures accelerate malic
  acid respiration, lowering total acidity at harvest.
- *Shifting wine map*: England now has 1,000+ vineyards; Denmark,
  Sweden, and southern Norway are producing wine. Traditional regions
  at lower latitudes face heat stress.
- *Extreme weather*: Increased frequency of late frosts (Burgundy
  2016, 2017, 2021), hailstorms, drought, and wildfire smoke taint
  (California 2020, Australia 2019--20).

*** 3.8.2 Adaptation Strategies

- Planting heat-tolerant and drought-resistant varieties (Touriga
  Nacional, Assyrtiko trialled in traditionally cooler regions)
- Seeking higher-altitude and cooler-aspect vineyard sites
- Adjusting canopy management for more shade
- Night harvesting to preserve acidity
- Drought-resistant rootstocks (110R, 140Ru)
- Acidification in the winery (permitted in some jurisdictions)
- Research into new crosses and resistant varieties (PIWI cultivars)

*** 3.8.3 Projections

- Up to 70--90% of traditional lowland wine regions (Greece, southern
  Spain, southern Italy, parts of California, southern Australia) may
  become unsuitable for quality viticulture by 2100 under high-emission
  scenarios.
- Emerging regions (England, Patagonia, Tasmania, Hokkaido, British
  Columbia) expected to become increasingly viable.
