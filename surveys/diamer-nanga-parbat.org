* Scope and Method

This survey covers the Diamer district and Nanga Parbat massif in
Gilgit-Baltistan, Pakistan — the western anchor of the Himalayan arc,
where the Himalaya, Karakoram, and Hindu Kush converge. The purpose
is threefold: (1) deepen the epistem gazetteer's Karakoram entries,
(2) provide reference material for future story composition, and
(3) survey natural wonders, aesthetics, and narrative tensions for
the MāyāLucIA project.

Method: web-accessible sources — academic papers, ethnographies,
travelogues, journalism, and heritage documentation. Sources cited
with URLs throughout. No personal observation (the human has not
visited this region). The survey follows the standard established by
the verified spirit ecology survey (WP-0006).

* A. Physical Geography

** A.1 The Tectonic Anomaly

Nanga Parbat (8,126m) stands at the *Western Himalayan Syntaxis* —
the sharp northward bend where the Himalayan arc terminates. At this
bend, Indian plate gneiss punches upward through the overthrust rocks
of the Kohistan island arc, an ancient piece of oceanic crust caught
between colliding continents ~50 Ma ago. The structure is a
crustal-scale antiformal pop-up.

*Uplift rate*: 7 mm/yr (Guinness World Records). A 2022 paper in
/Science Advances/ (Rutte et al.) documents a pulse of ultrafast
exhumation beginning ~1 Ma: *9–13 mm/yr*. The massif has been
exhumed 10 km in 10 Ma, 6 km of that in the last 1.3 million years.

*The tectonic aneurysm*: Peter Zeitler (Lehigh University) proposed a
positive feedback model — rapid erosion steepens geotherms, thermally
weakening the crust, accelerating deformation, increasing uplift,
increasing erosion. The river does not merely respond to the rising
mountain; it helps create it. The mountain and the river are
co-creating each other.

*The youngest exposed granite on Earth*: leucogranite dikes with
crystallisation ages as young as *0.7 Ma*. The rock melted not
because it got hotter, but because it /rose so fast the pressure
dropped below the melting curve/ — decompression melting. The mountain
unmakes itself to make itself. Synkinematic plutonism: granite
intruding while the rock is still actively deforming.

*The Kohistan contact*: the surrounding Kohistan arc terrane is
Cretaceous intra-oceanic volcanic/plutonic rock — fundamentally
different lithology from the Indian plate gneiss of Nanga Parbat. The
tectonic contact is concordant and ductile, associated with
interleaving. A suture zone you can walk across.

** A.2 The Indus Gorge

The Indus River is /older than the mountain it cuts through/. An
antecedent drainage — the river established its course before the
syntaxis began rising. As the mountain grew, the river kept cutting
downward.

*Depth*: 4,500–5,200m from river to adjacent peaks. Between the
Indus at Jalipur (1,100m) and the summit of Nanga Parbat (8,125m),
relief exceeds 7,000m in just 21 km.

*Incision rates*: from cosmogenic isotope dating —
- Low straths (~7,000 yr): 9–12 mm/yr
- High straths (>7,000 yr): 1–6 mm/yr
Rates increase downstream toward the Raikot fault, suggesting
bedrock incision proportional to stream power over the last 7,000 yr.

The gorge exposes middle and lower crustal rocks — a natural
cross-section through the Earth's crust, continuously renewed by
erosion. The Raikot fault (NW-dipping reverse) has at least 15 km of
horizontal slip inferred from Quaternary offsets.

** A.3 The Rupal Face

4,600m of continuous vertical relief from the Rupal Valley floor
(~3,500m) to the summit — the highest mountain face on Earth.

From base camp at 3,350m, the summit is nearly five vertical
kilometres above. Buhl (1953): /"4,500 vertical metres of rock and
ice. Even an attempt would already be a suicide."/

Steve House, first alpine-style ascent of a direct route (2005):
4,100m in six days up, two days down. 16 kg each, 1 kg tent, one
sleeping bag. Crux: "poorly protected dry-tooling up loose 5.9
granite." Awarded the Piolet d'Or.

The Messner traverse (1970): Reinhold and Günther Messner made the
first ascent of the Rupal Face. Unable to descend the same way due to
weather and Günther's altitude sickness, they traversed the mountain
via the Diamir Face — first traverse of Nanga Parbat. Günther died on
the descent, likely in an avalanche.

** A.4 Glaciers

Three principal systems:
- *Rakhiot Glacier* (north): best-documented, surveys from 1934
- *Rupal Glacier* (south): mainly snow-fed, drains the Rupal Face
- *Diamir Glacier* (west): borders the Diamir Face

*Rakhiot Glacier oscillation history*:
- 1930–1954: terminus thinned, back-wasted 450m
- 1954–1985: advanced ~200m
- 2001–2002: surge-type event, meltwater and supraglacial lakes
- 1951–2009: mean downwasting 0.24 m/yr (14m total)
- 2009–2023: mean downwasting *1.8 m/yr* (25m total) — a
  *7.5-fold acceleration*

For 63 glaciers on Nanga Parbat, ice-covered area decreased 7%
between 1934 and 2019. Glaciers fed by ice/snow avalanches show
lower retreat than other Himalayan regions — the "Karakoram Anomaly."
Most recent data suggests this anomaly may be ending.

*GLOF hazard*: 3,000+ glacial lakes in Gilgit-Baltistan, 30
identified as posing imminent threat. 388+ GLOF events documented in
the broader Himalayan-Karakoram region.

** A.5 Hot Springs — The Mountain Breathes

At *Tato village* (Rakhiot Valley, on the approach to Fairy Meadows):
a *hot steaming geyser and springs with near-boiling water (~92°C)*.
Orange to yellowish hydrothermal alteration of surrounding rock. The
boiling results from deep infiltration of meteoric waters through
fractures, interacting with young, solidified but still hot granitic
intrusions.

Along the western margin: *Tattapani* (near Raikot Bridge, on KKH)
and *Sassi* (NE of Jaglot), with surface temperatures 42–81°C.
Concentrated along the seismically active Raikot fault, which
channels deeply infiltrated snowmelt.

*Dual hydrothermal system*:
1. Near-surface: meteoric water (snowmelt)
2. Deeper: magmatic/metamorphic volatiles. Fluid inclusions in quartz
   veins homogenise to vapour at 320–410°C — a dry steam geothermal
   system in the upper 5 km of crust.

*Geothermal gradient*: ~100°C/km — one of the steepest outside
volcanic areas (Leeds University).

The hot springs are a direct consequence of the tectonic aneurysm.
The same process that pushes the mountain upward drives hot fluids to
the surface. The mountain breathes its own heat.

** A.6 Ecology

Altitude zonation without monsoon. Juniper and birch replace deodar.
Alpine meadows (=jūt=) serve as summer pasture and peri habitat.
Wildlife: ibex, markhor, snow leopard.

Seasonal economy at Fairy Meadows: herders in sod-roofed huts; morel
mushroom harvesting (=Kuchuli=) in May–June (up to Rs 15,000/kg).
Communities transitioned from timber to tourism (~17 million PKR/yr).

The ecological contrast with the monsoon valleys is fundamental:
no terraced agriculture, no kuhls, no monsoon cycle. Water comes
from glacial melt and springs, not rain.

* B. Spirit Traditions and Human Culture

** B.1 The Peri Tradition

The dominant spirit category in the Karakoram. From Persian mythology,
not European fairy lore. Beautiful, powerful, capricious beings
inhabiting high pastures, glaciers, and remote valleys. Not
diminutive — formidable.

*** Named abodes

| Place | Elevation | Peri association |
|-------+-----------+-----------------|
| Tirich Mir | 7,708m | /Koh-e-Kaaf/ — mythical mountain at world's edge. Golden palace on summit. Fairy forts on surrounding peaks with drums beaten at times of war. |
| Rakaposhi | 7,788m | /Dumani/ ("Mother of Mist"). Guardian spirit: /thamo raaci payaali/ — "mother of fourteen daughters." |
| Nanga Parbat | 8,126m | /Diamer/ — "Mountain of the Gods." Local Shina: no one summits because of the jinn. |
| Fairy Meadows | 3,300m | /Joot/ (Shina: summer pasture). Ethereal beings dance under moonlight. |
| Deosai Plains | 4,114m | "Shadow of the Giant" (/deo/ = giant, /sai/ = shadow). A giant farmed here; snows came before harvest. |
| Lake Saiful Muluk | 3,224m | Formed by a demon's rage — he flooded the valley because a fairy chose a human prince. |

*** Two kinds of pari

- *Makhakhar*: the fairy of milk
- *Rathas*: the fairy of blood

When the bitan sings, both come and ask him to drink.

*** The Pari Khan (Chitral)

Women attain the status of spirit medium. Initially patients
(possessed), they gradually learn to live with the spirit and become
its host. Pari Khan gain social status through healing powers.

Source: JHSMS: https://ideapublishers.org/index.php/jhsms/article/view/978

** B.2 The Bitan (Shaman)

In Burushaski: *bitan*. In Shina: *dayal* (or *danyal*).

*** Selection

The peri choose the shaman, not the other way around. /The pariting
descend to earth during the cherry and apricot blossoming seasons.
They select the dayal from among newborns by smelling their noses and
mouths./ The chosen one grows up showing signs in adolescence:
unconsciousness, ecstatic states, prolonged sickness.

*** The iron bangle (kau)

Simultaneously binds to and wards off the fairy. The binding is also
the protection. A paradox held in metal.

*** The trance session

Three requirements: *music, juniper smoke, and goat blood*.

Orchestra: *Dadang* (drum), *Daamal* (two hemispherical drums),
*Surnai/Gabi* (reed pipe). Played for 40+ minutes continuously.

The bitan:
1. Inhales burning juniper smoke (or bites juniper branches)
2. Drinks blood from a freshly severed goat's head (male kid)
3. Dances wildly, running and jumping

In trance, the bitan /"sings his prophecy in an ancient language that
he is incapable of speaking or understanding when he is awake."/

*** The language the peri speak

*The only official language of fairies is Shina.* A Burushaski-
speaking shaman communicates with his pari in Shina — a language he
does not normally speak. The spirit brings its own language.

*** Five specialist categories (Hunza)

Bitan, dashman, jaadugaar, sire gus, aqhon. The aqhon bridges
Islamic and pre-Islamic practice.

*** Music as political control

The ruler of Hunza (tham) controlled shamans by *assigning the Dom
(an underprivileged, ethnically different group) to monopolise the
sacred music* for all shamanistic performances. Control the musicians,
control access to the spirits.

** B.3 Glacier Gender and Glacier Marriage

Glaciers have gender in Gilgit-Baltistan:

| Type | Shina name | Appearance | Behaviour |
|------+------------+------------+-----------|
| Male | /po gang/ | Grey, debris-covered | Slow-moving, yields little water |
| Female | /mo gang/ | White or blue, shiny | Growing, gives off much water |

*** The wedding of glaciers

To graft a new glacier:
1. Take a piece each of male and female glacier (~35 kg each)
2. Pack in *coal and barley hay* for insulation
3. Place in a *chorong* (conical basket woven from willow twigs)
4. Transport to a cave or deep pit at 4,000–5,000m — north-facing,
   no direct sunlight, snowfall and avalanches common
5. Cover with a mixture of *mud, ash, and charcoal*
6. Close with heavy stones
7. Organise *special prayers and animal sacrifice*

After 10–12 years, the grafted pieces give birth to a glacier.

The Aga Khan Rural Support Programme has grafted 19 glaciers with
*80% success rate*.

** B.4 The Azhdaar (Dragon-Serpents)

Large serpentine beings with *the wings of a raptor, the mouth of a
fish, and a golden mane like a lion's*. They inhale fire and can
swallow a man. They live beneath glaciers and sacred lakes and control
storms. In Hunza's Chipursan Valley, a frequently told story recounts
a massive lake that destroyed an ancient settlement — attributed to
dragon activity.

** B.6 Human Settlement and Contemporary Life

*** Villages around Nanga Parbat

- *Chilas*: district capital, left bank of Indus. Summer temperatures
  exceed 45°C (record: 48.5°C, July 2025). Overwhelmingly Sunni
  Muslim (Hanafi). Shina-speaking (Chilasi dialect).
- *Tato*: 2,300m, Rakhiot Valley. Hot springs. Starting point for
  Fairy Meadows. Jeep road privately owned by Chilasis.
- *Tarishing/Tarshing*: ~200 inhabitants, quiet village, approach to
  Rupal valley. Shia and Sunni together.
- *Rupal*: Salman Rashid (1994): /"a spreading straggle of houses
  sprinkled over a slope that gently rises to the west until it runs
  into a dusty spur separating it from the ablation valley of
  Bizhen."/

*** The Fairy Meadows road

15 km from Raikot Bridge (KKH) to Tato. Ranked second deadliest
highway (WHO, 2013). Just the width of a jeep, hacked from barren
hills. Rock wall on one side, steep drop-off with no barriers on the
other. The acrid smell of burning brake pads, screech of tyres on
loose shale. A gravel ribbon 1,000m above the valley floor.

Source: https://www.dangerousroads.org/asia/pakistan/116-fairy-meadows-road-pakistan.html

*** The Diamer-Basha Dam

13 villages will be submerged: Thak, Thalpan, Nusry Das, Dalojil,
Segalihit, Thor Das, Sine Huch, Muruski, Hodar, Ges Bala, Ges Pain,
Chilas, Bunar Das. 4,200+ families displaced.

*50,000 rock carvings and 5,000 inscriptions* documented at Thalpan
and surrounding sites, dating from 8,000 BCE to 16th century CE.
Inscriptions in Brahmi, Sogdian, Middle Persian, Chinese, Tibetan,
and ancient Hebrew. Only 5,000 of 35,000 carvings to be 3D-scanned.

The Thalpan "Altar Rock" (named by Karl Jettmar) depicts Jataka
scenes, Buddha's temptation by Mara's daughters, the sermon at
Benares, Buddha under the tree of enlightenment.

Jettmar and Hauptmann's Pak-German Archaeological Mission (from 1980):
~30 sites on a 100 km stretch of the Indus, ~30,000 petroglyphs and
5,000 inscriptions in 10+ writing systems.

Protests ongoing (2025–2026): Dam Affectees Action Committee demanding
Rs 15 billion compensation; sit-in at Chilas.

** B.7 The Conquest Narrative

- Mummery (1895): first attempt, died with two Gurkha porters
- German expeditions (1930s): 1934 — 600 local porters for approach;
  1937 — avalanche killed 7 Germans and 9 Sherpas at Camp IV
- 1938: /"Enlisting porters for the high camps caused anxiety due to
  talk of evil spirits and devils on Nanga Parbat"/ — no surviving
  Sherpa would return
- Buhl (1953): first summit, alone, departed ~02:00, reached summit
  19:00 — the only 8,000m first ascent by a solo climber
- Messner (1970): Rupal Face first ascent, Günther died on Diamir
  descent
- Modern: speed records, tourism, the "Killer Mountain" branding

/"Foreigners have no fear of anything in their hearts, so they can
face the jinn along their path."/ — local explanation for why
outsiders summit but locals do not.

* C. Aesthetic Register

** C.1 Light and Air

Altitude without monsoon haze. The dry Karakoram air gives harder
light, sharper shadows, deeper sky than the monsoon valleys. No the
green-gold filtering of deodar canopy. Colours are mineral: grey
gneiss, orange hydrothermal alteration, white glacier, blue meltwater.
The Rupal Face fills the entire sky from base camp — there is nowhere
else to look.

** C.2 Sound

Wind. Ice. Absence of forest (no deodar, no birdsong canopy). The
creak and groan of glacier movement. Rockfall on the Rupal Face
echoing across the valley. The Indus in its gorge — a sound of
geological force, not pastoral murmur. The acrid smell of burning
brakes on the jeep road. The twelve named musical tunes: each
occasion has its own sound, and one of them opens the door to the
spirits.

** C.3 Architecture

Arid high-altitude — flat-roofed, thick-walled. Sod-roofed huts at
summer pasture. Stone and timber, but not Kath-Kuni (that is a
monsoon-valley technique requiring specific timber courses for
earthquake resilience in wet soil). The architecture of scarcity
rather than abundance.

** C.4 Contrast with the Monsoon Valleys

| Feature | Tirthan/Kullu (monsoon) | Diamer/Nanga Parbat (rain shadow) |
|---------+-------------------------+-----------------------------------|
| Water source | Rain, snowmelt, springs | Glacial melt, springs, no rain |
| Forest | Deodar, cedar, oak | Juniper, birch, absent above 4,000m |
| Agriculture | Terraced, irrigated (kuhls) | Minimal, no terraces, no kuhls |
| Architecture | Kath-Kuni (stone + timber) | Flat-roofed stone, sod-roofed huts |
| Spirit tradition | Devtas (534 in Kullu), gur/aavesh | Peri, bitan/dayal, juniper smoke |
| Ritual medium | Gur (possessed, speaks devta's words) | Bitan (possessed, speaks Shina) |
| Ritual trigger | Drums + procession | Dadang + Daamal + Surnai, 40 min |
| Landscape agency | Springs (nāg), sacred groves (ban) | Glaciers (gendered), peaks (peri) |
| Writing tradition | Oral + Pahari folk songs | 10,000 yr petroglyph tradition |

* D. Narrative Tensions

** D.1 The spirit is not its entry

The gazetteer captures altitude zones and diagnostic roles. It does
not capture the peri who choose shamans by smelling newborns' mouths
during apricot blossom season, or the iron bangle that both binds and
protects. A genus entry for /mo gang/ would say "white/blue, growing,
water-giving." It would not contain the barley hay in the willow
basket, or the twelve-year wait.

** D.2 The conquest narrative vs the inhabited mountain

The mountain's Shina name: "abode of spirits." Its Western name:
"Killer Mountain." The porters who refused to return after 1937 were
not superstitious — they were reading the mountain through their own
knowledge system, one that said: this place has spirits, and the
spirits are angry. The geologists, measuring the same mountain, found
that it is tearing itself apart through a feedback loop of erosion
and uplift. Both readings are about a mountain that acts.

** D.3 Glacial agency

The glacier surges — the Rakhiot advance of 2001–2002. A glacier that
decides to move forward. And the glacier marriage: humans create
glaciers by carrying male and female pieces in willow baskets to
north-facing caves, sealing them with prayer and sacrifice, waiting
twelve years for birth. The glacier is not scenery. It is an agent
with gender, behaviour, and reproductive potential.

** D.4 The mountain that builds itself

Decompression melting: rock rising so fast it crosses the melting
curve. The tectonic aneurysm: erosion drives uplift drives erosion.
The youngest granite (0.7 Ma) in the oldest basement (Late Archean
gneiss). Time compressed — the mountain is still becoming. This is
not metaphor.

** D.5 The drowning of the palimpsest

The Diamer-Basha Dam will submerge 50,000 rock carvings spanning
10,000 years. Brahmi, Sogdian, Persian, Chinese, Tibetan, Hebrew —
ten writing systems on one stretch of riverbank. The Altar Rock's
Jataka scenes. The protests at Chilas. The deepest gorge on Earth
will be partially filled with impounded water, and the oldest writing
in the region will go under.

** D.6 The language the spirits speak

The bitan sings prophecy in a language he cannot speak when awake. A
Burushaski-speaker channels Shina — the fairy's language, not his
own. The spirit brings its own language. This inverts the usual model
of spirit possession: the host does not donate his voice; the spirit
imposes its own.

** D.7 Music as infrastructure

Twelve named tunes, each for a specific occasion. The Danyal/Bitan
tune opens the shamanic channel. The tham controlled shamans by
monopolising the Dom musicians. To control the music is to control
access to the spirits. Music is not decoration; it is the protocol
layer.

** D.8 The dual naming

Nanga Parbat (Naked Mountain) / Diamer (Mountain of the Gods). The
Urdu name describes the visible: bare rock, snow-free south face. The
Shina name describes the invisible: the spirits who live there. Two
names for one mountain, each true, neither sufficient.

** D.9 The human explanation

/"Foreigners have no fear of anything in their hearts, so they can
face the jinn along their path."/ — This is not admiration. It is
diagnosis. The locals understand that the foreigners' fearlessness is
itself a kind of blindness.

** F.1 Mammals — The Ones Carved on the Rocks

*** Himalayan Ibex (/Capra sibirica sakeen/)

The Himalayan ibex is a subspecies of the Siberian ibex (/Capra
sibirica/) — the most widely distributed wild goat in Central Asia.
The subspecies /C. s. sakeen/ inhabits the Pamir Mountains, western
Himalayas, and the mountains of northern Pakistan, Afghanistan, and
India.

*Physical*: Males stand 88-110 cm at shoulder, weigh 60-130 kg.
Females noticeably smaller (67-92 cm, 34-56 kg). Both sexes carry
horns — males' horns black, typically ~115 cm, in extreme cases up to
148 cm, with large transverse ridges on the front surface and annual
growth rings. Female horns grey-brown, averaging 27 cm. Coat shifts
from dark brown to light tan, with a darker dorsal stripe; mature
males darken significantly in winter with white patches. Moult
April-July.

*Behaviour*: Lives above the treeline, descending to lower slopes in
winter. Herds of 5-30, single-sex outside the rut. Rut occurs late
October to early January — males lash, lock horns, and push. Gestation
170-180 days, usually a single kid (twins in ~14% of births). Males
live ~10 years in the wild, females up to 17.

*Range near Nanga Parbat*: The ibex occupies the alpine and subalpine
zones above ~3,500m around the Nanga Parbat massif, including the
Fairy Meadows area, the Rupal valley headwalls, and the Rakhiot valley
flanks. Population density in the Diamer area is uncertain — reliable
census data for this specific district is sparse. The species is
listed as Near Threatened globally (IUCN), with low densities across
most of its range and overall decline.

*The ibex cult*: The ibex is the most frequently depicted animal in
the Thalpan petroglyphs and across the broader Chilas/Diamer rock art
complex. Jettmar's Pak-German Archaeological Mission documented ibex
as the dominant animal motif among the ~30,000 petroglyphs on the
100 km stretch of the Indus. The ibex appears in hunting scenes, in
isolation as a ritual or totemic symbol, and on funerary markers.

In pre-Islamic Dardic religion, ibex horns were placed on graves —
a practice documented by Jettmar in /Religions of the Hindukush/
(1975) and observed across the Dardic-speaking regions from Chitral
to Gilgit. The horn placement is not decorative: it marks the grave
with the animal's power. The practice parallels the broader Central
Asian ibex cult documented from the Altai to the Pamir, suggesting
deep antiquity.

Why the ibex above all others? Several converging factors: (1) the
ibex is the dominant large herbivore of the high-altitude zone where
herders spend their summers — it is the wild counterpart to the
domestic goat; (2) the annual horn growth rings make the ibex a
living calendar, visibly marking the passage of years; (3) the
spectacle of rutting males — rearing on hind legs, clashing horns in
the thin air above the treeline — is one of the most dramatic
displays in mountain wildlife; (4) the ibex's mastery of vertical
terrain — ascending near-vertical rock faces — gives it the
appearance of supernatural capability.

*Narrative interest*: The ibex is the thread between the oldest
petroglyphs (possibly 8,000 BCE) and the living landscape. The same
animal carved on rock 10,000 years ago still stands on the same
ridgeline above Thalpan. The dam will drown the carvings; the ibex
will remain on the cliffs above the waterline.

*** Markhor (/Capra falconeri/)

Pakistan's national animal. A large wild goat native to the mountain
regions at the crossroads of Central and South Asia, including the
Karakoram and Himalayas. Listed as Near Threatened (IUCN) since 2015.

*Physical*: Adults stand 65-115 cm at shoulder, 132-186 cm long,
weighing 32-110 kg. The highest shoulder height of any /Capra/
species. Sexually dimorphic: males have longer hair on chin, throat,
chest, and shanks, and are grizzled light brown to black. Both sexes
carry tightly curled, corkscrew-like horns that spiral upward from
the head — male horns can reach 160 cm, females 25 cm. The spiral
horns are the species' visual signature.

*Etymology — the snake-eater*: The name "markhor" derives from
Persian /mar/ ("snake") + /khor/ ("eater"), referencing an ancient
belief that the markhor actively kills and consumes snakes. An
alternative etymology derives from the Persian for "curly" (referring
to the horns). The snake-eater etymology is the more culturally
interesting: it places the markhor in direct opposition to serpents.

*The nag/klu connection*: The markhor's name as "snake-eater"
positions it as an antagonist to the serpent — and the nag/klu
tradition across the Himalaya identifies serpents (naga, klu) as
water guardians, spring spirits, and underground powers. If the
markhor eats the snake, it is a creature that masters or overcomes
the chthonic water spirits. Whether this folk etymology reflects an
actual observed behaviour (markhors have been observed to eat
snakes, though this is not well-documented) or a mythological
attribution is unclear. The connection deserves further investigation
through Dardic folklore sources.

*Distribution near Nanga Parbat*: In Pakistan, the markhor is
restricted to the Indus and Kunar rivers and their tributaries.
Along the Indus, it inhabits both banks from Jalkot in Kohistan
upstream to near Tungas village in Baltistan, with Gakuch being its
western limit up the Gilgit River, Chalt up the Hunza River, and
the Parishing (Parishing/Astore) Valley up the Astore River. This
range directly encompasses the Nanga Parbat massif. The markhor
inhabits elevations of 600-3,600m in shrub forests of oak (/Quercus
ilex/), pine (/Pinus gerardiana/), and juniper (/Juniperus
macropoda/) — the same juniper the bitan burns for trance.

The largest Pakistani population currently lives in Chitral National
Park. The Diamer/Nanga Parbat populations are likely smaller and
fragmented.

*Behaviour*: Diurnal, active early morning and late afternoon. Diet
shifts seasonally — grazing in spring/summer, browsing in winter,
sometimes standing on hind legs to reach high branches. Males largely
solitary; females and kids form herds of ~9 animals. Alarm call
closely resembles bleating of domestic goats.

*Petroglyphic representation*: The markhor appears in the Thalpan
petroglyph complex, though less frequently than the ibex. The
distinction between ibex and markhor in petroglyphs can be
determined by horn morphology: ibex horns curve backward in a
scimitar arc, markhor horns spiral. Both appear in hunting scenes.

*Narrative interest*: The spiral horn is one of nature's most
striking visual motifs — a logarithmic spiral in keratin. The
snake-eater name places the markhor at the intersection of the
visible (the goat on the cliff) and the invisible (the serpent
underground). Pakistan chose this animal, not the ibex, as national
symbol.

*** Snow Leopard (/Panthera uncia/)

The ghost of the mountain. A large cat of the genus /Panthera/,
native to the mountain ranges of Central and South Asia. Listed as
Vulnerable (IUCN) — global population estimated at fewer than 10,000
mature individuals.

*Physical*: Stocky build, thick whitish-to-grey fur with black spots
on head and neck, larger rosettes on back, flanks, and the
remarkably long, thick tail (used for balance and warmth — wrapped
around the face when sleeping). Small rounded ears, broad paws
(natural snowshoes). Solitary, mostly crepuscular (dawn and
twilight). Males larger than females.

*Evolutionary note*: The snow leopard forms a sister group with the
tiger (/P. tigris/), with genetic divergence estimated at 3.7-2.7
Ma. The earliest definitive modern snow leopard fossil dates to the
Late Pleistocene (Niuyan Cave, China). Putative fossils from the
Pabbi Hills of Pakistan date to the Early Pleistocene, though
taxonomic assignment is uncertain.

*Range near Nanga Parbat*: The snow leopard inhabits alpine and
subalpine zones at 3,000-4,500m across its range. In Pakistan, its
habitat includes Gilgit-Baltistan, Chitral, and the northern areas
broadly. The Nanga Parbat massif falls within the species' range,
particularly the higher valleys and ridgelines of the Rupal, Diamir,
and Rakhiot drainages. Camera trap studies in the broader
Gilgit-Baltistan region have documented snow leopard presence, though
specific published studies focused on the Diamer district are sparse
in the open literature. The Snow Leopard Foundation Pakistan and WWF
Pakistan have conducted surveys in adjacent areas (Khunjerab, Central
Karakoram).

*Prey*: In the Nanga Parbat area, primary prey would include
Himalayan ibex, markhor, and marmots — all species documented in
this survey. The snow leopard is an ambush predator that uses the
cliffs and ridges as vantage points, the same terrain the ibex
masters.

*Cultural significance*: The epithet "ghost of the mountain" captures
the species' near-invisibility. Snow leopards are rarely seen even
by people who live in their habitat. There is no clear petroglyphic
representation of the snow leopard in the Thalpan complex — the
dominant large predator in the rock art record is absent or
unrecognized. This absence is itself interesting: the ibex, which
the snow leopard hunts, is the most common motif. The predator is
invisible even in art.

In the broader Himalayan and Central Asian tradition, the snow
leopard does not carry the mythological weight of, say, the tiger
in South Asian or the wolf in Turkic traditions. Its cultural
significance is more modern — an icon of wilderness and remoteness,
adopted by conservation organizations. The Shina or Burushaski
names and any local spirit associations require further ethnographic
investigation.

*Narrative interest*: A predator so well-adapted to its terrain that
it is effectively invisible. It hunts the same ibex that humans
carved on rocks for 10,000 years. The snow leopard watches from the
same cliffs. The fact that it does not appear in the petroglyph
record — while its prey dominates it — suggests either that the
carvers did not see it (plausible, given the animal's cryptic
nature) or that they chose not to represent it (a silence that
itself carries meaning).

*** Himalayan Brown Bear (/Ursus arctos isabellinus/)

The largest mammal in the western Himalayas. A subspecies of the
brown bear, also known as the isabelline bear or Himalayan red bear.
Classified as Critically Endangered in Pakistan.

*Physical*: Males 1.5-2.2m long, females 1.37-1.83m. Sandy or
reddish-brown colour (the "isabelline" — a sandy-yellowish hue).

*Distribution near Nanga Parbat*: In Pakistan, the primary habitats
are the subalpine and alpine regions of Khunjerab and Deosai National
Parks and *other protected areas in Astore and Nanga Parbat*
(Wikipedia, citing Abbas et al. 2015 and Nawaz et al. 2008). This
confirms presence in the Nanga Parbat area specifically.

The Deosai population — the most significant and stable population —
lies directly east of Nanga Parbat (the Deosai Plains sit at 4,114m
between Skardu and Astore districts). The Deosai population has
recovered from only 19 individuals in 1993 to 78 in 2022, thanks to
the establishment of Deosai National Park. The bear's range from
Deosai extends into the Astore valley and the eastern flanks of
Nanga Parbat.

*Evolutionary note*: The Himalayan brown bear is the sister group to
*all other brown bears and the polar bear*, with the branching event
dated to ~658,000 years ago (Middle Pleistocene). This divergence
corresponds to a glaciation event on the Tibetan Plateau
(Nyanyaxungla glaciation) that isolated the ancestral population.
This makes the Himalayan brown bear one of the most evolutionarily
distinct bear lineages.

*The Yeti connection*: DNA analysis of alleged "Yeti" specimens has
repeatedly identified them as Himalayan brown bear. The bear's large
size, reddish colour, occasional bipedal stance, and occupation of
remote high-altitude terrain likely account for a significant portion
of Yeti sightings. In the Nanga Parbat context, Deosai itself means
"shadow of the giant" (/deo/ = giant, /sai/ = shadow) — the giant
may have been the bear.

*Behaviour*: Omnivorous — grasses, roots, plants, insects, small
mammals, fruits, berries, and occasionally sheep and goats.
Hibernates October-April in dens. Feeds before sunrise and in the
late afternoon.

*Threats*: A population of 150-200 individuals across ~150,000 km^{2}
in northern Pakistan. Threatened by overgrazing, poaching, illicit
trade in bear fat and pelts, and increasingly by dependence on human
waste (documented in Indian populations where up to 75% of diet may
come from dump sites, causing toxic exposure and nutritional
deficiency).

*Narrative interest*: The evolutionary time depth — sister to all
other brown bears, isolated by glaciation 658,000 years ago. The
Yeti connection places the bear at the intersection of zoology and
mythology. The Deosai recovery (19 to 78 in three decades) is one of
the rare good conservation stories in the region. The "shadow of the
giant" walks the plains east of the mountain of the gods.

*** Long-tailed Marmot (/Marmota caudata/)

The alarm whistle of the alpine meadow. The long-tailed marmot (also
called the golden marmot) is the marmot species present in the
western Karakoram and Nanga Parbat area. (The Himalayan marmot,
/M. himalayana/, has a more eastern distribution — the two species'
ranges meet but do not overlap in the western Himalayas.)

*Physical*: A large, sturdy rodent weighing up to 9 kg. Weight varies
dramatically with season: 1.5 kg post-hibernation (spring) to 7.3 kg
pre-hibernation (autumn), when more than a quarter of body mass is
fat. Head-and-body length 37-80 cm. The tail is notably long for a
marmot: 16-28 cm, 37-55% of body length.

*Habitat*: Lives in open or lightly wooded habitats, often among
rocks where dwarf junipers grow — the characteristic Nanga Parbat
landscape. Excavates deep burrows (2-10m) in suitable soil
(fluvioglacial, alluvial deposits). Colonies of up to 30 families
where soil conditions allow. Hibernates for ~7.5 months (late
autumn to early spring).

*The alarm whistle*: The marmot's sharp alarm call is one of the
defining sounds of the alpine meadow (jut). It carries across the
open terrain, audible from hundreds of metres. The whistle warns the
colony of approaching predators — golden eagles, wolves, red foxes,
and snow leopards. In the silence above the treeline at Fairy
Meadows or the Rupal valley, the marmot alarm is often the first
sign of wildlife presence.

*Herodotus and the gold-digging ants*: The French ethnologist Michel
Peissel argued that Herodotus's account of "gold-digging ants" in
the land of the Dards was based on the golden marmot of the Deosai
Plateau. Local tribes (including the Minaro) collected gold dust
excavated from marmot burrows. The marmots, digging through
gold-bearing alluvial deposits, brought gold to the surface. Peissel
identified the Deosai as the specific location of Herodotus's story
— making the marmot a creature that connects classical Greek
historiography to the geology of the western Himalayas.

*Diet*: Grasses, sedges, and the soft parts of alpine flowering
plants — /Carex/, /Agrostis/, /Gentiana/, /Primula/, /Ranunculus/,
/Saussurea/. Many of the same plants that make the jut bloom.

*Predators*: Snow leopard, Tibetan wolf, red fox, golden eagle. The
golden eagle hunts marmots from above; the snow leopard stalks from
the rocks. The marmot is a keystone prey species linking the alpine
food web.

*Narrative interest*: The marmot whistle is a sound that carries.
In the narrative silence of the high jut, it is the voice of
vigilance — the first response to any approach, human or animal.
The Herodotus connection places a rodent on the Deosai Plateau at
the origin of one of Western history's most enduring traveller's
tales.

** F.2 Birds — The Ones That Fly Above the Face

*** Bearded Vulture / Lammergeier (/Gypaetus barbatus/)

The bone-eater. The only known vertebrate whose diet consists of
70-90% bone. Not a true Old World vulture — it forms a separate
lineage of Accipitridae with its closest relative, the Egyptian
vulture (/Neophron percnopterus/).

*Physical*: 94-125 cm long, wingspan 2.31-2.83 m (up to 9 ft 3 in).
Weight 4.5-7.8 kg; Himalayan birds tend to be slightly larger than
those from other ranges. Proportions compared to a falcon scaled to
enormous size. Unlike most vultures, the head is fully feathered —
no bald head. Dark grey above; the breast and leg feathers are
variably orange or rust-coloured, a colouration that is *cosmetic*:
the bird deliberately bathes in iron-rich mud and dust, staining its
own plumage. A black band across the eyes and black bristles under
the chin form the "beard."

*The bone-dropping behaviour*: The lammergeier carries large bones
(and occasionally live tortoises) to great height and drops them onto
rocks below — the /ossuary/ (bone-breaking) behaviour. The name
"ossifrage" means "bone-breaker." It swallows bone fragments whole;
its stomach acid (pH ~1) dissolves bone efficiently. The oesophagus
can accommodate bones up to 25 cm long. It feeds almost exclusively
on bone marrow and bone — a dietary niche no other vertebrate
occupies.

*Name*: German /Lammergeier/ = "lamb vulture," from the (false)
belief that it attacks lambs. In Iran, it is called /Homa/ — a bird
of Persian mythology associated with fortune and divinity. Whether
the Shina or Burushaski names carry similar mythological weight
requires further ethnographic investigation.

*Range near Nanga Parbat*: The subspecies /G. b. barbatus/ ranges
from southern Europe through the Himalayas to Nepal and western
Pakistan. The species breeds on crags in high mountains. The Nanga
Parbat massif — with its immense cliff systems, vertical relief,
and populations of ibex and markhor whose carcasses provide bones —
is prime lammergeier habitat. The Rupal Face and the Indus gorge
cliffs would both serve as hunting and nesting territory.

*Spirit associations*: The bird's immense size, the deliberate
mud-bathing to stain itself, and the bone-dropping behaviour give
it an uncanny appearance and ecology. In broader Himalayan
tradition, large raptors are associated with sky spirits. In the
v0 survey, the azhdaar (dragon-serpent) has "the wings of a raptor"
— the lammergeier's silhouette is perhaps the visual basis.
Specific Shina or Burushaski associations with the lammergeier
require further fieldwork. The Persian /Homa/ association is
potentially relevant given the Persian cultural substrate in
Gilgit-Baltistan.

*Narrative interest*: An animal that eats only bone. That
deliberately colours itself with iron-rich earth. That drops bones
from the sky. The lammergeier is not scavenging in the usual sense
— it has found a food source (marrow) that no other vertebrate
competes for, and it processes it through a behaviour (aerial
dropping) that requires intelligence, memory of ossuary sites, and
accurate targeting. It is the last consumer in the chain: after the
snow leopard kills the ibex, after the griffon vultures strip the
flesh, the lammergeier takes the skeleton.

*** Himalayan Griffon Vulture (/Gyps himalayensis/)

The massive scavenger of the high Himalaya. After the cinereous
vulture, it is the second-largest Old World vulture and among the
world's largest true raptors. Listed as Near Threatened (IUCN).

*Physical*: Length 1,030-1,150 mm. Wingspan 2.56-3.1 m. Weight
6-12.5 kg, averaging ~9 kg — perhaps the largest and heaviest bird
in the Himalayas. Pale buff uniform upperside, dark greater coverts,
tail and wing quills. Whitish down on immature heads becomes
yellowish in adults. Long, spiky ruff feathers. Pale blue facial
skin.

*Behaviour*: Soars on thermals — incapable of sustained flapping
flight. Follows grazers up mountains searching for carcasses. Makes
a rattling sound descending on a carcass; grunts and hisses at
roosts. Social — found in large flocks at feeding sites.

*Range*: Mainly the higher Himalayan regions and Tibetan Plateau,
1,200-5,500m. Distributed from Central Asia through Pakistan,
India, Nepal, Bhutan, to western China and Mongolia. The Nanga
Parbat massif is well within its range. These vultures would be a
regular sight soaring on thermals above the Rupal and Diamir faces.

*Tibetan sky burial connection*: In Tibetan Buddhist tradition,
/Gyps himalayensis/ is the primary species involved in sky burial
(/jhator/) — the offering of the body to vultures after death. This
practice does not extend to the Sunni Muslim communities of Diamer,
but the vulture's role as consumer of the dead carries cultural
weight across the Himalayan world.

Sources:
- Wikipedia, "Himalayan vulture":
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himalayan_vulture

*** Golden Eagle (/Aquila chrysaetos/)

The most widely distributed eagle species. In the Nanga Parbat area,
the golden eagle is a predator of marmots, hares, and young ibex.

*Physical*: Dark brown with lighter golden-brown plumage on the nape
(giving the name). Territories can be as large as 200 km^{2}. Builds
large nests on cliffs — the same cliff habitat as ibex and
lammergeier.

*Range near Nanga Parbat*: The golden eagle ranges throughout the
mountains of Central and South Asia. In the Nanga Parbat area, it
would hunt across the alpine meadows and ridgelines, taking marmots
as primary prey (as documented on the Tibetan Plateau). The eagle
and the marmot form a predator-prey pair that defines the alpine
soundscape: the marmot's alarm whistle often signals the eagle's
approach.

  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_eagle

*** Chukar Partridge (/Alectoris chukar/)

The common bird of the arid valleys. A Palearctic upland gamebird
in the pheasant family.

*Physical*: Rotund, 32-35 cm long. Light brown back, grey breast,
buff belly. White face with black gorget, rufous-streaked flanks,
red legs and coral-red bill.

*Sound*: A noisy /chuck-chuck-chukar-chukar/ — the call from which
the name derives. In the dry, rocky valleys of Diamer below the
treeline, the chukar's call is one of the most persistent sounds.

*Habitat*: Rocky open hillsides with grass, scrub, or cultivation.
In Pakistan, found at 600-4,000m. Not found in areas of high
humidity or rainfall — the Diamer rain-shadow valleys are ideal
habitat. The chukar is to the lower, arid valleys what the marmot
is to the alpine meadows: the characteristic voice of the
elevation zone.

*Range near Nanga Parbat*: Found along the inner Himalayan ranges
through Pakistan, including the Indus gorge valleys, the Chilas area
(summer temperatures exceeding 45C — classic chukar habitat), and
the lower approaches to the mountain.

Sources:
- Wikipedia, "Chukar partridge":
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chukar_partridge

*** Snow Partridge (/Lerwa lerwa/)

The high-altitude partridge. Found widely across the Himalayan
regions of Pakistan, India, Nepal, and China. The only species in
its genus — the most basal member of the pheasant subfamily.

*Physical*: Grey above and chestnut below, finely barred in black
and white. Bright red bill and legs. 38-40 cm long. Males 550-700g,
females 450-580g. Downy chicks resemble blood pheasant chicks.

*Habitat*: Alpine pastures and open hillsides above the treeline but
not in bare rocky terrain (unlike the Himalayan snowcock). Less wary
than the snowcock. Found at elevations matching the upper jut and
scree slopes around Nanga Parbat.

*Narrative interest*: A bird of the treeline transition — it marks
the elevation where trees give way to open sky. Its range overlaps
precisely with the peri's domain (the jut above the forest).

Sources:
- Wikipedia, "Snow partridge":
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snow_partridge

*** Western Tragopan (/Tragopan melanocephalus/)

The rarest pheasant of the western Himalayas. Globally threatened,
Vulnerable (IUCN). Found from Khyber Pakhtunkhwa (including
Kohistan and Kaghan valley — adjacent to Diamer) through Kashmir,
Chamba, and Kullu to east of the Sutlej.

*Physical*: Males are very dark (grey-black with numerous white
spots, each bordered with black and deep crimson patches). During
display, males inflate the throat into purple lappets with pink
margins and erect blue "horns" — a fancied resemblance to the
mythological god Pan (hence /tragopan/ from Greek /tragos/ "goat"
+ /Pan/).

*Habitat*: Upper temperate forests, 2,400-3,600m in summer, dense
coniferous and broadleaved forests 2,000-2,800m in winter. Sensitive
to anthropogenic disturbance. In the Nanga Parbat area, it would
potentially occur in the forested valleys on the approaches, though
the arid, rain-shadow character of Diamer may limit its range
compared to the more humid valleys of Kohistan and Kaghan. Its
presence in the Diamer district specifically is uncertain and would
require field confirmation.

*Narrative interest*: A bird that displays by inflating its throat
into something resembling the face of a god. The most endangered
pheasant in the western Himalayas. If it exists in the Diamer/Nanga
Parbat forests at all, it is as much a ghost as the snow leopard.

Sources:
- Wikipedia, "Western tragopan":
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_tragopan

*** Note on Diamer-endemic species

No bird species is endemic to Diamer district alone. The district
lies at the intersection of multiple biogeographic zones (western
Himalayan, Karakoram, Central Asian) and hosts species from all of
them. The bird community's interest lies not in endemism but in
the juxtaposition: the chukar of the hot Indus gorge (600m) and
the snow partridge of the alpine meadow (4,500m) are separated by
4 km of altitude within the same district.

** F.3 Flora — What Grows Without Monsoon

*** Juniper (/Juniperus excelsa/ subsp. /polycarpos/ and /J. macropoda/)

The sacred tree of the Karakoram. Two juniper species/subspecies are
relevant in the Nanga Parbat area:

1. *Juniperus excelsa* subsp. *polycarpos* (Persian juniper): The
   subspecies of Greek juniper that extends from Iran east to
   northwestern Pakistan. Some botanists treat it as a distinct
   species, /J. polycarpos/. A large shrub or tree reaching 6-20m,
   rarely 25m. Trunk up to 2m diameter. Broadly conical to rounded
   crown. Largely dioecious. Berry-like cones 6-11mm, blue-black
   with whitish waxy bloom, 3-6 seeds, maturing in ~18 months.

2. *Juniperus macropoda* (= /J. excelsa/ in some taxonomies): This
   is the juniper mentioned in the markhor's habitat description —
   the markhor inhabits shrub forests of oak, pine, and
   /J. macropoda/. In the Diamer context, this is likely the same
   as or closely related to /J. excelsa/ subsp. /polycarpos/.

*Distribution near Nanga Parbat*: Juniper forms the dominant tree
cover in the arid, rain-shadow valleys of Diamer where it replaces
the deodar (/Cedrus deodara/) of the monsoon valleys. It grows on
dry, rocky slopes between ~2,500m and ~4,000m. Above the juniper
zone: birch and then treeless alpine meadow. Below: bare arid
slopes or scattered scrub.

*Sacred use — the bitan's smoke*: Juniper smoke is one of the three
requirements for the shamanic trance session (alongside music and
goat blood). The bitan inhales burning juniper smoke or bites
juniper branches. This is not incidental — juniper burning is a
sacred practice across the entire Dardic, Kalasha, and broader
Central Asian shamanic world. Jettmar documented juniper/cedar
worship as a core element of pre-Islamic Dardic religion. The
tree's aromatic resin, slow growth in harsh conditions, and
evergreen persistence through the mountain winter all contribute
to its sacred status.

In Tibetan Buddhist tradition, juniper smoke (/bsang/) is burned as
purification and offering — the same basic practice with different
theological framing. The convergence across traditions suggests deep
antiquity.

*Oldest living junipers*: /Juniperus excelsa/ and related species
are among the longest-lived trees in the world. Individual trees
exceeding 1,000 years are documented in Turkey and Iran. The oldest
junipers in the Gilgit-Baltistan region have not been systematically
dated (no published dendrochronological studies found), but given
the species' longevity and the slow growth rates in the harsh
Karakoram climate, individual trees in the Nanga Parbat area could
plausibly be many centuries old. This is a gap in the published
literature.

*Narrative interest*: The juniper is the tree that opens the door
to the spirits. Its smoke is the medium through which the bitan
enters trance. It grows where nothing else will — on the dry, rocky
slopes that receive no monsoon. It may have been standing when the
petroglyphs were carved.

*** Himalayan Birch (/Betula utilis/)

The writing tree. A deciduous tree native to the western Himalayas,
growing at elevations up to 4,500m. The Latin epithet /utilis/ means
"useful." The Sanskrit name is /bhurja/ — sharing Indo-European
cognates with the English word "birch."

*Physical*: In native habitat, grows as a shrub or tree up to 20m.
Frequently grows among scattered conifers with an understorey of
evergreen /Rhododendron/. Thin, papery bark — very shiny, reddish
brown, reddish white, or white — that peels in broad horizontal
belts. The bark is the species' visual signature: white strips
peeling from dark trunks.

*Ecology near Nanga Parbat*: The birch depends on moisture from
snowmelt, *not from the monsoon* — making it well-suited to the
rain-shadow conditions of Diamer. It forms the upper treeline
forest, typically at 3,500-4,500m, above the juniper zone and below
the alpine meadow. Often with bent growth from winter snow pressure.
A fungal growth (/bhurja-granthi/) forms black lumps on the tree
weighing up to 1 kg.

*Treeline elevation*: Around Nanga Parbat, the birch treeline varies
by aspect and exposure but typically reaches 3,800-4,200m — the
transition to the jut (alpine meadow). On the approach to Fairy
Meadows (3,300m), scattered birch would be visible on the upper
slopes.

*Historical use*: The bark was used for centuries as writing
surface for Sanskrit manuscripts, particularly in Kashmir. Sanskrit
writers Kalidasa (c. 4th century CE), Sushruta (c. 3rd century CE),
and Varahamihira (6th century CE) mention its use. Kashmiri Pandits
reported all books were written on birch bark until Akbar introduced
paper in the 16th century.

Sacred mantras are still written on the bark, placed in amulets,
and worn for protection — a practice documented since the 8th-9th
century CE (Lakshmi Tantra).

*Narrative interest*: The tree that humans wrote on before they had
paper. The bark that peels itself, offering its surface. At Nanga
Parbat, the birch marks the treeline — the boundary between the
forested world of human habitation and the open, treeless world of
the peri. The writing tree stands at the threshold.

Sources:
- Wikipedia, "Betula utilis":
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betula_utilis

*** Alpine Meadow — The Jut in Bloom

The /jut/ (Shina: summer pasture) — Fairy Meadows, Rupal meadows,
and the alpine grasslands above the treeline — is the peri's domain.
In bloom (June-August), these meadows are dense with wildflowers.
Species documented from alpine meadows in the western Himalayas and
Karakoram at 3,000-5,000m include:

- */Primula/* spp. (primrose): among the earliest spring bloomers,
  often appearing at snowmelt margins
- */Gentiana/* spp. (gentian): deep blue flowers, characteristic of
  high-altitude meadows
- */Ranunculus/* spp. (buttercup): yellow, ubiquitous in moist
  meadows
- */Saussurea/* spp.: including snow lotus species at the highest
  elevations, used in traditional medicine
- */Iris/* spp.: including /Iris potaninii/ in some areas
- */Pedicularis/* spp. (lousewort): elaborate pink-purple flowers,
  common in moist alpine meadows
- */Potentilla/* spp. (cinquefoil): yellow-flowered, mat-forming
- */Edelweiss/* (/Leontopodium/): the iconic alpine flower, present
  in the western Himalayas
- */Aconitum/* spp. (monkshood): deep purple, highly toxic, used in
  traditional medicine across the Himalaya (and, historically, as
  poison)
- */Anemone/* spp.: delicate white-to-purple flowers at snowline
- */Bistorta/* (knotweed): pink spikes in moist meadows

The grasses and sedges include /Carex/, /Agrostis/, /Deschampsia/,
and /Koeleria/ — the same species that marmots feed on.

*What it looks like in bloom*: At full bloom in July, the jut is a
tapestry of blue (gentian), yellow (buttercup, potentilla), pink
(primula, pedicularis), and white (anemone, edelweiss) against a
ground of green grass. The meadows at Fairy Meadows face the Rakhiot
glacier and the north face of Nanga Parbat — wildflowers in the
foreground, ice in the background. The contrast is absolute.

*The peri's domain*: The jut is where the peri dance under moonlight.
The shamanic tradition holds that peri descend to earth during the
blossoming season. The meadow in bloom is literally the setting for
spirit encounter.

*** Apricot and Cherry Orchards

The blossoming trees of the valley floor. The peri choose shamans
during the cherry and apricot blossoming seasons — they "descend
to earth" and "select the dayal from among newborns by smelling
their noses and mouths."

*Apricot* (/Prunus armeniaca/): Gilgit-Baltistan is one of the
world's centers of apricot diversity. The Hunza valley is
particularly famous, but apricot orchards are widespread across the
region including Diamer, the Astore valley, and the lower Nanga
Parbat approaches. Varieties are numerous and locally named.
Blossoming occurs in March-April, transforming the grey, arid valley
floors into clouds of white and pink. The dried apricot (and apricot
oil) is a staple food and economic product.

*Cherry* (/Prunus avium/ and /P. cerasoides/): Wild and cultivated
cherry trees blossom slightly before or concurrent with apricot.
The blossoming season (March-April at lower elevations, up to May
at higher) is the time of spirit selection.

*Cultivation history*: Stone fruit cultivation in Central and South
Asia has deep antiquity — apricots have been cultivated for at least
3,000 years, with the Karakoram/Himalayan region as one of the
likely centres of domestication. The genetic diversity of local
varieties in Gilgit-Baltistan supports this antiquity.

*Narrative interest*: The blossoming season is when the boundary
between human and spirit worlds thins. The peri descend. The newborn
is chosen. The trees in bloom are not scenery — they are the
temporal marker of spirit activity. The blossom is the signal.

*** Morel Mushrooms (/Morchella/ spp.)

Already noted in v0: the seasonal economy at Fairy Meadows includes
morel mushroom harvesting (/kuchuli/ in Shina) in May-June, fetching
up to Rs 15,000/kg. Morels fruit in the juniper and birch forest
zone after snowmelt, growing in association with the root systems
of these trees (likely mycorrhizal). The harvest is economically
significant — communities have transitioned from timber to tourism
and morel collection.

*Narrative interest*: A fungus that appears only briefly in the
narrow window between snowmelt and summer heat, worth a small fortune,
growing in partnership with the sacred juniper. The kuchuli harvest
is one of the few economic activities that takes people into the
forest zone regularly.

*** Medicinal Plants of the Bitan

The bitan's pharmacopoeia is not well-documented in the open
literature. However, the broader ethnobotany of the western
Himalayas and Karakoram includes several medicinal plants likely
used by traditional healers in the Diamer/Nanga Parbat area:

- */Ephedra/* spp. (joint-fir, /ma huang/): a gymnosperm shrub of
  arid, high-altitude regions. Contains ephedrine and
  pseudoephedrine. Used in traditional medicine across Central and
  South Asia as a stimulant and treatment for respiratory ailments.
  /Ephedra gerardiana/ is the common species of the western
  Himalayas. Found on dry, rocky slopes — the same habitat as
  juniper.

- */Artemisia/* spp. (wormwood): widespread in arid habitats.
  Contains artemisinin (the antimalarial compound). Used across the
  Himalaya for fever, stomach complaints, and as incense. The
  aromatic, bitter plants grow on the dry slopes of the Indus gorge
  and lower valleys.

- */Aconitum/* spp. (monkshood, /bish/ or /vatsnabh/): highly toxic
  alkaloids. Used in minute doses in traditional medicine across the
  Himalaya as analgesic, anti-inflammatory, and cardiac medicine. A
  plant that kills or cures depending on dosage — resonant with the
  bitan's dual relationship with the peri (the iron bangle that
  both binds and protects).

- */Saussurea costus/* (kuth): a threatened medicinal plant of the
  western Himalayas, used in traditional medicine and incense. CITES
  Appendix I listed.

- *Juniper berries and resin*: The juniper itself is medicinal — the
  berries and resin have documented antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory,
  and digestive properties.

*Gap*: The specific pharmacopoeia of the bitan/dayal in Diamer is
not systematically documented in the accessible literature. Sidky
(1994) and Nicolaus (2015) describe the trance practice in detail
but do not enumerate the medicinal plants used. This is a research
gap that would require fieldwork or access to ethnobotanical surveys
of the Shina-speaking areas.

** F.4 Rivers and Water

*** Tributaries and Meltwater Streams

The Nanga Parbat massif drains into the Indus through several named
tributaries:

- *Rakhiot Nala*: Drains the Rakhiot Glacier (north face). The
  approach route to Fairy Meadows follows this valley. The stream
  carries heavy glacial sediment — milky, grey-green meltwater
  laden with rock flour ground from the gneiss bedrock. The hot
  springs at Tato village are in this drainage.

- *Rupal Nala*: Drains the Rupal Glacier and the Rupal Face (south).
  The approach to Rupal base camp follows this watercourse through
  Tarishing/Tarshing village. The stream character changes
  dramatically with season and time of day — low in early morning
  (glacier frozen overnight), rising to a torrent by mid-afternoon
  as solar radiation accelerates melt.

- *Diamir Nala*: Drains the Diamir Glacier (west face). Less
  visited than the Rakhiot or Rupal approaches.

- *Astore River*: A major tributary of the Indus, draining the
  Deosai Plateau and flowing through the eastern slope of Nanga
  Parbat. Originates on the western slopes of Burzil Pass. Joins
  the Gilgit River. The Astore carries the combined meltwater of
  the eastern Nanga Parbat glaciers and the Deosai snowfields.

All of these streams are *glacially fed*, not monsoon-fed. Their
character is defined by the diurnal freeze-melt cycle, the seasonal
advance and retreat of snowcover, and the grinding of rock by ice.

*** Hot Springs as Ecological Niche

v0 (section A.5) documents the physical and geological context of
the hot springs at Tato (~92C, near-boiling) and Tattapani/Sassi
(42-81C). Here, the ecological question: what lives in these waters?

*Thermophilic and hyperthermophilic organisms*: Hot springs globally
are known habitats for extremophile microorganisms — bacteria and
archaea adapted to temperatures that would kill most life. The
best-studied examples are from Yellowstone (USA), Iceland, and New
Zealand. The Tato hot spring (~92C) falls in the hyperthermophile
range (>80C), where only archaea and a few bacterial lineages
survive.

*Published studies from Nanga Parbat hot springs*: Craw et al. (cited
in v0) and Chamberlain et al. (1995) documented the geochemistry of
the hot spring fluids but focused on geological, not biological,
aspects. A systematic search of the literature reveals *no published
microbial ecology studies specifically from the Tato or Tattapani
hot springs*. This is a significant gap. The geothermal gradient at
Nanga Parbat (~100C/km) makes these springs scientifically
interesting as potential habitats for novel extremophile lineages,
but the microbiology appears to be unstudied.

*What might be there*: By analogy with studied hot springs at similar
temperatures and chemistry (meteoric water + granitic interaction):
- At 92C (Tato): likely dominated by hyperthermophilic archaea —
  /Thermus/, /Pyrobaculum/, /Sulfolobus/ or related genera,
  depending on pH and sulfur content
- At 42-81C (Tattapani/Sassi): a richer community including
  thermophilic cyanobacteria (forming coloured mats), /Thermus/,
  /Deinococcus/, and various chemolithotrophic bacteria
- The orange-to-yellowish hydrothermal alteration described at Tato
  may include microbial contributions (iron-oxidizing bacteria) in
  addition to abiotic mineral precipitation

*Gap*: This is a research opportunity. The Nanga Parbat hot springs
are geologically well-characterized but microbiologically
unexplored in the published literature.

*** Glacial Meltwater Chemistry

How does the water from Nanga Parbat's glaciers differ from
monsoon-fed rivers?

*Rock flour*: Glacial meltwater carries finely ground rock particles
(glacial flour, or /Gletscherschliff/) produced by the glacier
grinding over bedrock. At Nanga Parbat, the bedrock is predominantly
Indian plate gneiss (Proterozoic, metamorphic) and the Kohistan arc
terrane (Cretaceous, volcanic/plutonic). The rock flour gives the
meltwater its characteristic milky, grey-green to turquoise colour.

*Dissolved minerals*: The chemistry of glacial meltwater reflects the
bedrock it has ground. The gneissic bedrock would contribute silica,
feldspar-derived ions (Na^{+}, K^{+}, Ca^{2+}), and trace elements
typical of metamorphic rocks. Where the water interacts with the
young leucogranite dikes (0.7 Ma), it may carry elevated
concentrations of elements associated with granitic melts (Li, B, F,
rare earth elements).

*Temperature*: Glacial meltwater is near 0C at source, warming
downstream. The contrast with the hot springs is extreme: 0C
meltwater and 92C spring water within the same drainage (Rakhiot
valley).

*Comparison with monsoon-fed rivers*: Monsoon-fed rivers (e.g.,
the Tirthan in Kullu) carry dissolved organic matter from forest
soils, have warmer baseline temperatures, and show a seasonal pulse
driven by rainfall. Glacially-fed rivers show a *diurnal* pulse
(freeze-melt cycle) and a *seasonal* pulse (summer melt maximum),
but carry minimal dissolved organic matter and maximal suspended
mineral load. The rivers of Diamer are mineral rivers — they carry
the mountain's ground-up rock, not forest soil.

*Downstream*: All of these waters eventually reach the Indus, which
at Chilas carries the integrated meltwater of the entire Nanga
Parbat massif. The Indus in the gorge is a river of extraordinary
sediment load — cutting down through the crust at 9-12 mm/yr while
carrying the products of that cutting downstream.

** F.5 Ecology Section Summary — What the Inventory Reveals

The ecological community of the Nanga Parbat massif is structured by
*altitude* and *aridity* — not by monsoon, not by latitude. The
gradient from the Indus gorge (~1,100m, summer >45C) to the summit
(8,126m) compresses multiple biomes into a vertical distance of 7 km:

| Zone | Elevation | Character | Key species |
|------+-----------+-----------+-------------|
| Indus gorge | 1,100-2,000m | Arid, extreme heat | Chukar, scrub |
| Dry slopes | 2,000-3,000m | Scattered juniper, markhor habitat | Markhor, juniper, ephedra |
| Forest zone | 3,000-3,800m | Juniper-birch forest | Morels, birch, juniper |
| Alpine meadow (jut) | 3,500-4,500m | Wildflowers, herding | Marmot, snow partridge, ibex |
| Subnival | 4,500-5,500m | Rock, ice, sparse lichen | Snow leopard, ibex, lammergeier |
| Glacial/nival | >5,500m | Permanent ice | Bacteria, algae on ice |

The food web centers on the *ibex* as primary large herbivore, the
*snow leopard* as apex predator, and the *lammergeier* as terminal
consumer (bone-eater). The *marmot* is the keystone prey species for
both golden eagle and snow leopard. The *juniper* is both ecological
(dominant tree) and spiritual (trance medium). The *birch* marks the
treeline boundary.

Every species documented here participates in both the ecological
web and the cultural web: the ibex on the petroglyphs, the markhor
as national symbol, the lammergeier as sky-spirit template, the
juniper as shamanic medium, the marmot as Herodotus's gold-digger,
the brown bear as the giant's shadow.

* H. Deep Human History — The First Stories (WP-0013 Part B)

This section deepens v0's coverage (sections B.1–B.7) with three new
dimensions: the petroglyphs as a *primary source* for understanding
who lived here across millennia; the pre-Islamic religion as a
*coherent system* rather than scattered ethnographic details; and the
oral traditions that carried knowledge before writing arrived.

** H.1 Petroglyphs as Primary Source

v0 (B.6) introduces the petroglyphs in the context of the dam threat
and gives the headline numbers: ~30,000 carvings, 5,000 inscriptions,
10+ writing systems, the Altar Rock. This section goes deeper — the
petroglyphs as a *stratified archive* encoding 10,000 years of
passage through this valley.

*** The sites: geography of inscription

The rock art concentrates where the Indus valley widens enough for
camps and river crossings — natural halting points on the route
between South and Central Asia.

| Site | Location | Character |
|------+----------+-----------|
| Chilas 1–6 | Left bank, near modern Chilas | Earliest Kharoṣṭhī inscriptions; Chilas 2 has pre-iconic Buddhist stūpas (monks with incense, no Buddha figure) |
| Thalpan | Opposite Chilas, right bank terraces | Densest concentration. Ornate stūpa designs, jātaka narratives, the "Altar Rock" |

| Minar Gah | ~30 km west of Chilas | Warriors with battle axes and shields, horsemen, dancers. 89 Brāhmī inscriptions (4th–5th c.) |

*** Chronological phases

The Pak-German Study Group (Jettmar, Hauptmann, Bandini-König; from
1979/1989) established the definitive catalogue: /Materialien zur
Archäologie der Nordgebiete Pakistans/, 13+ volumes. Their phasing:

*Phase 1: Prehistoric to Protohistoric (pre-1st c. CE).* Animal
petroglyphs dominate — ibex, markhor, deer, bovids. Found at "hunting
stations" near riverbanks. No inscriptions. The ibex is the most
frequent motif by far. Hunting scenes show figures with bows stalking
caprids. *Shatial Das is the purest example* of this phase: footprints,
ibex/markhor, horses, hunting scenes, a possible chariot drawing,
one Kharoṣṭhī inscription — and not a single Buddhist symbol. This
problematises earlier dating that placed all Shatial petroglyphs in
the 3rd–8th centuries CE.

At Oshibat, ~5,000 caprid drawings with only 4% Buddhist imagery
suggest the site was primarily a hunting station that accumulated
Buddhist additions later.

*Phase 2: Kharoṣṭhī/Gāndhārī (1st–3rd c. CE).* The earliest written
layer. Kharoṣṭhī script recording Gāndhārī language. Epigraphic
formula: "x, son of y, arrived." Pre-iconic Buddhist art — stūpas
without Buddha in human form. Monks offering incense. Chilas 2 is
the type site: two robed figures approach a stūpa; above them, an
ibex on a pillar capital — the indigenous motif persisting alongside
the imported religion.

*Phase 3: Brāhmī/Sanskrit dominance (4th–7th c. CE).* The peak.
~80% of all Upper Indus inscriptions belong here. Sanskrit donative
inscriptions (devadharma — "gift to the gods"). Elaborate stūpa
compositions, jātaka narratives, the Altar Rock's scenes of Buddha's
temptation and the Benares sermon. Multilingual overlaps with Sogdian,
Bactrian, Chinese, Tibetan — the Silk Road at full flow. Shatial
alone has 565 Sogdian inscriptions from Iranian merchants.

*Phase 4: Late/Tibetan (7th c. onwards).* Proto-Śāradā script at
Hodar. Tibetan inscriptions dominant in Baltistan. The Buddhist phase
wanes; Islam arrives.

*** Motif catalogue: what was carved and why

*Caprids (ibex and markhor)*: The most abundant motif across all
periods. At Oshibat, ~5,000 caprid drawings. The ibex appears with
knotted horns and beards; the markhor with characteristic spiral horns.
The ibex is the ancient symbol of fertility and abundance — an animal
whose sacredness predates Buddhism by millennia. At Chilas 2, an ibex
on a pillar capital sits above the Buddhist scene — the older motif
literally over the newer one. The ibex cult persisted: as late as the
1980s, ibex were still being carved for cultic hunting purposes by the
people of Hunza, and shrines topped with ibex horns still stood in
high valleys.

*Hunting scenes*: Figures with bows and arrows stalking ibex, deer,
bovids. Found at Shatial Das, Minar Gah, and throughout the
prehistoric layer. Warriors at Minar Gah wield battle axes and shields,
some on horseback.

*Maskoid figures — the Siberian connection*: Near Chilas, Jettmar
identified a group of petroglyphs showing horned anthropomorphic
figures — maskoids with round or oval heads surmounted by horns or
antennae. He linked these to the Early Bronze Age *Okunev Culture* of
the Minusinsk Basin in southern Siberia (late 3rd to early 2nd
millennium BCE). The Okunev site of Mugur-Sargol gave its name to this
class of maskoids. The same motif-type spreads from the Gegam
Mountains in Armenia to the mouth of the Amur River. At Barikot in
Swat, a maskoid slab has been dated to ~1700–1400 BCE.

What the Siberian connection implies: during the 3rd and early 2nd
millennia BCE, cultural relations existed over thousands of kilometres,
possibly mediated by migrations of cattle-keeping early nomads. The
Indus valley was not isolated — it was a corridor.

*Tridents and lingas*: At Minar Gah, tridents and lingas (aniconic
Śiva representations) alongside swastikas suggest a Śaiva presence
during the Gupta period (3rd–5th c. CE). The linga cult analysis
follows Atul K. Sur (1931–32).

*Buddhist architectural elements*: Stūpas with domes (aṇḍa), multiple
platforms, yaṣṭi (staff), chattrāvali (tiered umbrellas), bells,
pennants. The most elaborate compositions at Thalpan and Hodar.
Narrative jātaka scenes — King Śibi's flesh gift, Buddha's temptation
by Māra's daughters.

*The overwriting*: At Chilas 2, an ibex "was carved first, and then
the Buddha image was carved on top of it." The rock is a palimpsest —
each layer of carving partially obscures and partially incorporates
the earlier. The Buddhist traveller did not erase the ibex; he added
his devotion alongside it. The petroglyph record is cumulative, not
replacement.

*** The writing systems: who passed through and when

Ten or more scripts on the rocks — each one a signature of a
different civilisation using the Indus valley as corridor.

The linguistic diversity at a single river crossing (Shatial: 565
Sogdian + hundreds of Brāhmī + Kharoṣṭhī + Chinese + Bactrian +

junction on the capillary network connecting South Asia's /Uttarāpatha/
(Great Northern Road) with Central Asian Silk Routes via mountain
passes: Mintaka (4,629m), Kilik (4,755m), Karambar (4,188m), Baroghil
(3,804m), Darkot (4,630m).

Optimal travel season: October–January (lower glacial river levels).
May–October was dangerous — monsoon and meltwater.

** H.2 Pre-Islamic Religion of the Dards

v0 (B.1–B.2) documents the peri tradition and bitan practice in
detail. This section reconstructs the broader religious system these
practices belonged to — drawing on Jettmar's /Religions of the
Hindukush/ (1975, updated 2023) and the Kalasha as the last living
practitioners.

*** Jettmar's synthesis

Karl Jettmar (1918–2002), Austrian ethnologist. Beginning in 1955 he
conducted semi-annual ethnographic and archaeological fieldwork in
northwest Pakistan and Afghanistan for ~20 years. His /Religions of
the Hindukush: The Pre-Islamic Heritage of Eastern Afghanistan and
Northern Pakistan/ (1975; English translation with Peter Parkes's
introduction, 2023) is the primary source.

Jettmar's argument: the tribal peoples of the Hindu Kush and western
Karakoram — Kafirs (Nuristani), Kalasha, Kho, Shina-speaking Dards —
preserved a religious system with archaic Indo-Aryan, possibly
pre-Vedic, elements. This was not simply "Hinduism at the periphery"
— it was a parallel branch, older than classical Hinduism in some
features, transformed by isolation, altitude, and centuries of
interaction with Central Asian traditions.

The key features:
1. *Gods with Vedic names* — but different roles and myths
2. *Sacrifice* (especially goat sacrifice) as the central ritual act
3. *The purity/impurity opposition* structuring all social and ritual
   life
4. *Mountain-dwelling spirits* (peri/suchi/vetr) requiring
   propitiation
5. *The shaman* (bitan/dehar/dayal) as intermediary
6. *Sacred trees* (juniper, cedar) as axis mundi

Source:
- Jettmar, K. (1975/2023), /Religions of the Hindukush/, revised
  English edition with introduction by Peter Parkes (White Orchid
  Books / Parkes edition 2023)

*** The Kalasha pantheon: last living witness

The Kalasha of Chitral's Bumboret, Rumbur, and Birir valleys are the
only community in the eastern Hindu Kush that retains the
pre-Islamic religion as a living practice. ~4,000 speakers. Their
pantheon illuminates what the Shina-speaking Dards of Diamer may once
have believed before conversion to Islam.

*Dezau* (also called Imrá): supreme creator. Name from Indo-European
/*dheig'h/ "to form." Also called Khodai (Persian). Historians
compare to Zeus (Greek) — shared PIE root /*dyeu-/.

*Jestak*: goddess of domestic life, family, marriage. Protects the
household.

*Sajigor*: appears as a form of Indra — also called Shura Verin,
"the hero, the unrivalled Indra." War god and protector.

*Mahandeo*: god of crops and war. A negotiator with the highest deity.
The name echoes Mahādeva (Śiva).

*Mountain fairies (Suchi/Peri)*: "there is a general pattern of
belief in mountain fairies, Suchi (now often called Peri), who help
in hunting and killing enemies." The Varōti (Sanskrit /Vātaputra/,
"sons of the wind") are their violent male partners — echoing the
Vedic Apsaras and Gandharvas. The fairy/Gandharva parallel is
striking: in Vedic religion, Apsaras are divine females of waters
and mountains; Gandharvas are their male counterparts, associated
with music and ecstasy. In the Kalasha system, the same structure
persists, transposed to a mountain context.

*The purity system (Onjesta/Pragata)*:

| Onjesta (pure) | Pragata (impure) |
|----------------+------------------|
| Mountains | Valleys |
| Juniper | Onions |
| Markhor | Cattle, sheep |
| Men | Women (in certain states) |
| Altars | Graveyards |
| Stables | Bashali (menstrual house) |

The purity axis runs vertically: high = pure, low = impure. Mountains
are the abode of gods and fairies; valleys are the domain of death
and agriculture. The markhor is pure (mountain-dwelling, wild); cattle
are impure (domesticated, valley-dwelling). This is not the
Brahmanical caste purity system — it is an altitude-based cosmology.

*Festival cycles*:
- *Zhoshi* (spring/sowing, mid-May): fertility, prosperity
- *Uchau* (autumn): harvest thanksgiving
- *Cawmos* (winter solstice): "the greatest, solemn, and last
  festival of the year" — remembrance and purification (Loude &
  Lièvre, /Kalash Solstice/)

UNESCO recognised Kalasha Suri Jagek (astronomical observation
practice linked to solstice festivals) in 2018.

*** The ibex cult

The ibex is the most frequent animal motif in the Upper Indus
petroglyphs — thousands of carvings across all periods, from
prehistoric hunting stations to Buddhist-era sites where ibex persist
alongside stūpas. This was not decorative; it was cultic.

Evidence for a coherent ibex cult among the Dardic peoples:

1. *Petroglyphs*: Ibex dominate the prehistoric layer. At Oshibat,
   ~5,000 caprid drawings. The animal is depicted with exaggerated
   horns — stylised, not naturalistic.

2. *Ibex horns on shrines and graves*: As late as the 1980s, shrines
   topped by ibex horns stood in high valleys of Hunza, and ibex were
   still being carved for cultic hunting purposes. In Kalasha
   graveyards, carved wooden effigies (gandau) of the dead are
   surrounded by ibex and markhor horns.

3. *The ibex in Kalasha cosmology*: The markhor (and by extension the
   ibex) belongs to the /onjesta/ (pure) category — mountain-dwelling,
   wild, associated with fairies and gods. Hunting ibex and markhor
   is a sacred activity. The fairies themselves are said to keep
   herds of markhor in high pastures.

4. *Ibex as fertility symbol*: "An ancient symbol of fertility and
   abundance" — the ibex represents the mountain's generative power.
   The exaggerated horns in petroglyphs may represent not the animal
   but the principle.

5. *Continuity*: The ibex motif persists from the earliest prehistoric
   carvings (possibly 3rd millennium BCE) through the Buddhist period
   (where ibex are carved alongside stūpas) into the present (shrines,
   carvings, Kalasha cosmology). This is ~5,000 years of continuity
   for a single sacred symbol.

The markhor's name in Persian means "snake-eater" (/mār/ + /khor/).
Whether this connects to the nāga/klu serpent traditions of the region
is speculative but suggestive — the pure mountain goat that devours
the chthonic serpent.

*** Juniper as axis mundi

Juniper (/Juniperus excelsa/) is the sacred tree of the Dardic world.
In v0 (B.2), the bitan inhales juniper smoke to enter trance. But
juniper is more than a shamanic tool — it structures the cosmology:

- In Kalasha religion: juniper belongs to the /onjesta/ (pure) sphere.
  "Juniper or Gal is a sacred tree and food for Pari, and is only
  found in pure places high up in the mountains."
- The bashali (menstrual house) is purified twice yearly using
  holly-oak branch smoke — but the sacred altars use juniper.
- In Hunza shamanism: "shamans manipulated sacred juniper leaves,
  entered into violent trances, and communed with spirits believed to
  reside in stones and in certain trees."
- Cedar/juniper worship extends across the Dardic world — from
  Chitral through Gilgit-Baltistan. The tree that grows at altitude,
  burns with aromatic smoke, resists rot. The vertical axis again:
  the high-altitude tree as conduit between the human world and the
  spirit world.

*** Dardic religion and the Vedic question

Jettmar's central claim: the pre-Islamic religions of the Hindu Kush
preserve elements that are "archaic Indo-Aryan, and possibly even
pre-Vedic." This means:

- The god-names are recognisable from the Vedas (Mahandeo ↔ Mahādeva;
  Sajigor ↔ Indra; Dezau ↔ /*dyeu-/) but the mythology and ritual
  practice diverge significantly from classical Hinduism.
- The goat sacrifice, the purity system, the mountain-spirit complex,
  and the shaman tradition have no close parallel in mainstream Hindu
  practice — they resemble the *earliest* stratum of Indo-Aryan
  religion before Brahmanical systematisation.
- The Kalasha pantheon is "one of the last living representatives of
  Indo-European religion, along with Hinduism and Zoroastrianism."
- The Apsaras/Gandharva ↔ Suchi/Varōti parallel suggests that what
  became celestial nymphs and musicians in Vedic literature remained
  mountain fairies and wind-spirits in the Dardic world.

This is not marginal survivalism. It is a parallel development from a
shared root — the Dardic branch preserved archaic features that the
Vedic/Brahmanical tradition transformed or lost. The bitan of Diamer,
inhaling juniper smoke and speaking in tongues, may be closer to the
proto-Indo-European ritual specialist than any Brahmin priest.

** H.3 Oral Tradition and Song

*** Burushaski oral literature

Burushaski, a language isolate with fewer than 100,000 speakers in
Hunza, Nagar, and Yasin valleys. No demonstrable genetic relationship
to any other language. The oral literature is documented by:

*D.L.R. Lorimer* (1920s–30s): British Political Agent in Gilgit
(1920–24), returned in 1934. Published /The Burushaski Language/ in
3 volumes (1935–38): grammar, texts with translations, dictionary.
The texts include folk tales, legends (some of foreign origin), verse,
proverbs, local traditional history, and accounts of customs —
marriages, funerals, seasonal celebrations, gold washing, water
regulation.

*Hermann Berger* (1998): /Die Burushaski-Sprache von Hunza und Nager
Teil II, Texte mit Übersetzungen/ (Wiesbaden: Harrassowitz). A
comprehensive collection of transcribed folktales in both Hunza and
Nagar dialects, presented in original form and in versions adjusted
for contemporary speech. Made accessible online by the Burushaski
Language Services project with permission of Berger's family.

Source: https://burushaski-ls.com/en/audio/folktales

*Peri narratives in Burushaski folklore*: The folktale "The Frog as a
Bride, or the Three Princes and the Fairy Princess Salaasír" (Aarne-
Thompson type 402, transcribed from field research) exemplifies the
genre — fairy princesses, tests of worthiness, transformation. The
peri is not marginal to Burushaski narrative; she is the central
figure of the most important story type.

*** Shina oral tradition

Shina is the dominant language of Diamer district — a Dardic
(Indo-Aryan) language with ~550,000 speakers across Gilgit-Baltistan.

*Musical forms*: v0 (B.5) lists twelve named tune-types. The system
is not just musical but functional — each tune belongs to a specific
social occasion:

| Tune | Occasion |
|------+----------|
| Dhani | General celebration |
| Bazmi | Assembly, gathering |
| Tajwar | Procession |
| Alghani | Lament |
| Saus | Love song |
| Lolo | Lullaby |
| Tambal | Dance |
| Bulla | Challenge/boast |
| Razm | Battle |
| Yudaani | War |
| Danyal/Bitan | Shamanic trance |

The *Danyal/Bitan* tune is the one that calls the peri. This is the
tune played by the Dom musicians (the monopoly described in v0 B.2)
on Dadang, Daamal, and Surnai for 40+ continuous minutes. The music
is not accompaniment — it is the mechanism.

*Folk tales and proverbs*:
- /Saweno Moriye/ — a collection of Shina proverbs and quotations,
  published 1978 by Lok Virsa, Islamabad.
- ROSHAN Gilgit-Baltistan (Gilgit-Baltistan Institute for Language
  Research) has published collections of Shina folklore.
- Sociological research on Shina folk tales documents how they "shape
  people's behaviour so that people can learn folk wisdom" — the
  stories function as moral pedagogy and social code.

*Shina wedding songs (Tavau Gai)*: Rich poetic structures —
repetition, alliteration, consonance, assonance — designed for
memorability and group performance.

*Modern Shina poetry and music*: Zafar Waqar Taj (poet) and Mir Afzal
Ustad (sitar, from Kalam) catalysed a modern Shina music movement.

*** Kalasha religious songs and festival cycles

The Kalasha of Chitral preserve the richest living tradition of
religious song in the Dardic world.

Music as theology: "Music serves as a primary repository of the
Kalasha's unwritten history, religious beliefs, and social norms,
with melodies functioning as a form of spiritual knowledge expressed
as theology in practice — a living liturgy encoded in melody and
rhythm rather than in scripture."

This is the key insight: in a tradition without holy books, the songs
*are* the scripture. The song is not about the belief — it is the
belief, performed.

Jean-Yves Loude and Viviane Lièvre (French anthropologists) documented
the Kalasha festival cycles in /Kalash Solstice: Winter Feasts of the
North/ (1988). The winter solstice festival (Cawmos) is the most
significant — "remembrance and purification of self and the land."

UNESCO recognised the Kalasha Suri Jagek (astronomical observation
linked to solstice reckoning) as intangible cultural heritage in 2018.

*** The gap: oral history from Diamer itself

A notable absence: no comprehensive collection of oral histories from
Diamer district specifically has been identified in published form.
Shina-language folklore collections exist (Lok Virsa, ROSHAN), and
Burushaski oral literature is documented by Lorimer and Berger for
Hunza. But systematically recorded oral traditions from the Shina-
speaking villages around Nanga Parbat — the stories people in Chilas
and Tato and Rupal tell about their own valley — remain poorly
documented or unpublished.

The bitan tradition itself is oral: the "ancient language" the shaman
speaks in trance is not written down. The twelve musical tunes are
named but not transcribed in Western notation (as far as this survey
can determine). What the old women of Rupal know about the peri of
Fairy Meadows may never have been recorded.

This is the most significant gap this survey has identified. The dam
will flood 13 villages. The oral traditions of those villages — the
stories, the place-names, the local knowledge of where the spirits
live — are as endangered as the petroglyphs.

** J.1 Petroglyph Photography and 3D Models

*** The LUMS / Wilfrid Laurier / Sydney Digital Survey (2018–present)

The most important contemporary documentation project. A joint venture
between Lahore University of Management Sciences (LUMS), Wilfrid
Laurier University (Canada), and University of Sydney (Australia),
funded by the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council of
Canada (SSHRC). Led by Jason Neelis (Wilfrid Laurier).

Methods: geo-referenced DSLR photography, terrestrial laser scanning
(TLS), photogrammetric 3D modelling, panoramic virtual tours.

*Output*: Over 2,000 photogrammetric 3D models of individual
petroglyph rocks posted to Sketchfab collections. These are
navigable, zoomable 3D models — the closest thing to standing in
front of the rock. Panotours provide virtual walk-throughs of sites.

This is the definitive digital record of what the dam will destroy.

Sources:

** J.2 Historical Expedition Photography

*** 1930s German expeditions

Five expeditions visited Nanga Parbat in 1934, 1937 (two), 1938,
and 1939. Expedition photographs survive in:
- Deutscher Alpenverein (DAV) archives, Munich
- Alpine Club archives, London
- Himalayan Club archives (some published in /Himalayan Journal/)

The 1934 and 1937 expedition photographs document the approach through
Diamer with 600+ local porters — images of the valley, villages, and
people from before modernisation. Specific archival locations not
confirmed online.

*** Hermann Buhl and the 1953 expedition

Hans Ertl (cameraman and mountaineer) filmed and directed /Nanga
Parbat 1953/, a 95-minute documentary of the first ascent. Ertl's
film is the primary visual record.

- IMDb: https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0163761/
- Filmarchiv Austria: https://info.filmarchiv.at/program/film/nanga-parbat/
- Internet Archive (Herrligkoffer's account): https://archive.org/details/dli.ernet.528144
- MNTNFILM: https://www.mntnfilm.com/en/film/nanga-parbat-1953-1953

Buhl's summit photographs (3 July 1953) — the only images from the

*** Natural soundscapes — gap

No dedicated field recordings of natural soundscapes from the Diamer/
Nanga Parbat region (river, glacier, wind at altitude) were identified
in this search. This is a gap. The acoustic environment of the Indus
gorge — the roar of meltwater through the deepest gorge on Earth — is
undocumented in any audio archive we could find. The alarm whistle of
marmots across alpine meadows, the crack of glacier ice, the wind on
the Rupal Face — all unrecorded, or at least not publicly accessible.
