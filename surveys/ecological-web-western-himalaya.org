* Scope and Method

This survey maps the ecological web of the Western Himalayan monsoon
valleys — the relationships between tectonic forces, altitude zonation,
species behaviour, spirit traditions, and human practice across five
story regions: Tirthan, Parvati, Baspa (Kinnaur), Spiti/Lahaul, and
the Sutlej corridor.

The survey complements the Diamer/Nanga Parbat surveys (WP-0012,
WP-0013), which documented the same chains for the Karakoram. The
monsoon valleys offer a contrast: where Diamer gets 200–300 mm/yr and
supports juniper steppe, the Tirthan gets 1200–1500 mm/yr and supports
cathedral deodar forest. The monsoon boundary — where one world becomes
the other — runs through the story geography.

* Part A: Altitude Zonation Chains — Monsoon Valleys of the Western Himalaya

The five regions form a transect from full monsoon influence (Tirthan)
through the transition zone (Baspa, Sutlej corridor) to the rain shadow
(Spiti/Lahaul). This transect is the organising principle: what happens
to altitude zonation as the monsoon weakens?

** A.1 Tirthan Valley

*** A.1.1 Geology

The Tirthan rises in the Tirath valley within GHNP and joins the Beas
at Larji (~900 m). The valley cuts through the Great Himalayan Range,
exposing rocks of the Higher Himalayan Crystalline (HHC) — predominantly
medium- to high-grade metamorphic rocks: mica schists, gneisses, and
migmatites. [training-knowledge]

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------+-------|
| Dominant rock | Mica schist, paragneiss (HHC) |
| Tectonic context | Main Central Thrust (MCT) zone; the MCT passes near the lower valley |
| Metamorphic grade | Amphibolite facies (kyanite-sillimanite grade) |
| Erosion rate | ~1–3 mm/yr (typical for monsoon-exposed Greater Himalaya) [training-knowledge] |

The MCT emplaces high-grade metamorphic rocks of the Higher Himalaya
over the lower-grade rocks of the Lesser Himalaya. The Tirthan valley
crosses this boundary. Below the MCT (downstream toward Larji): Lesser
Himalayan phyllites, slates, quartzites. Above it (upstream into GHNP):
mica schists and gneisses. [training-knowledge]

*** A.1.2 Altitude Zonation

The Tirthan spans ~900 m (Larji) to ~5800 m (peaks within GHNP). The
zonation closely mirrors the Parvati valley [cross-ref:
parbati-biodiversity.org], as both are GHNP catchments with the same
aspect, precipitation regime, and lithological substrate.

| Zone | Elevation | Dominant vegetation | Aspect shift |
|------+-----------+---------------------+--------------|
| Subtropical | 900–1800 m | Chir pine (/Pinus roxburghii/), scattered sal at lowest points. /Lantana/ and /Ageratina/ invasion below 2000 m. | S-facing: chir pine extends to ~1900 m. N-facing: transition to temperate at ~1600 m. |
| Moist Temperate | 1800–2700 m | Deodar cedar (/Cedrus deodara/), blue pine (/Pinus wallichiana/), ban oak (/Quercus leucotrichophora/), moru oak (/Q. floribunda/). Horse chestnut, maple, walnut in ravines. | S-facing: deodar thins by 2500 m. N-facing: dense, tall deodar persisting to 2700 m with epiphytic mosses. |
| Upper Temperate | 2700–3200 m | W. Himalayan spruce (/Picea smithiana/), Himalayan fir (/Abies pindrow/), kharsu oak (/Q. semecarpifolia/). Dense ringal bamboo (/Arundinaria/ spp.) understory. | N-facing ravines: fir dominates, dense and dark. S-facing: spruce-oak mosaic, more open. |
| Subalpine | 3200–3600 m | Himalayan birch (/Betula utilis/), /Rhododendron campanulatum/, /R. anthopogon/, juniper (/Juniperus/ spp.). Krummholz. | Treeline: S-facing to ~3800 m, N-facing to ~3400 m [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org]. |
| Alpine Meadow | 3600–4500 m | Wildflower meadows: /Primula/, /Potentilla/, /Anemone/, /Gentiana/, /Meconopsis/ (blue poppy), /Pedicularis/. Dwarf junipers, dwarf rhododendrons. | S-facing: drier, sparser cover. N-facing and moist hollows: denser meadow. |
| Nival | 4500–5800 m | Lichen-moss pioneer communities, cushion plants, saxifrages. Above ~5000 m: permanent snow/ice. | Permanent snowline: ~5000 m, shifting upward. |

/What makes Tirthan distinctive/: The upper Tirthan (above Ropa, into
the Tirath valley) is roadless — the most pristine of the GHNP
catchments. No hydropower development (unlike the Parvati, which has
the 800 MW Parbati-II HEP). The lower valley has more intact continuous
forest than the equivalent elevation in the Parvati. This makes the
Tirthan the best available reference for the "undisturbed" monsoon
valley zonation. [training-knowledge]

who fought Ravana to rescue Sita. In the Kullu valley — Raghunath Ji's

Himalayan griffon, massive and ruff-necked, is the most visually

**** Golden Eagle (/Aquila chrysaetos/)

| Language | Name | Source |
|----------+------+--------|
| Hindi | /Sōnā ukaab/ (golden eagle) | [training-knowledge] |
| Pahari (Kullu) | /Bāj/ or /Bājū/ (generic for large eagle) | [needs-verification] |

Ranges across alpine meadows and ridgelines above 3000 m. Takes

*** A.1.5 Monsoon Character

The Tirthan is fully within the monsoon domain. Annual precipitation
~1200–1500 mm, 60–70% falling June–September. The contrast with the
Karakoram [cross-ref: diamer-nanga-parbat.org] is total: where Diamer
gets 200–300 mm/yr and supports only scattered juniper, the Tirthan
gets 1200–1500 mm/yr and supports cathedral deodar forest.

** A.2 Parvati Valley

*** A.2.1 Geology

The Parvati flows from the Man Talai glacier to the Beas confluence at
Bhuntar (~1100 m). Like the Tirthan, it cuts through HHC rocks, but
with a notable addition: the hot springs at Manikaran (~95°C) and
Kheerganga (~50°C) indicate active geothermal systems.
[cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org]

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------+-------|
| Dominant rock | Mica schist, paragneiss (HHC); quartzite bands |
| Tectonic context | MCT zone in lower valley; Parvati thrust in mid-valley |
| Hot springs | Manikaran (~95°C), Kheerganga (~50°C); sulphurous, silica-depositing |
| Erosion rate | ~2–4 mm/yr (high: monsoon + steep gradient + active tectonics) [training-knowledge] |

/Geothermal comparison with Nanga Parbat/: Manikaran's hot springs echo
the Tato springs near Nanga Parbat (~92°C) but arise from a different
mechanism. At Nanga Parbat: decompression melting driving fluids through
the Raikot fault (tectonic aneurysm). At Manikaran: deep circulation
of meteoric water along active fault zones in the MCT system.
[training-knowledge; cross-ref: diamer-nanga-parbat.org § A.5]

*** A.2.2 Altitude Zonation

Comprehensively documented in parbati-biodiversity.org. Summarised
here for completeness:

| Zone | Elevation | Dominant vegetation | Notes |
|------+-----------+---------------------+-------|
| Subtropical | 1000–1800 m | Chir pine; scattered sal. /Lantana/, /Ageratina/ invasion. | Kasol (1640 m), Manikaran (1760 m) in transition zone [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org] |
| Moist Temperate | 1800–2700 m | Deodar cedar, blue pine, Himalayan oak. Maple, horse chestnut, alder near streams. | Cathedral forests. Epiphytes on old-growth trunks [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org] |
| Upper Temperate | 2700–3200 m | W. Himalayan spruce, Himalayan fir, brown oak. Dense ringal bamboo. | Western tragopan's kingdom — 6.5 birds/km² [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org] |
| Subalpine | 3200–3600 m | Himalayan birch, /R. campanulatum/, /R. anthopogon/, junipers. | Krummholz transition [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org] |
| Treeline | 3500–3800 m | /Betula utilis/ with /R. campanulatum/. | S-facing: 3800 m. N-facing: 3400 m. Without grazing: 200–400 m higher [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org] |
| Alpine Meadow | 3600–4500 m | Wildflower seas. /Primula/, /Meconopsis/, /Gentiana/, /Pedicularis/. | Over half of GHNP lies above 4000 m [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org] |
| Nival | 4500–6632 m | Pioneer lichen-moss. Cushion plants. 192 lichen species. | Permanent snowline ~5000 m [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org] |

/What distinguishes the Parvati from the Tirthan/: (1) Greater human
impact — the Parbati-II HEP (800 MW), road development, tourism at
Kasol/Kheerganga. (2) Geothermal features creating microclimatic
anomalies. (3) The Pin Parvati Pass (5319 m) — the ecological hinge
between monsoon and rain shadow. [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org]

*** A.2.3 Keystone Species

Identical to the Tirthan at the zone level [cross-ref:
parbati-biodiversity.org § Fauna], with Parvati-specific notes:

- *Snow leopard*: Only 1 detection in GHNP camera traps. At the
  periphery of range. The Pin Parvati Pass corridor potentially
  connects wet-side and dry-side populations.
  [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org]
- *Himalayan brown bear*: Present above 3500 m. 130–220 individuals
  across all of India and Pakistan. Critically Endangered in India.
  [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org]
- *Musk deer*: Endangered. Poached for the musk pod (worth more per
  gram than gold). The economic keystone of poaching pressure.
  [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org]

*** A.2.4 Monsoon Character

Full monsoon. ~1000–1500 mm. The Pin Parvati Pass marks the /monsoon
terminus/ for this valley system: on the Parvati side, 1000+ mm; on the
Spiti side, 200 mm. The pass is where the monsoon runs out of energy —
the most dramatic single-step moisture gradient in the Western Himalaya.
[training-knowledge]

** A.3 Baspa Valley (Sangla, Kinnaur)

*** A.3.1 Geology

The Baspa is a tributary of the Sutlej, joining it near Karcham. The
valley lies within the HHC, but with a crucial difference from the
Kullu valleys: its position further east and deeper within the
Himalayan arc exposes it to a more continental climate. Chitkul
(3450 m), the last village, sits near the Indo-Tibetan border.
[training-knowledge]

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------+-------|
| Dominant rock | Paragneiss, migmatite, amphibolite (HHC); Haimanta Group metasediments |
| Tectonic context | Between the MCT and the South Tibetan Detachment System (STDS). Leucogranite intrusions locally |
| Special features | Kinnaur Kailash range (6050 m) forms the eastern wall |
| Erosion rate | ~1–2 mm/yr (lower than Kullu — less monsoon, less fluvial erosion) [training-knowledge] |

The STDS brings Tethyan sedimentary rocks (fossiliferous marine
sediments of the Tethys Sea) into proximity with the high-grade
metamorphic basement. The transition from gneiss (valley floor) to
marine limestone (upper ridgeline) mirrors the deep-time story
documented for Nanga Parbat [cross-ref: diamer-deep-time.org § A.1] —
ocean floor lifted to mountain summit. [training-knowledge]

*** A.3.2 Altitude Zonation

The Baspa is the *transition zone* between full monsoon and rain shadow.
Precipitation is lower than Kullu (~600–800 mm/yr at Sangla, declining
upvalley), and the vegetation reflects this.

| Zone | Elevation | Dominant vegetation | Contrast with Kullu |
|------+-----------+---------------------+---------------------|
| Subtropical–Warm Temperate | 1500–2200 m | Chir pine, sparser than Kullu. Dry-deciduous elements (wild apricot, /Celtis/). | No /Lantana/ — too dry, too cold |
| Temperate | 2200–3000 m | Deodar cedar, blue pine, Himalayan oak — deodar less dominant. *Chilgoza pine* (/Pinus gerardiana/) appears on south-facing slopes. Apple and apricot orchards. | Chilgoza = key difference. Absent from Kullu. Marks the continental transition |
| Upper Temperate | 3000–3400 m | W. Himalayan spruce, Himalayan fir. Brown oak. Ringal bamboo thinner than Kullu. | Less bamboo, more open forest — lower moisture |
| Subalpine | 3400–3800 m | Himalayan birch, /R. campanulatum/, juniper. Birch more extensive than Kullu. | Treeline higher (S-facing to ~4000 m) — less cloud cover, more radiation |
| Alpine | 3800–4800 m | Alpine steppe elements: /Caragana/, /Artemisia/, /Astragalus/ alongside typical meadow species. | Steppe species mark continental transition. Absent from Kullu |
| Nival | 4800+ m | Cushion plants, saxifrages, lichen. Kinnaur Kailash (6050 m). | Higher snowline (~5200 m vs ~5000 m) — less precipitation |

**** The Chilgoza Pine (/Pinus gerardiana/)

The appearance of chilgoza pine is the single best ecological marker of
the transition from monsoon to continental climate. Named after Captain
Alexander Gerard, who explored Kinnaur in the 1810s–1820s. Three-needled
pine with large edible seeds. Distribution: Afghanistan (Nuristan),
Pakistan (Chitral, Dir, Swat, Waziristan), India (Kinnaur — the heart
of the Indian range). Absent from the monsoon-drenched Kullu valleys.
[training-knowledge]

Kinnaur contains the largest and most productive chilgoza forests in
India, principally in the Sutlej valley between Nichar and Pooh (~80 km).
Individual families hold customary rights to specific trees — one of
the most sophisticated traditional tree-tenure systems in the Himalaya.
Harvest: September–October. Cones hand-collected by climbing. Premium
prices, INR 2,000–4,000/kg retail (shelled). [training-knowledge]

*** A.3.3 Keystone Species

| Zone | Keystone species | Notes |
|------+------------------+-------|
| Temperate | Chilgoza pine | Continental indicator. Sacred grove management |
| Upper Temperate | Western tragopan | Eastern limit of range near the Sutlej. Presence in Baspa uncertain [needs-verification] |
| Alpine | Blue sheep (/Pseudois nayaur/) | Present on the drier, Tibetan-aspect slopes above Chitkul. Absent from Kullu. *The blue sheep signal*: a Tibetan Plateau species whose presence marks the penetration of trans-Himalayan ecology into the monsoon valleys |
| Alpine | Snow leopard | More likely than in Kullu — drier upper reaches and blue sheep create better habitat [training-knowledge] |

*** A.3.4 Monsoon Character

*Transitional.* ~600–800 mm at Sangla — roughly half of Kullu. By
Chitkul: ~400–500 mm. The monsoon still arrives, but attenuated. The
apple orchards exist in a precipitation sweet spot: enough rain for
fruit trees, not so much as to promote fungal disease. This is why
Kinnaur apples are famous and Kullu apples struggle with scab.
[training-knowledge]

The monsoon boundary is visible as a shift in forest character: lower
valley (below Sangla) has dense deodar reminiscent of Kullu; upper
valley (above Chitkul) grades into dry juniper steppe reminiscent of
Spiti. The transition happens within 30 km of valley length.
[training-knowledge]

** A.4 Spiti and Lahaul

*** A.4.1 Geology

***** Spiti

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------+-------|

| Tectonic context | Between the STDS and the Indus-Tsangpo Suture Zone. The Spiti synclinorium preserves the most complete Tethyan sequence in India |

*** A.4.2 Altitude Zonation

The zonation in Spiti is radically different from the monsoon valleys —
aridity replaces moisture as the controlling variable. This is "zonation
without monsoon" [cross-ref: diamer-nanga-parbat.org § F.5].

**** Spiti

| Zone | Elevation | Dominant vegetation | Contrast with monsoon valleys |
|------+-----------+---------------------+-------------------------------|
| Arid Valley Floor | 3200–3800 m | /Artemisia/, /Caragana/, /Ephedra/. Irrigated barley and pea. Willow and poplar (planted). | No natural forest — precipitation too low (~200 mm/yr) |
| Dry Steppe | 3800–4200 m | /Artemisia/, dry grassland, scattered /Juniperus/ and /Hippophae/ (sea buckthorn). | Juniper replaces deodar entirely. No ringal, no rhododendron — too dry |
| Alpine Dry Meadow | 4200–4800 m | Sparse /Carex/, /Kobresia/ sedge. /Saussurea/, /Oxytropis/. Blue sheep grazing grounds. | Not the wildflower seas of the monsoon alpine. Drier, sparser, different species |
| Subnival | 4800–5500 m | Cushion plants, lichens, scattered /Thylacospermum caespitosum/. | Extends lower than in monsoon valleys — cold desert |
| Nival | 5500+ m | Ice, bare rock. | Snowline at ~5500–5800 m — significantly higher than monsoon valleys (~5000 m) |

The *absence* of the moist temperate zone (1800–2700 m) is the
defining difference. Spiti's valley floor sits at 3200–3800 m — already
above the temperate zone elevation — and receives no monsoon moisture.

**** Lahaul

Lahaul shows the transition:

| Zone | Elevation | Dominant vegetation | Notes |
|------+-----------+---------------------+-------|
| Dry Temperate (S. Lahaul) | 2800–3500 m | /Cedrus deodara/ (sparse), /Pinus wallichiana/, /Juniperus/, willow, poplar. Deodar at its dry limit. | The last deodar before the rain shadow |
| Birch-Juniper | 3500–4000 m | /Betula utilis/, /Juniperus/ spp., /Salix/ spp. | Birch more robust than in Spiti — more moisture |
| Alpine | 4000–4800 m | Mixed: monsoon-type meadow species and steppe species coexist. /Primula/ and /Artemisia/ side by side. | The ecological frontier — both floras meet |

/The Rohtang divide/: On the south (Kullu) side, green forested slopes
receiving 1200+ mm. On the north (Lahaul) side, bare brown slopes
receiving 300–400 mm. Visible in a single day's drive — the most
dramatic demonstration of the rain shadow in the Indian Himalaya.
[training-knowledge]

**** Sky Burial and the Griffon

In the Spitian Buddhist tradition (Gelugpa, continuous with Tibetan
Buddhism since the 10th century — Tabo monastery founded 996 CE), the
Himalayan griffon is associated with /jhator/ (sky burial). The griffon
is not merely a scavenger — it is the vehicle of the soul's transition.
[training-knowledge]

The ḍākinī (/mkha' 'gro ma/, "sky-goer") shares the sky with the
griffon. In the Spitian Buddhist worldview, the sky is a domain of
spiritual agency. The griffon circling on thermals above Tabo or Ki
monastery is both ecological reality and spiritual presence.
[training-knowledge]

This connects to but differs from the Karakoram peri tradition
[cross-ref: diamer-nanga-parbat.org § B.1]: the peri inhabit meadows
and glaciers (terrestrial spirits); the ḍākinī inhabit the sky itself
(aerial spirits). Both occupy the zone above the treeline.

** A.5 Sutlej Corridor

*** A.5.1 Geology

The Sutlej is one of the great *antecedent* rivers of the Himalaya —
rivers that established their courses before the orogeny reached its
present height, maintaining their channels by cutting down as fast as
the mountains rose (Medlicott, 1864). The Sutlej rises on the Tibetan
Plateau near Mount Kailash and Lake Rakshastal (~4,600 m) and cuts
through the /entire width/ of the Himalaya. [training-knowledge]

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------+-------|
| Tectonic zones crossed | Sub-Himalaya (Siwalik) → Lesser Himalaya → MCT → HHC → STDS → Tethyan Himalaya — the entire Himalayan tectonic stack |
| Gorge depth | ~4,500 m local relief (river at ~1,500 m near Karcham; Kinnaur Kailash 6,050 m rises 20 km east) |
| Incision rate | Extreme — ~5–10 mm/yr locally [training-knowledge] |

The 2000 Parechu flood: landslide in Tibet dammed the Parechu River;
breach sent ~1,500 cumecs into the NJHEP infrastructure, causing severe
siltation. Demonstrated transboundary vulnerability — the lake was in
Chinese-controlled territory with no early warning.
[training-knowledge]

*** A.5.2 Altitude Zonation

The Sutlej corridor is unique: moving upstream from Rampur (1000 m) to
Nako (~3662 m), you transition from full monsoon to near-desert within
200 km. The corridor is a *gradient in real time*.

| Zone | Elevation | Location markers | Vegetation |
|------+-----------+------------------+------------|
| Subtropical | 800–1800 m | Rampur Bushahr | Chir pine, sal at lowest. Citrus orchards |
| Warm-Moist Temperate | 1800–2500 m | Sarahan, Jeori | Deodar, blue pine, oak. Bhimakali temple at Sarahan sits in deodar forest |
| Cool-Dry Temperate | 2500–3200 m | Kalpa (2960 m), Reckong Peo | Deodar thins. *Chilgoza pine* appears (S-facing). Apple orchards — Kinnaur apple country |
| Subalpine-Dry | 3200–3800 m | Nako, upper Sangla road | Birch, juniper. /Caragana/. Visibly drier than equivalent elevation in Kullu |
| Alpine Steppe | 3800–4500 m | Above Nako, toward Sumdo | /Artemisia/, /Ephedra/, /Caragana/. Blue sheep habitat. Converges with Spiti ecology |
| Nival | 4500+ m | Kinnaur Kailash (6050 m), Reo Purgyil (6816 m) | As Spiti |

/The Kalpa viewpoint/: From Kalpa (2960 m), the entire altitude
zonation from temperate forest to permanent snow is visible in a single
view — a 3000 m vertical transect compressed into ~10 km. South-facing
slopes show apple orchards and chilgoza; north-facing show dense deodar
and fir. [training-knowledge]

*** A.5.3 The Monsoon Wall

The Sutlej corridor shows the monsoon gradient /within a single valley/:

| Location | Elevation | Approx. precip (mm/yr) | Character |
|----------+-----------+------------------------+-----------|
| Rampur | 1000 m | ~1000–1200 | Full monsoon |
| Sarahan | 2100 m | ~800–1000 | Strong monsoon |
| Kalpa | 2960 m | ~500–700 | Weakening. Chilgoza appears |
| Nako | 3662 m | ~300–400 | Marginal. Steppe elements |
| Tabo | 3280 m | ~170–200 | Rain shadow. Cold desert. Continuous with Spiti |

The precipitation gradient — 1200 mm at Rampur to 200 mm at Tabo —
occurs within ~200 km of road. The corridor is a natural laboratory for
studying how zonation responds to declining moisture.
[training-knowledge]

Bookhagen & Burbank (2006, 2010) showed two precipitation maxima using
TRMM satellite data: (1) the Himalayan front (~1,000–2,000 m) and (2)
the MCT zone / Greater Himalaya front (~3,000–4,000 m). The rain shadow
begins /abruptly/ behind the Greater Himalaya crest. The "wall" is the
Greater Himalaya itself — in the Sutlej, the Tapri–Reckong Peo zone
where the river crosses the HHC and STDS. [training-knowledge]

/The tectonic-climatic coincidence/: the geological boundary
(MCT → HHC → STDS) and the climatic boundary (monsoon → rain shadow)
occur at approximately the same position. Not accidental: the MCT zone
is the structural front of the Higher Himalaya — the topographic barrier
that blocks the monsoon. The ecological zonation is a surface expression
of plate tectonics. [training-knowledge]

*** A.7.3 The Single Variable

The altitude zonation chains documented in this part are controlled by
one variable above all others: *precipitation*. Geology varies. Latitude
barely changes (31°N–33°N). Altitude range is similar. But precipitation
varies by a factor of 7.

This single variable determines whether deodar or juniper dominates,
whether the treeline is at 3500 m or 4200 m, whether the alpine zone is
wildflower meadow or dry steppe, whether the primary herbivore is tahr
or ibex, whether the human economy is rice or barley, whether the
spiritual tradition is devta-and-gur or Buddhist-and-lama.

The chain: *tectonic uplift → orographic barrier → monsoon interception
→ precipitation gradient → vegetation zonation → faunal assemblage →
human adaptation → spiritual tradition*.

The monsoon boundary is where the chain bifurcates. The five story
regions sit astride that boundary.

** B.1 Nag Devtas and Rivers

*** The river-nag correspondence

Every significant river and spring in the Kullu system carries a nag
devta association. The nag is the spirit /of/ the water body — its
temperament, its seasonal behaviour, its capacity to nourish or destroy.
[cross-ref: spirit-ecology-verified.org § 1.1]

- Nags are "aboriginal snake gods believed to be the spirits of springs
  and rivers" [cross-ref: spirit-ecology-verified.org, fn:ignca]
- Basu Nag (Vasuki) is the ancestor of all nagas in the valley, temple
  at Kamarhati in Kothi Naggar
- Nagas "regulate water — they bring rain and control floods"
- Offending a nag causes skin disease, crop failure, or drought

*** River-specific associations

| Sutlej | Nag worship along corridor in Kinnaur; transition from Hindu nag to Buddhist klu with altitude | Sutlej [training-knowledge] |

*** Buddhi Nagin and Serolsar Lake

Buddhi Nagin (the Old Serpent Mother) at Serolsar Lake above Jalori Pass
(~3,100 m). She /is/ the lake. The lake's water level, clarity, and
seasonal behaviour are read as signs of the nag's disposition. The Jalori
Pass marks a jurisdictional boundary: below to the south lies the
Tirthan/Banjar valley under Shringa Rishi's authority; the pass itself
is where Buddhi Nagin's domain begins. [cross-ref: kullu-devta.yaml;
spirit-ecology.org § 2.5; training-knowledge]

*** What the nag "knows" about the river

The nag devta tradition encodes specific hydrological knowledge:

1. *Spring protection*: Taboos against polluting springs, cutting trees
   near springs, or diverting water without propitiation function as
   watershed protection. The tree-spring connection is ecologically real —
   root systems stabilise catchments, canopy reduces evaporation.
2. *Seasonal flooding*: The community understands the nag's mood shifts
   with the monsoon. Heavy rain years = the nag is agitated; drought =
   the nag withholds.
3. *Hot springs as liminal spaces*: Manikaran (Parvati) — where
   underground and surface water worlds intersect. The nag's deep domain
   leaking into the human world. The geological reality (geothermal from
   tectonic activity) and the spiritual reading converge.
   [cross-ref: diamer-nanga-parbat.org § A.5 for geological parallel]
4. *The kuhl system*: Kuhls are traditional gravity-fed irrigation
   channels. The nag devta's authority extends to water allocation —
   disputes brought before the devta via the gur. [training-knowledge;
   needs-verification: specific ethnographic sources linking gur
   pronouncements to kuhl allocation]

*** The nag-klu transition across the monsoon boundary

| Region | Term | Water Context | Urgency |
|--------+------+---------------+---------|
| Kullu (monsoon) | Nag devta | Abundant water; springs, rivers, monsoon | Moderate — water present but must be kept clean |
| Kinnaur (transitional) | Nag/Nages + Klu (upper) | Decreasing monsoon; apple orchards depend on snowmelt | Increasing — water less reliable |
| Spiti (rain shadow) | Klu/lu | Arid high desert; <200 mm; water = survival | Existential — klu traditions encode water rationing |
| Lahaul (rain shadow) | Klu/lu + Hindu nag | Transitional; both traditions coexist | High — short growing season, glacial melt critical |

[cross-ref: spirit-ecology.org § 2.2; spiti-klu.yaml; kinnaur-devta.yaml]

The gradient: /the scarcer the water, the more elaborate and urgent the
serpent-spirit traditions/. In Spiti at 4,000 m with 170 mm rainfall,
the klu traditions are "functionally a water management system"
[cross-ref: spiti-klu.yaml]. In Kullu with 1,200 mm, the nag traditions
are important but not existential.

** B.2 Ibex, Markhor, and Petroglyphs: Does the Cult Cross the Monsoon Divide?

*** The Karakoram ibex cult

~30,000 petroglyphs on 100 km of the Indus, with ibex as the dominant
motif. Ibex horns on graves and shrines as late as the 1980s in Hunza.
The ibex in Kalasha cosmology belongs to /onjesta/ (pure) category.
Continuity from ~3rd millennium BCE to the present.
[cross-ref: diamer-nanga-parbat.org §§ F.1, H.2]

The markhor adds a layer: its name means "snake-eater" (Persian /mar/ +
/khor/), positioning it as antagonist to the nag/klu serpent tradition.
[cross-ref: diamer-nanga-parbat.org § F.1]

*** Does the ibex cult extend into the monsoon valleys?

*No, or only as a faint echo.* The ibex cult is Karakoram/Dardic-specific.
Converging factors:

1. *Biogeographic*: Himalayan ibex absent or rare at lower elevations in
   Kullu. The dominant ungulate is tahr and goral, not ibex.
2. *Cultural*: The ibex cult is tied to Dardic religion (Shina, Khowar,
   Kalasha speakers). The Kullu devta system is Pahari-Hindu, with a
   different symbolic vocabulary.
3. *No petroglyphs*: Different geology (schist/slate, not exposed hard
   gneiss), denser forest (hiding rock surfaces), different cultural
   tradition. [training-knowledge]
4. *Markhor range*: Does not extend into the Kullu or Parvati valleys.
   The markhor is a Karakoram-Indus species.
   [cross-ref: diamer-nanga-parbat.org § F.1]

*** Kinnaur and Spiti: a partial exception?

Ibex are present in Spiti (Pin Valley). Some rock art documented in the
Spiti valley (Francke, /Antiquities of Indian Tibet/, 1914–1926). Whether
ibex motifs appear requires specific archaeological survey.
[needs-verification]

The Buddhist overlay (Padmasambhava's "taming" of local spirits) would
have incorporated or suppressed any pre-Buddhist animal cult.
[cross-ref: spirit-ecology-verified.org § 2.4]

/The monsoon divide hypothesis/: The ibex cult maps to arid alpine
ecology where ibex is the dominant visible large animal. The devta cult
maps to monsoon-fed forest ecology where water and forest are the primary
concern. The divide is ecological before it is cultural.
[training-knowledge]

** B.5 The Ecological Chain: Spirit Traditions as Ecological Knowledge

*** Devtas as altitude-zone managers

| Zone | Altitude | Hindu-dominant | Buddhist/Tibetan-dominant |
|------+----------+----------------+--------------------------|
| Agricultural | 1500–2500 m | Devta (village deity) | Lha (territorial deity) |
| Forest belt | 2500–3500 m | Nag, sacred groves | Gnyan (tree/rock spirits) |

| Trans-Himalayan | 3500–5000 m | — | Lha, klu (water critical) |

[cross-ref: spirit-ecology.org § 2.1]

Valley-floor spirits are /civic/ (managing communities); mid-altitude
spirits are /ecological/ (managing forests and water); high-altitude
spirits are /elemental/ (managing weather, glaciers, passes).

*** Nag devtas and watershed hydrology

The nag tradition produces outcomes functionally equivalent to
hydrological management:

1. *Source protection*: Every spring with a nag shrine has an exclusion
   zone — no cutting, no construction, no pollution. Protects recharge.
2. *Drainage-basin jurisdiction*: Devta territories "follow drainage
   basins" at high correspondence [cross-ref: spirit-ecology.org]. The
   devta literally /governs a catchment/.
3. *Water allocation*: Disputes over kuhl water brought to the devta.
   The gur's pronouncement = a water allocation decision with social
   legitimacy. [training-knowledge]
4. *Flood/drought response*: Propitiation is also a crisis-response
   protocol — community gathering, gur trance, collective
   decision-making. [training-knowledge]

*** The spirit system as ecological memory

The deepest function is /memory/:

- *Taboos*: "Do not cut trees near springs" = "this spring's recharge
  area includes those trees"
- *Stories*: "The nag was angry and the spring went dry" = "deforestation
  caused spring failure"
- *Rituals*: Annual propitiation = annual attention to water source health
- *Gur pronouncements*: Aggregate community observation into
  authoritative guidance, updated annually

The spirit system is a distributed ecological database with a community
consensus protocol (the gur trance) and enforcement mechanism (fear of
spiritual retribution).

*** Current treeline dynamics

Three forces in tension [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org]:

1. *Climate warming* pushes potential treeline upward (~0.3°C/decade
   above 2000 m since the 1970s) [training-knowledge, needs-verification]
2. *Grazing pressure* suppresses actual treeline 200–400 m below its
   climatic potential [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org]
3. *Fire* maintains chir pine at lower elevations at the expense of
   broadleaf species [training-knowledge]

Net effect: the treeline is moving upward, slowly and unevenly. Where
GHNP has excluded grazing for 40 years, birch seedlings appear above the
current treeline — a natural experiment.

*** Invasive species at the lower boundary

[cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org]

- /Lantana camara/: spreading below 2000 m; projected to gain habitat
  by 2070
- /Ageratina adenophora/ ("Forest Killer"): native species decline
  29–40% in invaded sites

The "squeeze" on the forest: treeline advancing from above (slowly),
invasives encroaching from below (faster). [training-knowledge]

** C.2 Species Range Changes

*** Himalayan brown bear

IUCN Least Concern globally but Critically Endangered in India.
Estimated 130–220 individuals across India/Pakistan combined. In HP:
confirmed in GHNP upper catchments, Pin Valley NP. Very low numbers.
Drivers: habitat loss, human-bear conflict, reduced connectivity.
[cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org; training-knowledge]

*** Snow leopard

- India-wide: 718 individuals (SPAI 2024)
  [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org]
- HP: 83 individuals (2025)
- GHNP: 1 detection — marginal habitat
- Spiti: core habitat, stable under protection
- Pin Parvati corridor: potential but unconfirmed connectivity
  [cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org]

*** Western tragopan

Global population: <5,000. GHNP holds the world's largest known
population at 6.5 birds/km². Habitat: moist temperate forest 2400–3600 m
with dense ringal bamboo. GHNP is arguably the species' most secure
population globally. Within GHNP: stable to improving. Outside: declining.
[cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org; training-knowledge]

*** Musk deer

Endangered (IUCN). Males carry musk pods worth more than gold by weight.
Snaring remains the primary poaching method — indiscriminate. In
GHNP: present, anti-poaching patrols have reduced but not eliminated
snaring. [training-knowledge]

*** Himalayan vulture populations — the diclofenac crash

Three lowland /Gyps/ species suffered >97–99.9% decline from veterinary
diclofenac (identified 2003–2004). The drug caused acute renal failure.
India banned it for veterinary use in 2006. [training-knowledge]

The *Himalayan griffon* was less severely affected — its high-altitude
range and wild ungulate prey meant less exposure. The *lammergeier* was
not affected (bone diet, not soft tissue). [cross-ref:
diamer-nanga-parbat.org §§ F.2]

Cascading effects: feral dog population explosion, estimated 48,000
additional human rabies deaths, disruption of sky burial practice
(Spiti/Lahaul Buddhist communities). [training-knowledge]

"Run-of-river" disguises the actual impact: tunnels bypass kilometres of
natural channel. Diversion reaches run at 10–15% of natural flow. No

*** Glacial retreat

| Glacier | River | Retreat rate | Notes |
|---------+-------+--------------+-------|
| Bara Shigri | Chandra (Lahaul) | ~30–40 m/yr; accelerating since 1990s | Largest in HP. Retreated ~2.8 km since 1906 |

[training-knowledge]

Consequences are non-linear: short-term (increased meltwater),
medium-term ("peak water" — declining volume can no longer compensate),
long-term (severely reduced dry-season flow). [training-knowledge]

GLOF risk increasing as glacial retreat creates proglacial lakes. The
Sutlej is particularly vulnerable to transboundary GLOFs from Tibet.
[training-knowledge]

** C.4 Pastoral Migration Patterns

*** Gaddi transhumance

The Gaddis — semi-nomadic pastoralists based in Kangra, Chamba,
Dhauladhar foothills — conduct one of the great Himalayan migrations:
spring departure from lowlands (~800–1500 m), summer on alpine pastures
(3500–4500 m), autumn return before snow closes passes.
[training-knowledge]

| Aspect | Traditional (pre-1980s) | Current (2020s) | Driver |
|--------+------------------------+-----------------+--------|
| Flock size | 200–500+ sheep | Declining; 50–200 | Wool prices crashed with synthetics |
| Route availability | All passes open | GHNP restrictions since 1984 | Conservation policy |
| Timing | Consistent traditional dates | Shifting 1–3 weeks earlier | Earlier snowmelt |
| Younger generation | Sons followed fathers | Many pursuing education, tourism, government | Economic modernisation |
| Wool economy | Primary product; Kullu shawl industry | Kullu shawls now use Australian merino | Globalisation |

*** GHNP and pastoral conflict

GHNP (1984) displaced Gaddi grazing from traditional summer pastures
in upper Tirthan and Sainj. The irony: Gaddi grazing /maintains/ the
alpine meadows. Without it, meadows would transition to shrubland and
eventually forest. The "pristine" meadows are cultural landscapes.
[training-knowledge]

*** Kinnauri transhumance

Agro-pastoral, village-based. The apple economy has transformed
Kinnaur since the 1980s — far more profitable than pastoralism. Apple
cultivation is moving upward as lower elevations warm. [training-knowledge]

*** Spiti/Lahaul

Yak numbers declining as young people leave. The Atal Tunnel (2020) —
9.02 km under Rohtang, year-round access to Lahaul for the first
time — has accelerated cultural change. The traditional seasonal
rhythm (isolated winter, busy summer) is dissolving. [training-knowledge]

** C.5 The Broken Links

*** Vulture crash → carcass disposal → nutrient cycling

In Spiti/Lahaul (Buddhist): sky burial depends on griffons. Where
vultures are unavailable, bodies must be cremated (requiring scarce
firewood at altitude). In Hindu communities: carcasses persist, feeding
feral dogs. [training-knowledge]

*** Dams → blocked fish migration → river spirit traditions

Dam blocks upstream fish migration → snow trout cannot reach spawning
grounds → fish populations decline → river character changes → nag
devta presides over a diminished river. Some communities have resisted
hydropower on explicitly spiritual grounds — the dam insults the nag
devta. [training-knowledge; cross-ref: parbati-biodiversity.org]

*** Roads → fragmented corridors

| Road | Valley | Impact |
|------+--------+--------|
| NH-3 widening | Kullu/Beas | Continuous disturbance; fragments GHNP from western habitats |
| Parvati road (to Pulga) | Parvati | Built for Parbati-II. Opened roadless upper valley to traffic, tourism |
| Baspa road (to Chitkul) | Baspa | Opens Sangla valley. "Last village" now receives thousands of visitors |
| NH-5 / Hindustan-Tibet Highway | Sutlej | Chronic landslide zone. Widening for dam vehicles destabilises slopes |
| Atal Tunnel approach | Rohtang/Lahaul | Year-round access. Massive traffic increase |

*** Tourism pressure → waste, noise, displacement

| Location | Change | Consequence |
|----------+--------+-------------|
| Kasol | From obscure village to "Amsterdam of India" | Waste crisis. No sewage treatment. Garbage in Parvati river |
| Kheerganga | Hot springs became camping destination | HP High Court banned camping 2024, calling it a "landfill" |
| Chitkul | "Last village on Indo-Tibet border" tourist draw | Increasing traffic, waste, water pressure |
| Spiti (Kaza, Langza) | Post-Atal Tunnel tourism boom | Unprepared infrastructure. Waste decomposes extremely slowly at altitude |

*** The compounding effect

These links do not operate in isolation. Dams reduce flow /and/ block
fish /and/ generate traffic /and/ require roads that fragment habitat
/and/ those roads bring tourism /and/ tourism brings waste. Climate
warming advances treeline /and/ melts glaciers /and/ shifts species
ranges /and/ increases GLOF risk /and/ pushes apples uphill /and/
displaces pastoralists. Each individual impact might be survivable. The
compounding threatens the web.

** C.6 What Has Not Changed — Persistence and Resilience

Not all is loss:

- *GHNP works*. Forty years of protection have demonstrably improved
  habitat. Tragopan density is high. Treeline regeneration visible.
- *The devta tradition persists*. Nag devtas still worshipped. The gur
  still channels the devta's voice. Whatever the river's diminished
  physical state, its spiritual identity endures.
- *Gaddi migration continues*. Reduced, pressured, economically
  marginal — but the flocks still cross the Dhauladhar each spring.
- *Lammergeier persists*. Unaffected by diclofenac, the bearded vulture
  continues to soar and drop bones.
  [cross-ref: diamer-nanga-parbat.org §§ F.2]
- *The monsoon still arrives*. Timing and intensity shifting, but the
  mechanism persists. The rain-shadow boundary still shapes every
  ecological gradient.

* Part D: Cross-Reference with Diamer Surveys

The Diamer/Nanga Parbat surveys (WP-0012, WP-0013) documented the
ecological chain for the Karakoram. This survey documents the same chain
for the monsoon valleys. The contrast between them is itself an
ecological relationship — the monsoon divide creates two worlds from the
same tectonic material.

** D.1 Parallel Chains

| Link in chain | Monsoon valleys | Karakoram (Diamer) |
|---------------+-----------------+--------------------|
| Tectonic context | MCT/HHC/STDS — orderly stack | Western Syntaxis — tectonic aneurysm, 7 mm/yr uplift |
| Precipitation | 1000–1500 mm | 200–300 mm |
| Dominant tree | Deodar cedar (monsoon-requiring) | Juniper (drought-tolerant) |
| Forest character | Dense, multi-storey cathedral | Sparse, open, drought-stressed |
| Primary herbivore | Himalayan tahr (endemic, cliff-dwelling) | Ibex, markhor (Palaearctic, crag-dwelling) |
| Apex predator | Common leopard (mid-elevation); snow leopard (rare, peripheral) | Snow leopard (core habitat) |
| Scavenger chain | Griffon → lammergeier | Griffon → lammergeier (identical) |
| Sacred tree | Deodar (passive grove protection) | Juniper (active smoke ritual) |
| Water spirit | Nag devta (Hindu) | — (Dardic: peri, bitan) |
| Spirit economy | Devta system (gur as oracle) | Bitan-peri system (shaman as medium) |
| Petroglyph tradition | Absent | 30,000+ carvings; ibex dominant |
| Pastoral economy | Gaddi transhumance (sheep to bugiyāl) | Agro-pastoral (ibex/markhor as wild complement) |
| Contemporary threat | Hydropower + tourism + climate | Dam construction (Diamer-Basha) + KKH fragmentation |

** D.2 What the Contrast Reveals

The single variable that explains the ecological divergence is
precipitation. But the chains are not merely different — they are
/complementary/. The monsoon valleys and the Karakoram are two
expressions of the same Himalayan system:

- The *tahr* of the monsoon valleys and the *ibex* of the Karakoram
  occupy the same ecological niche (large cliff-dwelling herbivore)
  in different precipitation regimes
- The *deodar grove* and the *juniper smoke* serve the same spiritual
  function (connecting humans to the sacred through trees) with
  different mechanisms (passive preservation vs active consumption)
- The *nag devta* and the *klu* are the same water spirit in different
  languages and urgency levels
- The *Gaddi* and the *Karakoram pastoral* economies are both
  transhumant adaptations to altitude gradients, with the monsoon
  determining whether the gradient is forest-to-meadow or
  steppe-to-rock

The monsoon divide does not separate two unrelated worlds — it refracts
a single system into two spectra.
