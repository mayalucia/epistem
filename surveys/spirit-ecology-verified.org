* Part 1: Regional Spirit Taxonomies (Verified)

** 1.1 Kullu Valley --- The Living Devta System

*** The Devta Institution

Kullu is called the "Valley of Gods" (Dev Bhumi). A reference book
compiled by the Kullu district administration documents *534* deities
("live" gods and goddesses) in the valley.[fn:kullu534] Earlier
estimates place the count at over 250 participating in the Dussehra
festival,[fn:sahapedia] suggesting the 534 figure includes minor and
inactive devtas.

Every village and hamlet has its own deity --- a devi or devta. Each
devta is a juridical entity: it owns land, maintains a temple estate,
and governs community affairs through an institutional apparatus.
[fn:kulludussehra]

The devta committee (dev samiti) comprises:[fn:kullu534]
- *Kardar* --- manager of the temple and its estates
- *Gur* --- the oracle/medium through whom the devta speaks
- *Pujari* --- the priest who conducts formal worship
- *Bajantri* --- musicians who accompany the devta's processions

*** Hierarchy and Paramount Devta

At the apex stands *Raghunath Ji* (Lord Rama). His idol was brought
from Ayodhya circa 1651 CE during the reign of Raja Jagat Singh.
[fn:sahapedia] All other devtas are nominally subordinate to
Raghunath Ji. This hierarchy is enacted annually at the Kullu Dussehra
festival, when over 250--300 deities arrive in palanquins to pay
obeisance.[fn:instahimachal]

*** The Gur: Oracle and Medium

The gur (also spelled goor) is the central shamanic institution of the
Kullu system. Gods and goddesses communicate with the populace via an
oracle system of shamanic nature: the devic spirit invokes/possesses
the gur, who then converses with subjects to resolve grievances or
bestow blessings.[fn:mysterioushimachal_gur]

The process involves intense drumming, chanting, and invocation. Once
in trance, the devta speaks through the gur to diagnose afflictions,
prescribe remedies, and deliver judgments on community disputes.
Through their gurs, devis/devtas relay views about prevailing social,
ecological, cultural, or regional unrest and guide the community
toward course-corrections.[fn:mysterioushimachal_gur]

The gur is considered "more truthful than the kardars" in conveying
the devta's will.[fn:sahapedia]

*** Nag Devta: Serpent Deities and Water Spirits

The nagas are aboriginal snake gods believed to be the spirits of
springs and rivers. They are among the most numerous deity class in
Kullu, believed to have descended from Basu Nag (Vasuki), the father
of all nagas, whose temple is at Kamarhati in Kothi Naggar.[fn:ignca]

Nagas regulate water --- they bring rain and control floods. Their
worship reflects the belief that these demigods rule over the land as
owners and take care of the people and their possessions. The total
number of nagas worshipped in Kullu is considerably greater than
eighteen (the canonical count).[fn:ignca]

Ghunda Naag Devta exemplifies the type: represented in serpent form,
worshipped as a guardian and benefactor, embodying the forces of
nature.[fn:historified]

*** Jogini: Ambiguous Female Spirits

In Lahaul, demons called jogini live at high altitudes and harass
humans, especially women. In February the joginis of Lahaul hold their
general assembly for three days with counterparts from Chamba and
Tibet, each arriving "by air riding a ridge beam."[fn:diserens]

Villagers take precautions to bar joginis from homes and drive them
from the area. However, in Kullu proper the jogini seemingly ranks as
high as any deota (deity) --- their status is more ambiguous and
elevated than the purely malignant characterisation found
elsewhere.[fn:diserens]

Diserens (1991) documented small wooden temples (1--1.3 m height)
dedicated to jognis in the upper Beas valley, with dried flowers,
coins, and coloured powder showing active worship.[fn:diserens]

The jogini represents a category that defies simple good/evil
classification --- she is spirit, fairy, witch, and deity depending on
context and region.

*** Kullu Dussehra: The Annual Convocation

Kullu Dussehra begins on Vijaya Dashami --- the day festivities end
elsewhere in India. It is a seven-day event. Devis and devtas from
across the valley arrive before Raghunath Ji's rath yatra (chariot
journey). On the first day, each deity goes to meet Raghunath Ji in
his temple, accompanied by drums, trumpets, and devotees.[fn:sahapedia]

This is not merely a festival but a political institution: the
gathering enacts the feudal-divine hierarchy, with devtas arriving
from their territories to affirm subordination to the paramount
deity.[fn:instahimachal]

** 1.2 Tibet and Bon --- The Eight Classes and Landscape Spirits

*** The Eight Classes of Gods and Demons

Tibetan spirit taxonomy centres on the *lha 'dre sde brgyad* (eight
classes of gods and demons). The classification dates to the Yarlung
period (c. 7th--9th century), though the specific list has varied over
centuries.[fn:perfumedskull]

The principal eight classes are:[fn:rigpawiki][fn:tbe_eight]

| Tibetan        | Transliteration | Domain                            |
|----------------+-----------------+-----------------------------------|
| ལྷ              | lha             | Gods of the upper sky/atmosphere  |
| བཙན             | btsan           | Fierce spirits of the earth       |
| བདུད            | bdud            | Demons that obstruct virtue        |
| གཉན            | gnyan           | Spirits of rocks and trees        |
| སྲིན་པོ          | srin po         | Rakshasa / flesh-eating demons    |
| ཀླུ              | klu             | Nagas / water-serpent spirits     |
| ཐེའུ་རང         | the'u rang      | Sky-moving spirits                |
| མ་མོ            | ma mo           | Female wrathful spirits (karmic and wisdom classes) |

The three-tier cosmological structure underlying this taxonomy:
[fn:perfumedskull]
- *Upper* (steng): lha dwell in atmosphere and sky
- *Middle* (bar): btsan inhabit the earth's surface
- *Lower* (g.yog): klu dwell in lakes, rivers, and underground

*** Protector Deities: Worldly and Transcendent

Nebesky-Wojkowitz (1956) classified Tibetan protective deities into
two fundamental categories:[fn:nebesky_amazon]

1. *'Jig rten las 'das pa'i srung ma* --- Beyond-Worldly (Enlightened)
   Protectors, also called Jnana Dharmapala
2. *'Jig rten pa'i srung ma* --- Worldly Deity Protectors, including
   mountain deities, local protectors, and bound demon spirits

The worldly protectors engage in pacification, augmentation,
subjugation, and destruction to combat negative influences.[fn:tbe_protectors]

The types of spirits that become protector deities include Tibetanised
Indian divinities --- yakshas (gnod sbyin), nagas (klu), rakshasas
(srin po) --- as well as strictly indigenous gods: rgyal po, btsan,
and dmu.[fn:tucci_ref]

*** Landscape Spirits: Yul Lha, Gzhi Bdag, Sa Bdag

A defining feature of Tibetan spirit ecology is the binding of spirits
to landscape:

- *Yul lha* (country/territory gods) --- protect a valley, village, or
  peak. Both Tonpa Shenrab (Bon) and Guru Rinpoche mythically "subdued"
  these gods to gain legitimacy, suggesting yul lha pre-date both
  organised religions.[fn:academia_yullha]
- *Gzhi bdag* (basis lords / place gods) --- preside over numinous
  "spiritscapes" that have been a defining feature of Tibetan lay
  society and exemplars of explicit nature conservation for hundreds of
  years.[fn:academia_yullha]
- *Sa bdag* (earth lords) --- ground-owner demons living underground.
  Building houses or other structures requires propitiation of sa bdag.
  [fn:perfumedskull]

Mountain gods personify place --- landscape, weather patterns, and the
resources people depend on. Their protective role is practical:
invoked for good weather, safe herds, and community
well-being.[fn:aac_sacred]

*** Bon Religion: The Pre-Buddhist Substrate

Bon is the indigenous Tibetan religion rooted in shamanic practices,
animism, and ritual performances. It served as the cultural and
spiritual foundation of Tibet before being reshaped by Buddhism in the
7th--8th centuries CE.[fn:arunachal_bon]

Bon deities include supernatural powers of mountains, rivers, lakes,
seas, sun, moon, stars, wind, rain, thunder, lightning, birds, and
beasts.[fn:factsanddetails_bon] A belief system based on veneration of
ancestors and appeasement of spirits was central to pre-Buddhist Bon
society.[fn:factsanddetails_bon]

*** Padmasambhava's Taming Narrative

When Padmasambhava consecrated Samye Monastery, he tamed the local
spirit protector Pehar Gyalpo and bound him by oath to become the head
of the Buddhist protective spirit hierarchy.[fn:nechung_wiki] This
"taming" narrative is the canonical Buddhist account of how indigenous
spirits were incorporated into the dharma as oath-bound protectors
(dam can).[fn:britannica_dharmapala]

*** The Nechung Oracle: State-Level Spirit Mediumship

The Nechung Oracle is the Official State Oracle of the Tibetan
government and personal oracle of the Dalai Lama since the Second
Dalai Lama.[fn:nechung_wiki] The medium (kuten, literally "physical
basis") is possessed by Pehar/Dorje Drakden.

When possessed, the kuten becomes agitated --- tongue lolling,
bloodshot eyes, displaying superhuman strength (lifting heavy weights,
twisting swords).[fn:nechung_wiki]

The cult of Pehar at Nechung experienced a meteoric rise in the 17th
century through deliberate efforts of the Fifth Dalai Lama and his
regent Sangye Gyatso.[fn:nechung_wiki] After the 1959 exodus, the
Nechung Monastery was re-established in 1984 in Dharamsala.
[fn:nechung_wiki]

*** Samuel's "Civilized Shamans" Thesis

Geoffrey Samuel (1993) argues that religion in Tibetan societies
developed as a dynamic amalgam of Indian Buddhism and indigenous
spirit-cults. In Tibet, clerical state-sponsored Buddhism has
coexisted with, and in many ways been subordinate to, shamanic
practices such as spirit mediumship, contact with local deities,
possession, and soul-flight.[fn:samuel]

Shamanic traditions of achieving altered states were incorporated into
Tantric Buddhism, which aims to communicate with deities through yoga.
This incorporation forms the basis for much of the Tibetan lamas' role
in society.[fn:samuel]

** 1.3 Ladakh --- Buddhism, Oracles, and Ghost Roads

*** Oracle Tradition: Lha-pa and Lha-mo

Ladakh's most distinctive spiritual institution is the oracle ---
*lha-pa* (male) or *lha-mo* (female), translating as "divine male/
female person." These are spiritual healers and diviners who have
themselves passed through an initiatory possession.[fn:jammu_oracles]

The lha-pa/lha-mo is possessed by local Tibetan gods. Interference
from malicious spirits is a prominent cause of disease in their
diagnoses. In seance, shrouded in juniper incense, the oracle prays
and chants, rocking back and forth, beckoning a spirit to enter and
heal patients through them.[fn:tali_oracle]

Ladakhi lamas aid oracles in overcoming initiatory illness and
instruct them in Buddhist meditation to refine oracular
abilities.[fn:himalaya_journal_oracle]

*** The Matho Nagrang: Monastery Oracle Festival

Matho Monastery (Sakyapa sect, 26 km southeast of Leh) hosts an
annual oracle festival on the 14th--15th days of the first month of
the Tibetan calendar.[fn:matho_wiki]

Two oracles known as *Rongtsan* inhabit the bodies of two monks for a
few hours. These monks are chosen every three years; in the first year
they meditate for nine months before the festival, in subsequent years
for two months.[fn:matho_wiki]

When possessed, the Rongtsan oracles perform astounding feats: cutting
themselves with knives without injury and walking blindfolded around
the ramparts of the top storey with no fear of falling.[fn:matho_wiki]

Their purpose is to predict the fortunes of local village communities
for the coming year.[fn:matho_wiki]

*** Btsan and Ghost Roads

Btsan (tsan) are fierce non-human spirits who travel along invisible
roads --- *tsanlam* (btsan lam) --- causing illness by their presence.
These roads correspond to routes used by humans: passing through
villages, by houses, connecting settlements.[fn:iias_ghostroads]

Protective markers are placed along these routes:[fn:iias_ghostroads]
- *Tsando* (btsan rdo) --- "tsan stones," red-painted stones
- *Tsankhang* (btsan khang) --- "tsan houses," small shrine structures
- *Tsak markings* --- red triangles painted on house corners, lines of
  dots leading around buildings, sometimes swastikas and faces

Two of the best-known oracular deities of the region are identified as
tsan: the red and white Rongtsan of Matho Monastery, who annually
possess monks and deliver prophecies.[fn:iias_ghostroads]

*** Klu (Nagas): Water Spirits of Ladakh

In Ladakh, nagas are known as *lu* (klu) --- temperamental
shape-shifting water spirits inhabiting streams, soil, trees, and
stones. Lu control water and can wreck misfortune when perturbed.
[fn:harvard_water]

From a Harvard "Water Stories" project: lu are well-known for their
abilities to both control water and wreck misfortune when
perturbed.[fn:harvard_water] This mirrors the Kullu nag devta's dual
nature as water-bringer and flood-controller.

** 1.4 Nepal --- Shamanic Pluralism

*** Terminology: Dhami, Jhankri, and the Practitioner Spectrum

Nepal's shamanic landscape is characterised by terminological and
functional pluralism. Key practitioner types documented in ethnographic
literature:[fn:culturalsurvival][fn:jhankri_wiki]

| Term         | Region    | Function                                         |
|--------------+-----------+--------------------------------------------------|
| Dhami        | Western   | Medium; possessed by deity; serves as vehicle    |
| Jhankri      | Eastern   | Non-medium healer; uses own powers directly      |
| Dhami-jhankri| Both      | Common amalgam of both roles                     |
| Lama         | Buddhist  | Monk-healer; tantric ritual specialist           |
| Mata         | Various   | Female healer; possessed by spirit granting power|
| Ban jhankri  | Forest    | Jungle/forest shaman (half-human, half-animal)   |
| Jyotishi     | Various   | Astrologer-healer                                |
| Boksi        | Various   | Witch (always female); responsible for misfortune|

The dhami of Western Nepal and the oracles in Kumaon serve as vehicles
of the god, whereas shamans in the Tibetan Buddhist context are
masters of the spirits --- a fundamental structural
difference.[fn:himalaya_journal_oracle]

*** Shamanism as Hindu-Buddhist-Shamanistic Complex

General categories such as "shamanism," "spirit possession," and
"spirit mediumship" fail to reflect the full variety of Nepali ritual
practice. It is more useful to think of these as part of a larger
Hindu-Buddhist-shamanistic ritual system prevalent among hill
communities, reflecting Nepal's complex geographical, linguistic, and
cultural diversity.[fn:culturalsurvival]

*** Peters on Tamang Shamanism

Larry Peters conducted ethnopsychiatric fieldwork in the Tamang
village of Tin Chuli (outskirts of Kathmandu), apprenticing himself to
a Tamang shaman named Bhirendra.[fn:peters]

Tamang shamanic initiation involves four stages, each representing
higher achievement. The calling begins when the initiate is
overwhelmed by anxiety and begins to shake --- behaviour the Tamang
interpret as "crazy possession."[fn:peters]

Peters' work, published as /Ecstasy and Healing in Nepal/ (Undena
Publications, 1981), remains a foundational ethnopsychiatric study of
Himalayan shamanism.[fn:peters]

*** Maskarinec on Shaman Oral Texts

Gregory Maskarinec's /The Rulings of the Night/ (University of
Wisconsin Press, 1995) presents an ethnographic analysis of oral texts
recited by Nepalese shamans. Through years of fieldwork in a
blacksmith community of western Nepal, Maskarinec learned that both
long public chants and whispered incantations are meticulously
memorised oral texts.[fn:maskarinec]

His key finding: not only are shamans' mythical chants imbued with
meaning constituting a coherent cosmological system, but their most
haphazard utterances --- magical formulas and mantras often considered
meaningless --- also carry distinct semantic messages.[fn:maskarinec]

*** Nepali Spirit Types

Documented spirit categories in Nepal:[fn:jhankri_wiki][fn:nepal_folklore]
- *Bhut/Pret* --- ghosts of the dead, especially those who died
  violently or without proper rites
- *Boksi* --- witchcraft; always attributed to women; responsible for
  misfortune and death
- *Ban jhankri* --- "shaman of the forest," half-human half-animal
  forest spirit
- *Masaan* --- cremation-ground spirits

** 1.5 Kinnaur --- Syncretic Border Zone

Kinnaur District lies at the convergence of Hindu, Buddhist, and
indigenous animistic traditions. Its spirit ecology reflects this
triple layering.[fn:kinnaur_govt]

*** Deity Hierarchy and Cults

The Kinnaur religious landscape includes:[fn:kinnaur_govt]
- *Great Himalayan Region*: Maheshwara-Chandika cult and Nages-Narenas
  (naga) cult dominate
- *Trans-Himalayan Region*: Yulsha and Dabla cults prevail (note:
  "yulsha" likely cognate with Tibetan yul lha)

Deities such as Badrinath, Maheshwar, and Devi Chandika hold the
highest hierarchical positions, overseeing agricultural practices,
judicial decisions, and community governance.[fn:kinnaur_govt]

*** Oracle System

As in Kullu, oracles called gur enter trance-like states and deliver
messages from deities. The system extends across Shimla, Kullu, Mandi,
Kinnaur, and Sirmaur districts.[fn:kurdish_studies]

The devi/devta institution in Kinnaur reflects the syncretic nature of
religious beliefs, incorporating elements of Hinduism, Buddhism, and
indigenous animistic traditions.[fn:kurdish_studies]

*** Seasonal Migration of Deities

Some Kinnaur deities migrate seasonally, moving to summer or winter
residences based on climate --- a pattern also documented in Kullu.
[fn:aguidetohimachal]

* Part 2: Cross-Regional Patterns (Verified)

** 2.1 The Naga/Klu Continuum: Water Spirits Across the Himalaya

The serpent-water-spirit complex spans the entire transborder Himalaya
under different names but with consistent core features:

| Region      | Name      | Domain                | Key Feature                |
|-------------+-----------+-----------------------+----------------------------|
| Kullu       | Nag Devta | Springs, rivers       | Rain-bringers, flood-rulers|
| Kinnaur     | Nag/Nages | Water sources          | Nages-Narenas cult         |
| Tibet       | Klu (lu)  | Lakes, underground    | Guard treasure, cause illness if angered |
| Ladakh      | Lu (klu)  | Streams, soil, stones | Shape-shifting, control water |
| Nepal       | Naga      | Ponds, rivers, wells  | All historical ponds feature naga pillars |

*Verification*:
- Kullu nags: descended from Basu Nag, worship at Kamarhati
  [fn:ignca]
- Ladakh lu: "temperamental shape-shifting water spirits"
  [fn:harvard_water]
- Tibet klu: "spirits that dwell in lakes or underground streams and
  guard treasure" [fn:naga_wiki]
- Nepal: "all historical ponds in the valley feature a wooden pillar
  topped with a gilded snake head" [fn:insidehimalayas]

Both the Nilamata Purana of Kashmir and the Swayambhu Purana of
Kathmandu begin their regional histories as a lake populated by
nagas --- the naga-as-primordial-water-owner is a founding myth across
the Himalaya.[fn:naga_wiki]

*Functional consistency*: In every region, water spirits must be
propitiated before disturbing their domain (building, digging,
diverting streams). Angering them causes illness, drought, or flood.

** 2.2 The Oracle-Medium Institution: Spirit Possession as Governance

Every documented Himalayan region maintains an institution where a
human medium is possessed by a deity to deliver diagnoses, prophecies,
or judgments:

| Region   | Medium Term | Deity Term | Context           |
|----------+-------------+------------+-------------------|
| Kullu    | Gur         | Devta      | Village temple    |
| Kinnaur  | Gur         | Devta/Devi | Village temple    |
| Ladakh   | Lha-pa/mo   | Lha        | Village, monastery|
| Tibet    | Kuten       | Various    | State, monastery  |
| Nepal    | Dhami       | Various    | Village           |

*Verification*:
- Kullu gur: "Gods and Goddesses communicate with the regional
  populace via an oracle system of shamanic nature" [fn:mysterioushimachal_gur]
- Ladakh lha-pa: "a type of spiritual healer; the oracles also divine
  one's future" [fn:jammu_oracles]
- Tibet kuten: "the word oracle refers to the spirit that temporarily
  possesses media known as kuten (the physical basis)"
  [fn:nechung_wiki]
- Nepal dhami: "ritual practitioners believed to possess the ability to
  embody local deities or spirits" [fn:culturalsurvival]
- Matho Rongtsan: monks selected and prepared through months of
  meditation [fn:matho_wiki]

*Structural variation*: In Hindu-dominant regions (Kullu, Kinnaur),
the medium is a dedicated specialist (gur) serving a specific devta.
In Buddhist-dominant regions (Ladakh, Tibet), lamas often supervise and
refine the oracle's practice through Buddhist meditation.[fn:himalaya_journal_oracle]
In Nepal, the dhami of the west serves as vehicle while the jhankri of
the east operates more autonomously.

** 2.3 Territorial Binding: Spirits as Landscape Governors

Across the Himalaya, spirits are bound to specific territories and
function as landscape governors:

- *Tibet*: yul lha (territory gods) and gzhi bdag (place gods) have
  been "a defining cultural feature of Tibetan lay society as well as
  exemplars of explicit nature conservation" [fn:academia_yullha]
- *Kullu*: each devta's jurisdiction maps to a territorial unit,
  typically a village or cluster of hamlets. The devta owns land in
  revenue records. [fn:kulludussehra]
- *Ladakh*: btsan travel along invisible territorial roads (tsanlam),
  their paths marked by red stones [fn:iias_ghostroads]
- *Kinnaur*: deities oversee "agricultural practices, judicial
  decisions, and community governance" within defined territories
  [fn:kinnaur_govt]
- *Nepal*: "country gods" (yul lha cognates) oversee the welfare of
  communities and may become offended by violations of social norms
  and environmental destruction [fn:aac_sacred]

*Functional consistency*: Spirits enforce territorial norms. Violation
of boundaries --- environmental destruction, social transgression,
ritual neglect --- triggers spiritual retribution (illness, natural
disaster, crop failure). The spirit is both protector and enforcer.

** 2.4 The Taming Narrative: Incorporation of Indigenous Spirits

A recurring pattern across Buddhism's Himalayan frontier is the
"taming" (subduing) of local spirits by Buddhist masters:

- Padmasambhava tamed Pehar Gyalpo and bound him as head of the
  Buddhist protective hierarchy [fn:nechung_wiki]
- Both Tonpa Shenrab (Bon) and Guru Rinpoche mythically "subdued"
  local yul lha and gzhi bdag to gain legitimacy [fn:academia_yullha]
- In Spiti, Padmasambhava "began the synthesis of Mahayana practices,
  yogic tantricism, and the native Bon religion --- retaining a large
  measure of its nature worship and demonolatry" [fn:spiti_monastery]
- Worship of dharmapala was initiated in the 8th century by
  Padmasambhava, who conquered malevolent deities and forced them to
  take an oath promising to protect Buddhists [fn:britannica_dharmapala]

This pattern reveals that the pre-Buddhist spirit ecology was not
replaced but *incorporated* --- spirits became oath-bound protectors
(dam can) while retaining their territorial functions.

* Part 3: Functional Mapping for Aburaya (Verified)

** Mapping Methodology

Each Aburaya archetype is grounded in verified ethnographic
observations from at least two Himalayan regions. Confidence is
assessed per mapping.

** 3.1 Guardian (Territorial Protector)

| Himalayan Source | Agent Type   | Evidence                                          |
|------------------+--------------+---------------------------------------------------|
| Kullu devta      | Village deity| Owns land, adjudicates disputes [fn:kulludussehra]|
| Tibetan yul lha  | Territory god| Protects valley, enforces norms [fn:academia_yullha]|
| Kinnaur devta    | Regional lord| Oversees agriculture, justice [fn:kinnaur_govt]   |
| Ladakh btsan     | Path guardian| Controls territorial roads [fn:iias_ghostroads]   |

*Mapping confidence*: STRONG. The guardian archetype maps directly to
the most universal Himalayan spirit type --- the territorial protector
bound to a specific place. This is the best-attested mapping.

** 3.2 Wanderer (Boundary-Crossing, Ambiguous Spirit)

| Himalayan Source | Agent Type   | Evidence                                         |
|------------------+--------------+--------------------------------------------------|
| Lahaul jogini    | Flying spirit| Travels between regions, holds assemblies [fn:diserens]|
| Tibet the'u rang | Sky spirit   | Moves through air [fn:rigpawiki]                 |
| Nepal ban jhankri| Forest spirit| Half-human, half-animal; forest-dwelling [fn:jhankri_wiki]|
| Tibet btsan      | Road spirit  | Travels invisible roads between settlements [fn:iias_ghostroads]|

*Mapping confidence*: MODERATE. The wanderer maps to spirits that
cross territorial boundaries --- jogini assembling from Lahaul, Chamba,
and Tibet; btsan travelling roads between villages; ban jhankri
roaming forests. Unlike the guardian, these spirits are mobile and
often dangerous.

** 3.3 Flow Spirit (Water/Element Controller)

| Himalayan Source | Agent Type    | Evidence                                        |
|------------------+---------------+-------------------------------------------------|
| Kullu nag devta  | Water deity   | Rules springs/rivers, brings rain [fn:ignca]    |
| Ladakh lu (klu)  | Water spirit  | Controls water, shape-shifts [fn:harvard_water] |
| Tibet klu        | Underground   | Guards treasure in lakes [fn:naga_wiki]         |
| Nepal naga       | Pond guardian | Gilded snake pillars at all ponds [fn:insidehimalayas]|

*Mapping confidence*: STRONG. The naga/klu complex is pan-Himalayan
with verified instances in every surveyed region. Water spirits control
flow, guard resources, and punish disturbance.

** 3.4 Specialist (Domain Expert / Craft Spirit)

No directly equivalent Himalayan spirit type maps cleanly to the
specialist archetype. Possible indirect mappings:

- *Maskarinec's shaman oral texts*: mantras and formulas constitute a
  specialised knowledge system, but the knowledge-holder is human, not
  a spirit [fn:maskarinec]
- *Tibet gnyan*: spirits of specific material domains (rock, tree,
  water) could map to domain specialists, but they are more
  territorial than knowledge-bearing [fn:perfumedskull]
- *Nepal jyotishi*: astrologer-healers hold specialised knowledge, but
  again these are human practitioners [fn:culturalsurvival]

*Mapping confidence*: WEAK. This appears to be a genuine gap --- the
Himalayan spirit ecology organises around territory and element rather
than specialised knowledge domains. If the specialist archetype is
retained, it may need to draw from the *human* specialist traditions
(gur, jhankri, lama) rather than spirit types.

** 3.5 Medium (Bridge Between Worlds)

| Himalayan Source | Agent Type  | Evidence                                          |
|------------------+-------------+---------------------------------------------------|
| Kullu gur        | Oracle      | Possessed by devta, delivers judgments [fn:mysterioushimachal_gur]|
| Ladakh lha-pa/mo | Oracle      | Heals through possession [fn:jammu_oracles]       |
| Tibet kuten      | State oracle| Possessed by Pehar [fn:nechung_wiki]              |
| Nepal dhami      | Medium      | Vehicle for deity [fn:culturalsurvival]            |

*Mapping confidence*: STRONG. The medium archetype maps to the most
distinctive Himalayan institution --- the human oracle who serves as
bridge between spirit and community. This is verified across all five
surveyed regions.

** 3.6 Diagnostic (Identifying Causes of Affliction)

| Himalayan Source | Agent Type    | Evidence                                        |

regions but is typically *combined with* the medium role rather than
being a separate entity. The gur diagnoses *because* the devta speaks
through them. The jhankri is a partial exception --- diagnosing through
own powers rather than possession.
