* Scope and Method

This file covers the modern era of Diamer and Nanga Parbat — from
the first summit in 1953 to the present. The period is defined by
mountaineering tragedy and triumph, political violence, mega-
infrastructure, and the transformation of a remote district into a
contested node of tourism, energy politics, and cultural erasure.

Method: web-accessible sources — mountaineering journals, news
archives, academic papers, heritage databases, and journalism.
Sources cited with URLs throughout. Where sources conflict, the
conflict is noted. Where verification is impossible, the gap is
stated.

* D.1 Messner and the Post-War Mountaineers

** D.1.1 Context: Herrligkoffer and the German Obsession

Before Messner, before the post-war golden age, there is Karl Maria
Herrligkoffer (1916–1991) — the Munich physician who made Nanga
Parbat his life's obsession. Herrligkoffer was the half-brother of
Willy Merkl, who died on Nanga Parbat in the catastrophic 1934
expedition. This personal connection drove Herrligkoffer to organise
expedition after expedition to the mountain: 1953, 1961, 1962, 1963,
1964, 1968, 1970, 1975, 1981, 1982 — ten expeditions spanning three
decades.

Herrligkoffer was controversial. He was not himself a strong
technical climber, but he insisted on leading as expedition
organiser, controlling communications, and retaining rights over
expedition accounts. This pattern of control would fuel the most
bitter controversy in Himalayan mountaineering history.

- Source: [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Herrligkoffer][Karl Herrligkoffer — Wikipedia]]

** D.1.2 The First Ascent: Hermann Buhl, 1953

On 3 July 1953, Austrian climber Hermann Buhl made the first ascent
of Nanga Parbat — alone, without supplemental oxygen, in a 41-hour
solo push from the last camp. He reached the summit at 7 pm and was
forced to spend the night standing on a narrow ledge near the top.
He returned to camp the following day, severely frostbitten, having
lost two toes.

The expedition was led by Herrligkoffer. Buhl had defied orders to
turn back. The relationship between the two men deteriorated
afterward into legal disputes over expedition accounts and book
rights.

Buhl's solo ascent remains one of the most extraordinary feats in
mountaineering history. He died four years later on Chogolisa (7665m)
when a cornice collapsed beneath him.

- Source: [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1953_German%E2%80%93Austrian_Nanga_Parbat_expedition][1953 German-Austrian Nanga Parbat expedition — Wikipedia]]

** D.1.3 The Diamir Face: Kinshofer, Mannhardt, Löw (1962)

On 22 June 1962, Germans Toni Kinshofer, Anderl Mannhardt, and
Siegfried Löw completed the second ascent of Nanga Parbat and the
first ascent via the Diamir Face, on yet another Herrligkoffer
expedition.

Their route avoided the avalanche-prone central Diamir Face by
ascending a buttress on the left side, reaching the Kinshofer
Icefield, and traversing the Bazhin Gap to the summit. This route
has become the standard climbing route on Nanga Parbat, known as the
*Kinshofer Route*, and remains the most commonly used route to this
day.

*** The Descent Disaster

The descent proved catastrophic. Löw lagged behind. Kinshofer
climbed back up toward him, but Löw fell past and out onto the
glacier. Badly injured, he was cared for by Kinshofer while
Mannhardt descended past empty Camp IV to Camp III for help. Löw
died during the descent. Kinshofer and Mannhardt survived but
suffered severe frostbite.

Toni Kinshofer himself would die in 1966, falling while ice climbing
in the Bavarian Alps — age 31.

- Source: [[http://publications.americanalpineclub.org/articles/12196352404/Asia-Pakistan-Nanga-Parbat-Diamir-Face][AAC Publications — Nanga Parbat, Diamir Face (1963 AAJ)]]

** D.1.4 The Messner Tragedy (1970)

This is the central drama of Nanga Parbat's modern history — a story
that took 35 years and forensic DNA analysis to resolve.

*** The Expedition

In June 1970, Herrligkoffer led a large expedition to attempt the
first ascent of the Rupal Face — the highest mountain wall on Earth,
rising 4,500m from the Rupal Valley to the summit. The team included
two brothers from South Tyrol: *Reinhold Messner* (age 25) and
*Günther Messner* (age 23). Günther joined the expedition late,
filling an opening on the team.

*** The Summit Push

On 27 June 1970, Reinhold Messner reached the summit of Nanga Parbat
via the Rupal Face — the first ascent of this enormous wall.
According to Reinhold's account, Günther followed him to the summit
unexpectedly, arriving in a deteriorated state.

The brothers bivouacked near the summit overnight, near the so-called
Merkl Notch, because a descent of the Rupal Face in the dark seemed
impossible given Günther's exhaustion and altitude sickness. By
morning, Reinhold judged that Günther could not safely descend the
Rupal Face. They chose instead to descend the gentler Diamir Face on
the opposite side of the mountain — a traverse that had never been
done before.

*** Günther's Death

During the descent of the Diamir Face, Reinhold left Günther resting
while he reconnoitred the route ahead. When he returned, Günther was
gone. Reinhold searched for hours but found no trace. He concluded
that an avalanche had swept his brother away.

Reinhold continued down alone, suffering severe frostbite. He lost
seven toes and was rescued by local woodcutters in the Diamir Valley.

*** The 35-Year Controversy

Reinhold's account was immediately challenged by other expedition
members, principally *Hans Saler* and *Max von Kienlin*. Their
counter-narrative held that:

- Günther never crossed the summit with Reinhold.
- Reinhold descended the Diamir Face alone to claim the first traverse.
- Günther was left near the summit to descend the Rupal Face
  by himself, where he died alone and unseen.

The dispute became extraordinarily bitter. It spawned more than a
dozen lawsuits. It produced competing books: Messner's own "Die Rote
Rakete am Nanga Parbat" (later withdrawn under court order from
Herrligkoffer), Saler's "Between Light and Shadows", and von
Kienlin's "The Traverse" (English translations of the latter two
were prevented by court order obtained by Messner). The controversy
was further complicated by a post-expedition love affair between
Reinhold Messner and von Kienlin's wife, which some saw as tainting
the accusations with personal revenge.

*** "The Naked Mountain" (2003)

Messner's memoir, /The Naked Mountain: Nanga Parbat — Brother, Death
and Solitude/ (German 2002, English 2003, The Mountaineers Books),
is his definitive account of the 1970 expedition. It is an
extraordinary document — part climbing narrative, part grief memoir,
part legal brief. The American Alpine Club's review noted its
"intense and deeply felt" quality while acknowledging the unresolved
factual disputes.

*** The Boot on the Glacier (2005)

In July 2005, three Pakistani guides discovered human remains on the
Diamir Face at approximately 4,300 metres — a skull fragment, a boot
containing a foot, and other bone fragments, exposed by glacial
retreat.

Reinhold recognised the brown leather Lowa Triple boot and the jacket.

On 21 October 2005, scientists at the University of Innsbruck
completed DNA analysis of tissue samples from the remains. The DNA
confirmed: the remains were Günther Messner's.

The location — on the Diamir Face, not the Rupal Face — supported
Reinhold's account. Günther had indeed crossed the summit and
descended the Diamir side with his brother. He had indeed perished
on the Diamir Face, as Reinhold had always maintained.

*** The Second Boot (2022)

In 2022, a second boot belonging to Günther was recovered from the
glacier and returned to Reinhold — a moment of deeply personal
closure more than fifty years after the tragedy.

*** Assessment

The DNA evidence resolved the factual question: Günther was on the
Diamir Face when he died. But it did not resolve all questions about
the expedition dynamics — particularly the rocket signal controversy
(whether a red rocket fired from base camp was a summit signal or an
abort order) and the degree to which Herrligkoffer's expedition
management contributed to the disaster. The ethical complexity of
the event — two brothers, one left behind, one who survived and
became the most famous mountaineer in history — remains a permanent
feature of Nanga Parbat's human landscape.

** D.1.5 First Female Ascent: Liliane Barrard (1984)

On 27 June 1984, *Liliane Barrard* of France became the first woman
to summit Nanga Parbat, climbing without supplementary oxygen. She
climbed with her husband Maurice Barrard; the couple were known in
French mountaineering as "the highest couple."

Two years later, in 1986, both Liliane and Maurice summited K2 — but
disappeared during the descent. Liliane's body was found a month
later at approximately 5,330m. Maurice's body was discovered twelve
years later on the glacier above Base Camp.

** D.1.6 First Winter Ascent: Sadpara, Txikon, Moro (2016)

On 26 February 2016, at 3:17 pm, three climbers completed the first
winter ascent of Nanga Parbat:

- *Muhammad Ali Sadpara* (Pakistan)
- *Alex Txikon* (Spain)
- *Simone Moro* (Italy)

A fourth team member, *Tamara Lunger* (Italy), stopped just short of
the summit due to extreme fatigue.

The ascent was achieved after *31 unsuccessful winter attempts* on the
mountain over the preceding decades. Nanga Parbat had been the last
of the fourteen 8,000-metre peaks to resist a winter ascent (K2 was
later climbed in winter in January 2021).

Ali Sadpara became a national hero in Pakistan. His achievement was
all the more remarkable given that he came from the village of
Sadpara near Skardu, a high-altitude community with no tradition of
professional mountaineering infrastructure. He went on to summit
eight 8,000-metre peaks.

Sadpara died on K2 on 5 February 2021, along with Icelandic climber
John Snorri Sigurjónsson and Chilean climber Juan Pablo Mohr. His
body was found in July 2021, approximately 300 metres below the
Bottleneck at ~7,900m. His son Sajid, who had turned back due to an
oxygen regulator failure, survived.

- Source: [[https://www.planetmountain.com/en/news/alpinism/nanga-parbat-summit-and-first-winter-ascent-by-simone-moro-ali-sadpara-and-alex-txikon.html][Nanga Parbat summit and first winter ascent — PlanetMountain]]

** D.1.7 The Second Winter Ascent: Revol and Mackiewicz (2018)

On 25 January 2018, *Élisabeth Revol* (France) and *Tomasz
Mackiewicz* (Poland) reached the summit of Nanga Parbat in winter —
the second winter ascent. This was Mackiewicz's seventh attempt at a
winter ascent of the mountain.

At the summit, when Revol asked Mackiewicz how he was feeling, he
replied: "I can't see you. I can't see anything." He had removed
his goggles and was suffering from snow blindness. He was also
developing altitude sickness and severe frostbite.

The descent became a survival crisis. Mackiewicz could no longer
walk. Revol sheltered him in a crevasse at approximately 7,280m and
descended alone, triggering a rescue via satellite communication.

The rescue operation was remarkable. Two hundred kilometres away,
a Polish team was attempting the first winter ascent of K2. Four of
their climbers — *Adam Bielecki*, *Denis Urubko*, *Jarosław Botor*,
and *Piotr Tomala* — abandoned their own expedition and were
helicoptered to Nanga Parbat by Pakistani military pilots led by
Lt. Col. Anjum Rafique.

Bielecki and Urubko climbed through the night and reached Revol,
guiding her down to safety. They could not reach Mackiewicz in the
deteriorating conditions. He was declared dead.

Revol lost several fingers and toes to frostbite. She became the
second woman to summit an 8,000-metre peak in winter (after
Wanda Rutkiewicz on Everest in 1980). Revol later published a memoir,
/To Live/, dedicated to Mackiewicz.

** D.1.8 The Death Toll

Nanga Parbat earned its epithet "Killer Mountain" before anyone
stood on top of it. Thirty-one people died on the mountain before
Buhl's first ascent in 1953, most of them on the German expeditions
of the 1930s. The worst single disaster was in 1937, when an
avalanche at Camp IV below Rakhiot Peak killed all sixteen men in
the camp — seven Germans and nine Sherpas.

*** Overall Statistics (approximate, as of 2024)

| Mountain | Total Deaths | Summits | Fatality Ratio |
|---------------+--------------+---------+--------------------------|
| Annapurna I | ~75 | ~250 | ~32 deaths per 100 summits |
| K2 | ~96 | ~800 | ~23 deaths per 100 summits |
| Nanga Parbat | ~80+ | ~400 | ~21 deaths per 100 summits |

/Note/: These numbers vary across sources and change with each season.
The fatality ratio (deaths per 100 summits) is the meaningful
comparator, not absolute death counts.

Before 1990, Nanga Parbat's fatality rate was an astonishing *77%*.
The modern rate of approximately 21% reflects improved gear, weather
forecasting, and route knowledge — but the mountain remains among the
deadliest in the world.

Annapurna I holds the highest fatality-to-summit ratio overall. K2
has a higher absolute death toll but more summits. Nanga Parbat's
claim to the "Killer Mountain" title rests primarily on its
pre-1953 history, when expedition after expedition ended in disaster.

* D.2 The 2013 Massacre

** D.2.1 The Attack

On the night of 22–23 June 2013, approximately sixteen gunmen
attacked a mountaineering base camp on the Diamir Face of Nanga
Parbat, in Gilgit-Baltistan, Pakistan. The attackers were reportedly
dressed in Gilgit-Baltistan Scouts uniforms, which enabled them to
approach the camp and pass through security checkpoints.

The gunmen entered the tents, lined up the climbers, and shot them.
Eleven people were killed — ten foreign mountaineers and one
Pakistani guide. The base camp was at approximately 4,200m on the
Diamir side.

Two climbers survived by hiding: a Chinese climber who concealed
himself in a sleeping bag, and another who hid behind equipment.

*** Igor Svergun

Igor Svergun deserves particular mention. He was not merely an
amateur climber but a distinguished biophysicist — a leading
researcher in small-angle X-ray scattering at the European Molecular
Biology Laboratory (EMBL) in Hamburg. His work on protein structure
determination was internationally recognised.

- Source: [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igor_Svergun][Igor Svergun — Wikipedia]]

*** Chen Honglu

According to reporting in Dawn, the Pakistani English-language
newspaper, Chen Honglu — the Chinese-American dual citizen — may have
been the intended kidnapping target rather than a murder victim. The
plan reportedly was to take him hostage and trade him for a Taliban
commander held in Afghanistan. When the attackers entered the camp,
Chen burst from his tent and physically tackled one of the gunmen
using martial arts. The militant, named Mujeeb, panicked and shot
him, reportedly disrupting the original kidnapping plan and
escalating the operation into mass murder.

This account, if accurate, means the massacre of all eleven people
may have resulted from one man's decision to fight back — a detail
of unbearable moral weight.

- Source: [[https://www.dawn.com/news/1115891][Chinese-American was target of Nanga Parbat massacre — Dawn]]

** D.2.3 The Attackers

Two militant groups claimed responsibility:

*Jundullah* — a sectarian extremist group with a track record of
attacks in Gilgit-Baltistan — was the first to claim the attack.
Jundullah (sometimes spelled Jundallah) operated primarily against
Shia communities in the region.

The *Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan (TTP)* also claimed responsibility,
stating the attack was retaliation for the US drone strike that
killed Wali-ur-Rehman, a senior Taliban commander, on 29 May 2013
— less than a month before the massacre.

The dual claims complicate attribution. Some analysts believe the
attack was planned by Jundullah operatives with TTP support; others
see the TTP claim as opportunistic.

** D.2.4 The Trial and Convictions

The attackers were tried in military courts established under
Pakistan's *National Action Plan* against terrorism, which was adopted
after the 2014 Peshawar school massacre. Thirteen militants were
convicted and sentenced to death. The death warrants were signed by
the Army Chief.

One convicted attacker specifically named in reporting was *Irfan
Ullah*, son of Khudayar, condemned to death for participation in the
base camp assault.

The convicts were shifted to Adiala Jail in Rawalpindi. As of the
most recent reporting available, the status of the executions is
unclear — Pakistan has carried out some terrorism-related executions
but the process has faced delays and legal challenges.

** D.2.5 The Aftermath

The immediate impact on Pakistan's mountain tourism was devastating:

- Ninety percent of climbing expeditions were cancelled in the weeks
  following the attack.
- In the summer of 2014, *zero international climbing teams* attempted
  Nanga Parbat.
- Nationwide, foreign climbing groups dropped from an average of ~75
  per year to approximately 30 in 2014.
- The estimated annual cost to Pakistan's economy was *US$100 million*
  in lost tourism revenue.

The Pakistan Army deployed military escorts for all mountaineering
expeditions in Gilgit-Baltistan. Security checkpoints were
established on all access roads. The level of military presence in
the region increased significantly.

Recovery was slow but has been substantial. By the early 2020s,
international expeditions had returned to pre-attack levels, and
Gilgit-Baltistan is now broadly regarded as one of the safest
regions in Pakistan, supported by enhanced security, improved local
infrastructure, and community-driven tourism initiatives.

* D.3 Klára Kolouchová (2025)

** D.3.1 The Climber

*Klára Kolouchová* (6 September 1978 – 3 July 2025) was a Czech
mountaineer, author, and educator. She was the first Czech woman to
summit the three highest peaks on Earth — Everest, K2, and
Kangchenjunga — and had summited five 8,000-metre peaks in total:

| # | Peak | Date | Notes |
|---+---------------+-------------------+---------------------------------------|
| 1 | Cho Oyu | 9 March 2006 | 8,188m; her first 8,000er |
| 2 | Everest | 16 May 2007 | First Czech woman to summit Everest |
| 3 | Kangchenjunga | Spring 2019 | 8,586m |
| 4 | K2 | Summer 2019 | First Czech woman; ~20th woman globally |
| 5 | Annapurna I | 2024 | 8,091m |

She also completed the *Seven Summits* (highest peak on each
continent) and four of the *Volcanic Seven Summits*.

Beyond mountaineering, Kolouchová published two books: /Himálajský
deník/ (Himalayan Diary), documenting her Everest expedition, and
/Nahoře fouká/ ("It Blows at the Top", 2022). She was affiliated with
Anglo-American University in Prague. She was married and had two
children, Emma and Cyril.

- Source: [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kl%C3%A1ra_Kolouchov%C3%A1][Klára Kolouchová — Wikipedia]]
- Source: [[https://english.radio.cz/klara-kolouchova-tragedy-trailblazing-czech-mountaineer-dies-nanga-parbat-8855891][Klára Kolouchová tragedy — Radio Prague International]]
- Source: [[https://www.aauni.edu/news/in-memoriam-klara-kolouchova/][In Memoriam: Klára Kolouchová — Anglo-American University in Prague]]

** D.3.2 The Death

On 3 July 2025, Kolouchová died on Nanga Parbat at age 46. She fell
from the mountain between Camp 1 and Camp 2 during descent.

Her Sherpa, *Taraman Tamang*, was approximately 40–50 metres behind
her. He heard a scream and saw her fall outside the fixed rope. They
were on their way back to Camp 2 at the time.

Early reports cited an oxygen tank explosion as the cause, but this
was dismissed by Czech mountaineer *Jan Trávníček*, who pointed to a
fatal fall as the likely explanation.

The circumstances raise the persistent question of fixed-rope
security on high-altitude routes — particularly the gap between
clipping in and out of anchors, when a climber is momentarily
unprotected.

** D.3.3 Response

The Czech mountaineering community mourned her as a pioneer. The

* D.4 The KKH and Tourism

** D.4.1 The Karakoram Highway

The *Karakoram Highway* (KKH) is the highest paved international road
in the world, connecting Hasan Abdal in Punjab, Pakistan to Kashgar
in Xinjiang, China via the Khunjerab Pass (4,714m). It is sometimes
called the *Eighth Wonder of the World* — a title that recognises
both its engineering and its human cost.

*** Construction

Construction was a joint Pakistan-China project spanning approximately
two decades. The dates in sources vary:

- Engineering surveys began in 1959.
- Active construction began in 1966 (some sources say 1962).
- The road was completed in 1978–1979.
- It was opened to the public in 1986.

The Chinese government initially proposed routing the highway over
the Mintaka Pass, but in 1966 recommended the steeper *Khunjerab
Pass* instead, citing the former's vulnerability to air strikes — a
Cold War calculation embedded in civil engineering.

*** The Death Toll

The human cost of the highway was staggering. The most commonly
cited figure is:

- *810 Pakistani workers* killed
- *200+ Chinese workers* killed (some sources report ~82 Chinese;
  the higher figure of ~200 appears in more sources and is likely
  more accurate)

Deaths were primarily from landslides, rockfalls, and falls. The
terrain the road traverses — the gorges of the Indus, Hunza, and
Gilgit rivers through active seismic zones — is among the most
geologically unstable on Earth.

Over 140 Chinese workers who died during construction are buried in a
Chinese cemetery in Gilgit, which is maintained to this day by a
Pakistani gravekeeper.

/Note on the discrepancy/: The figure of 82 Chinese deaths appears in
some Pakistani government sources. The figure of ~200 appears in
Chinese state media (CGTN, Xinhua) and multiple independent
accounts. It is possible the lower figure counts only deaths on the
Pakistani side of the border, while the higher figure includes deaths
on the Chinese section. The true number may never be established.

- Source: [[https://www.pakistantoday.com.pk/2023/08/16/kkh-the-highway-constructed-with-blood-flesh-of-chinese-pakistani-workers/][KKH — the highway constructed with flesh & blood — Pakistan Today]]
- Source: [[https://news.cgtn.com/news/3d41544e794d444e/share.html][Karakoram Highway: Memorial for China-Pakistan friendship — CGTN]]
- Source: [[https://english.news.cn/20250217/1d20445df9774f11a9a6a731e16b7b49/c.html][Pakistani gravekeeper honors Karakoram Highway builders — Xinhua]]

*** Impact on Diamer

Before the KKH, the Diamer district was profoundly isolated. Chilas,
the district capital, was reachable only by foot trails or mule
track. The highway connected the Indus valley to the national road
network and, via the Khunjerab Pass, to China.

The transformation was not merely logistical but cultural: goods,
ideas, military presence, and outsiders began flowing through a
valley that had been effectively autonomous for centuries. The KKH
did not bring modernity to Diamer — it brought Diamer into the
state's field of vision.

** D.4.2 The Road to Fairy Meadows

The *Fairy Meadows Jeep Track* runs approximately 15 km from Raikot
Bridge on the KKH to the village of Tato (also spelled Tattu), at the
edge of the Fairy Meadows National Park. It is often described as
one of the most dangerous roads in the world.

The track is:
- Unpaved gravel and loose shale
- Approximately one jeep wide (no room to pass in most sections)
- Without guardrails
- Carved into cliffs approximately 1,000 metres above the valley floor
- Accessible only to local drivers in modified 4x4 vehicles

Visitors describe "the acrid smell of burning brake pads, the screech
of tyres on loose shale, and the terrifying sight of a gravel ribbon
carved into a cliff... completely devoid of guardrails."

Only local drivers are permitted to operate on the track. Foreign
tourists and their vehicles are prohibited — a regulation that
functions both as safety measure and as economic protection for the
local transport operators.

From Tato, a 5 km hiking trail (approximately 3–4 hours) leads to
Fairy Meadows proper — an alpine meadow at approximately 3,300m with
direct views of the Nanga Parbat Rupal Face.

*** The Paradox of Access

The road's very danger acts as a regulatory mechanism. As one travel
writer observed, "the sheer terror of the track prevents the
devastating effects of mass industrialised tourism." The difficulty
of access has preserved Fairy Meadows from the overdevelopment that
has degraded comparable sites elsewhere in northern Pakistan.

- Source: [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairy_Meadows_National_Park][Fairy Meadows National Park — Wikipedia]]
- Source: [[https://againstthecompass.com/en/fairy-meadows-trek-nanga-parbat-base-camp/][Fairy Meadows trek & Nanga Parbat Base Camp — Against the Compass]]

** D.4.3 Fairy Meadows and the Tourism Boom

Fairy Meadows was declared a *National Park* by the Government of
Pakistan in 1995. Since 1992, local communities have operated camping
sites and accommodation in the area.

*** Infrastructure

The meadow and its approaches now support:

- Camping sites and tent rentals (~500 PKR / ~$4.70 per night with
  sleeping bag)
- Basic bungalows (~1,000 PKR / ~$9.50 per night)
- Upgraded bungalows and "luxury" accommodation (relative term at
  3,300m)
- Food stalls and basic restaurants
- The camping area known as "Raikot Serai" covers approximately 800
  hectares

The six-month tourist season runs from April through September. The
site generates approximately PKR 17 million annually from tourism
(food, transport, accommodation).

*** Who Visits

The visitor profile has shifted dramatically in recent years:

- *Pakistani domestic tourists* now constitute the majority of
  visitors, driven by improved road access from Islamabad (via the
  KKH) and the social media visibility of the site.
- *International trekkers* have returned since the security
  improvements following the 2013 massacre, though in smaller numbers
  than the domestic market.
- The *Nanga Parbat Base Camp trek* (from Fairy Meadows via Beyal
  Camp and the Raikot Glacier viewpoint) is the primary draw for
  serious trekkers — approximately 5–6 hours each way.

Social media has been transformative. Fairy Meadows is now one of the
most photographed and hashtagged locations in Pakistan. This
visibility has created both opportunity (local income) and pressure
(overcrowding in peak season, waste management problems, cultural
friction between conservative local communities and urban Pakistani
visitors).

- Source: [[https://www.ultimategearlists.com/destinations/fairy-meadows-nanga-parbat-base-camp-pakistan][Fairy Meadows & Nanga Parbat Base Camp Guide — Ultimate Gear Lists]]
- Source: [[https://www.jackandjilltravel.com/fairy-meadows-nanga-parbat/][The Fairy Meadows in Pakistan and Hiking Nanga Parbat Basecamp — Jack and Jill Travel]]

** D.4.4 The Base Camp Trek

The standard Nanga Parbat Base Camp trek from Fairy Meadows follows
this route:

| Stage | From | To | Time | Notes |
|-------+-----------------------+----------------------+---------------+-------------------------------|
| 1 | Fairy Meadows (3,300m) | Beyal Camp (3,500m) | 60–90 min | Gentle ascent through forest |
| 2 | Beyal Camp | Raikot Glacier VP | ~60 min | Views of glacier and icefall |
| 3 | Raikot Glacier VP | Base Camp (3,967m) | 90–120 min | Steep final 30 min |

The round trip from Fairy Meadows to Base Camp and back is typically
done in a single long day (10–12 hours) or with an overnight at Beyal
Camp.

The infrastructure is minimal: Beyal Camp has a few tea houses and
camping spots. The trail beyond Beyal is rough and unmarked in
places. There are no rescue facilities between Fairy Meadows and
Base Camp.

* D.5 The Dam Workers

** D.5.1 The Diamer-Basha Dam

The *Diamer-Basha Dam* (also spelled Diamer-Bhasha) is a concrete-
filled gravity dam under construction on the River Indus, situated
between Kohistan district (Khyber Pakhtunkhwa) and Diamer district
(Gilgit-Baltistan).

*** Key Facts

| Parameter | Value |
|---------------------------+---------------------------------------------|
| River | Indus |
| Type | Roller-Compacted Concrete (RCC) gravity dam |
| Height | 272 metres |
| Power generation capacity | 4,500 MW |
| Storage capacity | 6.4 million acre-feet (MAF) |
| Reservoir length | ~100 km |
| Annual electricity output | 18.1 billion kWh |
| Total cost | ~Rs. 1,406.5 billion (~US$5 billion) |
| Construction contract | Signed 13 May 2020 |
| Contractor | Power China–FWO Joint Venture |
| Expected completion | ~2028–2029 |

If completed as designed, it will be one of the tallest dams in the
world and the largest in Pakistan.

- Source: [[https://wapda.gov.pk/diamer-basha-dam-project/][Diamer Basha Dam Project — WAPDA]]
- Source: [[https://nishadil.com/news/diamer-bhasha-dam-pakistans-future-water-and-power-hub-set-for-major-construction-milestone/a2e072731fddc4a047c3d2fa0d52b8fc][Diamer-Bhasha Dam: RCC Work Begins 2025 — Nishadil]]

** D.5.2 What the Dam Erases: The Petroglyphs

The dam reservoir will flood a stretch of the Indus gorge containing
one of the densest concentrations of rock art in the world.
Approximately *35,000 petroglyphs* will be affected — carvings and
inscriptions dating from the Neolithic (~8,000 BCE) through the
Buddhist period to the Islamic era.

The inscriptions are in multiple ancient scripts and languages:
*Brāhmī, Sogdian, Middle Persian, Chinese, Tibetan*, and what some
researchers have identified as ancient Hebrew. They represent
millennia of travellers along the Indus corridor — pilgrims,
merchants, armies, monks — leaving their marks on the rocks.

*** UNESCO Status

The petroglyphs are *not* on the UNESCO World Heritage List, which
deprives them of certain international legal protections. The World
Monuments Fund (WMF) has listed them as endangered heritage, but the
absence of World Heritage designation limits the international
community's leverage.

*** Salvage Efforts

WAPDA (the Water and Power Development Authority) has developed a
*Cultural Heritage Management Plan* that includes:

- 3D scanning of 5,000 selected carvings (out of 35,000)
- The selection was made by *Harald Hauptmann* (1936–2018), former
  head of the Pak-German Archaeological Mission and professor at
  Heidelberg University, who led decades of petroglyph documentation
  in the region.
- Digital archiving of the scan data
- Full-scale replicas for display in a planned museum
- Cultural tourism promotion in Gilgit-Baltistan

The salvage covers approximately *14%* of the total petroglyph
inventory. The remaining 30,000 carvings will be submerged.

WAPDA has announced plans to *digitise* remaining carvings as part of
an expanded documentation programme, but the scale of the task versus
the construction timeline raises questions about feasibility.

** D.5.3 Displacement

The dam reservoir will flood *31 villages*, affecting approximately
*28,650 people* and displacing more than *4,135 households*.

A 2024 academic study (published in the MDPI journal /Sustainability/)
documented the social impact of the project delay on affected
communities:

- Households have been living in uncertainty about relocation and
  rehabilitation for years, as construction timelines have shifted
  repeatedly.
- Only *37% of affected households* expressed willingness to resettle
  in the proposed model villages — indicating deep community
  resistance to the resettlement plan.
- Concerns include loss of agricultural land, disruption of social
  networks, inadequate compensation, and the erasure of ancestral
  connections to place.

The people of Diamer are predominantly Shina-speaking, with strong
clan and village identities. Resettlement to "model villages" —
designed on a grid, without reference to the social geography of the
original settlements — represents a fundamental rupture in the
relationship between community and landscape.

- Source: [[https://www.mdpi.com/2071-1050/17/6/2387][Development-Induced Displacement: A Case Study of Diamer Basha Dam — MDPI Sustainability]]
- Source: [[https://dialogue.earth/en/water/pakistan-breaks-ground-for-dream-dam-project-at-diamer/][Pakistan breaks ground for dream dam project at Diamer — Dialogue Earth]]

** D.5.4 The Workers

The dam is being built by the *Power China–FWO Joint Venture* (Frontier
Works Organisation, a division of the Pakistan Army, partnered with
the Chinese state corporation). Power China holds a 70% stake; FWO
holds 30%. The workforce includes:

- Over 17,000 Chinese workers expected across the project's duration
- Pakistani labourers, many from the local Diamer and Kohistan
  communities
- FWO military-engineering personnel

In September 2025, the project advertised 130 new positions through
the POWERCHINA-FWO joint venture: civil and planning engineers,
dumper drivers, heavy equipment operators, masons, drillers, steel
fixers, carpenters, and general labourers. Applications were processed
through the Collector and Deputy Commissioner Diamer offices, with
emphasis on "training and hiring local workers to ensure community
participation."

- Source: [[https://www.nation.com.pk/18-Sep-2025/diamer-basha-dam-creates-130-job-opportunities-for-locals][Diamer Basha Dam creates 130 job opportunities for locals — The Nation]]

Detailed reporting on the workforce — their origins, conditions,
perspectives on the petroglyphs — remains sparse. The project operates
in a region with limited press access, military involvement in
construction, and security restrictions inherited from the post-2013
environment.

** D.5.5 The Voices of the Displaced

The displaced communities have not been silent — but their voices
appear primarily in protest coverage, not in the dam's official
narrative. In 2025, the *Huqooq Do, Dam Banao Tehreek* ("Give Rights,
Build the Dam" Movement) organised sustained protests that shut the
Karakoram Highway at Bab-e-Chilas for over 42 days.

*** Named voices

*Attaullah Khan*, leader of the Diamer-Bhasha Dam Affectees Action
Committee, articulated the loss in terms that compensation frameworks
cannot capture:

#+begin_quote
"There cannot be a compensation for the graves of our ancestors, our
culture, tradition and history."
#+end_quote

--- Attaullah Khan, reported in Dialogue Earth (2020) and subsequent
coverage.

*Shafiullah Danish*, 27 years old, from Thor village — one of the
thirteen villages that will be submerged — expressed the frustration
of a generation that has grown up under the dam's shadow:

#+begin_quote
"The indifferent attitude of the government will one day make us
rebels. We are not allowed to even speak for our rights."
#+end_quote

--- Shafiullah Danish, reported in Dialogue Earth (2020).

*Muhammad Shafique*, also from Thore village:

#+begin_quote
"We sacrificed our culture and even our graveyards in the larger
national interest but the government did not honour its commitment.
For a tribal man, nothing is more important than the graveyard of his
ancestors."
#+end_quote

--- Muhammad Shafique, reported in Dialogue Earth / The Third Pole.

*Ziaul Haq*, a sawmill and furniture factory owner from Thoril Nala
village in Diamer, whose business faces destruction without
compensation, supports eight children through school and college:

#+begin_quote
"The government takes away land, factory and house without
compensation."
#+end_quote

--- Ziaul Haq, reported in Lok Sujag (2025).

*Sahibzada*, 67 years old, from Upper Kohistan, had two separate
ancestral homes classified as one for compensation purposes and
received a single "Choolha Package" (Rs 3.5 million) for two houses
and a shop. He has been fighting through the courts without
resolution.

*** The protest movement

The Affectees Committee's 32-point charter of demands includes:
resettlement of affected families, allotment of homes in Islamabad
and Abbottabad at nominal rates, construction of model residential
colonies in Diamer, jobs in the project, educational scholarships,
and wheat subsidies. The government states Rs 151 billion has been
disbursed in compensation, but affected communities dispute the
adequacy and distribution.

The movement's slogan — /Huqooq Do, Dam Banao/ ("Give Rights, Build
the Dam") — is notable for what it does /not/ say. The protesters do
not oppose the dam. They accept the national interest argument. They
ask only to be treated as participants rather than obstacles.

** D.5.6 The Larger Question

The Diamer-Basha Dam crystallises a tension that runs through this
entire survey series: the relationship between infrastructure,
memory, and power. The Karakoram Highway brought the state's gaze to
Diamer. The dam brings the state's need — for electricity, for water
storage, for control of the Indus. The petroglyphs are 10,000 years
of human presence being sacrificed for a reservoir that will silt up
in a century.

This is not an argument against the dam — Pakistan's energy and water
needs are real and urgent. It is an observation that the cost is being
tallied in the wrong currency. Thirty thousand rock carvings are not
"heritage" in the abstract. They are the signatures of individuals
who stood in a specific place and made a mark. Each one is an act of
presence. The dam will erase 86% of them without a record.
