* Scope and Method

This survey covers the voices systematically absent from the written
record of Diamer district and Nanga Parbat: women, porters, local
storytellers, and the oral traditions that exist (or once existed)
outside the colonial-academic-mountaineering textual apparatus.

Method: extensive web searching across academic databases, archive
catalogues, mountaineering histories, folklore collections, and
journalism. Each section documents what was searched for, what was
found, and --- critically --- what was not found. The absences are
themselves findings.

*A warning*: Part E was always expected to yield the thinnest results.
The WP stated: "the absence is itself data." This proved accurate.
What follows is an honest accounting of a search through structured
silence.

** E.1.1 The Old Women of Rupal and the Peri Stories

*Searched for*: documentation of women as keepers of peri/fairy
stories in Rupal village and surrounding Diamer settlements; women's
spirit narratives from the Nanga Parbat region.

*Found*: Nothing specific to Rupal or Diamer women as peri
storytellers. The silence is near-total. What exists is contextual
and from adjacent regions:

- The /pari khan/ tradition (see below) documents women as spirit
  mediums in Chitral, establishing that women-spirit relationships are
  a recognized institution in the broader northern Pakistan region.
- Shamanic practice in Gilgit-Baltistan involves the /bitan/
  (Burushaski) or /dhinal/ (Shina) --- the shaman who communes with
  /pari/ (fairies/spirits) --- but published accounts focus
  overwhelmingly on male practitioners. The bitans "fall into a trance
  and converse with the Pari in the Shina language, while drinking
  blood from a freshly severed goat's head." The ritual requires
  specific music called /Shireen zuban/ ("sweet tongue"), played on
  dadang (drum), daamal, and surnai.
  [fn:bitan1] [[https://republicpolicy.com/shamanism-in-gilgit-baltistan/][Republic Policy: Shamanism in Gilgit Baltistan]]
  [fn:bitan2] [[https://www.youlinmagazine.com/story/shamanism-in-gilgit-baltistan/MjI0MA==][Youlin Magazine: Shamanism in Gilgit Baltistan]]
  [fn:bitan3] [[https://misbahayat.medium.com/shamanism-taming-fairies-of-the-north-66e7cbc44f90][Misbah Hayat: Shamanism --- Taming Fairies of the North]]

- Women's roles in spirit traditions are attested but undocumented.
  The /pari khan/ literature from Chitral (see E.1.2) strongly
  suggests analogous practices exist across the region, but no
  fieldworker appears to have specifically sought out elderly women in
  Diamer as spirit-narrative keepers.

*Not found*: Any published account from women of Rupal or Diamer
about their spirit traditions. Any named woman storyteller from the
region. Any recording or transcript of a woman telling a peri story
from the Nanga Parbat valleys.

*Assessment*: The peri stories almost certainly exist in living
memory. The gap is not in the tradition but in the documentation.
No one with the access, language skills, and intent has published
from this specific intersection.

** E.1.2 The Pari Khan Tradition

*Searched for*: pari khan tradition, women as spirit mediums in
northern Pakistan, specifically any evidence from Diamer district.

*Found*: A well-documented tradition --- but from Chitral, not Diamer:

The /pari khan/ is a woman who attains the status of spirit medium in
Chitral, Pakistan. The process begins when certain women experience
illnesses "locally interpreted as spirit possession." Treatment
involves either exorcism or "domestication" of the spirit. In the
latter case, the possessed woman learns to live with the spirit and
becomes its host --- a /pari khan/.[fn:pk1]

The social dynamics are striking: a woman who becomes a pari khan
"gains considerable attention and social status by virtue of her
alleged spiritual powers to heal various illnesses." She gains
"exemptions from regular household chores" and elevation "to the
status of matriarch," often with "monetary advantages within the
traditionally male-dominated society."[fn:pk2]

Academic analysis frames this as a response to patriarchal constraint:
the practice is "rooted in patriarchal culture and oppressive norms
that frustrate women to the extent of illnesses." Spirit mediumship
becomes an idiom through which women claim authority in a society
that otherwise denies it.[fn:pk3]

[fn:pk1] [[https://ojs.ahss.org.pk/journal/article/view/401][Annals of Human and Social Sciences: Unveiling the Healing Rituals of Spirit Media (Pari Khan) in Chitral]]
[fn:pk2] [[https://www.researchgate.net/publication/375992404][ResearchGate: Unveiling the Healing Rituals of Spirit Media (Pari Khan) in Chitral: A Dramaturgical Analysis]]
[fn:pk3] [[https://ideapublishers.org/index.php/jhsms/article/view/978][JHSMS: From Hostage to Host --- Spirit Possession, Mediumship, and Gender Relations in Chitral]]

*Does this exist in Diamer specifically?* No published evidence was
found. The /bitan/ tradition documented in Gilgit-Baltistan involves
both men and women, but published accounts overwhelmingly describe
male shamans. The structural similarity to the pari khan tradition is
suggestive --- both involve spirit mediumship, both use trance, both
involve healing --- but the specific gendered institution of the pari
khan as documented in Chitral has not been reported from Diamer in any
source found.

*The gap*: Chitral has been studied by Peter Parkes (University of
Kent) and others who specifically investigated spirit beliefs and
gender. Diamer has not received comparable ethnographic attention
to gender and spirit practice.

** E.1.3 Women's Accounts from the Region

*Searched for*: memoirs, oral histories, ethnographies of women from
Gilgit-Baltistan, gender studies of northern Pakistan.

*Found*:

- *Kathryn Besio (2005)*: "Telling Stories to Hear Autoethnography:
  Researching Women's Lives in Northern Pakistan." Published in
  /Gender, Place & Culture/, Vol. 12, No. 3. Research conducted in
  Askole village, 1997-1998. Uses sewing machines as an analytical
  tool for "conveying the 'real' effects of colonialism, imperialism,
  and globalization in the lives of women."[fn:besio]

- *Anna Grieser (2014)*: "Fieldwork Encounters: Being Foreign and
  Female in Gilgit." Published in /Ethnoscripts/, Vol. 16, No. 1
  (special issue: Anthropology of Gilgit-Baltistan). Examines the
  challenges of being a foreign female researcher in Gilgit's
  gender-segregated society, noting that "transgressions of
  gender-boundaries during fieldwork resulted in multiplying and
  uncontrollable rumors."[fn:grieser]

- *Humera Dinar (2020)*: An ethnographic study of women-led
  businesses in Gilgit-Baltistan examining gendered entrepreneurship.
  Published in /South Asian Survey/.[fn:dinar]

- *Purdue University dissertation*: "Development and Marginalization:
  Gender, Infrastructure, and State-making in Gilgit-Baltistan,
  Pakistan" --- an anthropological study described as "anthropology
  conducted at home" by a researcher from the region.[fn:purdue]

[fn:besio] [[https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09663690500202566][Gender, Place & Culture: Telling Stories to Hear Autoethnography]]
[fn:grieser] [[https://journals.sub.uni-hamburg.de/ethnoscripts/article/view/779][Ethnoscripts: Fieldwork Encounters: Being Foreign and Female in Gilgit]]
[fn:dinar] [[https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0973174120983129][South Asian Survey: Women-Led Businesses in Gilgit-Baltistan]]
[fn:purdue] [[https://hammer.purdue.edu/articles/thesis/Development_and_Marginalization_Gender_Infrastructure_and_State-making_in_Gilgit-Baltistan_Pakistan/12743876][Purdue: Development and Marginalization in Gilgit-Baltistan]]

*Not found*: Any published first-person account by a woman from
Diamer district. No memoir, no autobiography, no oral history
collection from Diamer women specifically. The Besio and Grieser
studies are from Askole (Baltistan) and Gilgit town respectively ---
not from the Shina-speaking Diamer heartland.

** E.1.4 Women Mountaineers on Nanga Parbat

*Searched for*: female summiteers of Nanga Parbat, their stories.

*Found*:

- *Liliane Barrard* (France): First woman to climb Nanga Parbat,
  reaching the summit on 27 June 1984 via the Rupal Face, without
  supplementary oxygen, alongside her husband Maurice Barrard. She
  later died descending K2 in 1986.[fn:barrard]

- *Wanda Rutkiewicz* (Poland): Summited Nanga Parbat in 1985. First
  woman to summit K2 (1986). Disappeared on Kangchenjunga in 1992.
  Her story is one of extraordinary determination --- and of the
  1980s as a particularly lethal decade for women on 8,000m
  peaks.[fn:rutkiewicz]

- *Naila Kiani and Samina Baig*: First Pakistani women to summit
  Nanga Parbat, 2 July 2023.[fn:naila]

- *Sheikha Asma Al Thani* (Qatar): First Qatari woman to summit
  Nanga Parbat, 2025.[fn:asma]

- *Klara Kolouchova* (Czech Republic): Pioneering Czech mountaineer
  who died on Nanga Parbat in 2025 during a summit attempt.[fn:klara]

- *Alison Hargreaves* (UK): Did not climb Nanga Parbat, but her
  story intersects with the mountain: she died on K2 in 1995, and
  her son Tom Ballard died on Nanga Parbat in 2019. The
  intergenerational tragedy is among the most haunting in
  mountaineering.[fn:alison]

[fn:barrard] [[https://www.guinnessworldrecords.com/world-records/665357-first-ascent-of-nanga-parbat-female][Guinness: First Female Ascent of Nanga Parbat]]
[fn:rutkiewicz] [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wanda_Rutkiewicz][Wikipedia: Wanda Rutkiewicz]]
[fn:naila] [[https://www.thenews.com.pk/latest/1086231-naila-kiani-becomes-first-pakistani-woman-to-scale-nanga-parbat][The News: Naila Kiani Becomes First Pakistani Woman to Scale Nanga Parbat]]
[fn:asma] [[https://www.nation.com.pk/09-Jul-2025/sheikha-asma-becomes-first-qatari-woman-to-summit-nanga-parbat][The Nation: Sheikha Asma Summits Nanga Parbat]]
[fn:klara] [[https://english.radio.cz/klara-kolouchova-tragedy-trailblazing-czech-mountaineer-dies-nanga-parbat-8855891][Radio Prague: Klara Kolouchova Dies on Nanga Parbat]]
[fn:alison] [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alison_Hargreaves][Wikipedia: Alison Hargreaves]]

*Note the pattern*: These are all foreign women. No woman from Diamer,
no woman from Gilgit-Baltistan, appears in the mountaineering history
of their own mountain until Naila Kiani and Samina Baig in 2023. The
mountain that towers over their villages was climbed by people from
France, Poland, Qatar --- but not by local women for nearly 40 years
after the first female ascent.

** E.1.5 Emily Overend Lorimer (1881--1949)

*Searched for*: Emily Lorimer's documentation of women's lives in
the Gilgit region.

*Found*: Emily Overend Lorimer was an Anglo-Irish journalist,
linguist, political analyst, and writer --- and one of the most
significant Western women to have lived in and written about the
northern areas of Pakistan.

She accompanied her husband D.L.R. Lorimer to Gilgit when he was
posted as Political Agent in 1920. Asked by his superior whether she
would be willing to go to the "hardship posting" of Gilgit, she
replied: "I'm willing to go with my husband to HELL."[fn:eol1]

During four years in Gilgit (1920-1924) and a return expedition
(1934-1935), Emily documented daily life extensively:

- /Language Hunting in the Karakoram/ (1939): Her account of the
  1934-35 stay in Hunza while D.L.R. studied Burushaski. Covers
  "houses, land, marriage customs, harvest festivals, crafts, and
  care of children" --- daily life of the Burusho people recorded
  with ethnographic attentiveness.[fn:eol2]
  *Available on archive.org*: [[https://archive.org/details/dli.pahar.2738][archive.org/details/dli.pahar.2738]]

- "Domestic and Outdoor Life in Hunza: A Peasant Community whose
  Language is Unique" and "The Crafts and Husbandry of the Burusho"
  --- illustrated articles in /The Illustrated London News/, October
  1935.

- The Lorimer papers are archived at SOAS via the Royal Asiatic
  Society.[fn:eol3]

[fn:eol1] [[https://foxedquarterly.com/lesley-downer-e-o-lorimer-literary-review/][Slightly Foxed: E. O. Lorimer]]
[fn:eol2] [[https://archive.org/details/dli.pahar.2738][Archive.org: Language Hunting in the Karakoram]]
[fn:eol3] [[https://royalasiaticsociety.org/david-lockhart-robertson-lorimer-and-emily-overend-lorimer/][Royal Asiatic Society: The Lorimer Papers]]

*Relevance to Diamer*: Emily Lorimer's work focuses on *Hunza*
(Burushaski-speaking), not on *Diamer* (Shina-speaking). Gilgit
served as the administrative centre; Hunza was the cultural focus.
Diamer/Chilas lies south and was less accessible, more conservative,
and less visited by colonial wives. Emily's documentation of women's
lives --- "marriage customs," "care of children," domestic craft ---
is among the richest from the broader region, but it does not extend
to the Shina-speaking communities of Diamer.

*Not found*: Any comparable female observer who spent extended time
in Diamer specifically and documented women's lives there.

** E.1.6 Female Researchers in Diamer

*Searched for*: female anthropologists or researchers who conducted
fieldwork specifically in Diamer district.

*Found*: Ruth Laila Schmidt has researched the Shina language of
Indus Kohistan (adjacent to Diamer) for 30+ years, collaborating
with Pakistani colleagues Muhammad Manzar Zarin and Razwal
Kohistani.[fn:schmidt] Her work on oral history (see E.4) is the
closest to sustained female scholarly presence in the linguistic
and cultural space of Diamer.

Anna Grieser and other contributors to the 2014 /Ethnoscripts/
special issue on "Anthropology of Gilgit-Baltistan" worked in Gilgit
proper, not Diamer.[fn:sokefeld]

[fn:schmidt] [[https://d1i1jdw69xsqx0.cloudfront.net/digitalhimalaya/collections/journals/ebhr/pdf/EBHR_25&26_03.pdf][Schmidt: The Oral History of the Darma Lineage of Indus Kohistan]]
[fn:sokefeld] [[https://www.ssoar.info/ssoar/bitstream/handle/document/61329/ssoar-ethnoscripts-2014-1-sokefeld-Anthropology_of_Gilgit-Baltistan_introduction.pdf][Sokefeld: Anthropology of Gilgit-Baltistan (Introduction)]]

*Not found*: Any female researcher who conducted sustained
ethnographic fieldwork in Diamer district and published results.
The sectarian violence of the 1980s-2000s (see E.5) made Diamer
particularly dangerous and difficult for fieldwork of any kind.

** E.2.1 The Structural Asymmetry

Mountaineering has been called "the most literary of all sports."
Climbers tend to be educated, articulate, and prolific writers.
Sherpas and local porters tend not to write books. The result is a
vast literature in which the people who carry the heaviest loads and
face the greatest risk per unit of pay are the least represented as
narrating subjects.

Sherry Ortner's /Life and Death on Mt. Everest: Sherpas and
Himalayan Mountaineering/ (Princeton, 2001) is the definitive
anthropological study of this asymmetry. Of the approximately 300
climbers who have died on Everest, more than one-third have been
Sherpas. Yet most published accounts focus on Western climbers.[fn:ortner]

In the specific case of Nanga Parbat, the asymmetry is extreme:
the German expeditions of the 1930s produced a substantial literature
in German and English --- expedition accounts, Alpine Journal
reports, Himalayan Journal articles, memoirs --- but the Sherpa
porters who died alongside (and in greater numbers than) the German
climbers left almost no textual trace in their own voices.

[fn:ortner] [[https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691074481/life-and-death-on-mt-everest][Princeton UP: Life and Death on Mt. Everest]]

** E.2.2 The 1934 Disaster: Named Dead

The 1934 expedition, led by Willy Merkl with backing from Nazi
Germany, ended in disaster when a storm trapped climbers and porters
above 7,000m. Three German climbers and six Sherpa porters died:

*The German dead*: Willy Merkl, Willo Welzenbach, Uli Wieland.

*The Sherpa dead*:
1. *Gaylay* --- died alongside Merkl, probably 14-15 July. They were
   stranded together above the Silver Saddle, unable to descend.
2. *Nima Norbu* --- perished 8 July during a forced bivouac at a
   secondary camp under the Silver Saddle.
3. *Dakshi* --- died in the secondary camp above Camp VII on the
   night of 11 July.
4. *Pintso Norbu (Pinzo Norbu)* --- died near Camp V "on the track
   from sheer exhaustion."
5. *Nima Dorje* --- died near Camp V, also of exhaustion on the
   descent.
6. *Nima Tashi* --- died near Camp V alongside Nima Dorje and Pintso
   Norbu.[fn:1934wiki][fn:1934hj]

[fn:1934wiki] [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1934_Nanga_Parbat_climbing_disaster][Wikipedia: 1934 Nanga Parbat Climbing Disaster]]
[fn:1934hj] [[https://www.himalayanclub.org/hj/7/2/the-german-himalayan-expedition-to-nanga-parbat-1934/][Himalayan Journal: The German Himalayan Expedition to Nanga Parbat, 1934]]

*The sole Sherpa survivor*: *Ang Tsering* (1905-2002). He survived
seven days battling through the storm and was the last man to reach
safety. Jonathan Neale interviewed Ang Tsering in 2000, when Tsering
was approximately 95 years old, for the book /Tigers of the Snow/
(2002). This is the single most important first-person Sherpa account
from the Nanga Parbat expeditions.[fn:neale]

*What we know about these men*: Almost nothing beyond their names,
the camps they died at, and the manner of their deaths. Gaylay's
loyalty in staying with Merkl is mentioned in every account as an
act of extraordinary devotion --- but it is always told from the
perspective of what it means for Merkl's story, not Gaylay's. Where
was Gaylay from? Did he have a family? What was his life before and
beyond Nanga Parbat? The record is silent.

[fn:neale] [[https://www.amazon.com/Tigers-Snow-Fateful-Sherpas-Mountaineering/dp/0312266235][Amazon: Tigers of the Snow by Jonathan Neale]]

** E.2.3 The 1937 Disaster: Sixteen Dead Under Avalanche

On approximately 14 June 1937, an avalanche struck Camp IV below
Rakhiot Peak, killing the entire team: seven Germans and nine
Sherpas. All sixteen bodies were buried under the snow.

*The German dead*: Karl Wien, Hans Hartmann, Adi Gottner, Gunther
Hepp, Pert Fankhauser, Martin Pfeffer, Peter Mullritter.

*The Sherpa dead*:
1. *Pasang* --- described as "one of their best porters"
2. *Nim Tsering*
3. *Mambahadur*
4. *Kami*
5. *Gyaljen Monjo*
6. *Jigmay*
7. *Chong Karma*
8. *Ang Tsering II*
9. *Da Thondup*
[fn:1937hj]

[fn:1937hj] [[https://www.himalayanclub.org/hj/10/11/nanga-parbat-1937/][Himalayan Journal: Nanga Parbat, 1937]]

*The asymmetry in death*: The German dead are remembered in alpine
club memorials, in expedition histories, in the narrative of German
mountaineering as national endeavour. The Sherpa dead are listed in
footnotes. None of the nine have biographical entries in any source
found. None have memorial plaques. Their families received whatever
compensation the expedition contract provided --- and then vanished
from the record.

** E.2.4 Local Chilas Porters

*Searched for*: names or oral histories of local men from Chilas who
carried loads for the German expeditions of the 1930s.

*Found*: The 1934 expedition specifically moved away from using local
porters. The Himalayan Journal records that the 1932 attempt "suffered
from the unreliability of local porters," which led the 1934
expedition to engage trained Sherpa and Bhutia porters from
Darjeeling.[fn:1934hj_porters]

The expedition did engage 35 Sherpa and Bhutia porters from
Darjeeling, described as "an elite corps of porters trained in
mountain craft and discipline by the British Mount Everest and German
Kangchenjunga Expeditions." Named among them: Lewa (first Sirdar),
Nima Thondup, Wangdi Nurbu, Pasang, Jigmay Isherung.[fn:1934hj_porters]

[fn:1934hj_porters] [[https://www.himalayanclub.org/hj/7/2/the-german-himalayan-expedition-to-nanga-parbat-1934/][Himalayan Journal: The German Himalayan Expedition to Nanga Parbat, 1934]]

*Not found*: Any names of local Chilas men who worked as low-altitude
porters or carriers for these expeditions. No oral histories from
Diamer families about their involvement in the expeditions. The local
men who carried loads from Chilas to base camp are entirely absent
from the record --- they fall below even the Sherpa porters in the
hierarchy of remembrance.

** E.2.5 Contemporary Porter/Guide Accounts

*Searched for*: published interviews, documentaries, or accounts from
contemporary porters and guides working in tourism around Nanga Parbat.

*Found*: Fragments, not narratives. The travel blog and trekking
literature names a few local guides but never centres their
perspective:

- *Ghani* — a guide on the Around Nanga Parbat / Mazeno La trek,
  described as "the best friend of Tomek Mackiewicz in Pakistan."
  The trekker notes: "We spent a lot of time together and I enjoyed
  his stories about Tomek" — stories about the Polish climber who
  died on Nanga Parbat in the 2018 winter attempt. Ghani's own
  story — where he is from, how he became a guide, what the mountain
  means to him — is not recorded.[fn:mazeno]
- *Faquir* and *Aid* — named as guide companions on the same trek.
  No biographical detail.[fn:mazeno]

The pattern is consistent: local guides appear as named supporting
characters in Western trekking accounts, never as narrating subjects.
Their knowledge of the mountain — routes, weather, local names for
features, spirit traditions — is consumed but not documented.

No equivalent of the Sherpa memoir tradition exists for local Diamer
guides. No "porter's-eye view" of modern tourism on Nanga Parbat has
been published.

[fn:mazeno] [[https://www.thetrekblog.com/blog/2025/7/29/nanga-parbat-mazeno-la][Pakistan Nanga Parbat — Mazeno La — The Trek Blog (2025)]]

** E.2.6 The Porter Literature

*Searched for*: Elizabeth Hawley's archives, the Himalayan Database,
oral history projects focused on porters.

*Found*:

- *Elizabeth Hawley (1923-2018)* and the *Himalayan Database*: Hawley
  spent 60 years in Kathmandu documenting every Himalayan expedition.
  Her archive, digitised by Richard Salisbury, contains expedition
  records, member lists, and outcomes. It covers Nepal Himalaya
  primarily --- Nanga Parbat, being in Pakistan, falls partially
  outside her systematic coverage. The database is invaluable for
  statistical analysis of deaths, routes, and outcomes --- but it
  records climbers and Sherpas as data points, not as narrating
  subjects.[fn:hawley]

- *Jonathan Neale, /Tigers of the Snow/ (2002)*: The single most
  important attempt to recover Sherpa voices from Nanga Parbat.
  Neale interviewed about 20 retired climbing Sherpas and sirdars,
  "as well as in some cases their wives or widows." His central
  informant was Ang Tsering. The book reframes the 1934 expedition
  from the Sherpa perspective, describing it as "the decisive moment
  in the transformation of 'coolies' into the Sherpas as climbing
  legends."[fn:neale2]

- *Ang Tharkay memoir*: /Sherpa: The Memoir of Ang Tharkay/ --- an
  autobiography by one of the most renowned Sherpas of early
  Himalayan exploration (born 1908), long available only in French,
  recently translated to English. Not specific to Nanga Parbat but
  part of the thin porter-memoir tradition.[fn:tharkay]

[fn:hawley] [[https://www.himalayandatabase.com/history.html][The Himalayan Database: History]]
[fn:neale2] [[https://www.kirkusreviews.com/book-reviews/jonathan-neale/tigers-of-the-snow/][Kirkus Reviews: Tigers of the Snow]]
[fn:tharkay] [[https://www.everand.com/book/846257687/Sherpa-The-Memoir-of-Ang-Tharkay][Everand: Sherpa --- The Memoir of Ang Tharkay]]

*The broader picture*: As one journalist summarised, "Sherpas' real
stories are seldom written."[fn:mongabay] The porter memoir is an
almost-empty genre. Climbers write books; porters carry them.

[fn:mongabay] [[https://news.mongabay.com/2022/08/sherpas-real-stories-are-seldom-written-qa-with-authors-ankit-babu-adhikari-pradeep-bashyal/][Mongabay: Sherpas' Real Stories Are Seldom Written]]

** E.3.1 The Question

Does the Shina oral tradition have its own love stories, analogous to
Heer Ranjha (Punjabi), Sassi Punnun (Sindhi), Sohni Mahiwal
(Punjabi/Sindhi), Adam Khan and Durkhanai (Pashto), or Laila Majnun
(Persian)?

*Answer*: Yes. At least one has been found, and traces of others exist.

** E.3.2 Yurmas ga Malang: The Shina Love Story

*Khalifa Rehmat Jan Malang* (1885-1971) of SherQilla, Punial, Ghizer
district, was a scholar and legendary Sufi poet who wrote in Shina
and Persian. His love story --- /Yurmas ga Malang/ --- "has earned a
distinguished place in Shina poetry and literature, comparable to
timeless tales such as Heer Ranjha in Punjabi, Sassi Punnuh in
Sindhi, Adam Khan and Durkhanai in Pashto, and Layla Majnun in
Persian."[fn:malang1]

The narrative: Khalifa Rehmat Jan harboured deep love for a maiden
named Yurmas but remained silent during her lifetime. Yurmas died of
illness, and in his anguish he channelled his grief into poetry ---
becoming "the pioneer of contemporary Shina poetry." His work earned
him the epithet "SherQilla ka Malang" ("the Malang of SherQilla").[fn:malang2]

A book, /Malangay Samutari/, was published in 2012 and launched at
Karakoram International University in Gilgit, collecting and
analysing his work.[fn:malang3]

[fn:malang1] [[https://muqadima.wordpress.com/2017/09/08/khalifa-rehmad-jan-malang-a-legend-1885-to-1971/][Voice of Gilgit Baltistan: Khalifa Rehmat Jan Malang, a Legend]]
[fn:malang2] [[https://medium.com/@muazam.sapru5/yourmas-ga-malang-4058da1ae125][Muazam Sapru: Yurmas ga Malang]]
[fn:malang3] [[https://pamirtimes.net/2012/11/17/malangay-samutari-launched-at-kiu-gilgit/][Pamir Times: Malangay Samutari Launched at KIU]]

*Significance*: This is a real love story, not a folktale --- the
poet lived and died within living memory. It is the Shina
equivalent of the great romances of the subcontinent. But note: it
comes from Ghizer district (Punial), not from Diamer. The
Shina-speaking area is broad; Diamer is one part of it.

** E.3.3 Burushaski Folktale Romances

*Searched for*: love stories in Lorimer's Burushaski folktale

** E.4.2 Fragments That Exist

*** Ghulam Muhammad (1905)

/Festivals and Folklore of Gilgit/ (1905), published in /Memoirs of
the Asiatic Society of Bengal/. Available on archive.org.[fn:ghulam]
This is among the earliest published collections of folklore from the
Gilgit region, predating most Western ethnographic work. Covers Gilgit
proper, not Diamer specifically.

[fn:ghulam] [[https://archive.org/details/cu31924023651064][Archive.org: Festivals and Folklore of Gilgit --- Ghulam Muhammad]]

*** D.L.R. Lorimer (1935)

/The Burushaski Language/, Vol. II --- texts and translations in
Burushaski. Burushaski, not Shina; Hunza, not Diamer. But part of
the broader knowledge ecology of the region. (See E.3.4.)

*** Karl Jettmar (1955--1990s)

Jettmar conducted fieldwork in northwestern Pakistan from 1955 to
the mid-1970s. His primary focus was petroglyphs and rock
inscriptions, but his ethnographic work touched on oral traditions
insofar as they related to the petroglyphic record:

- /Beyond the Gorges of the Indus: Archaeology Before Excavation/
  (2002) --- Jettmar's major synthesis, available on archive.org.[fn:jettmar1]
- "Non-Buddhist Traditions in the Petroglyphs of the Indus Valley"
  (1985) --- published by Heidelberg University.[fn:jettmar2]
- /Antiquities of Northern Pakistan/, Vols. 1-2 (1989+), with
  D. Konig and V. Thewalt --- rock inscriptions of the Indus
  Valley.[fn:jettmar3]

Jettmar documented connections between petroglyphs and local oral
traditions, but his work is primarily archaeological, not
ethnographic. He records what the rocks say, not what the people say
about themselves.

[fn:jettmar1] [[https://archive.org/details/dli.pahar.3734][Archive.org: Beyond the Gorges of the Indus]]
[fn:jettmar2] [[https://archiv.ub.uni-heidelberg.de/propylaeumdok/6457/1/Jettmar_Non_Buddhist_traditions_1985.pdf][Heidelberg: Non-Buddhist Traditions in Petroglyphs]]
[fn:jettmar3] See Jettmar, Konig, Thewalt. /Antiquities of Northern Pakistan/. Verlag Philipp von Zabern, 1989.

*** Ahmad Hasan Dani (1920--2009)

The pioneer of rock art studies in Pakistan. His key works on
Diamer:

- /Chilas: The City of Naga Parvat (Dyamar)/ --- the most focused
  study of Chilas in the archaeological literature.
- /Human Records on the Karakorum Highway/ --- broader scope.
- Leader of the Pak-German Study Group (with Jettmar and Harald
  Hauptmann) from 1980, conducting archaeological explorations in
  Diamer and Hunza.[fn:dani]

*Oral traditions*: Dani's work is primarily archaeological. His
publications document rock carvings and inscriptions, occasionally
referencing oral traditions that relate to the sites, but no
systematic collection of oral history appears in the sources
located.

[fn:dani] [[https://www.dawn.com/news/948707][Dawn: Dr Dani's Contribution to History and Archaeology]]

*** Carla F. Radloff with Shakil Ahmad Shakil (1998)

/Folktales in the Shina of Gilgit: Text, Grammatical Analysis and
Commentary/ (SIL, Islamabad, 1998). Part of the "Studies in
Languages of Northern Pakistan" series, Vol. 2. The 199-page volume
contains folktales recorded in Shina from the Gilgit region, with
both original texts and grammatical analysis.[fn:radloff]

This is the *closest thing to a systematic Shina folktale collection*
found. However: it covers Gilgit, not Diamer specifically, and it
was produced primarily as a linguistic resource (grammatical analysis
is the frame), not as an ethnographic folklore collection.

*Available as PDF from SIL*: [[https://www.sil.org/system/files/reapdata/13/82/56/138256623622760949003113935743299913976/742_CRadloff_Folktales_Gilgit_of_Shina_1998.pdf][SIL: Folktales in the Shina of Gilgit]]

[fn:radloff] [[https://www.sil.org/resources/archives/742][SIL Global: Folktales in the Shina of Gilgit]]

*** Ruth Laila Schmidt (2003)

"The Oral History of the Darma Lineage of Indus Kohistan." Published
in /European Bulletin of Himalayan Research/ (EBHR), Nos. 25/26
(2003/2004).

Schmidt documents legends of Shina speakers in Kohistan describing a
"tribal revolt" by ancestors of the Darma lineage in which former
autocratic rulers were killed. This event, which also marked "the
victory over pre-Islamic religion," is "vividly remembered by the
elders as the foundational moment of local rule."[fn:schmidt2]

Schmidt's careful analysis suggests the events can be dated to the
early nineteenth century, but "the stories retain the memory of an
ancient kingdom dating back about a thousand years." Her key insight:
"Oral history, though not easily linked to a precise time-axis, is
a storehouse of transmuted memories which are kept alive even in
periods of fundamental change."

[fn:schmidt2] [[https://d1i1jdw69xsqx0.cloudfront.net/digitalhimalaya/collections/journals/ebhr/pdf/EBHR_25&26_03.pdf][EBHR: The Oral History of the Darma Lineage]]

** E.4.6 The Diamer Basha Dam Threat

One crucial context for oral tradition: the Diamer Basha Dam, under
construction on the Indus, will inundate 37,051 rock carvings on
5,928 boulders --- carvings dating from 8,000 BCE to the 16th
century CE, in Brahmi, Sogdian, Middle Persian, Chinese, Tibetan,
and Hebrew scripts.[fn:dam1]

WAPDA has signed a Rs 46.5 million agreement for digitisation and 3D
modelling of "significant rock carvings within the impact zone."
But there is *no comparable effort to document the oral traditions*
of the communities that will be displaced. The carvings are being
digitised; the stories are not.[fn:dam2]

In 2015, vandals separately destroyed centuries-old rock carvings at
Chilas, suggesting that even the physical heritage is not safe.[fn:dam3]

[fn:dam1] [[https://dialogue.earth/en/water/pakistans-diamer-basha-dam-will-drown-ancient-carvings/][Dialogue Earth: Pakistan's Diamer Basha Dam Will Drown Ancient Carvings]]

[fn:dam3] [[https://pamirtimes.net/2015/01/29/chilas-vandals-destroy-centuries-old-rock-carvings/][Pamir Times: Vandals Destroy Centuries-Old Rock Carvings at Chilas]]

** E.5.1 The Paradox

Nanga Parbat is among the most documented mountains in the world.
The German expeditions of the 1930s alone produced thousands of pages
of expedition literature. Hermann Buhl's solo first ascent in 1953
is one of the great stories of mountaineering. The mountain's geology
has been studied by teams from Lehigh, ETH Zurich, and Heidelberg.
Its petroglyphs have been catalogued in multi-volume scholarly series.

And yet: the community that lives in its shadow --- the Shina-speaking
people of Diamer --- has almost no presence in the published record
as narrating subjects. They are documented, not documenting.
Described, not describing. Present as backdrop, not as voice.

This is not accidental. It is structural.

** E.5.2 Seven Causes of Silence

*** 1. Oral vs. Literate: The Missing Script

Shina had no standardised written form until the late 20th century.
T. Grahame Bailey produced the first scholarly grammar in 1924 using
Roman transliteration. Various proposed orthographies followed in
the 1960s, but "only in the late 2010s has Shina orthography been
standardised and primers as well as dictionaries endorsed by the
territorial government of Gilgit-Baltistan have been published."[fn:script]

This means: for most of recorded history, Shina speakers could not
write in their own language. The choice was to compose in Urdu,
Persian, or English --- all of which required education in a
non-native medium --- or to remain within oral tradition. The
written record of the region is therefore necessarily produced by
outsiders or by locals writing in languages not their own.

[fn:script] [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shina_language][Wikipedia: Shina Language --- Orthography]]

*** 2. Colonial Extraction of Knowledge

The pattern is consistent across the colonial Himalaya: outsiders
arrive, observe, document, and publish. The British political agents,
German mountaineers, Austrian ethnographers, and Norwegian linguists
who studied the Gilgit-Baltistan region all produced knowledge *about*
local people. The local people themselves did not produce knowledge
*for* the outside world.

Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak's question --- "Can the Subaltern Speak?"
(1988) --- resonates here with particular force. Spivak argues that
the subaltern (the structurally excluded) cannot "speak" in the
sense of having their perspective "recognised as meaningful and
authoritative" within dominant institutions, archives, and
theoretical frameworks. "Epistemic violence" operates not through
physical force but through "defining what counts as knowledge, who
has the right to produce knowledge, and what experiences are
considered valid."[fn:spivak]

The Diamer case is a textbook illustration. The knowledge that
"counts" about Nanga Parbat is geological, mountaineering, and
archaeological. The knowledge that exists in Shina-speaking oral
tradition --- about spirits, about the mountain's character, about
love and loss and the meaning of the landscape --- does not register
as knowledge in the institutional apparatus.

[fn:spivak] [[https://jan.ucc.nau.edu/~sj6/Spivak%20CanTheSubalternSpeak.pdf][Spivak: Can the Subaltern Speak? (PDF)]]

*** 3. Gender Barriers to Publication

In a society with strict gender segregation --- where female
ethnographers report that "transgressions of gender-boundaries
during fieldwork resulted in multiplying and uncontrollable
rumors"[fn:grieser2] --- the knowledge held by women is doubly
excluded. Women's stories are told to other women, in domestic
spaces, in a language without a standard script, in a region
difficult to access, in a cultural context where female researchers
face extreme methodological challenges.

The pari khan tradition documented in Chitral (E.1.2) demonstrates
that women's spirit knowledge is real, institutionalised, and socially
significant. The near-total absence of equivalent documentation from
Diamer reflects not the absence of the tradition but the absence of
anyone positioned to document it.

[fn:grieser2] [[https://journals.sub.uni-hamburg.de/ethnoscripts/article/view/779][Ethnoscripts: Fieldwork Encounters]]

*** 4. Political Instability and Sectarian Violence

Diamer has been among the most dangerous districts in
Gilgit-Baltistan. In the 1988 Gilgit Massacre, a Sunni /lashkar/
comprising thousands from Mansehra, Chilas, and Kohistan attacked
Shia communities; estimates of dead range from 150 to 900.[fn:sect1]

The violence intensified in the 2000s and 2010s:
- February 2012: 18 Shia pilgrims killed on the Karakoram Highway
  in Kohistan.
- April 2012: 20 killed at Chilas.
- August 2012: 22 killed near Babusar Pass.[fn:sect2]

General Zia ul Haq's Islamisation policies (1977-1988) "injected
sectarian poison in Gilgit-Baltistan, especially in Diamer."[fn:sect3]

This sustained violence made Diamer extremely dangerous for
fieldwork. Foreign researchers stayed away. Local researchers
faced threats from militant groups. The window for ethnographic
documentation --- never wide --- closed further with each cycle of
violence.

[fn:sect1] [[https://www.files.ethz.ch/isn/143360/IB_GilgitBaltistan.pdf][IDSA: Sectarian Strife in Gilgit-Baltistan]]
[fn:sect2] [[https://herald.dawn.com/news/1398713][Dawn Herald: The Relentless Spread of Religious Violence in Diamer District]]
[fn:sect3] [[https://pamirtimes.net/2013/08/16/why-anxious-about-chilas/][Pamir Times: Why Anxious About Chilas?]]

*** 5. The Karakoram Highway Paradox

The completion of the Karakoram Highway in 1978 opened Diamer to
vehicular traffic for the first time. The expectation might be that
accessibility would lead to cultural documentation. The opposite
occurred: the highway brought modernisation, monetisation of the
subsistence economy, introduction of consumer goods, and the erosion
of traditional lifeways --- faster than documentation could
capture them.[fn:kkh]

The Aga Khan Trust for Culture noted that "increased accessibility,
coupled with the impact of tourism, introduced a rapid
transformation of local customs and economic patterns."[fn:aktc2] The
highway delivered not documentation but disruption.

[fn:kkh] [[https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/modern-asian-studies/article/abs/karakoram-highway-the-impact-of-road-construction-on-mountain-societies/7D2CCD12E734018D0B1E40ED015D2A7F][Cambridge: The Karakoram Highway --- Impact of Road Construction on Mountain Societies]]
[fn:aktc2] [[https://the.akdn/en/where-we-work/south-asia/pakistan/conservation-and-development-in-gilgit-baltistan-pakistan][AKDN: Conservation and Development in Gilgit-Baltistan]]

*** 6. Language Barriers in Published Research

Most research on Diamer is published in English or German --- in
journals, monographs, and databases inaccessible to the communities
being studied. The Heidelberg rock-art volumes, the Alpine Journal
expedition reports, the Norwegian linguistic studies --- none of
these are available to a Shina-speaking reader in Chilas.

Knowledge flows in one direction: out of Diamer, into Western
academic institutions. It does not flow back. The communities that
are the subject of study cannot read the studies.

*** 7. The Digital Divide

What exists online about Diamer is overwhelmingly produced by
outsiders: mountaineering bloggers, travel agencies, geological
researchers, heritage organizations. The local digital presence is
thin: some Shina music on SoundCloud and Spotify[fn:digital1], some
Facebook pages, some YouTube content. But the deep oral tradition ---
the stories, the songs, the spirit narratives --- exists "only in
oral memory," as the WP warned, not in any searchable digital form.

The Diamer Basha Dam digitisation project (E.4.6) is telling: Rs
46.5 million for 3D scanning of rock carvings, Rs 0 (as far as can
be determined) for recording the oral traditions of the people who
live among those carvings.

[fn:digital1] [[https://www.gbsongs.net/][GB Songs: Gilgit-Baltistan Music]]

** E.5.3 The Compounding Effect

These seven factors do not operate independently. They compound:

- A woman in Diamer speaks a language that had no standard script
  until the 2010s, in a region made dangerous by sectarian violence,
  in a society where gender segregation limits her contact with
  researchers, in a country where research funding flows to
  geological surveys and dam engineering rather than oral history, in
  a world where the published record requires English or German or
  Urdu --- none of which is her first language.

- A porter on Nanga Parbat in 1934 carried loads for an expedition
  backed by Nazi Germany, in a language (Nepali/Sherpa) that had
  limited literary tradition, for researchers who saw him as labour
  rather than informant, in a system where his death was recorded as
  a data point and his life was not recorded at all.

The silence is not a single failure. It is a /system of exclusion/
with multiple reinforcing mechanisms. Each barrier alone might be
overcome; together they produce near-total erasure.

** E.5.4 What Spivak's Framework Reveals

Spivak's concept of "epistemic violence" clarifies what is happening:
the violence is not in the absence of speech but in the absence of
/listening structures/. The women of Rupal have stories. The porters
of 1934 had lives. The oral tradition of Diamer is (or was) alive.
The problem is not that these voices do not exist but that no
apparatus exists to receive them as /knowledge/.

The published record is not a neutral repository of what is known.
It is a shaped artefact reflecting who had power, who had literacy,
who had access to journals and publishers, who was considered a
valid knowledge-producer. By these criteria, the Shina-speaking
communities of Diamer --- especially women, especially the poor,
especially those outside the colonial-academic network --- were
systematically excluded.

"The most documented mountain in the Karakoram has the
least-documented local community" is not a paradox. It is a
predictable consequence of who does the documenting and why.

** E.5.5 What Remains Possible

The oral tradition is not dead. It is merely unrecorded.

Ruth Laila Schmidt's work demonstrates that sustained, respectful,
linguistically competent engagement with Shina-speaking communities
can recover oral histories of remarkable depth --- "a storehouse of
transmuted memories kept alive even in periods of fundamental
change."

The window is narrowing. The elders who remember the old stories are
aging. The Karakoram Highway, the Diamer Basha Dam, smartphones,
and satellite television are transforming the cultural landscape
faster than at any point in history. What is not recorded in the
next decade may be lost.

But something could still be done. A project with:
- Shina-speaking female researchers
- Recording equipment and transcription support
- Community consent and participation
- Publication in Shina as well as English
- Long time horizons (years, not weeks)

...could still recover much of what this survey has documented as
absent. The structural silence is man-made. It can, in principle,
be un-made.
