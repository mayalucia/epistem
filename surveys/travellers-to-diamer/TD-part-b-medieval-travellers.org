* Overview

This survey covers the period from the arrival of Islam to the end of Dogra
expansion: roughly 700--1850 CE. The Diamer region during this long span
was never the centre of any empire. It sat at the edge --- north of Kashmir,
south of Gilgit, west of Baltistan --- a zone of Shina-speaking independent
communities through which trade routes threaded and armies occasionally
passed. The question for each traveller or ruler is: did they actually reach
Diamer, or did they describe it from a distance?

The sections proceed chronologically: Arab geographers who knew the
subcontinent's northern mountains from secondary knowledge; Central Asian
and local rulers who actually controlled the territory; Mughals whose
empire reached Kashmir but whose grip on the mountains beyond was tenuous;
and finally the Sikh and Dogra campaigns that brought the region into the
orbit of Lahore and then Jammu.

#+begin_quote
*Epistemic note*: For much of this period, Diamer itself produced no
written records. The Shina-speaking communities of Chilas, Darel, Tangir,
and their tributaries were pre-literate until Islamic conversion brought
Arabic script. Our sources are overwhelmingly external --- Chinese annals,
Persian chronicles, Mughal administrative documents, and British military
reports looking backward. The rock inscriptions and petroglyphs (50,000+
at sites along the Indus in Diamer district) are the territory's own voice,
but they are pre-Islamic and fall silent around the 10th--11th century.
#+end_quote

* B.1 Arab Geographers and Travellers

** B.1.1 The Problem of Distance

The great Arab geographers of the 9th--12th centuries produced remarkable
maps and geographical encyclopaedias, but their knowledge of the mountains
north of Kashmir was indirect. The Hindu Kush and Karakoram lay beyond the
frontiers of the Abbasid Caliphate. Travellers who crossed into India
typically used the passes of Afghanistan (Khyber, Bolan, or Khawak), well
south of the Gilgit-Diamer corridor. No Arab geographer is known to have
visited Diamer directly.

** B.1.2 Al-Masudi (c. 896--956)

- *Full name*: Abu al-Hasan Ali ibn al-Husayn al-Masudi
- *Origin*: Baghdad
- *Active*: 10th century; died c. 956 CE
- *Key work*: /Muruj al-Dhahab wa-Ma'adin al-Jawhar/ ("The Meadows of Gold
  and Mines of Gems")
- *Relevance to Diamer*: Indirect

Al-Masudi has been called the "Herodotus of the Arabs." He travelled
extensively --- to the Indus Valley, the western coast of India, Sri Lanka,
East Africa, and the Caspian. His /Meadows of Gold/ combines universal
history with scientific geography and includes descriptions of India's
rivers (Indus, Ganges) and mountains. He depicted the Indus and Ganges on
his maps, with Sri Lanka at the subcontinent's southern tip.

However, al-Masudi's India travels focused on the Deccan plateau, the
western coast, and the Indus valley lowlands. There is no evidence that he
reached the upper Indus in the Diamer-Gilgit region, or that he described
the mountains north of Kashmir with any specificity.

** B.1.3 Al-Biruni (973--1048)

- *Full name*: Abu Rayhan Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Biruni
- *Origin*: Khwarezm (modern Uzbekistan)
- *Active*: Came to India with Mahmud of Ghazni's forces, 1017--1030 CE
- *Key work*: /Tarikh al-Hind/ ("Kitab al-Hind") completed c. 1030 CE
- *Relevance to Diamer*: Significant but indirect

Al-Biruni is the Arab-world scholar most likely to have known of the
mountains north of Kashmir. He spent over a decade in India, studied
Sanskrit, and engaged deeply with Indian science and geography. His
geographical contributions are remarkable:

*** What al-Biruni knew about the northern mountains

1. *Kashmir*: He characterised Kashmir as "a plateau surrounded by high
   inaccessible mountains" and recognised its strategic position bridging
   Tibetan culture, Inner Asia, and the Indian subcontinent. He noted
   restricted access to the valley.

2. *Mountain height measurement*: At Nandana Fort in the Salt Range (Pind
   Dadan Khan, modern Pakistan), al-Biruni devised a trigonometric method
   to measure the Earth's radius by observing the height of a mountain and
   the dip of the horizon. His result (equivalent to ~3,929 English miles)
   was 99% accurate. This demonstrates his sophisticated understanding of
   mountain topography.

3. *The Indus sources*: Al-Biruni records descriptions of Kashmir and the
   source of the Indus, suggesting awareness of the river's northern
   headwaters.

4. *Northern frontier*: He was cognisant of the geostrategic position of
   Kashmir along vital trade corridors connecting the Indian subcontinent
   with Inner Asia.

*** Did he mention Diamer or Nanga Parbat specifically?

No direct reference to Diamer, Chilas, or the mountain later called Nanga
Parbat has been identified in the available translations of /Kitab al-Hind/.
His description of the mountains north of Kashmir is general --- "high
inaccessible mountains" --- rather than naming specific peaks or valleys.
The Gilgit region was under Buddhist or Tibetan influence at this time,
outside the sphere of the Ghaznavid campaigns that brought al-Biruni to
India.

** B.1.4 Al-Idrisi (1100--1165)

- *Full name*: Abu Abdullah Muhammad al-Idrisi
- *Origin*: Ceuta (modern Morocco)
- *Active*: Court of King Roger II, Palermo, Sicily
- *Key work*: /Nuzhat al-Mushtaq fi Ikhtiraq al-Afaq/ ("Tabula Rogeriana"),
  completed 1154 CE
- *Relevance to Diamer*: Minimal

Al-Idrisi produced the most advanced medieval world map, engraved on a
six-foot silver disc over 15 years of work at the Norman court in Sicily.
His geography divides the known world into seven climatic zones and
seventy sections. He drew on earlier Arab geographers plus reports from
Norman travellers and merchants.

Al-Idrisi's maps include the Indian subcontinent, but his knowledge of the
mountains north of Kashmir was derived from earlier sources rather than
direct observation. No specific reference to the Gilgit-Diamer region has
been identified in the /Tabula Rogeriana/.

- *Primary source*: Description at Library of Congress

  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_al-Idrisi
- *Reference*: National Geographic education resource
  https://education.nationalgeographic.org/resource/al-idrisi/

** B.1.5 Ibn Battuta (1304--1369)

- *Full name*: Abu Abdullah Muhammad Ibn Battuta
- *Origin*: Tangier, Morocco
- *Journey to India*: 1332--1333 CE
- *Key work*: /Rihla/ ("A Gift to Those Who Contemplate the Wonders of Cities
  and the Marvels of Travelling"), dictated to Ibn Juzayy
- *Relevance to Diamer*: None --- he did not pass through the region

*** Ibn Battuta's actual route to India

Ibn Battuta crossed into India via Afghanistan, not via the northern
mountains. His route:

1. From Khorasan through Afghanistan
2. Crossed the Hindu Kush via the *Khawak Pass* at ~4,000 m / 13,000 ft
3. Through Ghazni and Kabul
4. Reached the Indus River on *12 September 1333*
5. Entered Sindh, then proceeded to Multan, Lahore, and Delhi

*** The Hindu Kush passage

Ibn Battuta's account of the Hindu Kush is vivid. He wrote:

#+begin_quote
"After this I proceeded to the city of Barwan, in the road to which is a
high mountain, covered with snow and exceedingly cold; they call it the
Hindu Kush, that is Hindu-slayer, because most of the slaves brought
thither from India die on account of the intenseness of the cold."
#+end_quote

This passage gives us the famous folk etymology of "Hindu Kush." But the
Khawak Pass lies far to the west of Diamer --- it is in the Afghan Hindu Kush,
near Baghlan, some 500 km west of Chilas. Ibn Battuta's Indus crossing was
in Sindh, at the southern end of the river, not in the northern gorges near
Diamer.

*** Assessment

Ibn Battuta came nowhere near Diamer. His route through Afghanistan to
Sindh bypassed the entire Karakoram-Western Himalaya mountain system.
He is irrelevant to Diamer's specific history, but his Hindu Kush passage
provides the best medieval Arab account of the broader mountain system.

- *Primary source*: /Rihla/ of Ibn Battuta, Gibb translation, available via
  Internet Archive and multiple publishers
- *Reference*: Wikipedia, "The Travels of Ibn Battuta"
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Travels_of_Ibn_Battuta
- *Reference*: Britannica, "Ibn Battuta"
  https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ibn-Battuta
- *Reference*: The Conversation, "Ibn Battuta: a 14th-century judge..."

None of these travellers visited or specifically described Diamer. Al-Biruni
comes closest, with his description of Kashmir's "high inaccessible mountains"
to the north and his knowledge of the Indus sources. The Diamer region in
this period was under Buddhist (Patola Shahi) and then Tibetan influence,
outside the circuits of Arab-world travellers.

* B.2 Central Asian and Local Rulers

** B.2.1 The Patola Shahi Dynasty (c. 6th--8th century CE)

- *Also known as*: Palola Shahi, Gilgit Shahis, rulers of "Little Bolur" (Chinese: 小勃律)
- *Religion*: Vajrayana Buddhism
- *Capital*: Gilgit
- *Period*: c. 6th century -- c. 750 CE
- *Relevance to Diamer*: High --- they controlled the route through the region

*** Historical context

The Patola Shahis were a Buddhist dynasty ruling from Gilgit over a kingdom
located on the strategic trans-Himalayan trade route (now the Karakoram
Highway corridor). Their kingdom controlled the passage between Central Asia,
Tibet, and the Indian subcontinent. Chinese Tang dynasty records refer to
the kingdom as "Bolur" (勃律), subdivided into Greater Bolur (Baltistan)
and Lesser Bolur (Gilgit).

*** Known rulers

The first known Patola Shahi ruler, per Tang annals, was King *Somana*
(6th century CE). The best-documented ruler is *Navasurendraditya-nandin*
(r. c. 644--655 CE), whose reign is attested by numerous Sanskrit
inscriptions, including the Danyor Rock Inscriptions on the left bank of
the Gilgit River.

*** The Gilgit Manuscripts

In 1931, cattle grazers discovered a wooden box inside a circular chamber
within a Buddhist stupa near Gilgit. It contained birch-bark manuscripts
dating to the 5th--6th century CE --- Buddhist canonical texts including the
Lotus Sutra. A second set was excavated at Naupur in 1938, along with
Buddhist bronze cult objects bearing Patola Shahi dedicatory inscriptions.
These are among the oldest surviving manuscript collections in South Asia.

*** Tibet-Tang rivalry over Gilgit

The Diamer-Gilgit region became a pawn in the great-power rivalry between
the Tibetan Empire and Tang China:

- *656 CE*: Tibetan Empire attacks Patola Shahis
- *Late 600s*: Tibet wrests control from Tang dynasty
- *737/738*: Tibetan troops under Minister Bel Kyesang Dongtsab conquer
  Little Palola
- *747 CE*: Tang general *Gao Xianzhi* (a Korean-origin general serving
  Tang) leads a remarkable campaign of ~10,000 cavalry across the Pamirs,
  defeats Tibet's forces, captures the Patola Shahi king and queen, and
  temporarily restores Tang control over the Gilgit passes
- *755 CE*: The An Lushan Rebellion forces Tang withdrawal from Central Asia;
  Chinese influence in the region collapses permanently

*** Connection to Diamer

The Patola Shahis controlled the Indus corridor through which modern Diamer
lies. The tens of thousands of Buddhist petroglyphs along the Indus at
Chilas, Thalpan, and Shatial --- documented by the Pakistan-German
Archaeological Mission (Karl Jettmar from 1980, Harald Hauptmann from 1989)
--- were carved during and around the Patola Shahi period. These include
stupas, lotus-seated Bodhisattvas (especially Maitreya), and inscriptions
in Kharoshthi, Brahmi, Sogdian, and other scripts. An estimated 50,000
petroglyphs and 5,000 inscriptions have been documented across northern
Pakistan's Karakoram Highway corridor.

The rock art evidence tells us that the Indus valley through Diamer was a
major thoroughfare for Buddhist travellers, merchants, and pilgrims during
the Patola Shahi period. Diamer was not a backwater --- it was on a silk
road branch.

** B.2.2 The Decline of Buddhism and the Transition Period (8th--14th century)

The collapse of Patola Shahi power after the Tang withdrawal left the
Diamer-Gilgit region in a political vacuum. The archaeological evidence
from rock art tells a clear story:

- *8th century*: Iranian (possibly Tibetan) influence replaces Buddhist
  patronage. Production of petroglyphs declines.
- *9th--10th century*: A completely new ethnic element appears along the
  Indus at Chilas sites, with radically different artistic styles,
  indicating that Buddhism had lost its prominent position.
- *11th--12th century*: The last datable pre-Islamic monuments. After this,
  the rock-carving tradition ceases entirely.

This transition period is poorly documented in written sources. The Shina-
speaking communities who inhabit Diamer today were likely establishing
themselves during this period, but they left no written records.

** B.2.3 The Trakhan Dynasty of Gilgit (c. 7th century -- c. 1810)

- *Origin*: Turkic tribes; some sources say Zoroastrian background
- *Religion*: Initially Zoroastrian or syncretic; later Ismaili Muslim
- *Capital*: Gilgit
- *Duration*: Several centuries; ended c. 1810 with the death of Raja Abbas
- *Relevance to Diamer*: Direct --- Chilas, Darel, Tangir were within the
  Trakhan sphere of influence

*** Origins and establishment

Turkic tribes arrived in Gilgit during the 7th century and founded what
became known as the Trakhan dynasty. The rulers of Yasin, Punial, and
Chitral had close ties with the Trakhan dynasty, and the tribal regions of
Gor, Darel, Chilas, Tangir, and Herban had their origin and history linked
with the Trakhan rulers.

*** Notable rulers (selected)

| Ruler              | Dates (approx)  | Notes                                    |
|--------------------+------------------+------------------------------------------|
| Raja Trakhan       | 1310--1335       | Ismaili ruler of Gilgit                  |
| Raja Somul         | 1335--1390       | Successor                                |
| Raja Khusaro Khan  | 1390--1435       |                                          |
| Raja Hyder Khan    | 1435--1480       |                                          |
| Raja Chalis Khan   | 1480--1515       |                                          |
| Raja Nur Khan      | 1515--1565       |                                          |
| Shah Mirza I       | enthroned 1127   | Earlier period, formal enthronement      |

The Ismailis ruled Gilgit for approximately 255 years during the Trakhan
period.

*** Relationship to Diamer

The Trakhan rulers exercised suzerainty over the Diamer region, but actual
control was mediated through local Shina-speaking communities who governed
themselves through jirga (council of elders). The British later called the
Indus valley below Chilas and its tributary valleys --- Darel, Tangir,
Harban, Sazin, Kandia --- "Yaghestan" ("land of the free"). These
communities were "a nest of tiny republics; there was one in almost every
side valley, each loosely guided by a jirga but effectively leaderless,
all at war with one another and feuding internally."

The Trakhans provided an overarching political framework, but the Diamer
communities fiercely resisted external domination.

** B.2.4 The Spread of Islam in Diamer

- *Period*: c. 14th--16th century (approximate)
- *Mechanism*: Sufi missionaries, not military conquest
- *Sect*: Sunni (Deobandi school, introduced by Pashtun preachers)

*** How Islam reached the region

Islam arrived in Gilgit-Baltistan through multiple vectors, at different
times, with different sectarian orientations:

| Region    | Sect       | Source of conversion                     | Period (approx)  |
|-----------+------------+------------------------------------------+------------------|
| Baltistan | Shia       | Via Kashmir and Central Asia             | 14th--15th c.    |
| Gilgit    | Ismaili    | Trakhan rulers, Central Asian Sufis      | 14th c. onward   |
| Diamer    | Sunni      | Sufi missionaries from Kaghan/Swat       | 14th--16th c.    |

*** Conversion in Diamer specifically

The ancestors of Chilas were converted to Islam by a Sufi named *Syed Noor
Shah* (also known as Ghazi Baba), who came from the Kaghan Valley. He was
the first person to preach Islam in Thak (a locality in Chilas) and built
the first mosque there, which reportedly still stands.

In the Darel and Tangir valleys, Islam came from Swat, brought by Pashtun
preachers. The Deobandi school of Sunni Islam became dominant in Diamer.

*** The broader context

The Islamisation of the northern areas was part of a wider movement
connected to Sufi orders spreading from Kashmir and Central Asia. The most
influential figure for Kashmir itself was *Mir Sayyid Ali Hamadani*
(c. 1314--1384), a Persian Sufi of the Kubrawiya order who arrived in
Kashmir around 1372 CE with 700 artisans and scholars. While Hamadani's
direct impact was on Kashmir rather than Diamer, his movement created the
conditions under which Islam spread northward into the mountain valleys.

*** The pre-Islamic legacy

The Buddhist rock carvings at Chilas show that conversion was gradual.
Buddhist artistic traditions persisted until the 11th--12th century, well
after Islam had arrived in neighbouring regions. The replacement was not
violent iconoclasm but a slow cultural transition as trade routes shifted
and new religious authorities gained influence.

** B.2.5 Ali Sher Khan Anchan and the Maqpon Conquest of Diamer (c. 1600)

- *Full name*: Ali Sher Khan Anchan
- *Origin*: Skardu, Baltistan
- *Dynasty*: Maqpon
- *Reign*: c. 1580/1595--1633
- *Religion*: Muslim (Shia/Nourbakhshi)
- *Relevance to Diamer*: Direct military conquest

*** Who was Ali Sher Khan Anchan?

The greatest of the Balti rulers. He unified Baltistan and expanded the
Maqpon kingdom to its maximum extent: Ladakh and western Tibet in the east,
Chitral and Kafiristan in the west, Gilgit and Hunza in the north.

*** The conquest of Diamer

Incessant raids and attacks on Roundu, Dras, Gultari, and Shigar by people
from Gilgit, Chilas, and Astore forced Ali Sher Anchan to march with a
strong army. He conquered *Astore, Gilgit, Hunza, Nagar, and Chilas*. From
Gilgit he advanced to conquer Chitral and Kafiristan.

*** The Chilas Fort

The fort at Chilas (*Qila Chilas*) was *constructed by Ali Sher Khan Anchan*
in the late 16th century during his campaigns. This is a critical finding
for the Dara Shikoh question (see B.3.1 below).

*** Mughal connections

Ali Sher Khan Anchan had friendly relations with the Mughal court. He
established marital alliances with the Gyalpo (ruler) of Ladakh and the
Trakhan ruler of Gilgit. He sent musicians from Skardu to Delhi to learn
Indian music; Mughal architecture influenced the region under his reign.
He also introduced polo to the Gilgit region.

He was succeeded by Adam Khan in 1633.

* B.3 Mughal Connections

** B.3.1 The Dara Shikoh Fort Question -- RESOLVED

#+begin_quote
*Claim*: A fort at Chilas was built by Dara Shikoh, the Mughal prince and
eldest son of Shah Jahan.

*Verdict*: Almost certainly *false*. The attribution is a misidentification.
The fort at Chilas (Qila Chilas) was built by *Ali Sher Khan Anchan* of the
Maqpon dynasty in the late 16th century, during his military campaigns
that conquered Astore, Gilgit, Hunza, Nagar, and Chilas.
#+end_quote

*** The evidence against Dara Shikoh

1. *No primary Mughal source attests to it.* Dara Shikoh's movements are
   well documented in Mughal chronicles (the /Alamgirnama/, the accounts of
   Niccolao Manucci, and multiple court histories). None place him at
   Chilas or anywhere in the northern mountain territories.

2. *Dara Shikoh's known life.* He was born 1615, was appointed governor of
   various Mughal provinces (Allahabad, Punjab, Multan, and the Kabul subah
   at different times), but his intellectual interests (Upanishad translation,
   Sufi mysticism, the /Majma ul-Bahrain/) kept him near the Mughal
   heartland. The war of succession (1657--1659) ended with his defeat at
   the Battle of Samugarh (May 1658) and execution on 30 August 1659. There
   is no documented period when he would have been building forts at the
   edge of the empire.

3. *The fort pre-dates Dara Shikoh.* Ali Sher Khan Anchan reigned
   c. 1580--1633, dying before Dara Shikoh reached adulthood. The Chilas
   fort was built during Anchan's campaigns of conquest in the late 16th
   century.

4. *Mughal administrative reach.* The Mughal empire conquered Kashmir in
   1586 (Akbar) and Shah Jahan carved it out as a separate subah. But
   Mughal control did not extend effectively beyond the Kashmir valley into
   the independent Shina-speaking territories of Diamer. The Mughals had
   *diplomatic* relations with rulers of Gilgit and Baltistan (Ali Sher
   Khan Anchan had friendly ties with the Mughal court), but they did not
   garrison the area.

5. *Local tourism attribution.* The "Dara Shikoh Fort" label appears in
   modern tourism materials (e.g., Gilgit-Baltistan tourism department
   websites). This is likely a prestige attribution --- associating a local
   landmark with a famous Mughal prince to enhance its historical cachet.
   Similar misattributions are common across South Asia.

*** How the misattribution likely arose

The Maqpon conquest of Chilas coincided roughly with the Mughal period, and
Ali Sher Khan Anchan had documented connections to the Mughal court. Over
centuries of oral transmission, the local fort may have become associated
with the Mughal dynasty generally, and then with its most romanticised
figure, Dara Shikoh --- the philosopher-prince, translator of the
Upanishads, tragic heir. Dara Shikoh's name carries cultural prestige that
a Balti ruler's does not, especially in a Sunni region where Mughal
identity resonates.

** B.3.2 Mughal Administration and the Northern Frontier

*** Akbar's conquest of Kashmir (1586)

Akbar annexed Kashmir and initially placed it under the Kabul subah. The
Mughal interest in Kashmir was strategic (controlling passes to Central Asia)
and personal (the Mughal emperors loved Kashmir as a summer retreat). But
the mountains north of the Kashmir valley --- Diamer, Gilgit, Baltistan ---
lay beyond effective Mughal control.

*** Shah Jahan's extension (by 1634)

Shah Jahan carved Kashmir out as a separate subah. His son Salim (later
Jahangir) married a princess of Baltistan, and by 1634, Shah Jahan had
captured Ladakh and Baltistan, incorporating them into the Kashmir province.
This brought Mughal claims closer to Diamer, but the fiercely independent
Shina communities of Chilas, Darel, and Tangir were never effectively
administered by the Mughals.

*** Abu'l Fazl's Ain-i-Akbari

The /Ain-i-Akbari/, Abu'l Fazl's administrative compendium of Akbar's empire
(written 1589--1596), describes the fifteen subahs in detail. It includes
coverage of the Kashmir subah and references to the /Rajatarangini/ (the
12th-century Sanskrit chronicle of Kashmir's kings). However, the /Ain-i-Akbari/
treats the northern mountain territories as frontier zones beyond
detailed administrative description. Gilgit and Chilas do not appear as
administered units --- they were known but not governed.

** B.3.3 Mughal-era connections via the Maqpon dynasty

The most concrete Mughal-era link to Diamer runs through the Maqpon rulers
of Skardu, particularly Ali Sher Khan Anchan (see B.2.5 above). The Maqpons
unified Gilgit-Baltistan with Chitral and Ladakh, maintained friendly
relations with the Mughal court, and adopted Mughal architectural styles.
This was a relationship of equals and allies, not of imperial centre and
administered province.

* B.4 Sikh and Dogra Campaigns (1839--1850)

** B.4.1 Zorawar Singh's Invasion of Baltistan (1839--1840)

- *Full name*: General Zorawar Singh Kahluria
- *Origin*: Kahlur (Bilaspur), a Rajput principality in the Himalayan foothills
- *Served*: Gulab Singh, Dogra ruler of Jammu (vassal of the Sikh Empire)
- *Campaign*: Conquest of Baltistan, winter 1839/40
- *Relevance to Diamer*: Indirect but significant --- the Dogra conquest of
  Baltistan set the stage for later campaigns into Gilgit and Chilas

*** Background

After conquering Ladakh for Gulab Singh in 1834--1840, Zorawar Singh turned
his attention to Baltistan. He exploited a succession dispute in the Maqpon
dynasty: Prince Muhammad Shah, disinherited by his father Raja Ahmad Shah
of Skardu, sought Dogra support.

*** The campaign

With an army of approximately 15,000 (Dogras augmented by a large Ladakhi
contingent), Zorawar Singh invaded Baltistan in the winter of 1839/40.

Key events:

1. *Bridge-building across the Indus*: Mehta Basti Ram and his men cut
   trees and built a temporary bridge over the icy Indus at night.
2. *Battle at Wanko Pass*: While the Dogras were crossing the Indus, Balti
   forces under Ghulam Hussain and Wazir Sultan Beg attacked. After fierce
   fighting, Ghulam Hussain was killed and Sultan Beg retreated with
   casualties of ~2,000 dead and 1,000 wounded.
3. *Initial setback*: An advance brigade of 5,000 under Nidhan Singh lost
   its way in the snow, was surrounded, and many soldiers perished from cold.
4. *Siege of Skardu*: Zorawar Singh besieged Skardu fort for fifteen days.
   Ahmad Shah surrendered.

*** Aftermath

Ahmad Shah was deposed. His son Muhammad Shah was installed as ruler under
Dogra suzerainty, ordered to pay annual tribute of Rs. 7,000. Zorawar Singh
took control of Kharmang, Khapalu, Tolti, Parkuta, Shigar, Rondu, and Astor.

*** Note on Diamer

Zorawar Singh's campaign targeted Baltistan (Skardu), not Diamer directly.
But the conquest of Astor brought Dogra forces close to the Diamer region
and established the military infrastructure for later interventions.

** B.4.2 Zorawar Singh's Death at To-yo, Tibet (12 December 1841)

Having conquered Ladakh and Baltistan, Zorawar Singh received permission from
Maharaja Sher Singh to invade western Tibet (Ngari Khorsum) in 1841. He
stormed the fort at Taklakot on 6 September 1841.

But the Tibetan counter-attack came at the *Battle of To-yo* on 12 December
1841. In the early exchange of fire, Zorawar was wounded in the right
shoulder by a matchlock ball. Despite the wound, he killed four or five
Tibetans before a lance was hurled through his chest. He fell without
uttering a sound.

His death demoralised the Dogra forces. Many were captured; many more
perished in the hastily organised flight from Tibet in the bitter winter
cold.

*** Significance for Diamer

Zorawar Singh never reached Diamer. His campaigns operated on the
Ladakh-Baltistan-Tibet axis. But his conquests established Dogra control
over the southern and eastern approaches to Gilgit-Baltistan, creating the
political framework within which Diamer would eventually be subjugated.

- *Reference*: Wikipedia, "Dogra-Tibetan war"
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dogra%E2%80%93Tibetan_war

** B.4.3 The Sikh Intervention in Gilgit (1842--1843)

- *Commander*: Colonel Nathu Shah
- *Sent by*: Sheikh Ghulam Mohi-ud-Din, Kashmir Governor of the Sikh Empire
- *Context*: The murder of Sikander Shah of Gilgit by Raja Gohar Aman of Yasin

*** Events

After Sikander Shah of Gilgit was killed by Raja Gohar Aman of Yasin, the
Sikh governor of Kashmir dispatched Colonel Nathu Shah with a Sikh force to
restore order. The Sikh forces:

1. Defeated Gohar Aman
2. Established a garrison at Gilgit
3. Reinstated Karim Khan (brother of the late Sikander Shah) as vassal ruler

By the end of 1843, Sikh forces had secured Gilgit, Astore, and Chilas. A
strong Sikh garrison was stationed at Gilgit under a thanedar (military
commandant).

*** Significance

This was the *first external military garrison established at Chilas*
(beyond Ali Sher Khan Anchan's earlier conquest). The Sikh presence in
Chilas was brief and tenuous, but it established the precedent of external
military control that the Dogras and British would continue.

- *Reference*: Wikipedia, "Sikh intervention in Gilgit"
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sikh_intervention_in_Gilgit

** B.4.4 The Treaty of Amritsar (16 March 1846) and Its Consequences

*** The sale of Kashmir

After the First Anglo-Sikh War (1845--1846), the British East India Company
sold Kashmir to Raja Gulab Singh of Jammu for 75 lakh (7.5 million)
Nanakshahi rupees under the Treaty of Amritsar. The treaty transferred
sovereignty over the Kashmir Valley along with Jammu, Ladakh, Baltistan,
and Gilgit.

This was the foundational document for the princely state of Jammu and
Kashmir, and it brought Diamer --- at least on paper --- under Dogra sovereignty.

*** Dogra troops replace Sikhs

In 1846, Dogra soldiers relieved the Sikh troops posted at Astore and
Gilgit. Nathu Shah and Karim Khan both transferred their allegiance to
Gulab Singh and continued local administration.

** B.4.5 Nathu Shah's Death and the Fall of Gilgit (1848)

- *Date*: 1848
- *Killed by*: Forces of the Mir of Hunza

*** Events

After the Treaty of Amritsar, Nathu Shah remained in Gilgit as Gulab
Singh's commander. In 1848, he led a force to attack the Hunza valley on
behalf of Gulab Singh. The expedition was a disaster: Nathu Shah and his
force were destroyed by Hunza forces. Gilgit fort fell into the hands of
the Hunza Raja and his Yasin and Punial allies.

*** Aftermath

Gilgit was soon reconquered by Gulab Singh's Dogra troops, but the
incident demonstrated the fragility of external control over the northern
territories. In 1852, Gilgit's inhabitants drove their new rulers out in
an uprising, with the support of Raja Gohar Aman.

- *Reference*: Wikipedia, "Gilgit"
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilgit

** B.4.6 The Dogra Conquest of Chilas (1850--1852)

The fort of Chilas was conquered by Gulab Singh's forces in 1850. The Dogra
forces destroyed the existing Chilas fort in 1851 during a punitive attack,
but Chilas was occupied in 1852 --- at the cost of 400 Dogra casualties.

Dogra rule was imposed on Chilas in 1852, though the Chilasis continued to
enjoy substantial internal independence. The region's fierce resistance to
external control --- the legacy of the "Yaghestan" tradition --- meant that
Dogra sovereignty was always nominal in the Darel, Tangir, and surrounding
valleys.

** B.4.7 The Dogra Garrison at Gilgit --- Life at the Edge of Empire

After the initial turbulence of the 1840s--1850s, the Dogra garrison at
Gilgit settled into a precarious existence:

- *1877*: British establish an "Officer on Special Duty" at Gilgit to
  monitor the passes (the "Great Game" period --- concern about Russian
  expansion toward India).
- *1882*: The position is withdrawn.
- *1889*: A permanent British Political Agent is established at Gilgit,
  creating the *Gilgit Agency*.
- *1913*: The Jammu and Kashmir State Forces at the Gilgit garrison are
  replaced by the British-officered *Gilgit Scouts*.
- *1935*: Maharaja Gulab Singh's successor is forced to lease the Gilgit
  Agency to the British for 60 years. The region is effectively administered
  by the British until 1947.
- *1 July 1947*: The leased area is retroceded to the Maharaja.
- *1--2 November 1947*: The Gilgit Scouts under Major William Brown take
  the Dogra governor into protective custody and raise the Pakistan flag.

Throughout this period, Chilas and the Diamer valleys remained the most
difficult territory --- the "Yaghestan" where no external authority could
fully impose order.

* Synthesis: Who Actually Reached Diamer?

| Figure / Dynasty       | Period        | Reached Diamer? | Nature of contact                 |
|------------------------+---------------+-----------------+-----------------------------------|
| Al-Masudi              | c. 940s       | No              | Described India's rivers/lowlands |
| Al-Biruni              | c. 1017--1030 | No              | Knew Kashmir mountains, Indus     |
| Al-Idrisi              | c. 1154       | No              | Compiled from earlier sources     |
| Ibn Battuta            | 1333          | No              | Crossed Hindu Kush far to west    |
| Patola Shahis          | c. 6th--8th c. | Yes (controlled) | Buddhist rulers of Gilgit         |
| Trakhan dynasty        | c. 7th--19th c. | Yes (suzerainty) | Gilgit rulers, linked to Chilas   |
| Sufi missionaries      | c. 14th--16th c. | Yes             | Converted Chilas to Islam         |
| Ali Sher Khan Anchan   | c. 1600       | Yes (conquest)  | Built Chilas fort, conquered area |
| Mughals (Akbar--Aurangzeb) | 1586--1707  | No (Kashmir only) | Diplomatic ties via Maqpons       |
| Dara Shikoh            | 1615--1659    | *No* (misattrib.) | Fort attribution is false         |
| Sikh forces            | 1842--1843    | Yes (garrison)  | Secured Chilas under Nathu Shah   |
| Zorawar Singh          | 1839--1841    | No (Baltistan)  | Conquered adjacent territories    |
| Dogra forces           | 1850--1852    | Yes (conquest)  | Destroyed and rebuilt Chilas fort |

The pattern is clear: Diamer was visited primarily by *conquerors and
missionaries*, not by travellers and geographers. The great Arab scholars
knew of the region's mountains only from afar. The people who came to Diamer
came to control it, convert it, or pass through it on trade routes. The
region's own people --- the Shina-speaking communities of the "Yaghestan" ---
left no written records of their own, but they fiercely resisted every
attempt at external domination, from the Patola Shahis to the Dogras.
