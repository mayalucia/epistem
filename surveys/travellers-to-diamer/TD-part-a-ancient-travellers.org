* Introduction

The upper Indus valley through Diamer district — the stretch from Shatial
to Chilas and beyond toward Gilgit — is one of the most densely inscribed
landscapes on Earth. Approximately 30,000 petroglyphs and 5,000 inscriptions
in more than 10 writing systems have been documented on a 100 km stretch of
river canyon [cite:tracce:kkhcarvings]. This is not accidental. The gorge of
the Indus at this point is narrow, violent, and unavoidable: anyone travelling
between Central Asia and the Indian subcontinent via the Karakoram or the
western Himalaya had to pass through here. For at least ten thousand years,
they left their marks on the desert-varnished boulders that line the banks.

This survey covers the ancient travellers — from the earliest anonymous
petroglyph makers of the Mesolithic to the last Buddhist pilgrims before the
Islamic transformation of the region. The account is organised by community
rather than strict chronology, since many of these groups overlapped in time.

* A.1 The Petroglyph Makers (~8000 BCE – 1000 CE)

** A.1.1 The Earliest Carvers: Mesolithic Hunters

The earliest rock carvings in the upper Indus valley date to approximately the
ninth millennium BCE — the late Stone Age / early Mesolithic [cite:encyclopedia:rockart].
These are the oldest anthropogenic marks in the region, preceding agriculture, metals,
and organised religion by thousands of years.

The subjects are almost exclusively wild animals: ibex with large curving horns,
markhor, snow leopards, and hunting scenes. The hunter himself is rarely depicted
in the earliest layers. These carvings are typically found closest to the riverbank,
on boulders with heavy desert varnish — the darkest patina indicating the longest
exposure [cite:highasia:rockart].

Who were these people? The evidence is indirect. They were likely semi-nomadic
hunter-gatherer groups moving up the Indus drainage during interglacial periods,
pursuing alpine game. Beginning in the Upper Palaeolithic, some 20,000 to 10,000
years ago, people began moving up these rivers in pursuit of game, with the
earliest anthropomorphic figures and animal petroglyphs of large horned goats,
sheep, ibex, and snow leopards probably dating to this time [cite:encyclopedia:rockart].

No settlement sites from this period have been identified in Diamer proper.
The carvers remain anonymous — known only by what they left on rock.

** A.1.2 The Bronze Age Transition: Herders and Giants

By the third millennium BCE, the character of the carvings changes. Large
engravings of humped zebu cattle appear, indicating that herders — not just
hunters — were now frequenting the Indus gorge [cite:encyclopedia:rockart].
Agriculture had started in the wider region, and carvings of horses appear for
the first time [cite:ancientconnection:buddhistrockart].

A striking feature of this period is the appearance of approximately 50 carvings
of "giants" — large human-like figures, all depicted without facial features
[cite:ancientconnection:buddhistrockart]. Their purpose and identity remain
debated. They may represent deities, ancestral figures, or ritual participants.

** A.1.3 The Okunev Culture Connection: Siberia to the Indus

One of the most remarkable findings from the upper Indus rock art is its
connection to the Okunev culture of south Siberia. The Okunev culture was a
pastoralist society of the early Bronze Age (late 3rd to early 2nd millennium
BCE) based in the Minusinsk Basin on the middle and upper Yenisei river
[cite:wikipedia:okunev].

Okunev-style petroglyphs — specifically three distinctive mask types dating to
the 3rd millennium BCE — have been identified in the upper reaches of the Indus,
near Chilas, connected to Okunev engravings by their main motifs and stylistic
peculiarities [cite:academia:petroglyphsmigrations]. This implies a migration or
cultural transmission corridor spanning approximately 2,500 km from the Yenisei
to the Indus.

The Sayan complex of archaeological cultures of the Early Bronze Age, composed
mainly of descendants of the Afanasyevo and Okunevo tribes, appears to have
formed a cultural entity that migrated southwards to the upper reaches of the
Indus and further westwards to eastern Anatolia [cite:academia:petroglyphsmigrations].
The distribution of Okunev-style motifs such as the Mugur-Sargol representations
across regions from China to the upper Indus indicates cultural diffusion from
Okunev traditions [cite:ulukayin:okunev].

#+begin_quote
*Epistemic status*: The Okunev–Indus connection is proposed on the basis of
stylistic similarity in petroglyphs across distant sites. It is supported by
multiple scholars but remains a hypothesis about cultural transmission rather
than a demonstrated migration. No DNA evidence or settlement archaeology
directly links the two regions in this period.
#+end_quote

** A.1.4 Scythian and Steppe Nomad Influence (~1000 – 200 BCE)

At the beginning of the first millennium BCE, a new artistic vocabulary appears
in the upper Indus carvings: the Eurasian Animal Style. Stags and ibex with
large backward-curving horns, depicted in dynamic movement, can be linked to
Scythian (Saka) artistic traditions dating to the eighth century BCE, and to
even earlier animal-style art from Siberia dating from the eleventh to ninth
centuries BCE [cite:encyclopedia:rockart].

These carvings were left by nomadic groups — referred to in the rock art
literature as the Sakan tribes — who moved down the river valleys from the
Eurasian steppes. Their presence marks the beginning of the upper Indus gorge
as a transit corridor rather than merely a hunting ground.

** A.1.5 Karl Jettmar's Periodisation

Karl Jettmar (1918–2002), professor of ethnology at Heidelberg, was the
founding figure of systematic rock art research in the upper Indus. When the
Karakoram Highway was inaugurated in 1978, exposing thousands of previously
inaccessible engravings, Jettmar recognised their significance. In 1980, he
and Ahmad Hasan Dani launched the Pak-German Archaeological Mission (later the
Pak-German Study Group) to document the sites systematically
[cite:dawn:threatenedcarvings].

Jettmar's major publications include:

- /Between Gandhara and the Silk Roads: Rock Carvings along the Karakorum
  Highway/ (1987, with Volker Thewalt) [cite:google:jettmarbetween]
- /Antiquities of Northern Pakistan/ (ANP), 5 volumes (1989–2004), the
  definitive corpus [cite:archive:anp1; cite:archive:anp2]

Jettmar identified three broad chronological phases in the rock art
[cite:tracce:kkhcarvings]:

| Phase | Approximate Date | Characteristics |
|-------+------------------+-------------------------------------------------------|
| Prehistoric | ~8000 BCE – 1st c. BCE | Animals, hunting scenes, demon-like creatures |
| Buddhist | ~1st c. CE – 9th/10th c. CE | Stupas, Buddhas, Bodhisattvas, inscriptions in Brahmi, Kharosthi, Sogdian |
| Post-Buddhist | ~9th/10th c. – 14th/15th c. CE | Axes, sun-symbols; transition to Islamic period |

Within the prehistoric phase, sub-periods can be distinguished by style:
early hunting scenes (Mesolithic), zebu and giant figures (Bronze Age),
Eurasian Animal Style (Iron Age / Scythian influence). Within the Buddhist
phase, a dense overlay of stupa carvings, donor inscriptions, and narrative
panels (including Jataka scenes) accumulated over roughly a millennium.

Jettmar's later work (1989) moved away from a simple ethno-archaeological
interpretation that would directly imply a chronological distinction between
Buddhist and non-Buddhist carvings. His spatial-oriented approach considers
the carvings as grouped together within bigger but still specific, contained
mountain sites [cite:researchgate:chilasrockart].

#+begin_quote
*Note on periodisation terminology*: Some secondary sources refer to "Period
I–V" in connection with Jettmar. This five-period scheme appears in the
Encyclopaedia Iranica article on Chilas and in Dani's later work, refining
the three-phase model. The exact correspondence between numbering schemes
varies across publications. The three-phase model given here follows Jettmar's
own primary publications as described in the TRACCE/rupestre.net summary and
the Encyclopedia.com entry.
#+end_quote

** A.1.6 The Thalpan Bridge Site

Thalpan is a small village on the Indus opposite Chilas town, the headquarters
of Diamer district. The rock art site known as Thalpan Bridge (or Thalpan I)
is the densest concentration of petroglyphs in the entire upper Indus
[cite:researchgate:thalpanmonastic].

Thousands of rock carvings have been documented here by the Pak-German
Archaeological Mission from the late 1970s onward, published across six
dedicated volumes [cite:researchgate:thalpanmonastic]. The majority are
Buddhist in nature — stupas, Buddhas, Bodhisattvas, and donor inscriptions —
though prehistoric and post-Buddhist layers are also present.

The site's significance lies in:

1. *Density*: More carvings per unit area than any comparable site in South or
   Central Asia
2. *Temporal depth*: Carvings spanning roughly ten millennia on the same
   boulders
3. *Multilingualism*: Inscriptions in Kharosthi, Brahmi (both early and late
   variants), Proto-Sarada, Sogdian, Chinese, and possibly Hebrew
   [cite:tracce:kkhcarvings]
4. *Narrative content*: Jataka scenes, particularly the Sibi Jataka
   [cite:thenews:tracinghistories]
5. *Monastic context*: Recent discovery of a possible Buddhist monastic
   complex at Thalpan [cite:researchgate:thalpanmonastic]

The Chilas I site (directly across the river from Thalpan Bridge, above the
Karakoram Highway) contains complementary material, including prominent panels
of Bodhisattva Avalokiteshvara and Bodhisattva Maitreya [cite:fridaytimes:buddhistchilas].

*** Threat

The proposed Diamer-Basha Dam, if built, would submerge many of the most
significant rock art sites in the region, including portions of the Thalpan
and Chilas concentrations [cite:dawn:threatenedcarvings].

* A.2 Chinese Buddhist Pilgrims

** A.2.1 Faxian (法顯) — ~399 CE

| Field | Detail |
|-------+--------|
| *Name* | Faxian (Fa-Hien, Fa-hsien) |
| *Dates* | c. 337 – c. 422 CE |
| *Origin* | Shanxi province, China |
| *Purpose* | Pilgrimage to India to obtain Buddhist vinaya (monastic discipline) texts |
| *Route through Diamer* | Crossed the Indus gorge via the śaṅkupatha (suspended crossing / peg-path) |
| *What he wrote* | /Foguo Ji/ (佛國記), "A Record of the Buddhist Kingdoms" |
| *What happened to him* | Returned to China via Sri Lanka and the sea route; translated texts until his death |
| *Primary source* | James Legge (tr.), /A Record of Buddhistic Kingdoms/ (1886) |

*** The Śaṅkupatha: Faxian's Own Account

Faxian left Chang'an in 399 CE with a group of fellow monks, travelling
westward through the Taklamakan and then south across the high passes toward
India. His account of crossing the Indus gorge is one of the most vivid
passages in early travel literature.

From James Legge's translation (1886), available on Project Gutenberg
[cite:gutenberg:faxian]:

#+begin_quote
"The way was difficult and rugged, (running along) a bank exceedingly
precipitous, which rose up there, a hill-like wall of rock, 10,000 cubits from
the base. When one approached the edge of it, his eyes became unsteady; and if
he wished to go forward in the same direction, there was no place on which he
could place his foot; and beneath were the waters of the river called the
Indus. In former times men had chiselled paths along the rocks, and distributed
ladders on the face of them, to the number altogether of 700, at the bottom of
which there was a suspension bridge of ropes, by which the river was crossed,
its banks being there eighty paces apart."
#+end_quote

The chapter heading in Legge's translation reads: "Crossing of the Indus. When
Buddhism First Crossed the River for the East." (Chapter VII.)

*** The Peg-Path Technique

The Sanskrit term for this crossing method is *śaṅkupatha* — literally
"peg-path" — rendered in Chinese as *xuándù* (懸度, "suspended crossing").
Faxian's term is *bàngtī* (棒梯, "pole-step"), describing the same technique
from the traveller's perspective: wooden pegs driven into the rock face,
serving as footholds for traversing vertical cliff walls above the river
[cite:falk:sankupatha; cite:academia:falksuccessors].

Harry Falk's comprehensive study, "The Suspended Crossing (śaṅkupatha) in the
Gorges of the Indus River as described by Chinese Pilgrims Faxian, Dharmodgata
and Xuanzang" (2020), published on Academia.edu, reconstructs the likely
location of the crossing and compares the accounts of multiple pilgrims
[cite:falk:sankupatha].

Faxian appears to have followed a relatively simple path through the Yasin
Valley and across the Dadarili pass to reach the Indus gorge
[cite:falk:sankupatha].

** A.2.2 Dharmodgata (曇摩竭) — Early 5th Century CE

| Field | Detail |
|-------+--------|
| *Name* | Dharmodgata (Tánmójié) |
| *Dates* | Active early 5th century CE, contemporary or near-contemporary of Faxian |
| *Origin* | China (Eastern Jin dynasty) |
| *Purpose* | Buddhist pilgrimage |
| *Route through Diamer* | Crossed the Indus gorge via the śaṅkupatha, same general corridor as Faxian |
| *What he left behind* | Account recorded in the /Gaosengzhuan/ (Biographies of Eminent Monks) |
| *Primary source* | /Gaosengzhuan/ (compiled by Huijiao, 519 CE) |

Dharmodgata was Faxian's direct successor on the route. His account, preserved
in the /Gaosengzhuan/, describes using /yì/ (杙, "pegs") to climb the rock on
the bank of the Indus — a slightly different term from Faxian's /bàngtī/ but
referring to the same peg-path technique [cite:falk:sankupatha].

The terminological difference is significant for Falk's reconstruction: it
suggests that different pilgrims experienced (or at least described) slightly
different variants of the crossing infrastructure, which may have been
maintained and modified over decades.

** A.2.3 Xuanzang (玄奘) — ~630 CE

| Field | Detail |
|-------+--------|
| *Name* | Xuanzang (Hsüan-tsang) |
| *Dates* | 602–664 CE |
| *Origin* | Henan province, China |
| *Purpose* | Buddhist pilgrimage and study; collection of sutras |
| *Route through Diamer* | Entered the subcontinent via the Karakoram passes; crossed the Indus |
| *What he wrote* | /Da Tang Xiyu Ji/ (大唐西域記), "Great Tang Records on the Western Regions" (646 CE) |
| *What happened to him* | Returned to China with 657 texts; spent the rest of his life translating |
| *Primary source* | Li Rongxi (tr.), /The Great Tang Dynasty Record of the Western Regions/ (BDK English Tripitaka, 1996) |

*** The Route and the Capsized Boat

Xuanzang left Chang'an in 629 CE, travelling westward through Central Asia
and entering the Indian subcontinent from the northwest. His account describes
visiting Uddiyana (Swat) and various Buddhist sites along the way
[cite:factsanddetails:xuanzangindia].

The most dramatic incident on the Indus occurred in 644 CE, on the return
journey. At Hund on the Indus, a storm rose up and overturned his boats. He
lost approximately 50 of his precious manuscripts and an unspecified quantity
of flower seeds he was bringing back to China [cite:factsanddetails:xuanzanwestern].

According to the biographical sources, his scriptures and images were loaded in
a boat with companions to sail across the river, while Xuanzang himself waded
through on his elephant, with a man appointed to take care of the scriptures.
The loss was devastating. He sent to Udyana for extra copies of his scriptures,
waiting two months at Kapisa hoping for their arrival
[cite:factsanddetails:xuanzangindia].

Notably, Xuanzang does not recount this accident in the /Da Tang Xiyu Ji/
itself; it appears only in his later letters to monks in India requesting
replacement copies [cite:wikipedia:recordswesternregions].

*** The Peg-Path in Xuanzang's Account

Xuanzang also describes crossing the Indus gorges using a technique he calls
/zhuóyì/ (斲杙, "hitting-peg") — lexically related to both Faxian's /bàngtī/
and Dharmodgata's /yì/. This confirms that the śaṅkupatha infrastructure was
still in use more than two centuries after Faxian's crossing
[cite:falk:sankupatha].

** A.2.4 Other Chinese Pilgrims

The śaṅkupatha crossing was used by Chinese monks from the Han dynasty through
to the Song dynasty — a span of roughly one thousand years
[cite:falk:sankupatha]. Harry Falk's paper catalogues the known pilgrims who
attempted the crossing. Most are known only from brief mentions in monastic
biographies.

Chinese rock inscriptions have been found at Shatial and Thak Das near Chilas,
including carved pagoda images — evidence of Chinese pilgrims marking their
passage through the gorge [cite:unesco:chineserockinscrip; cite:academia:pagodasindus].

* A.3 Sogdian, Persian, and Other Silk Road Travellers

** A.3.1 The Sogdian Inscriptions at Shatial

Shatial is located approximately 60 km west of Chilas on the Indus. It was a
critical transit point — a natural crossing site where the river narrowed
sufficiently for a bridge or ferry [cite:wikipedia:shatial].

At the Shatial bridge site, more than 1,000 inscriptions and 700 petroglyphs
have been documented. Of these, Iranian merchants left behind over 550
inscriptions in the Sogdian language, dating from the 3rd to 7th centuries CE.
In addition, nine inscriptions in the extinct Bactrian language and two each in
Middle Persian and Parthian have been identified
[cite:iranicaonline:chilas; cite:wikipedia:shatial].

*** Who Were They?

The Sogdians were an Iranian-speaking people based in Sogdiana (centred on
Samarkand and Bukhara in modern Uzbekistan). From the 4th to 8th centuries CE,
they dominated east–west trade across Central Asia, their language serving as
the lingua franca of the Silk Road [cite:sogdians.si:introduction;
cite:iranicaonline:sogdiantrade].

The Shatial inscriptions are mostly brief: personal names, patronymics,
sometimes a prayer or a dedication. They are the marks of merchants pausing
at a river crossing, recording their presence before continuing onward.

*** What Were They Trading?

Sogdian merchants traded a diverse range of goods: musk, silk, silverware,
slaves, precious metals and stones, furs, amber, relics, paper, spices, brass,
curcuma, sal ammoniac, medicinal plants, candy sugar, and perfumes
[cite:factsanddetails:sogdianmerchants; cite:iranicaonline:sogdiantrade]. Among
the most prized were horses from the Ferghana Valley, gemstones from India,
musk from Tibet, and furs from the northern steppes.

*** Where Were They Going?

A trade route from Sogdiana crossed the Indus near Shatial and terminated in an
emporium on the southern bank. Here the Sogdians traded with Buddhist merchants
(who had Iranian names) from the south, who could move more freely through the
territory of their respective protectors on the southern bank
[cite:iranicaonline:chilas]. This suggests a structured trade system with
intermediaries rather than end-to-end caravan routes.

** A.3.2 Nicholas Sims-Williams and the Corpus

The definitive scholarly study of the Sogdian inscriptions is:

- Nicholas Sims-Williams, /Sogdian and Other Iranian Inscriptions of the Upper
  Indus, I/ (London: School of Oriental and African Studies, 1989), part of the
  /Corpus Inscriptionum Iranicarum/ series. 36 pages, 170 plates
  [cite:cambridge:simswilliamsreview; cite:worldcat:simswilliams].

Sims-Williams also published on the Iranian inscriptions of Shatial specifically:
"The Iranian Inscriptions of Shatial," /Indologica Taurinensia/ 23–24 (1997–98)
[cite:openedition:simswilliamsshatial].

Nicholas Sims-Williams is Research Professor of Iranian and Central Asian
Studies at SOAS, University of London, and the foremost authority on Sogdian
and Bactrian languages [cite:wikipedia:simswilliams].

** A.3.3 Aramaic/Hebrew Inscriptions — Disputed

Some sources mention Hebrew inscriptions among the multilingual record at
Shatial and Thalpan [cite:tracce:kkhcarvings; cite:storymaps:upperindus].
These claims require careful handling.

The Kharosthi script — the dominant writing system for Gandhara and the
northwest in the early centuries CE — was itself derived from Aramaic, the
official script of the Achaemenid Persian state chancelleries
[cite:archnet:hauptmann]. This means that superficially "Aramaic-looking"
inscriptions may in fact be Kharosthi.

Whether any inscriptions at these sites are genuinely Hebrew (as opposed to
Aramaic, Kharosthi, or misidentified Iranian scripts) remains disputed in the
academic literature. The detailed archaeological accounts by Sims-Williams,
Jettmar, and Dani emphasise Kharosthi, Brahmi, Sogdian, Bactrian, Middle
Persian, and Parthian without prominently featuring Hebrew.

#+begin_quote
*Epistemic status*: The existence of Hebrew inscriptions in the upper Indus is
mentioned in some overview sources but not confirmed in the specialist
epigraphy. Treat as uncertain.
#+end_quote

** A.3.4 Sassanid Persian Presence

After the Sassanid dynasty secured Iran under Ardashir I, the second emperor
Shapur I (240–270 CE) extended his authority eastward into what is now Pakistan,
with the previously autonomous Kushans obliged to accept his suzerainty. While
the Kushan empire declined at the end of the 3rd century, Sassanid cultural and
political influence remained relevant in the northwest
[cite:storymaps:upperindus].

The Middle Persian and Parthian inscriptions at Shatial (two each) likely date
to the Sassanid period (224–651 CE). These are far fewer than the Sogdian
inscriptions, suggesting that Sassanid subjects were less frequent travellers
on this particular route, or less inclined to inscribe their presence.

The Kushans, who controlled this region before the Sassanid period, were
influenced by the Sassanid conception of kingship, which spread through trade
in Sassanid silverware and textiles depicting emperors hunting or dispensing
justice [cite:storymaps:upperindus].

* A.4 The Buddhist Period Summit (~100 BCE – 700 CE)

** A.4.1 Who Built the Monasteries?

The Buddhist presence in the upper Indus was not merely transient. Recent
survey work has identified a possible Buddhist monastic complex at Thalpan
itself [cite:researchgate:thalpanmonastic], suggesting permanent or
semi-permanent religious establishments alongside the itinerant pilgrims and
traders.

The builders and patrons likely included:

1. *Local rulers*: The Patola Shahis (Palola Shahis) were a dynasty of
   Buddhist kings who ruled the Kingdom of Gilgit from the 6th to 8th century
   CE. Their kingdom sat on the strategic trans-Himalayan trade route now known
   as the Karakoram Highway [cite:wikipedia:patolashahis].
2. *Gandharan monks*: The rock art shows strong Gandharan stylistic influence,
   with stupas and Bodhisattva figures closely paralleling Gandharan sculpture
   traditions [cite:springer:routesbeyondgandhara].
3. *Donor–travellers*: Many inscriptions are dedicatory — recording the gift
   of a stupa carving by a named individual, often with a prayer for merit.

** A.4.2 The Jataka Scenes

Among the most significant carvings at Thalpan are narrative panels depicting
Jataka tales — stories of the Buddha's previous incarnations. According to
Ahmad Hasan Dani (1983), the boulder art at Thalpan depicts Jataka stories of
the Buddha, notably the Sibi Jataka — showing a standing Bodhisattva holding a
balance in his hand, illustrating the tale of King Sibi who offered his own
flesh to save a dove [cite:thenews:tracinghistories; cite:youlin:thalpanbuddhist].

Other Buddhist motifs include:

- Stupas of varying styles and sizes (the most numerous single motif)
- Figures of Bodhisattva Avalokiteshvara and Bodhisattva Maitreya
  [cite:fridaytimes:buddhistchilas]
- Tridents, swastikas, and other auspicious symbols
- Linga representations (3rd–5th centuries CE) [cite:highasia:rockart]

** A.4.3 Was This a Major Pilgrim Route?

The density of Buddhist inscriptions — approximately 2,000 in the main Indus
valley south of Gilgit alone, in both Kharosthi and two variants of Brahmi
script [cite:iranicaonline:chilas] — strongly indicates that this was a major
pilgrim and trade route. The inscriptions were written by "pilgrims, travellers,
traders and others — mainly in the 4th and 5th centuries" [cite:highasia:rockart].

The presence of inscriptions in 10+ writing systems and languages (Kharosthi,
Brahmi, Proto-Sarada, Sogdian, Bactrian, Middle Persian, Parthian, Chinese,
and possibly Hebrew) makes the upper Indus gorge one of the most multilingual
archaeological corridors in the world [cite:tracce:kkhcarvings].

The region was not merely a transit corridor: Gilgit and Kashmir in this period
were the seat of a major school of Buddhist art, producing ornate early medieval
bronze icons that also greatly influenced metallic statue production in the
Swat Valley [cite:wikipedia:patolashahis].

** A.4.4 Ahmad Hasan Dani's Work

Ahmad Hasan Dani (1920–2009) was Pakistan's foremost archaeologist, historian,
and linguist. His contributions to the study of Diamer's rock art are
foundational:

- /Chilas: The City of Nanga Parbat (Dyamar)/ (1983) — the first
  comprehensive study of Chilas rock art and inscriptions
  [cite:thenews:tracinghistories]
- Co-direction (with Karl Jettmar) of the Pak-German Study Group from 1980
  onward [cite:wikipedia:dani]
- Transcription and interpretation of Kharosthi inscriptions, including the
  "Kaboa" (Kamboa) inscription
- /History of Northern Areas of Pakistan: Up to 2000 A.D./ (2001)
  [cite:google:danihistory]

Dani's partnership with Jettmar produced the most comprehensive documentation
of any rock art corpus in South Asia: a German-Pakistani survey group that
discovered several thousand rock carvings and about 2,000 inscriptions in the
main Indus valley south of Gilgit [cite:iranicaonline:chilas].

** A.4.5 When and Why Did Buddhist Presence End?

The decline of Buddhism in Diamer was not a single event but a centuries-long
transformation driven by multiple factors:

1. *Geopolitical disruption (7th–8th century)*: The Patola Shahi kingdom was
   caught between the Tang dynasty and the Tibetan Empire. Around 650 CE,
   Gilgit came under Tang suzerainty. In 737/738, Tibetan troops took control.
   In 747, the Tang general Gao Xianzhi recaptured the region. But by 755,
   the An Lushan Rebellion forced Tang withdrawal from Central Asia, ending
   Chinese protection of the Buddhist corridor [cite:wikipedia:patolashahis].

2. *Economic disruption*: The collapse of Tang power in Central Asia disrupted
   the Silk Road trade that had sustained the Buddhist communities along the
   upper Indus.

3. *Gradual Islamisation (8th–14th century)*: Islam arrived gradually.
   Buddhism survived in Gilgit-Baltistan until the 13th–14th century, perhaps
   slightly longer in the nearby Swat Valley [cite:wikipedia:buddhismpakistan].
   Sufi missionaries, notably Mir Sayyid Ali Hamadani (14th century) and the
   Noorbakshia Sufi sect, were the principal agents of conversion
   [cite:wikipedia:baltipeople].

4. *Local transformation*: The Maqpon kings who rose in Baltistan from the
   13th century onward adopted Islam, and most of the population had converted
   by the end of the 17th century [cite:wikipedia:baltipeople].

In Diamer district specifically, the rock art shows the transition: the
Buddhist period gives way to a post-Buddhist phase characterised by axes and
sun-symbols [cite:tracce:kkhcarvings], before the Islamic period brought an
end to figurative rock carving entirely.

#+begin_quote
*Note*: Despite conversion, the Balti people and other groups in the region
still retain many cultural traits of pre-Islamic Bon and Tibetan Buddhist
rituals within their society [cite:wikipedia:baltipeople].
#+end_quote
