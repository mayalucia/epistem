* Scope and Method

This survey covers the colonial and mountaineering era of the Diamer
district and Nanga Parbat massif (1835--1953): the period when European
surveyors, climbers, and administrators arrived in a landscape already
densely inhabited by Shina-speaking communities with their own names,
stories, and spiritual understanding of the mountain. The survey
documents who came, what they did, who died, and---where sources
permit---what the people already living there thought about all of it.

Method: web-accessible sources---academic papers, expedition accounts
published in the /Himalayan Journal/, the /Alpine Journal/, American
Alpine Club publications, memoirs, Wikipedia (cross-checked), and
digitised primary texts on the Internet Archive. Each claim carries a
source citation with URL. Where quotations are used, the source text
is identified. No personal observation.

** Note on Names

The mountain has at least three names:

- *Nanga Parbat* (Kashmiri/Sanskrit: nagna parvata, "naked mountain")
  --- the name that entered European maps through the Great
  Trigonometrical Survey.
- *Diamer* / *Diamir* (Shina, from Sanskrit dēva + mēru) --- "mountain
  of God" or "heavenly mountain." Some sources give "Deo Mir" = "King
  of the Mountains." The Diamer district takes its name from this.
- *Schicksalsberg* (German: "mountain of destiny") --- the name the
  Nazi propaganda apparatus attached to it after the 1934 disaster.

The first two are indigenous. The third is colonial mythology. This
survey uses "Nanga Parbat" as the conventional name but notes the
others where context demands.

* C.1 Early European Arrivals (1835--1860s)

** C.1.1 The Great Trigonometrical Survey

The Great Trigonometrical Survey of India, begun in 1802, reached the
northwestern frontier regions by the mid-19th century. Nanga Parbat
was observed and triangulated from distant stations in Kashmir long
before any European set foot near it. The mountain's height was
computed at approximately 26,660 feet (8,126 m), making it the ninth
highest in the world. The name "Nanga Parbat" entered Survey records
from Kashmiri informants --- the Sanskrit-derived name that Kashmiris
used for the distant white peak visible from the Vale.

The Survey did not record the Shina name /Diamer/, which was the name
used by the people who actually lived at the mountain's base. This is
a pattern repeated across the Himalaya: the names that entered
European cartography were often those of distant observers, not local
inhabitants.

** C.1.2 Godfrey Thomas Vigne (1835--1838)

Godfrey Thomas Vigne (pronounced "Vine"), an English traveller and
artist, was among the first Europeans to penetrate the Gilgit-Baltistan
region. Between 1835 and 1838, Vigne travelled extensively through
Kashmir, Ladakh, and Baltistan, reaching areas no European had
previously visited.

- *1835*: Reached the terminal of the Chogo Lungma glacier. Passed
  through Deosai, the high plateau between Kashmir and Baltistan.
- *1837*: Explored the Shyok River.
- *1838*: Explored the Saltoro Valley and the Bilafond glacier from
  Khaplu, searching for the Saltoro Pass.

Vigne gained the trust of Ahmed Shah, the Raja of Skardu, and acquired
antiquities and manuscripts. He witnessed the Raja's forces ambush a
band of robbers --- "every single one of them being dispatched either
by musket or by sword."

His account was published in 1842:

#+begin_quote
/Travels in Kashmir, Ladak, Iskardo, the Countries Adjoining the
Mountain-Course of the Indus, and the Himalaya, North of the Panjab/
(2 vols., 13 maps and plates, including a large folding map).
#+end_quote

Vigne's travels predate any systematic European exploration of the
Nanga Parbat massif itself. He did not approach the mountain closely,
but his routes through Baltistan and toward Gilgit opened the region
to European awareness.

** C.1.3 Henry Haversham Godwin-Austen (1857--1861)

Lieutenant (later Lieutenant-Colonel) Henry Haversham Godwin-Austen
was trained by Thomas Montgomerie in the Survey of India. Despite his
youth (he was 23 when he began), his skills as topographer, geologist,
explorer, and draughtsman earned him extensive fieldwork in the
Karakoram.

From 1857 to 1861, Godwin-Austen worked his way through the immense
moraines of the Baltoro glacier, producing the first detailed
topographic and geological sketches of the Karakoram interior. He was
described as "the only scientific man of his time with firsthand
knowledge of" the Karakoram. His planetable drawings exhibited
"striking geological detail woven into brush-drawings."

K2 (8,611 m), the world's second highest peak, was later proposed to
be named "Mount Godwin-Austen" in his honour, though the designation
K2 prevailed. The Godwin-Austen Glacier at the base of K2 carries his
name.

Fever contracted during survey work undermined his health and caused
his premature retirement from the Survey.

** C.1.4 The Schlagintweit Brothers (1854--1857)

Hermann, Adolf, and Robert von Schlagintweit, three brothers from
Munich, were commissioned by the British East India Company (with
support from Alexander von Humboldt and the King of Prussia) to study
the Earth's magnetic field in South and Central Asia.

*Adolf Schlagintweit* (1829--1857) is credited as the first European
to see Nanga Parbat at close range, making a sketch of the mountain
from the south around 1856. The brothers were also the first Europeans
to cross the Kunlun Mountains and among the first to explore the
region between the Karakoram and Kunlun.

Adolf's fate was tragic: in 1857, he was murdered in Kashgar, mistaken
for a Chinese spy and executed by the local ruler Wali Khan. He was
27 years old.

A peak in the Nanga Parbat range (5,971 m) was named "Schlagintweit
Peak" and received its first ascent in 2009.

* C.2 Mummery's Expedition (1895)

** C.2.1 Albert Frederick Mummery

Albert Frederick Mummery (1855--1895) was a British mountaineer
considered the founder of guideless and sport climbing. In the Alps,
he established a revolutionary approach: small parties, no guides,
technical difficulty sought rather than avoided. His book /My Climbs
in the Alps and Caucasus/ (1895, published posthumously) is a
foundational text of mountaineering literature.

By 1895, Mummery was the most accomplished rock climber of his
generation. His decision to attempt an 8,000-metre peak in the
Himalaya---with a tiny party and in alpine style---was fifty years
ahead of its time. It was the first attempt on any 8,000-metre peak
in history.

Note: Ragobir and Goman Singh are often called "porters" in European
accounts. They were climbing companions who went to the highest points
with Mummery.

** C.2.3 The Attempt

The party approached via the Diamir (western) face. Mummery, with
Ragobir, climbed to approximately 6,100 m on what is now called the
"Mummery Rib"---the first time humans had climbed so high on an
8,000-metre peak. At this altitude, Ragobir suffered severe symptoms
consistent with high-altitude pulmonary oedema (HAPE), forcing a
retreat.

After this attempt, Mummery decided to reconnoitre an alternative
route via the Rakhiot (northern) face. On 24 August 1895, Mummery,
Ragobir Thapa, and Goman Singh set out to cross a high pass from
the Diamir side to the Rakhiot Glacier.

They were never seen again.

** C.2.4 The Disappearance

Collie and Hastings waited. When the three failed to return, Collie
sent scouts. A local man, Lor Khan, found the abandoned rucksacks
still in position near the pass, but no trace of the men.

Collie concluded that an avalanche must have swept the trio away
while crossing a snow-covered pass or glacier on the Rakhiot side.
Their bodies were never recovered.

The Himalayan Journal later published a detailed reconstruction:

#+begin_quote
"When at last there was no sign of him, even after Lor Khan...reported
the rucksacks still in position, Collie knew that a disaster had
occurred."
#+end_quote

Collie's final tribute to Mummery, published in the Alpine Journal and
later collected in /From the Himalaya to Skye/, stands as one of the
most poignant passages in mountaineering literature:

#+begin_quote
"The pitiless mountains have claimed him and among the snow-laden
glaciers of the mighty hills he rests; the curves of the wind-moulded
cornice, the delicate undulations of the fissured snow cover him,
while the grim precipices and the great brown rocks, bending down
into an immeasurable space, and the snow peaks he loved so well keep
and guard and watch over the spot where he lies."
#+end_quote

--- J. Norman Collie, in /From the Himalaya to Skye/ (repr.
Ripping Yarns, 2003), originally published in the /Alpine Journal/.
Also reproduced in: Himalayan Journal Vol. 3, "The Passing of
Mummery."

** C.2.6 Legacy

Mummery's attempt established Nanga Parbat's reputation as a mountain
that resists human ambition. His alpine-style approach---small party,
no siege tactics, no supplemental oxygen---would not become the
dominant paradigm for 8,000-metre climbing until Reinhold Messner's
revolution in the 1970s and 1980s, eighty years later.

The Mummery Rib on the Diamir Face bears his name.

* C.3 The German Obsession (1932--1953)

** C.3.1 Why Nanga Parbat?

By the 1930s, British expeditions had claimed the Everest and
Kangchenjunga attempts. K2 was contested. For German and Austrian
mountaineers, shut out of the "British" peaks, Nanga Parbat was the
available prize: an 8,000-metre giant in a region accessible from
British India without passing through Tibet. The /Deutscher und
Oesterreichischer Alpenverein/ (German and Austrian Alpine Club)
backed the effort. After the Nazis rose to power in 1933, the mountain
became a national project.

** C.3.2 The 1932 Reconnaissance: Willy Merkl

*Leader*: Willy Merkl (1900--1934), a Munich railway official and
accomplished alpinist.

*Party*: German-American expedition. Members included Merkl, Fritz
Bechtold, Peter Aschenbrenner, and several American climbers including
Rand Herron and Elizabeth Knowlton.

*Achievement*: The party reconnoitred the Rakhiot approach, ascending
to approximately 7,000 m on the East Ridge. They established that a
route existed via the Rakhiot Glacier, over Rakhiot Peak, across the
Silver Plateau, to the summit pyramid. No fatalities.

*Significance*: Merkl's reconnaissance provided the route intelligence
for all subsequent German expeditions through the 1930s. The Rakhiot
approach became the "standard" route and the site of all the
catastrophes that followed.

** C.3.3 The 1934 Disaster

*** The Expedition

*Leader*: Willy Merkl (second attempt).

*Funding*: The Deutsche Himalaya-Stiftung (German Himalayan
Foundation), founded in 1936 but whose precursor organisation funded
the 1934 expedition. By this point, the Nazi regime had identified
Himalayan conquest as a propaganda vehicle. The expedition received
state support.

*Team* (Europeans): Willy Merkl, Fritz Bechtold, Peter Aschenbrenner,
Erwin Schneider, Willo Welzenbach, Uli Wieland, Alfred Drexel, and
others. 35 Sherpa and Bhutia porters from Darjeeling.

*** The Approach March

The expedition's 570 loads were brought up the Jhelum river and across
Wular Lake to Bandipura, where *600 local coolies* awaited them. The
march crossed the Tragbal Pass (11,586 ft) and the Burzil Pass
(13,775 ft) --- "the crossing of the wintry Burzil pass made severe
demands on the porters and the whole organization." By 13 May they
reached Talliche, where they first sighted Nanga Parbat, then
continued through Chilas to the Rakhiot bridge.

At the "Märchenwiese" (Fairy Meadow), local coolies were dismissed and
forty newly arrived Balti porters taken on for the higher camps.

*** The First Death: Alfred Drexel

On 8 June 1934, Alfred Drexel died at Camp 2 (5,350 m) of what was
almost certainly high-altitude pulmonary oedema (HAPE) --- then
unrecognised. He developed a severe headache and respiratory
distress; attempts to administer oxygen failed. A formal funeral was
conducted because, as Bechtold recorded, "the Balti porters most
certainly would never have gone beyond Camp 2" without it. The
porters' understanding of death on the mountain demanded ritual
acknowledgement.

*** The Storm and the Retreat

On 6 July, Peter Aschenbrenner and Erwin Schneider reached an
estimated height of 7,895 m --- the highest point achieved. But
weather was deteriorating. On 7 July, a ferocious storm trapped
fourteen climbers at approximately 7,480 m near the Silver Saddle.

Aschenbrenner and Schneider descended rapidly, reaching safety but
controversially leaving others behind (a decision debated for decades).
The remaining climbers, weakened and without adequate supplies, began a
desperate retreat that lasted days.

Willy Merkl, Willo Welzenbach, and Uli Wieland died of exhaustion,
exposure, and altitude sickness during the descent. Six Sherpa porters
died alongside them or attempting to bring them down.

** C.3.4 The 1937 Catastrophe

*** The Decision to Return

Despite the 1934 disaster, the Deutsche Himalaya-Stiftung organised a
new expedition for 1937. The mountain had become the /Schicksalsberg/
--- the "mountain of destiny" --- and turning back was politically
unthinkable under the Nazi regime.

*** The Expedition

*Leader*: Karl Wien (1906--1937), a respected Munich mountaineer.

*Team* (climbers and scientists): Karl Wien, Hans Hartmann, Adolf
Göttner, Martin Pfeffer, Günther Hepp, Peter Müllritter, Pert
Fankhauser (climbers); Ulrich Luft and Karl Troll (scientists, not
present at Camp IV).

*Sherpa porters*: Nine men from Darjeeling, including Pasang Norbu
(a survivor of 1934).

*** The Avalanche

Sometime between 14 and 16 June 1937, a massive serac collapsed from
the hanging glacier on Rakhiot Peak. The resulting avalanche swept
across the flat terrace where Camp IV was established, burying the
tents under metres of ice and packed snow. The avalanche ran 1,200 m
across terrain that had seemed safe.

All sixteen men in camp---seven Germans and nine Sherpas---were killed
in their sleeping bags. There were no survivors at the camp.

*** Discovery

On 17 June, Ulrich Luft, ascending from a lower camp, found "a
gigantic ice avalanche" covering the Camp IV site. A serac 300 metres
above had broken away, creating "a vast field of enormous ice-blocks
and debris." The camp was buried under approximately 2.5 metres of
ice.

Search parties took until 15 July to excavate the site. They found
the climbers "lying peacefully in their sleeping-bags" with diaries
dated through 14 June.

Paul Bauer received the news in Munich on 20 June --- a journalist
telephoned with "the terrible news that seven climbers and nine
porters had been killed on Nanga Parbat by an avalanche."

*** The Sherpa Leader's Request

The Sherpa leader Nursang, who was not at Camp IV, requested that the
Sherpa dead be left undisturbed per their religious beliefs.

** C.3.5 The 1938 Expedition: Paul Bauer

*** Porter Recruitment and the Mountain's Reputation

Paul Bauer led the 1938 expedition, the first after the 1937
catastrophe. The mountain had now killed 26 people in two
expeditions. Recruiting porters proved deeply difficult.

Bauer recorded in the /Himalayan Journal/:

#+begin_quote
"Enlisting the porters for the high camps caused me some anxiety, for
there was much talk in Darjeeling about evil spirits and devils on
Nanga Parbat, and none of the surviving Sherpa porters were willing to
go again."
#+end_quote

--- Paul Bauer, "Nanga Parbat, 1938," /Himalayan Journal/, Vol. 11
(1939).

This passage is significant: the Sherpa community in Darjeeling had
developed its own spiritual interpretation of the mountain's lethality.
The talk of "evil spirits and devils" was not superstition opposing
science---it was a community's framework for processing repeated,
inexplicable mass death. The mountain was understood to be /inhabited/
by malevolent forces, and going there was not merely dangerous but
spiritually polluting.

Bauer ultimately hired ten young, less-experienced Sherpas and Bhutias
through the assistance of the Himalayan Club and his longtime porter
Nursang.

*** The Expedition

Bauer, mindful of the previous disasters, was cautious. The
expedition was plagued by bad weather. Bauer ordered the party down
before the Silver Saddle was reached. *No fatalities.* But no
significant progress beyond previous high points either.

** C.3.6 The 1939 Reconnaissance: Aufschnaiter and Harrer

*** The Expedition

*Leader*: Peter Aufschnaiter (1899--1973), a Tyrolean mountaineer and
cartographer.

*Team*: Heinrich Harrer, Ludwig "Lutz" Chicken, Hans Lobenhoffer.

The small four-man party explored the Diamir (western) Face, seeking
an alternative to the deadly Rakhiot route. They concluded that the
Diamir Face was viable---a judgment that would be vindicated decades
later when Reinhold Messner made the first solo ascent of an
8,000-metre peak by that route (1978).

*** The Outbreak of War

Having completed the reconnaissance, the party was in Karachi at the
end of August 1939, waiting for a freighter home. On 3 September,
Britain declared war on Germany. All four were arrested as enemy aliens
and interned.

They were transferred first to Ahmednagar (near Bombay), then to a
larger detention camp at Dehra Dun, where they were held with
approximately 1,000 other enemy aliens.

*** The Escape

On 29 April 1944, a group of seven escaped: Harrer, Aufschnaiter,
Bruno Treipel, Hans Kopp, Sattler, and two others (Rolf Magener and
Heins von Have, disguised as British officers).

Magener and von Have took the train to Calcutta and reached the
Japanese army in Burma. The others headed for Tibet. After Sattler
dropped out on 10 May, the remaining four crossed the Tsang Chok-la
Pass (5,896 m) on 17 May 1944. Eventually only Aufschnaiter and
Harrer continued, reaching Lhasa on 15 January 1946.

Harrer's account of his seven years in Tibet became one of the most
famous travel books of the 20th century. Aufschnaiter, the more
serious scholar, produced invaluable cartographic and ethnographic
work on Tibet that remains underappreciated.

** C.3.7 The Schicksalsberg Narrative

The /Schicksalsberg/ ("mountain of destiny") narrative requires its
own examination because it shaped how an entire generation understood
Nanga Parbat---and because it is still repeated, often uncritically.

*** Origins

After the 1934 disaster killed four German climbers (Merkl,
Welzenbach, Wieland, Drexel), the Nazi propaganda apparatus seized the
narrative. The dead were styled as heroes who had fallen in a struggle
of "blood and soil" --- the mountain as adversary in a test of racial
will.

The Deutsche Himalaja-Stiftung (German Himalayan Foundation), founded
in Munich in 1936, was tasked with organising future expeditions. Its
explicit goal was the first ascent of the "/Schicksalsberg der
Deutschen/" --- "the destiny mountain of the Germans." The Foundation
was politically supported by the NSDAP and served as a propaganda
instrument, responsible "not only for spectacular headlines about
expeditions but also for the popularization of athletic training for
the impending war."

*** Instrumentalisation

The Nazis used Nanga Parbat for:
- Demonstrating German will and racial vigour
- Justifying sacrifice (the dead as martyrs)
- Projecting imperial ambition beyond Europe
- The 1937 catastrophe was exploited in radio broadcasts and newsreels

*** Post-war Continuity

The narrative survived 1945 in diluted form. Karl Herrligkoffer, Willy
Merkl's half-brother, organised the 1953 expedition explicitly as a
"/Willy-Merkl-Gedächtnis-Expedition/" (Willy Merkl Memorial
Expedition). The mountain remained "Germany's mountain" in popular
imagination well into the 1960s, even as its geopolitical context
(Pakistan, not British India) rendered the framing absurd.

** C.3.8 Hermann Buhl and the First Ascent (1953)

*** The Expedition

*Official name*: Deutsch-Österreichische Willy-Merkl-Gedächtnis-Expedition
(German-Austrian Willy Merkl Memorial Expedition).

*Leader*: Karl Maria Herrligkoffer (1916--1991), a Munich physician
and Willy Merkl's half-brother. Herrligkoffer had no significant
mountaineering credentials. His claim to leadership was genealogical:
his brother had died on the mountain, and he had secured the permit
and funding.

*Climbing team*: Hermann Buhl, Otto Kempter, Hans Ertl, Walter
Frauenberger, Albert Bitterling, Kuno Rainer, and others.

*** The Mutiny

On 30 June 1953, Herrligkoffer radioed an order to retreat from base
camp. The four lead climbers --- Buhl, Frauenberger, Ertl, and
Kempter --- ignored the order. They continued climbing, establishing a
precarious Camp V at 6,950 m on the East Ridge.

*** The Solo Summit Push

On the night of 2--3 July 1953, Hermann Buhl (1924--1957) left Camp V
alone at approximately 02:30. No other climber was fit or willing to
accompany him. He carried no supplemental oxygen.

His account, published as /Nanga Parbat Pilgrimage/ (German: /Achttausend
drüber und drunter/, 1954), describes the seventeen-hour ascent:

#+begin_quote
"I walked and walked, climbed and climbed, for hours and hours...
But this was quite different; now it was an incredible irresistible
urge that drove my exhausted body onward."
#+end_quote

--- Hermann Buhl, /Nanga Parbat Pilgrimage/ (English trans. Hugh Merrick,
1956).

*** Pervitin

During the ascent and descent, Buhl took Pervitin
(methamphetamine) --- two pills on the way up, three on the way down.
Pervitin was widely used in the German and Austrian military during
World War II; it prevents sleep and suppresses fatigue. Buhl's use of
it was not concealed; he reported it openly in his account. At the
extreme altitudes, he experienced "a dangerous state of apathy"
and perceptual illusions.

*** The Summit

Buhl reached the summit at approximately 19:00 on 3 July 1953, after
seventeen hours of continuous climbing from Camp V. He was alone. He
had carried an ice axe, which he planted on the summit, and a
small Pakistani flag and the pennant of his Innsbruck climbing club.
No German flag.

*** The Standing Bivouac

Descending in darkness, Buhl was forced to halt around 21:00 at
approximately 8,000 m. He spent the night standing on a narrow ledge,
holding a small handhold with one hand. He dozed occasionally but
maintained his balance. After a sleepless night, he resumed his descent
at 04:00.

*** The Return

After *41 hours* of continuous effort --- ascending, summiting, the
open bivouac, and descending --- Buhl arrived back at Camp V in states
of extreme exhaustion and dehydration. He had severe frostbite on
several toes, which were later amputated.

*** The Aftermath

Herrligkoffer received Buhl's success badly. Buhl had defied orders,
summited alone, planted a Pakistani flag rather than a German one, and
become an international hero without Herrligkoffer's permission.
Herrligkoffer attempted to minimise Buhl's role in public accounts.
Buhl retaliated by publishing /Nanga Parbat Pilgrimage/ and giving
lectures without Herrligkoffer's contractual permission, leading to
legal disputes.

*** Buhl's Death

Hermann Buhl died on 27 June 1957 on Chogolisa (7,665 m), when a
cornice collapsed beneath him. His body was never recovered. He was
32 years old.

* C.4 British Colonial Administration

** C.4.1 The Gilgit Agency

The Gilgit Agency was the British administrative unit responsible for
the northernmost frontier of British India, established to monitor
Russian movements in Central Asia (the "Great Game") and to control
the passes linking India to Xinjiang and Afghanistan.

*** Timeline

- *1877*: Major John Biddulph appointed as first British agent in
  Gilgit (Officer on Special Duty), tasked with monitoring the
  Baroghil and Ishkoman passes and safeguarding trade caravans.
  The posting lasted until 1881.
- *1889*: Permanent Political Agent established in Gilgit.
- *1889--1894*: Colonel Algernon Durand served as Political Agent.
  Directed the 1891 Hunza-Nagar Campaign (capture of Nilt Fort).
  Wounded in action.
- *1920--1924*: D. L. R. Lorimer served as Political Agent.
- *1935*: The Gilgit Agency was leased by the British from the Maharaja
  of Jammu & Kashmir for 60 years.
- *1 August 1947*: Lease returned to the Maharaja upon British withdrawal.
- *1 November 1947*: The Gilgit Scouts revolt.

** C.4.2 John Biddulph and /Tribes of the Hindoo Koosh/ (1880)

Major John Biddulph (1840--1921) served as the first British Political
Agent in Gilgit (1877--1881) and took part in the Yarkand Mission of
1873--74. His book, published in 1880, is the first systematic
ethnography of the peoples of the Hindu Kush and Karakoram:

#+begin_quote
/Tribes of the Hindoo Koosh/ (Calcutta: Office of the Superintendent
of Government Printing, 1880).
#+end_quote

The book covers the inhabitants, culture, society, and history of
Yasin, Torwal, Bushkar, Baltistan, and Dardistan. Chapters address
geography, castes and administration, customs, festivals, religions,
and regional princes. Extensive appendices document the grammar and
vocabulary of ten dialects and languages "scarcely studied by
academics" at that time.

Biddulph's work is invaluable precisely because it records societies
before the full weight of colonial administration transformed them.
It is also a colonial document, written for colonial purposes --- the
knowledge was "invaluable to contemporary diplomats and military
leaders who were extending British influence in northern India and
Central Asia."

** C.4.3 Algernon Durand and /The Making of a Frontier/ (1899)

Colonel Algernon George Arnold Durand (1854--1923) served as Political
Agent in Gilgit from 1889 to 1894. His brother, Mortimer Durand, was
the Foreign Secretary of the Government of India who drew the
Durand Line.

Algernon Durand's memoir:

#+begin_quote
/The Making of a Frontier: Five Years' Experiences and Adventures in

John Murray, 1899).
#+end_quote

The book covers the 1891 Hunza-Nagar Campaign in detail, including the
assault on Nilt Fort. Durand was wounded during the campaign and later
served as Military Secretary to the Viceroy of India.

** C.4.4 D. L. R. Lorimer: Political Agent and Linguist (1920--1924)

David Lockhart Robertson Lorimer (1876--1962), a Scottish-born
British Army officer in the Indian Political Service, served as
Political Agent in Gilgit from 1920 to 1924. He is one of the rare
colonial administrators whose lasting contribution was not military or
political but linguistic.

*** Linguistic Work

During his Gilgit years, Lorimer studied Khowar, Shina, and
Burushaski---the latter being a language isolate spoken in the Hunza
and Nagar valleys, unrelated to any known language family. In 1934 he
returned to the area for a year to complete fieldwork.

His publications:
- /The Burushaski Language/, Vol. I: Introduction and Grammar (1935)
- /The Burushaski Language/, Vol. II: Texts and Translations (1935)
- /The Burushaski Language/, Vol. III: Vocabulary (1938)
- /The Wakhi Language/ (1958)
- /The Dum'ki Language/ (1939)
- /Syntax of Colloquial Pashtu/ (1915)

Lorimer's Burushaski documentation remains the foundational reference
for the study of this isolate.

*** What He Documented Beyond Linguistics

Lorimer also collected "information of an ethnographic nature" during
his fieldwork. His papers are held at the School of Oriental and
African Studies (SOAS), London.

** C.4.5 Emily Overend Lorimer

Emily Overend Lorimer OBE (1881--1949), D.L.R.'s wife, was an
Anglo-Irish journalist, linguist, political analyst, and writer in her
own right. She accompanied her husband to Gilgit and to the 1934--35
return trip to the Hunza-Nagar region.

*** Publications

- /Language Hunting in the Karakoram/ (1939) --- her account of the
  1934--35 expedition, written for a popular audience. Documents daily
  life, crafts, and society in Hunza.
- Articles in /The Illustrated London News/ (19 October 1935):
  - "The Crafts and Husbandry of the Burusho: A Mountain People in
    the 'Apex' of India"
  - "Domestic and Outdoor Life in Hunza: A Peasant Community whose
    Language is Unique"
- Co-author with D.L.R. of /Persian Tales/ (1919), fifty-eight
  traditional folktales from Kermani and Bakhtiari dialects.

Emily Lorimer's writing provides a dimension absent from most colonial
accounts: attention to domestic life, women's work, and the material
culture of everyday existence.

** C.4.6 The Gilgit Scouts and the 1947 Revolt

*** The Force

The Gilgit Scouts were a paramilitary force established by the British
to patrol the northern frontier. Recruited locally from Gilgit,
Hunza, Nagar, and surrounding districts, they were officered by
British and later seconded Indian Army officers.

*** The 1947 Revolt

When British India was partitioned on 15 August 1947, the Gilgit
Agency's lease was returned to the Maharaja of Jammu & Kashmir.
The Maharaja's accession to India on 26 October 1947 placed
Gilgit---a predominantly Muslim region with cultural and trade ties to
the west and north---under Indian sovereignty.

On *1 November 1947*, Major William Alexander Brown, the British
officer commanding the Gilgit Scouts, organised a coup d'etat
(codenamed *Operation Datta Khel*). The Scouts arrested the
Maharaja's governor, Brigadier Ghansara Singh, and took control of
Gilgit.

On *2 November*, the Pakistani flag was raised on the old tower in the
Gilgit Scout Lines. On 16 November, a Pakistani political agent, Khan
Mohammad Alam Khan, arrived and assumed administration.

*** Brown's Role

Major Brown later wrote a memoir, /Gilgit Rebellion: The Major Who
Mutinied Over Partition of India/, in which he described his motives.
While claiming to act independently, Brown was covertly supported by
certain British individuals sympathetic to Pakistan. The British
government never officially sanctioned his actions, but King George VI
later awarded Brown an OBE---suggesting tacit approval.

*** Consequences

The revolt led directly to Pakistan's control of Gilgit-Baltistan.
Combined with the tribal invasion of Kashmir and the subsequent
Indo-Pakistani war, it created the territorial division that persists
today. Gilgit-Baltistan remains constitutionally ambiguous: neither a
full province of Pakistan nor part of the contested Azad Kashmir.
